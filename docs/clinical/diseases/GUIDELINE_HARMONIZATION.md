## 4. Areas of Disagreement

### 4.1 IgA Nephropathy Steroid Use

**Nature of Disagreement:** Optimal patient selection, dosing, and duration of corticosteroid therapy in IgA nephropathy.

**KDIGO Position:**
- After 3 months of optimized supportive care (ACEi/ARB +/- SGLT2i), consider steroids if proteinuria >0.75 g/day despite supportive therapy
- Use reduced-dose regimen per TESTING trial: oral methylprednisolone 0.4 mg/kg/day (max 32 mg) x 2 months then taper over 4-6 months
- Caution advised if eGFR <30 mL/min/1.73m2
- Do not use steroids with eGFR <15 or in elderly
- Recommend MEST-C assessment before steroid decision

**ERA/EDTA Position:**
- Recommend steroid therapy after 3 months optimized supportive care if proteinuria >0.75 g/day
- Intravenous methylprednisolone pulse (0.5-1.0 g/month x 3) followed by oral prednisone taper
- Avoid high cumulative doses; use limited course approach
- Consider STOP-IgAN trial findings: steroids reduce proteinuria but increase adverse events
- Caution in advanced CKD

**ISN Position:**
- Consider steroids if proteinuria >1 g/day despite maximal supportive care
- Risk-benefit assessment critical; obesity, diabetes, osteoporosis, infection risk considered
- MEST-C score with C1/C2 classification guides decision
- Use reduced-dose regimen

**Key Disagreement Points:**
1. **Proteinuria threshold for initiation:** KDIGO/ERA 0.75 g/day vs ISN 1.0 g/day
2. **Corticosteroid dosing:** Reduced-dose TESTING protocol (KDIGO) vs intravenous pulse + oral (ERA/EDTA)
3. **eGFR cutoff:** KDIGO more restrictive at <30 than ISN
4. **Duration:** 6 month reduced course (KDIGO) vs variable up to 12 months (ERA)
5. **Role of MEST-C score:** All recognize but KDIGO more integrated into decision-making
6. **Combination with other immunosuppression:** KDIGO more conservative; ISN more open to trials

**Harmonization Potential:**
- Moderate agreement on patient selection criteria
- Increased agreement around reduced-dose steroids
- SGLT2i addition may reduce need for steroids going forward
- Future complement inhibition (iptacopan) and APRIL blockade may obviate need for steroids

### 4.2 Lupus Nephritis Classification

**Nature of Disagreement:** Optimal initial immunosuppression choice, steroid dosing, and classification of lupus nephritis subtypes.

**KDIGO Position:**
- MMF 2-3 g/day or IV cyclophosphamide for Class III/IV induction
- MMF for Class V membranous lupus if nephrotic
- Voclosporin + MMF as alternative for multi-target therapy
- Belimumab add-on for incomplete response
- Reduced steroid regimens recommended
- Duration of maintenance: minimum 3 years

**ACR Position:**
- MMF or cyclophosphamide for Class III/IV induction
- MMF preferred for African American/Hispanic patients
- Lower threshold for adding belimumab
- Support voclosporin as add-on
- Steroid taper to <10 mg/day by 3 months
- Maintenance for minimum 1-2 years after complete remission

**EULAR/ERA-EDTA Position:**
- MMF preferred for induction; cyclophosphamide reserved for severe or refractory
- Belimumab as adjunctive therapy from start in active LN
- Voclosporin/tacrolimus as alternative for multi-target therapy
- Hydroxychloroquine for all patients without contraindication
- Aggressive steroid minimization
- At least 3-5 years maintenance therapy
**ISN Position:**
- MMF first-line; cyclophosphamide for severe/proliferative
- Calcineurin inhibitors for resistant disease
- Belimumab for persistent activity despite MMF
- Biomarker-guided therapy emerging
- Steroid minimization

**Key Disagreement Points:**
1. **Belimumab positioning:** Add-on after incomplete response (KDIGO) vs adjunctive from start (EULAR)
2. **Cyclophosphamide vs MMF as first-line:** KDIGO equal; EULAR/ERA-EDTA MMF preferred
3. **Maintenance duration:** ACR more flexible (1-2 years) vs KDIGO (3 years) vs EULAR (3-5 years)
4. **Multi-target therapy with CNI:** Voclosporin more established in KDIGO vs tacrolimus in EULAR
5. **Renal response definition:** Minor differences in proteinuria thresholds
6. **Biopsy for Class V vs Class V + III/IV:** Management differences between guidelines

**Harmonization Potential:**
- High agreement on overall framework
- Increasing convergence on MMF as preferred induction
- Steroid minimization universally recommended
- Belimumab timing remains area of active research
- Expected harmonization with BLISS-LN, ALMS, and voclosporin trial data integration

### 4.3 Rituximab in Membranous Nephropathy

**Nature of Disagreement:** Optimal dosing regimen, monitoring strategy, and patient selection for rituximab therapy.

**KDIGO Position:**
- Rituximab recommended as first-line immunotherapy for primary MN
- Dosing options: 375 mg/m2 x 2-4 weekly doses OR 1 g x 2 doses 2 weeks apart
- Anti-PLA2R titer monitoring q3 months
- Re-treatment if PLA2R rises or proteinuria relapses
- Alternative: cyclophosphamide + steroids (modified Ponticelli) if rituximab unavailable

**ERA/EDTA Position:**
- Rituximab first-line therapy
- Prefer fixed-dose regimen: 1 g x 2 weeks apart
- Use anti-PLA2R to define immunological remission (antibody negative)
- Clinical remission follows immunological remission by 6-12 months
- Re-treatment based on antibody reappearance
- CNI as second-line alternative

**ISN Position:**
- Rituximab first-line for moderate-to-high risk patients
- Risk stratification: anti-PLA2R titer, proteinuria, eGFR, age
- 1 g x 2 regimen or 375 mg/m2 x 4
- Monitor anti-PLA2R q1-3 months
- CNI if rituximab contraindicated
- Cyclophosphamide reserved for severe/progressive disease

**Key Disagreement Points:**
1. **Dosing regimen:** KDIGO allows both approaches; ERA/EDTA prefers 1 g x 2
2. **Re-treatment criteria:** Antibody-based (ERA/EDTA) vs antibody + proteinuria (KDIGO)
3. **Risk stratification:** Formal vs informal
4. **Cyclophosphamide role:** Second-line (KDIGO, ISN) vs alternative when rituximab unavailable (ERA)
5. **CNI positioning:** Alternative (KDIGO) vs second-line (ERA/EDTA)
6. **Immunological vs clinical remission as endpoint:** ERA/EDTA emphasizes immunological first

**Harmonization Potential:**
- High agreement that rituximab is first-line
- Convergence on anti-PLA2R monitoring
- Dosing differences minor and based on center experience
- Cyclophosphamide role decreasing across all guidelines
- Future: obinutuzumab, anti-CD20 agents may further align practice

### 4.4 C3 Glomerulopathy Treatment

**Nature of Disagreement:** Role of immunosuppression, complement inhibitor therapy, and plasma exchange.

**KDIGO Position:**
- Supportive care with ACEi/ARB first-line
- Complement evaluation (C3, C4, CH50, CFH, CFI, CFB, anti-CFH antibodies)
- Genetic testing for complement regulatory genes and CFHR1-5
- Eculizumab: consider in rapidly progressive disease or severe DDD
- Immunosuppression not routinely recommended
- PLEX for anti-CFH autoantibody-positive disease
- No clear steroid recommendation

**ISN Position:**
- Comprehensive complement and genetic workup
- Supportive care as baseline
- Eculizumab in progressive disease despite supportive therapy
- Consider immunosuppression (steroids, MMF) in selected cases with inflammatory activity
- Anti-CFH antibody disease: PLEX + MMF + steroids
- Investigational therapies: complement pathway inhibitors (C3, factor B, factor D inhibitors)

**ERA/EDTA Position:**
- Full complement workup essential
- Supportive care with ACEi/ARB
- Limited evidence for immunosuppression; trial of steroids considered
- Eculizumab for progressive disease
- PLEX for complement-activating states or anti-CFH disease
- CFH replacement therapy investigational

**Key Disagreement Points:**
1. **Steroid efficacy:** KDIGO essentially no role; ISN/ERA variable consideration
2. **MMF role:** KDIGO not recommended; ISN may consider; ERA limited evidence
3. **Eculizumab threshold:** KDIGO restrictive; ISN and ERA broader based on progression
4. **Empiric immunosuppression:** KDIGO avoids; others may trial
5. **Genetic testing scope:** All agree but interpretation varies
6. **Anti-CFH disease management:** KDIGO focused PLEX; ISN PLEX + MMF + steroids

**Harmonization Potential:**
- Agreement on supportive care primacy
- Complement evaluation universally recognized
- Anti-CFH disease management converging
- Future complement inhibitor data will drive convergence
- Current best practice evolving rapidly

### 4.5 Transplant Screening Protocols

**Nature of Disagreement:** Surveillance biopsy protocols, DSA monitoring frequency, and BK virus screening duration.

**KDIGO Position:**
- Protocol biopsies: consider for high-risk patients; not mandatory
- DSA monitoring: baseline; then q3 months for first year; then annually
- BK virus screening: monthly for 6 months, then q3 months for 2 years
- EBV monitoring: in high-risk (D+/R-)
- Surveillance biopsies not routine

**AST Position:**
- Protocol biopsies: recommended in high-risk centers; useful for subclinical rejection
- DSA monitoring: baseline, 3, 6, 12 months, then annually; more frequent if DSA+
- BK screening: monthly for 6 months, then q3 months to 2 years
- EBV PCR q1-3 months for first year in high-risk
- Consider surveillance biopsies at 3, 12 months

**ISN Position:**
- DSA monitoring essential; schedule per center protocol
- BK screening standard
- Protocol biopsies: consider in high immunological risk
- Molecular diagnostics emerging

**Banff Position:**
- Protocol biopsies key for classification validation
- Histologic monitoring essential for research
- Surveillance biopsies provide important data

**Key Disagreement Points:**
1. **Protocol biopsy routine vs selective:** AST more proactive; KDIGO more selective
2. **DSA monitoring frequency:** q3 months (AST) vs q3-12 months (KDIGO)
3. **Duration of BK screening:** 2 years (all) but intensity varies
4. **Role of molecular microscopy:** Banff supportive; KDIGO not yet standard
5. **Cost-effectiveness considerations:** KDIGO more cost-conscious
6. **Subclinical rejection treatment:** AST more aggressive; KDIGo individualized

**Harmonization Potential:**
- DSA monitoring increasingly accepted
- BK screening protocols converging
- Protocol biopsy acceptance varies by region/resource
- Molecular diagnostics likely to increase adoption
- Resource-dependent variation expected to persist

## Appendix: Consensus Recommendations

### A.1 Methodology for Consensus Determination

Consensus levels are defined as follows:
- **High Concordance:** >80% of guidelines agree on recommendation; minimal or no variation in language
- **Moderate Concordance:** 60-80% agreement; minor differences in thresholds, dosing, or patient selection
- **Low Concordance:** <60% agreement; significant differences in approach or contradictory recommendations

### A.2 High Concordance Recommendations (Harmonized)

1. ACEi/ARB is first-line therapy for all proteinuric CKD regardless of etiology (proteinuria >0.5 g/day)
2. BP target of <130/80 mmHg for all CKD patients; lower targets (<120/80) for those with significant proteinuria
3. Kidney biopsy is essential for diagnosis of glomerular diseases; performed before initiating immunosuppression
4. MMF or cyclophosphamide with corticosteroids remains standard induction for proliferative lupus nephritis Class III/IV
5. Hydroxychloroquine should be used in all patients with lupus nephritis unless contraindicated
6. Rituximab is first-line for induction and maintenance of ANCA-associated vasculitis
7. Rituximab is first-line immunotherapy for primary membranous nephropathy
8. Anti-PLA2R antibody testing is essential for diagnosis and monitoring of membranous nephropathy
9. Prednisone 1 mg/kg/day is first-line for MCD and primary FSGS (adults)
10. Plasma exchange + cyclophosphamide + steroids is standard for anti-GBM disease
11. BK virus PCR screening is mandatory post-transplant: monthly x6 months then quarterly x2 years
12. DSA monitoring is required post-transplant for AMR risk stratification
13. Tacrolimus + mycophenolate + steroids is standard initial immunosuppression for kidney transplant
14. SGLT2 inhibitors are recommended for CKD with type 2 diabetes regardless of glycemic control
15. Finerenone provides additional renal benefit in T2DM with CKD on maximal ACEi/ARB
16. Influenza and pneumococcal vaccines should be administered to all CKD and transplant patients
17. Live vaccines are contraindicated in immunosuppressed patients
18. MMF and cyclophosphamide are contraindicated during pregnancy; azathioprine and HCQ are acceptable
19. Complement evaluation (C3, C4, CH50, CFH, CFI, CFB, anti-CFH) should be performed for all suspected C3G
20. Genetic testing is recommended for suspected Alport syndrome and steroid-resistant FSGS

### A.3 Moderately Concordant Recommendations (Areas Requiring Individualization)

1. IgAN steroid initiation threshold: 0.75-1.0 g/day variation; individualize by risk
2. Lupus nephritis maintenance duration: 2-5 years based on response and relapse risk
3. Belimumab timing in lupus nephritis: add-on after incomplete response vs upfront adjunctive
4. Voclosporin positioning: multi-target therapy component vs second-line CNI alternative
5. Steroid dosing in ANCA vasculitis: standard vs PEXIVAS reduced-dose regimen
6. Plasma exchange in severe ANCA: selective use in dialysis-dependent or DAH
7. Protocol biopsy post-transplant: routine in high-risk centers vs case-by-case
8. CNI minimization strategies: conversion to mTOR-I vs belatacept vs dose reduction
9. Complement inhibitor use in C3G/DDD: threshold for initiation varies by guideline
10. MMF role in C3G: may be considered in selected patients with inflammatory activity

### A.4 Low Concordance Recommendations (Active Debate)

1. Routine surveillance biopsy in all transplant recipients vs selective approach
2. Tonsillectomy in IgA nephropathy: recommended only in selected patients with recurrent tonsillitis
3. Fish oil in IgA nephropathy: variable recommendations from not recommended to consider
4. Rituximab in primary FSGS: limited evidence; more accepted in recurrent post-transplant FSGS
5. Eculizumab in C3G/DDD: threshold for use varies significantly by guideline
6. Belatacept as first-line vs alternative immunosuppression in low-risk transplant
7. Extended corticosteroid therapy beyond 6 months in lupus nephritis
8. Best second-line therapy for steroid-resistant MCD: CNI vs cyclophosphamide vs rituximab
9. Role of mTOR inhibitors in de novo transplant immunosuppression vs conversion only

### A.9 Guideline Implementation Barriers

| Provider knowledge gaps | Inconsistent guideline application | CME programs; decision support tools | Professional societies |
| Patient non-adherence | Medication non-adherence in CKD | Multidisciplinary adherence programs | All stakeholders |

| Health literacy | Low understanding of complex regimens | Patient education materials | Foundation support |
| Care coordination | Fragmented nephrology-primary care transitions | Integrated care models | Health systems |

This Guideline Harmonization document was prepared as part of the GDES V4.2 project (Deliverable 8 of 12). The document systematically compares recommendations from major international nephrology guideline organizations to identify areas of concordance, discordance, and knowledge gaps.

**Methodology:**
1. Systematic review of all published guideline documents from KDIGO (2020-2021), ERA/EDTA (2022-2023), ISN (2023-2024), ASN (2023), KDOQI (2020-2022), ACR (2021-2022), AST (2022-2023), EULAR (2022-2023), and Banff (2019-2023)
2. Extraction of disease-specific and cross-cutting recommendations
3. Mapping of recommendations to harmonization framework
4. Consensus assessment by domain experts
5. Identification of knowledge gaps and future research priorities

**Limitations:**
- Guidelines published in different years may reflect different evidence bases
- Regional practice patterns influence guideline development
- Resource availability differs across settings
- Language barriers may affect interpretation

**Update Plan:**
- Annual review of new guideline publications
- Integration of major trial results (TESTING, PROTECT, TRANSFORM, etc.)
- Dynamic living document aligned with GDES V4.2 update cycle
