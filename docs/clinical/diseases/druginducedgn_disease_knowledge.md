## 2. Disease Record — Full 21-Field Schema

### 2.1 Definition

Drug-Induced Glomerular Disease encompasses a heterogeneous group of glomerular injuries caused by exposure to therapeutic agents. The most common patterns include: NSAID-induced minimal change disease (MCD) and membranous nephropathy (MN), lithium-induced MCD and FSGS, pamidronate-induced collapsing FSGS, interferon-induced collapsing FSGS and thrombotic microangiopathy (TMA), anti-TNF agent-induced lupus-like GN, immune checkpoint inhibitor (ICI)-induced acute tubulointerstitial nephritis (ATIN) and collapsing GN, and heroin-associated FSGS (heroin nephropathy). The hallmark is a clear temporal relationship between drug exposure and renal injury, and improvement/resolution upon drug withdrawal.

### 2.2 Epidemiology

Prevalence: drug-induced GN accounts for <1% of all GN diagnoses but is likely underdiagnosed. Largest categories: NSAIDs (most common drug class — 0.5-1% of chronic NSAID users develop MCD/MN), lithium (0.5-2% develop MCD/FSGS), immune checkpoint inhibitors (1-5% develop AKI — ATIN > glomerular), anti-TNF agents (0.5-1% develop lupus-like or ANCA-like disease), bisphosphonates (pamidronate: 10-20% of high-dose users develop collapsing FSGS), interferon-alpha (collapsing FSGS/TMA in 1-5%), heroin (FSGS in 5-10% of IV users). Age/sex: varies by drug. Increasing incidence with ICI therapy.

### 2.3 Aetiology

NSAIDs: COX-1/COX-2 inhibitors (ibuprofen, naproxen, diclofenac, indomethacin, celecoxib). Mechanism: altered T-cell cytokine profile (Th2 shift) leading to podocyte injury. Lithium: chronic therapy (years). Direct podocyte toxicity via GSK3-beta inhibition. Pamidronate (not zoledronate): high-dose IV for myeloma/bone metastases. Direct podocyte toxicity via FPP synthase inhibition. Interferons (alpha, beta, gamma): direct podocyte injury via JAK-STAT activation. Immune checkpoint inhibitors (anti-PD-1, anti-CTLA-4): T-cell activation against renal antigens (ATIN) ± podocyte injury (collapsing GN). Anti-TNF agents (infliximab, adalimumab, etanercept): type I interferon induction leading to lupus-like disease. Heroin: direct podocyte toxicity + APOL1 risk allele interaction. Other drugs: anti-epileptics (phenytoin), antibiotics (rifampin, vancomycin), allopurinol, gold, penicillamine (MCD/MN).

### 2.4 Pathophysiology

NSAIDs: inhibit COX-1/COX-2, shunting arachidonic acid metabolism toward leukotriene production. This promotes Th2-dominant T-cell response and cytokine release (IL-4, IL-13) that directly injures podocytes. The result is podocyte foot process effacement (MCD pattern) or immune complex deposition (MN pattern — usually PLA2R-negative). Lithium: enters podocytes via sodium channels, inhibits GSK3-beta, disrupting podocyte cytoskeleton and slit diaphragm integrity. Leads to MCD or FSGS after years of therapy. Pamidronate: inhibits FPP synthase in the mevalonate pathway, causing podocyte metabolic dysregulation and cell death. Produces collapsing FSGS (identical to HIVAN). Interferons: activate JAK-STAT signaling in podocytes, inducing cell cycle re-entry and dedifferentiation — collapsing FSGS phenotype. ICIs: remove PD-1-mediated immune tolerance, allowing autoreactive T-cells to attack renal tissue. ATIN is most common; podocyte injury less common but severe. Heroin: direct podocyte toxicity exacerbated by APOL1 risk variants.

### 2.5 Clinical Presentation

VARIABLE BY DRUG: NSAID-MCD: acute nephrotic syndrome weeks-months after NSAID use. Hematuria common. Hypertension rare. Resolution upon drug withdrawal (weeks). NSAID-MN: nephrotic syndrome with slower onset. Lithium-MCD/FSGS: insidious onset of proteinuria after years of therapy. May be nephrotic or subnephrotic. Pamidronate/IFN collapsing FSGS: nephrotic-range proteinuria, rapid eGFR decline, often ESKD. ICI-related: AKI (ATIN most common — sterile pyuria, eosinophilia), rarely nephrotic syndrome (collapsing GN). Anti-TNF lupus: arthralgia, rash, serositis, proteinuria, hematuria. Heroin nephropathy: nephrotic syndrome, progressive CKD over months-years, ESKD common. COMMON: temporal association with drug, improvement on withdrawal, recurrence on re-challenge.

### 2.6 Diagnostic Criteria

| # | Criterion | Essential | Evidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Temporal relationship between drug exposure and renal disease onset | Yes | 1 |
| 2 | Biopsy-proven glomerular injury pattern consistent with known drug effect | Yes | 1 |
| 3 | Improvement or resolution of proteinuria/renal function after drug withdrawal | Yes | 2 |
| 4 | Recurrence upon drug re-challenge (if documented) | No | 2 |
| 5 | Exclusion of other primary glomerular diseases (PLA2R-negative MN, idiopathic MCD, primary FSGS) | Yes | 1 |
| 6 | Recognition of classic drug-injury patterns (collapsing FSGS with pamidronate/IFN, MCD with NSAIDs) | No | 2 |

### 2.7 Differential Diagnosis

minimalChangeDisease, focalSegmentalGlomerulosclerosis, membranous, lupus, hivan, iga

### 2.8 Laboratory Findings

  * 
NSAID-MCD: nephrotic-range proteinuria, normal eGFR usually, microscopic hematuria (30%)  * NSAID-MN: subnephrotic or nephrotic proteinuria, PLA2R negative (important distinction from primary MN)  * Lithium: proteinuria (variable), eGFR variable, lithium level may be therapeutic  * Pamidronate/IFN collapsing FSGS: heavy proteinuria, rapid eGFR decline, ESKD often  * ICI-ATIN: AKI, sterile pyuria, WBC casts, eosinophiluria, eosinophilia (50%)  * ICI-collapsing GN: nephrotic proteinuria, rapid eGFR decline  * Anti-TNF lupus: ANA positive, anti-dsDNA positive, low C3/C4, proteinuria, hematuria  * Heroin nephropathy: nephrotic proteinuria, progressive eGFR decline  * Biopsy: definitive diagnosis

### 2.9 Biopsy Findings

  * 
NSAID-MCD: minimal change disease — normal LM, negative IF, podocyte foot process effacement on EM  * NSAID-MN: membranous pattern — subepithelial deposits, PLA2R-negative on IF, IgG4 may be present or absent  * Lithium-MCD/FSGS: MCD or FSGS patterns, morphologically identical to idiopathic forms  * Pamidronate/IFN collapsing FSGS: collapsing glomerulopathy identical to HIVAN — podocyte hyperplasia, capillary collapse, TRI on EM (if IFN: TRI present)  * ICI-ATIN: dense interstitial infiltrate (T-cells, plasma cells, eosinophils), tubulitis, ± granulomas  * ICI-collapsing GN: collapsing FSGS pattern, ± ATIN  * Anti-TNF lupus: full-house IF (IgG, IgA, IgM, C3, C1q) — indistinguishable from idiopathic lupus nephritis  * Heroin nephropathy: FSGS (classic NOS type or perihilar variant), IgM/IgG segmental sclerosis

### 2.11 Risk Stratification

| Factor | Impact | Risk Level |
|---|---|---|
| Duration of NSAID use >1 year | Higher risk of MN than MCD | Moderate |
| Lithium therapy >10 years | Higher risk of FSGS (irreversible) vs MCD (reversible) | Moderate |
| High-dose pamidronate (myeloma) | Up to 20% risk of collapsing FSGS | High |
| ICI combination therapy (anti-PD-1 + anti-CTLA-4) | Higher risk of ATIN (5-10%) vs monotherapy (1-3%) | High |
| Heroin use + APOL1 risk alleles | Very high risk of ESKD from heroin nephropathy | High |

### 2.12 Treatment Overview

FIRST-LINE: Drug withdrawal — the cornerstone of therapy. Most drug-induced GN resolves (weeks-months) after stopping the offending agent. NSAID-MCD: resolves in 4-12 weeks; corticosteroids rarely needed. Lithium-MCD: switch to alternative mood stabilizer; 60-80% improve. Lithium-FSGS: may not reverse after sclerosis established. Pamidronate/IFN collapsing FSGS: stop drug; steroids may help (limited). ICI-ATIN: steroids (prednisone 1 mg/kg) — rapid improvement. ICI-collapsing GN: stop ICI; steroids; may not reverse. Anti-TNF lupus: stop anti-TNF; may need steroids/MMF if severe. Heroin nephropathy: stop heroin; ACEi/ARB; often progresses despite cessation. SUPPORTIVE: RAASi for proteinuria, BP control. Corticosteroids for severe or progressive cases. Transplant: for ESKD — no recurrence if drug avoided. Immunosuppression generally avoided (drug-induced = stop the drug).

### 2.13 Treatment Algorithm

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | Identify and discontinue the offending drug — document temporal relationship |
| 2 | Renal biopsy to confirm pattern of injury and exclude idiopathic GN |
| 3 | NSAID-MCD: withdrawal alone; consider prednisone if persistent >12 weeks |
| 4 | Lithium MCD: switch to valproate or atypical antipsychotic. Monitor proteinuria |
| 5 | Pamidronate/IFN collapsing FSGS: stop drug. Consider prednisone 1 mg/kg (limited evidence) |
| 6 | ICI-ATIN: prednisone 1 mg/kg tapered over 4-8 weeks. ICI can often be resumed after resolution |
| 7 | ICI-collapsing GN: stop ICI permanently. Prednisone + MMF if severe. May not recover |
| 8 | Anti-TNF lupus: stop anti-TNF. Add hydroxychloroquine or steroids if moderate-severe |
| 9 | Heroin nephropathy: drug cessation + RAASi. Often progresses. ESKD management |
| 10 | Supportive: RAASi for proteinuria, BP <130/80 |

### 2.14 Monitoring Protocol

After drug withdrawal: Q1 month: eGFR, UPCR, BP for 3 months then Q3 months until stable. For persistent proteinuria: continue RAASi, Q3-6 monthly monitoring. Biopsy-confirmed FSGS or collapsing GN: monitor more frequently (Q1-3 months). If rechallenge needed (rare): monitor weekly for first month for proteinuria and eGFR. Lithium monitoring during therapy: UPCR, eGFR Q6 months.

### 2.15 Complications

  * 
ESKD — from irreversible FSGS/collapsing GN (pamidronate, IFN, heroin, long-standing lithium)  * Lithium toxicity if dose not adjusted for declining eGFR  * Steroid dependence in persistent NSAID-MCD  * ICI-ATIN: chronic kidney disease if not treated promptly with steroids  * Heroin nephropathy: rapid progression to ESKD

### 2.16 Relapse Information

Drug-induced GN does not relapse if the offending drug is avoided. Re-exposure to the same drug typically causes recurrence. Switching to a different drug in the same class (e.g., another NSAID) may also cause recurrence — class effect. Lithium: switching from lithium to another mood stabiliser resolves MCD but FSGS changes may be irreversible. If the drug is avoided, there is no disease recurrence. No need for immunosuppression once resolved.

### 2.17 Long-Term Prognosis

EXCELLENT for most: NSAID-MCD resolves fully in 4-12 weeks in >90%. NSAID-MN resolves in 3-12 months. Lithium-MCD: 60-80% resolve with drug switch. GUARDED: Pamidronate/IFN collapsing FSGS — 50% ESKD despite drug cessation. ICI-collapsing GN — often progresses. Heroin nephropathy — 90% ESKD within 2 years without cessation. Lithium-FSGS: may not reverse. Anti-TNF lupus: resolves upon drug withdrawal. Transplant: no recurrence if offending drug avoided.

### 2.18 Evidence Summary

No RCTs for drug-induced GN. Evidence from case series, registry data, and drug surveillance. NSAID-MCD: well-established association with hundreds of case reports. Lithium: longitudinal studies show 0.5-2% risk over 20 years. Pamidronate: case series (n=50+) showing collapsing FSGS. ICIs: growing literature — ATIN incidence 1-5%. Anti-TNF lupus: registry data from BIOBADASER showing 0.5-1% risk. Heroin nephropathy: classic studies from 1970s-1990s; APOL1 association described 2010+.
