## 1. Document Purpose

This document presents all 8 validated clinical cases for C3 Glomerulopathy covering the full spectrum of presentation types, including monoclonal gammopathy-associated C3GN, post-transplant recurrence, crescentic RPGN presentation, eculizumab therapy, CFH genetic mutation, paediatric DDD with retinal drusen, MPGN-pattern C3GN, and classic DDD with partial lipodystrophy. Each case includes presentation, diagnostic workup, management, and teaching points.

## 3. Case 1: C3GN Associated with Monoclonal Gammopathy

### 3.1 Presentation

A 62-year-old man presents with 3-month history of lower extremity oedema, foamy urine, and fatigue. He has a history of MGUS (IgG kappa) diagnosed 5 years ago but never treated.

### 3.2 Examination

- BP: 148/92 mmHg
- 2+ pitting oedema in lower extremities
- No skin rash, no arthritis

### 3.3 Laboratory Findings

| Parameter | Result | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| Serum creatinine | 1.8 mg/dL (eGFR 38) | CKD Stage 3b |
| UPCR | 4.2 g/g | Nephrotic-range proteinuria |
| Serum albumin | 2.8 g/dL | Low |
| C3 | 45 mg/dL (low) | Alternative pathway activation |
| C4 | 28 mg/dL (normal) | Key distinguishing feature |
| C3NeF | Positive | Supports C3G diagnosis |
| SPEP/UPEP/sFLC | IgG kappa monoclonal; free kappa 85 mg/L | MGUS confirmed |

### 3.4 Biopsy

- **LM:** MPGN pattern with mesangial hypercellularity, endocapillary proliferation, GBM double contours
- **IF:** Dominant C3 (3+) with trace IgG; C1q negative
- **EM:** Mesangial and subendothelial electron-dense deposits

### 3.5 Diagnosis

C3 Glomerulonephritis (C3GN) associated with monoclonal gammopathy of undetermined significance (MGUS).

### 3.6 Management

- MMF 1 g BID
- Prednisone 0.8 mg/kg/day (60 mg) with planned taper
- RAAS inhibition (lisinopril 20 mg/day)
- Haematology evaluation: bone marrow biopsy shows 8% kappa-restricted plasma cells
- Clone-directed therapy: daratumumab-based regimen initiated

### 3.7 Outcome

- **6 months:** UPCR improved to 1.2 g/g; eGFR stable at 40; C3 normalised
- **12 months:** UPCR 0.4 g/g; eGFR 42; haematologic response (M-spike reduced 80%)
- **24 months:** Sustained renal remission; clone in partial remission

### 3.8 Teaching Points

1. C3G in patients >40 years always requires MGUS workup
2. Clone-directed therapy can improve both haematologic and renal outcomes
3. MMF+steroids remain first-line for active C3GN even with MGUS
4. Multidisciplinary care (nephrology + haematology) essential

## 4. Case 2: Recurrent C3GN in Renal Transplant

### 4.1 Presentation

A 45-year-old woman with ESKD from C3GN received a deceased-donor renal transplant 18 months ago. Protocol biopsy at 12 months shows recurrent C3GN. She now has rising creatinine and new proteinuria.

### 4.2 Examination

- BP: 136/84 mmHg
- No oedema
- Graft non-tender

### 4.3 Laboratory Findings

| Parameter | Result | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| Serum creatinine | 1.5 mg/dL (eGFR 42) | Decline from baseline 1.0 |
| UPCR | 1.8 g/g | New proteinuria |
| C3 | 52 mg/dL (low) | Persistent complement activation |
| C3NeF | Positive | Same autoantibody as pre-transplant |
| Donor-specific antibodies | Negative | No rejection |

### 4.4 Biopsy (Protocol + Indication)

- **LM:** Mesangial hypercellularity, endocapillary proliferation (mild), no rejection
- **IF:** Dominant C3 (2+) 
- **EM:** Subendothelial deposits consistent with recurrent C3GN

### 4.5 Diagnosis

Recurrent C3 Glomerulonephritis in renal allograft.

### 4.6 Management

- Pre-emptive complement inhibitor recommended but not initiated at transplant
- Eculizumab 900 mg IV weekly x 4, then 1200 mg every 2 weeks
- MMF continued (maintenance immunosuppression)
- Meningococcal vaccination (quadrivalent + B) before eculizumab start

### 4.7 Outcome

- **3 months:** UPCR decreased to 0.6 g/g; Cr stable at 1.3 mg/dL
- **6 months:** C3 normalised; UPCR 0.3 g/g; eGFR 48
- **12 months:** Protocol biopsy shows reduced C3 deposits; no progression
- **24 months:** Sustained remission on eculizumab

### 4.8 Teaching Points

1. C3G has the highest post-transplant recurrence rate of all GNs (50-80%)
2. Pre-emptive complement inhibitor therapy should be strongly considered
3. Recurrence can occur even with adequate maintenance immunosuppression
4. C3NeF persistence predicts recurrence

## 5. Case 3: Crescentic C3GN Presenting as RPGN

### 5.1 Presentation

A 28-year-old woman presents with 2-week history of oliguria, gross haematuria, and rapidly rising creatinine. She reports fatigue and mild dyspnoea.

### 5.2 Examination

- BP: 162/98 mmHg
- Bibasilar crackles (volume overload)
- 1+ pitting oedema
- No palpable purpura

### 5.3 Laboratory Findings

| Parameter | Result | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| Serum creatinine | 4.2 mg/dL (eGFR 15) | Rapidly declining |
| UPCR | 5.6 g/g | Nephrotic-range |
| C3 | 38 mg/dL (very low) | Marked complement activation |
| C4 | 30 mg/dL (normal) | Alternative pathway |
| ANCA | Negative | Excludes vasculitis |
| Anti-GBM | Negative | Excludes Goodpasture |
| C3NeF | Positive | |

### 5.4 Biopsy

- **LM:** 40% crescents (cellular/fibrocellular); endocapillary proliferation; mesangial hypercellularity
- **IF:** Dominant C3 (3+) with trace IgG
- **EM:** Mesangial and subepithelial deposits; no intramembranous ribbon-like deposits

### 5.5 Diagnosis

Crescentic C3GN presenting as rapidly progressive glomerulonephritis (RPGN).

### 5.6 Management

- Pulse methylprednisolone 1 g IV x 3 days
- Cyclophosphamide 750 mg/m2 IV monthly
- Oral prednisone 1 mg/kg/day taper
- RAAS inhibition, BP control
- PLEX considered but ANCA/GBM negative; deferred

### 5.7 Outcome

- **Month 1:** Cr improved to 2.8 mg/dL; oliguria resolved
- **Month 3:** Cr 1.9 mg/dL (eGFR 34); UPCR 1.8 g/g
- **Month 6:** Cr 1.5 mg/dL; UPCR 0.8 g/g; C3 normalised
- **12 months:** Transitioned to MMF 1 g BID maintenance

### 5.8 Teaching Points

1. RPGN presentation in C3G requires aggressive immunosuppression
2. Cyclophosphamide + steroids remains standard for crescentic disease
3. C3NeF positivity supports diagnosis but does not guide therapy in acute phase
4. Long-term MMF maintenance recommended after cyclophosphamide induction

## 6. Case 4: Refractory C3GN Treated with Eculizumab

### 6.1 Presentation

A 38-year-old man with biopsy-proven C3GN failed MMF + steroid therapy after 8 months. UPCR remains 3.2 g/g, Cr rising to 2.1 mg/dL (eGFR 34).

### 6.2 Examination

- BP: 144/90 mmHg
- Mild oedema
- No extra-renal manifestations

### 6.3 Laboratory Findings

| Parameter | Result | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| Cr | 2.1 mg/dL | Declining |
| UPCR | 3.2 g/g | Persistent nephrotic |
| C3 | 48 mg/dL (low) | Persistent activation |
| CH50 | Low | Terminal pathway activation |
| C5b-9 | Elevated | MAC-mediated injury |

### 6.4 Biopsy (Repeat)

- **LM:** Persistent endocapillary proliferation; no change from baseline
- **IF:** C3 (2+) persistent
- **EM:** Deposits unchanged

### 6.5 Diagnosis

Refractory C3GN with evidence of terminal complement pathway activation.

### 6.6 Management

- Eculizumab 900 mg IV weekly x 4, then 1200 mg every 2 weeks
- Meningococcal vaccination (quadrivalent ACWY + MenB)
- Continue MMF 1 g BID; taper steroids
- RAASi continued

### 6.7 Outcome

- **3 months:** C3 normalised; UPCR 1.5 g/g; Cr stable at 2.0
- **6 months:** UPCR 0.7 g/g; CH50 <10% (effective blockade)
- **12 months:** UPCR 0.4 g/g; Cr 1.6 mg/dL (eGFR 48); C3 normal
- **24 months:** Sustained on eculizumab; no infectious complications

### 6.8 Teaching Points

1. Eculizumab (C5 blockade) is most effective when terminal complement activation is confirmed
2. CH50 monitoring ensures adequate complement blockade
3. Response may be delayed (3-6 months); patience required
4. Meningococcal prophylaxis is mandatory

## 7. Case 5: C3G with CFH Genetic Mutation

### 7.1 Presentation

A 24-year-old woman with no significant medical history presents with nephrotic-range proteinuria and hypertension. Family history: her brother had ESKD of unknown cause at age 30.

### 7.2 Examination

- BP: 158/96 mmHg
- Periorbital oedema
- No rash or arthritis

### 7.3 Laboratory Findings

| Parameter | Result | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| Creatinine | 1.6 mg/dL (eGFR 44) | CKD Stage 3a |
| UPCR | 5.8 g/g | Nephrotic |
| C3 | 32 mg/dL (very low) | Severe activation |
| C4 | 26 mg/dL (normal) | |
| C3NeF | Negative | Not autoantibody-mediated |
| CFH levels | 8 mg/L (low, normal 200-800) | Factor H deficiency |
| CFI | Normal | |

### 7.4 Biopsy

- **LM:** MPGN pattern with mesangial hypercellularity, endocapillary proliferation
- **IF:** Dominant C3 (3+)
- **EM:** Intramembranous and subendothelial deposits

### 7.5 Genetic Testing

- **CFH:** Heterozygous missense mutation (c.319C>T, p.Arg107Trp) in complement control protein domain 1
- **CFHR1-5:** No rearrangement detected

### 7.6 Diagnosis

C3 Glomerulopathy due to CFH mutation causing factor H deficiency.

### 7.7 Management

- Fresh frozen plasma (FFP) 15 mL/kg IV every 2 weeks
- RAAS inhibition (losartan 100 mg/day)
- BP control
- MMF + steroids considered but patient preferred plasma monotherapy
- Family screening for CFH mutation

### 7.8 Outcome

- **3 months:** C3 improved to 65 mg/dL; UPCR 3.2 g/g
- **6 months:** C3 normalised; UPCR 1.5 g/g; Cr 1.3 mg/dL
- **12 months:** UPCR 0.6 g/g; eGFR stable at 50
- **24 months:** Sustained improvement; considering complement inhibitor for complete remission

### 7.9 Teaching Points

1. CFH mutation is the most common genetic cause of C3G
2. Low factor H level + normal C3NeF suggests genetic aetiology
3. Plasma therapy provides exogenous factor H to restore regulation
4. Genetic counselling and family screening essential
5. Complement inhibitor may be more effective than plasma in the long term

## 8. Case 6: Paediatric DDD with Retinal Drusen

### 8.1 Presentation

A 10-year-old boy presents with 2-week history of periorbital oedema and foamy urine. He also reports blurry vision in the past month. No family history of kidney disease.

### 8.2 Examination

- Weight: 38 kg
- BP: 118/72 mmHg (normal for age)
- Periorbital and lower extremity oedema
- Fundoscopy: bilateral retinal drusen (drusen bodies) in macula

### 8.3 Laboratory Findings

| Parameter | Result | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| Cr | 0.6 mg/dL | Normal |
| UPCR | 6.5 g/g | Nephrotic |
| Albumin | 2.2 g/dL | Low |
| C3 | 28 mg/dL (very low) | Severe activation |
| C4 | 24 mg/dL (normal) | |
| C3NeF | Positive (high titre) | |

### 8.4 Biopsy

- **LM:** MPGN pattern with mesangial hypercellularity
- **IF:** Dominant C3 (3+); trace IgM
- **EM:** Intramembranous, ribbon-like, extremely electron-dense deposits — pathognomonic for DDD

### 8.5 Diagnosis

Dense Deposit Disease (DDD) with retinal drusen.

### 8.6 Management

- Prednisone 60 mg/m2/day (45 mg) for 4 weeks, then taper
- Supportive: RAASi, sodium restriction, statin
- Ophthalmology referral for drusen management
- Genetic testing: CFH, CFI, CFB, C3, CD46

### 8.7 Outcome

- **4 weeks:** Partial response; UPCR 3.2 g/g
- **8 weeks:** MMF 600 mg/m2 BID added for inadequate response
- **6 months:** UPCR 1.2 g/g; Cr normal; C3 62 mg/dL
- **12 months:** UPCR 0.6 g/g; vision stable; drusen unchanged on OCT
- **24 months:** MMF continued; no relapse; eGFR >90

### 8.8 Teaching Points

1. DDD is more common in children and presents with nephrotic syndrome
2. Retinal drusen are pathognomonic extra-renal manifestation of DDD
3. MMF should be added early if steroid response inadequate
4. Children with DDD have better renal prognosis than adults
5. Long-term ophthalmology surveillance needed

## 9. Case 7: C3GN with MPGN Pattern and C3NeF Positivity

### 9.1 Presentation

A 32-year-old man presents with microscopic haematuria found on routine physical exam. He also reports mild lower extremity oedema for 2 months.

### 9.2 Examination

- BP: 134/82 mmHg
- Trace oedema
- No rash or joint pain

### 9.3 Laboratory Findings

| Parameter | Result | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| Cr | 1.1 mg/dL (eGFR 72) | Mildly reduced |
| UPCR | 2.8 g/g | Moderate proteinuria |
| Albumin | 3.4 g/dL | Low-normal |
| C3 | 56 mg/dL (low) | |
| C4 | 32 mg/dL (normal) | |
| C3NeF | Strongly positive | Key aetiologic finding |

### 9.4 Biopsy

- **LM:** MPGN pattern with mesangial hypercellularity, double contours, endocapillary proliferation
- **IF:** Dominant C3 (3+); no significant Ig
- **EM:** Mesangial and subendothelial deposits; no intramembranous ribbon-like deposits

### 9.5 Diagnosis

C3 Glomerulonephritis (C3GN) with MPGN pattern, C3NeF positive.

### 9.6 Management

- MMF 1 g BID
- Prednisone 0.6 mg/kg/day (50 mg) with taper
- RAAS inhibition (ramipril 10 mg/day)
- Vaccination: pneumococcal, influenza

### 9.7 Outcome

- **3 months:** UPCR 1.2 g/g; Cr stable
- **6 months:** UPCR 0.5 g/g; C3 normalised; Cr 1.0 mg/dL
- **12 months:** Complete remission (UPCR 0.2 g/g); MMF continued; steroids tapered off
- **18 months:** Remission maintained; C3NeF titre declining
- **24 months:** MMF dose reduced to 500 mg BID

### 9.8 Teaching Points

1. Classical C3GN presentation: young adult with MPGN pattern on biopsy
2. C3NeF is the most common autoantibody finding in C3GN
3. MMF + steroid regimen achieves remission in 40-60% of C3GN
4. C3 normalisation is an early marker of treatment response
5. Long-term MMF maintenance may be needed to prevent relapse

## 10. Case 8: DDD with Acquired Partial Lipodystrophy

### 10.1 Presentation

An 8-year-old girl presents with facial fat loss noticed over 6 months and new-onset periorbital oedema. She has also had two episodes of Neisseria meningitidis infection in the past year.

### 10.2 Examination

- BP: 110/70 mmHg
- Facial: sunken cheeks, loss of buccal fat pad (acquired partial lipodystrophy)
- Mild peripheral oedema
- Fundoscopy: no drusen

### 10.3 Laboratory Findings

| Parameter | Result | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| Cr | 0.5 mg/dL | Normal |
| UPCR | 4.8 g/g | Nephrotic |
| Albumin | 2.4 g/dL | Low |
| C3 | <20 mg/dL (undetectable) | Profoundly low |
| C4 | 30 mg/dL (normal) | |
| C3NeF | Strongly positive (high titre) | |
| CH50 | <10% | Severely depressed |

### 10.4 Biopsy

- **LM:** MPGN pattern with mesangial hypercellularity
- **IF:** Dominant C3 (3+); C5b-9 positive
- **EM:** Intramembranous, ribbon-like, extremely electron-dense deposits

### 10.5 Diagnosis

Dense Deposit Disease (DDD) with acquired partial lipodystrophy and recurrent Neisseria infections.

### 10.6 Management

- MMF 600 mg/m2 BID
- Prednisone 60 mg/m2/day (32 mg) with taper
- Meningococcal vaccination + amoxicillin prophylaxis (history of Neisseria)
- RAAS inhibition (enalapril 0.2 mg/kg/day)
- Ophthalmology and endocrinology consultations
- Eculizumab considered given recurrent Neisseria (paradoxical: C5 blockade increases Neisseria risk but ongoing complement deficiency already permissive)

### 10.7 Outcome

- **3 months:** UPCR 2.8 g/g; C3 improving to 35 mg/dL
- **6 months:** UPCR 1.0 g/g; C3 55 mg/dL; no further infections
- **12 months:** UPCR 0.5 g/g; C3 normalised; lipodystrophy stable
- **24 months:** MMF maintenance; no relapse; eGFR >90

### 10.8 Teaching Points

1. DDD can present with acquired partial lipodystrophy (APL) due to complement-mediated adipocyte destruction
2. Recurrent Neisseria infections are a known complication of terminal complement deficiency
3. C3NeF-induced C3 consumption creates an acquired complement deficiency state
4. Paradoxically, C5 blockade with eculizumab may increase Neisseria risk
5. MMF + steroids can achieve remission even in DDD, though response rate lower than C3GN
