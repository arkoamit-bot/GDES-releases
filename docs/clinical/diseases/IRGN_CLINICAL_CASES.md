## 1. Classic Childhood PSGN after Strep Pharyngitis

### 1.1 Presentation

An 8-year-old boy presents with 3-day history of cola-coloured urine, periorbital oedema, and reduced urine output. Two weeks ago he had a sore throat treated with symptomatic measures. No previous renal history. Family history negative.

### 1.2 Examination

BP 145/95 mmHg (P95+10). Periorbital and bilateral lower extremity oedema (1+). Throat: mild erythema without exudate. Lungs clear. No rash or arthritis.

### 1.3 Diagnosis

Classic post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis (PSGN) — typical childhood presentation

### 1.4 Management

Amlodipine 2.5 mg daily for hypertension. Penicillin V 250 mg BID x10 days. Fluid restriction 1 L/day. Sodium restriction 2 g/day. Bed rest during acute phase.

### 1.5 Outcome

Day 5: BP normalised. Day 10: urine output improved. Day 14: Cr 0.6 mg/dL. Week 4: C3 68 mg/dL, UPCR 0.4 g/g. Month 3: C3 normal, UPCR 0.2 g/g, Cr normal. Month 6: fully recovered. Month 12: discharged.

### 1.6 Teaching Points

1. See text
2. Key diagnostic features: Classic post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis (PSGN) — typical childhood presentation
3. Treatment based on severity and histology

## 2. Adult PSGN with Crescentic Transformation

### 2.1 Presentation

A 42-year-old man presents with 1-week history of oliguria, dark urine, and progressive dyspnoea. History of hypertension and diabetes. Three weeks ago he had a skin infection on his leg that resolved without antibiotics.

### 2.2 Examination

BP 168/104 mmHg. Bibasilar crackles. 2+ pitting oedema to knees. Infected insect bite on right lower leg (healing). Distended neck veins.

### 2.3 Diagnosis

Post-streptococcal GN (impetigo-associated) with crescentic transformation in an adult

### 2.4 Management

Furosemide 40 mg IV BID. Methylprednisolone 500 mg IV daily x3 then prednisone 60 mg daily taper. Amoxicillin-clavulanate for cellulitis. Cyclophosphamide held as crescents <50% and improving. MMF 1 g BID added at week 4 for persistent proteinuria.

### 2.5 Outcome

Week 1: diuresis started, Cr improved to 2.8. Week 4: C3 58 mg/dL, Cr 1.8, UPCR 2.5. Month 3: C3 normal, Cr 1.4, UPCR 1.2. Month 6: Cr 1.2, UPCR 0.6. Month 12: Cr 1.1 (eGFR 65), UPCR 0.3. Persistent mild CKD but stable.

### 2.6 Teaching Points

1. See text
2. Key diagnostic features: Post-streptococcal GN (impetigo-associated) with crescentic transformation in an adult
3. Treatment based on severity and histology

## 3. Rapidly Progressive GN due to PSGN

### 3.1 Presentation

A 35-year-old woman presents with 5-day history of oligoanuria, haematuria, and rising creatinine from 1.0 to 5.2 mg/dL. She had pharyngitis treated with antibiotics 10 days ago.

### 3.2 Examination

BP 172/104 mmHg. Pulmonary oedema on CXR. Anasarca. Confused (possible hypertensive encephalopathy).

### 3.3 Diagnosis

Rapidly progressive GN (RPGN) due to severe crescentic PSGN

### 3.4 Management

Haemodialysis initiated (3 sessions). Methylprednisolone 1 g IV x3. Cyclophosphamide 750 mg/m2 IV x1 (held further due to neutropenia). Prednisone 60 mg daily taper. Plasmapheresis x5 sessions (equivocal benefit).

### 3.5 Outcome

Week 2: dialysis discontinued (urine output returned). Week 4: Cr 3.5, C3 45. Month 3: Cr 2.2, UPCR 2.0. Month 6: Cr 1.8 (eGFR 38), UPCR 0.8. Month 12: Cr 1.6, UPCR 0.4. Stage 3b CKD.

### 3.6 Teaching Points

1. See text
2. Key diagnostic features: Rapidly progressive GN (RPGN) due to severe crescentic PSGN
3. Treatment based on severity and histology

## 4. Adult PSGN with Nephrotic-Range Proteinuria

### 4.1 Presentation

A 50-year-old man with well-controlled type 2 diabetes presents with 3-week history of progressive leg oedema, foamy urine, and weight gain. He had a respiratory tract infection 4 weeks ago that resolved spontaneously.

### 4.2 Examination

BP 152/94 mmHg. Bilateral 3+ pitting oedema to thighs. Ascites. Lungs clear. Fundoscopy: background diabetic retinopathy.

### 4.3 Diagnosis

Post-streptococcal GN with nephrotic-range proteinuria in a diabetic adult

### 4.4 Management

Furosemide 40 mg BID. Losartan 100 mg daily. Atorvastatin 20 mg daily. No steroids. Tight glycaemic control.

### 4.5 Outcome

Month 1: UPCR 4.2, Cr 2.0, C3 52. Month 3: UPCR 2.5, Cr 1.8, C3 normal. Month 6: UPCR 1.2, Cr 1.7 (eGFR 38). Month 12: UPCR 0.6, Cr 1.6. Persistent proteinuria but improving; CKD stage 3a.

### 4.6 Teaching Points

1. See text
2. Key diagnostic features: Post-streptococcal GN with nephrotic-range proteinuria in a diabetic adult
3. Treatment based on severity and histology

## 5. Staphylococcal IRGN in an Elderly Patient

### 5.1 Presentation

A 72-year-old woman with diabetes, hypertension, and prosthetic mitral valve presents with 1-week history of oliguria, confusion, and dark urine. She was treated 3 weeks ago for MRSA wound infection following hip surgery.

### 5.2 Examination

BP 166/98 mmHg. Temperature 38.2°C. Disoriented. Bilateral crackles. 2+ oedema. Infected surgical wound (right hip) with purulent drainage.

### 5.3 Diagnosis

Staphylococcus aureus infection-related GN (MRSA) in elderly patient with prosthetic valve — IRGN variant

### 5.4 Management

IV vancomycin 15 mg/kg BID with therapeutic monitoring. TOE: small vegetation on prosthetic mitral valve without abscess. Furosemide 40 mg IV BID. Amlodipine 5 mg for BP. No steroids (infection risk). No dialysis (Cr improved with antibiotics).

### 5.5 Outcome

Week 2: blood cultures sterile, Cr improved to 3.0. Week 4: Cr 2.2, C3 normalising. Month 3: Cr 1.8 (eGFR 28), UPCR 0.8. Month 6: Cr 1.6, UPCR 0.4. Stage 3b CKD. Completed 6 weeks vancomycin.

### 5.6 Teaching Points

1. See text
2. Key diagnostic features: Staphylococcus aureus infection-related GN (MRSA) in elderly patient with prosthetic valve — IRGN variant
3. Treatment based on severity and histology

## 6. Viral Infection-Related GN from Influenza

### 6.1 Presentation

A 35-year-old previously healthy woman presents with 4-day history of haematuria, oliguria, and bilateral flank pain. She had influenza A infection 10 days ago with fever, myalgia, and cough that resolved.

### 6.2 Examination

BP 138/86 mmHg. Mild periorbital oedema. Costovertebral angle tenderness bilaterally. No rash or arthritis.

### 6.3 Diagnosis

Viral infection-related GN (Influenza A-associated acute glomerulonephritis)

### 6.4 Management

Supportive: rest, hydration, sodium restriction. No antibiotics or antivirals (influenza infection resolved). BP remained acceptable without medication.

### 6.5 Outcome

Week 2: Cr improved to 1.2, urine output normalised. Week 4: C3 normal, UPCR 0.3 g/g. Month 3: Cr 0.9 (eGFR >90), UPCR 0.1. Month 6: fully recovered. Discharged.

### 6.6 Teaching Points

1. See text
2. Key diagnostic features: Viral infection-related GN (Influenza A-associated acute glomerulonephritis)
3. Treatment based on severity and histology

## 7. Recurrent PSGN with Complement Deficiency

### 7.1 Presentation

A 12-year-old girl presents with her SECOND episode of acute nephritic syndrome. She had documented PSGN at age 8 with complete recovery. Current episode occurred 10 days after strep pharyngitis. She has a history of recurrent sinopulmonary infections.

### 7.2 Examination

BP 140/92 mmHg. Periorbital oedema. Throat erythematous. No rash.

### 7.3 Diagnosis

Recurrent post-streptococcal GN with underlying complement factor I deficiency

### 7.4 Management

Penicillin V 250 mg BID x10 days. Amlodipine 2.5 mg daily. Supportive care. Pneumococcal and meningococcal vaccination. Antibiotic prophylaxis (amoxicillin 250 mg daily) for recurrent infections. Genetic testing confirmed CFI heterozygous mutation.

### 7.5 Outcome

Week 4: C3 improved to 45 mg/dL, Cr 0.8, UPCR 0.5. Month 3: C3 52 mg/dL. Month 6: UPCR 0.3, Cr normal. On prophylactic antibiotics. No further infections at 12-month follow-up. Counselled on C3G risk and need for lifelong monitoring.

### 7.6 Teaching Points

1. See text
2. Key diagnostic features: Recurrent post-streptococcal GN with underlying complement factor I deficiency
3. Treatment based on severity and histology

## 8. Long-Term Remission After Crescentic PSGN

### 8.1 Presentation

A 28-year-old woman now 5 years after severe PSGN presents for annual follow-up. She had RPGN with 65% crescents at age 23, treated with pulse steroids and cyclophosphamide. She made a partial recovery with stable CKD stage 3a.

### 8.2 Examination

BP 128/78 mmHg (on lisinopril 10 mg). No oedema. Fundoscopy normal.

### 8.3 Diagnosis

Stable long-term remission after severe crescentic PSGN — CKD stage 3a secondary to PSGN

### 8.4 Management

Lisinopril 10 mg daily. No immunosuppression. Healthy diet, regular exercise. Avoids NSAIDs.

### 8.5 Outcome

Consistently stable Cr 1.2-1.4, eGFR 50-60, UPCR <0.3 over 5 years. No recurrence of GN. Married, planning pregnancy (pre-conception counselling done — low risk of recurrence). Continues annual nephrology follow-up.

### 8.6 Teaching Points

1. See text
2. Key diagnostic features: Stable long-term remission after severe crescentic PSGN — CKD stage 3a secondary to PSGN
3. Treatment based on severity and histology
