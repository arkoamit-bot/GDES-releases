## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Purpose

The GDES V4.2 Monitoring Protocol Library consolidates all drug-specific and disease-specific monitoring protocols into a single, reusable reference. Where V4.1 embedded monitoring schedules within individual disease guidelines, V4.2 decouples monitoring protocols from disease pathways, creating standalone protocol templates that can be composed into any treatment algorithm.

### 1.2 Rationale for Centralization

V4.1 presented monitoring as disease-bound. Clinicians managing patients on overlapping therapies (e.g., a transplant patient with lupus nephritis receiving both MMF and tacrolimus) were required to cross-reference multiple documents. Centralization eliminates redundancy, reduces interpretation error, and ensures a single source of truth for every monitoring parameter.

### 1.3 How to Use This Library

Each drug-specific protocol provides a self-contained monitoring schedule. Disease-specific frameworks layer disease activity parameters on top of drug protocols. Clinicians should:

1. Identify the drug(s) being administered and open the corresponding protocol(s).
2. Identify the underlying disease and overlay the disease-specific framework.
3. Apply the general renal monitoring framework as a minimum floor of surveillance.
4. Escalate or de-escalate monitoring frequency based on clinical stability.

### 1.4 Evidence Grading

All monitoring recommendations carry an evidence grade:

| Grade | Definition |
|-------|-----------|
| **A** | High-quality evidence from RCTs; strong recommendation |
| **B** | Moderate-quality evidence from cohort studies or RCTs with limitations |
| **C** | Low-quality evidence from case series, expert consensus, or biological plausibility |
| **D** | Very low-quality evidence; expert opinion only |

### 2.1 RAS Inhibitor (ACEi/ARB) Monitoring

#### Overview

RAS inhibitors (ACE inhibitors or angiotensin receptor blockers) are first-line therapy for proteinuric kidney disease. They reduce intraglomerular pressure, lower proteinuria by 30-40%, and slow GFR decline. Monitoring focuses on haemodynamic effects (hypotension, hyperkalaemia, acute kidney injury) and long-term renoprotection.

#### Baseline Investigations

| Test | Rationale |
|------|-----------|
| Serum creatinine and eGFR | Establish renal function before initiating therapy |
| Serum potassium | Risk of hyperkalaemia; contraindication if K+ > 5.5 mmol/L |
| Urine protein-to-creatinine ratio (uPCR) | Baseline proteinuria for response assessment |
| Urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio (uACR) | Alternative proteinuria marker |
| Blood pressure | Baseline for titration targets |
| Renal ultrasound | Exclude renal artery stenosis if clinical suspicion |
| Fasting glucose / HbA1c | Metabolic baseline |
| Full blood count | Anaemia baseline (ACEi can worsen erythropoietin response) |
| Uric acid | May rise on ACEi/ARB |

#### Monitoring Schedule

| Parameter | Frequency | Clinical Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|---------------------|
| Serum creatinine / eGFR | Baseline, 1-2 weeks post-initiation, then every 1-3 months | Accept up to 30% rise in creatinine from baseline; beyond this suggests renal artery stenosis or volume depletion |
| Serum potassium | Baseline, 1 week, 1 month, then every 1-3 months | Hyperkalaemia is the most common adverse effect; increased risk with CKD stage 4-5, diabetes, concurrent K+-sparing diuretics |
| Blood pressure | Every visit; self-monitoring at home encouraged | Target < 130/80 mmHg; symptomatic hypotension may indicate over-titration |
| Urine protein (uPCR/uACR) | Baseline, 3 months, 6 months, then every 6-12 months | Therapeutic response defined as >= 30% reduction in proteinuria at 3-6 months |
| Serum sodium | Every 3-6 months | Hyponatraemia can occur, especially with concurrent diuretics |
| Haemoglobin | Every 3-6 months | ACEi can reduce erythropoietin responsiveness; may worsen anaemia |
| Uric acid | Every 6-12 months | ARBs may increase uric acid levels |

#### Safety Monitoring

| Event | Action |
|-------|--------|
| Creatinine rise > 30% from baseline | Check volume status, reduce dose or hold; investigate renal artery stenosis if persistent |
| K+ >= 5.5 mmol/L | Dietary potassium restriction, review concurrent medications, consider sodium zirconium cyclosilicate |
| Symptomatic hypotension | Reduce dose, assess volume status, review antihypertensives |
| Angioedema (ACEi-specific) | Discontinue permanently; switch to ARB if needed |
| Persistent dry cough (ACEi) | Switch to ARB |
| New-onset proteinuria worsening | Reassess diagnosis, consider renal biopsy |

#### Response Assessment

- **Primary:** >= 30% reduction in uPCR or uACR at 3-6 months
- **Secondary:** Stabilisation of eGFR slope; blood pressure at target
- **Non-response:** If no reduction in proteinuria at 6 months at maximally tolerated dose, reassess adherence and diagnosis

#### Dose Adjustment Criteria

| Scenario | Action |
|----------|--------|
| K+ 5.0-5.4 mmol/L | Dietary counselling, recheck in 1-2 weeks; continue current dose |
| K+ >= 5.5 mmol/L | Reduce dose or hold; consider potassium binder |
| Creatinine rise 20-30% | Continue at current dose; recheck in 1 week |
| Creatinine rise > 30% | Withhold; investigate cause; restart at lower dose when resolved |
| Hypotension (SBP < 90 mmHg) | Reduce dose or withhold; reassess volume status |

#### Treatment Discontinuation Criteria

- Life-threatening hyperkalaemia
- Angioedema (ACEi)
- Renal artery stenosis confirmed
- Persistent symptomatic hypotension despite dose reduction
- Pregnancy (teratogenic)
- Bilateral renal artery stenosis

#### Long-Term Surveillance

- Annual electrolytes, renal function, and proteinuria
- Ongoing blood pressure monitoring with target maintenance
- Cardiovascular risk reassessment annually
- Monitor for new-onset diabetes (ACEi may be protective)

### 2.2 SGLT2 Inhibitor Monitoring

#### Overview

Sodium-glucose co-transporter 2 inhibitors (dapagliflozin, empagliflozin) reduce intraglomerular pressure via tubuloglomerular feedback, providing renoprotection independent of glycaemic control. Monitoring focuses on acute haemodynamic GFR dip, volume status, genitourinary infections, and diabetic ketoacidosis risk.

#### Baseline Investigations

| Test | Rationale |
|------|-----------|
| Serum creatinine and eGFR | eGFR must be >= 20 mL/min/1.73m2 for dapagliflozin (DKD indication) |
| HbA1c | Assess glycaemic status; not required for non-diabetic kidney disease indications |
| Blood pressure | Baseline for hypotension risk assessment |
| Urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio | Baseline proteinuria |
| Serum potassium | Baseline, especially if on RAAS inhibitors |
| Urinalysis | Exclude active urinary tract infection |
| eGFR trends (prior 3 months) | Document trajectory; an eGFR dip is expected and acceptable |
| Volume status assessment | Clinical assessment of dehydration risk |

#### Monitoring Schedule

| Parameter | Frequency | Clinical Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|---------------------|
| Serum creatinine / eGFR | Baseline, 2-4 weeks post-initiation, then every 1-3 months | Initial eGFR dip of 3-5 mL/min is expected and haemodynamic (not structural); stabilize by 4 weeks |
| Blood pressure | Baseline, 1 month, then every 3 months | SGLT2i cause modest BP reduction (3-5 mmHg systolic); additive with other antihypertensives |
| Urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio | Baseline, 3 months, 6 months, then every 6-12 months | Proteinuria reduction typically evident by 3 months |
| HbA1c (diabetic patients) | Every 3 months for first year, then every 6 months | Risk of hypoglycaemia when combined with insulin or sulfonylureas |
| Serum ketones (if symptomatic) | As clinically indicated | Euglycaemic DKA risk; check if nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, or malaise |
| Urinalysis for infection | As clinically indicated | Genitourinary mycotic infections, especially in women |
| Weight / volume status | Every 1-3 months | Volume depletion risk; particularly in elderly or those on diuretics |

#### Safety Monitoring

| Event | Action |
|-------|--------|
| eGFR dip > 10 mL/min from baseline within first 4 weeks | Exclude volume depletion, nephrotoxins, obstruction; if no cause, consider drug withdrawal |
| Genitourinary infection | Treat; may continue SGLT2i after resolution |
| Euglycaemic DKA | Discontinue SGLT2i; manage DKA per protocol |
| Fournier's gangrene (rare) | Immediate discontinuation; surgical emergency |
| Volume depletion / hypotension | Hold during acute illness; ensure adequate hydration |

#### Response Assessment

- **Primary:** Stabilisation or improvement of eGFR slope at 12 months
- **Secondary:** >= 30% reduction in uACR; reduced oedema
- **Expected trajectory:** Initial dip followed by plateau superior to untreated trajectory

#### Dose Adjustment Criteria

| Scenario | Action |
|----------|--------|
| eGFR dip 3-5 mL/min (first month) | Expected; no dose adjustment |
| eGFR dip > 10 mL/min | Hold drug; reassess volume status and nephrotoxin exposure |
| Symptomatic hypotension | Hold during acute illness; resume when euvolaemic |
| Recurrent UTI / mycotic infection | Consider discontinuation if severe or recurrent |
| HbA1c < 53 mmol/mol on insulin | Reduce insulin dose to avoid hypoglycaemia |

#### Treatment Discontinuation Criteria

- eGFR consistently < 20 mL/min/1.73m2 (check individual product labelling)
- Recurrent serious genitourinary infections
- History of recurrent DKA
- Planned surgery (hold 3-4 days before major surgery)

#### Long-Term Surveillance

- Annual renal function and proteinuria monitoring
- Cardiovascular event monitoring (MACE, heart failure hospitalisation)
- Bone density if concerns about fracture risk (emerging data)

### 2.3 Corticosteroid (Prednisolone) Monitoring

#### Overview

Prednisolone remains the backbone of immunosuppressive therapy in many glomerular diseases. Its broad immunosuppressive and anti-inflammatory effects come with substantial metabolic toxicity. Monitoring must balance efficacy against cumulative harm, particularly with prolonged use.

#### Baseline Investigations

| Test | Rationale |
|------|-----------|
| Blood glucose (fasting or random) and HbA1c | Steroid-induced hyperglycaemia baseline |
| Blood pressure | Steroid-induced hypertension baseline |
| Body weight and BMI | Metabolic monitoring baseline |
| DEXA scan | If prolonged high-dose therapy anticipated (age > 55, postmenopausal, prior fragility fracture) |
| Serum calcium, 25-OH vitamin D | Bone metabolism baseline |
| Lipid profile | Metabolic syndrome baseline |
| Ophthalmological assessment | Baseline cataract/glaucoma screening if high-risk |
| Hepatitis B serology (HBsAg, anti-HBc) | Risk of reactivation with immunosuppression |
| Tuberculosis screening (IGRA or TST) | Risk of TB reactivation |
| Serum creatinine, eGFR, electrolytes | Renal and electrolyte baseline |
| Full blood count | Leucocyte baseline |

#### Monitoring Schedule

| Parameter | Frequency | Clinical Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|---------------------|
| Blood glucose (fasting/random) | Weekly for first month during high-dose therapy; then every 1-3 months | Steroid-induced hyperglycaemia occurs in 20-40% of patients; often manifests within first week |
| Blood pressure | Every visit during high-dose therapy; monthly during taper | Steroid-induced hypertension; dose-dependent |
| Body weight | Every 1-3 months | Weight gain, fluid retention, Cushingoid features |
| HbA1c | Every 3 months if glucose elevated | Tracks glycaemic control over time |
| Serum lipids | Baseline, 3 months, then annually | Steroid-induced dyslipidaemia |
| Ophthalmological review | Annually during treatment; sooner if visual symptoms | Posterior subcapsular cataracts, glaucoma |
| Serum calcium and vitamin D | Every 6-12 months | Steroid-induced osteoporosis; supplement vitamin D |
| DEXA scan | Baseline if high-risk; repeat every 1-2 years if continued therapy | Osteoporosis screening and monitoring |
| Mood and psychiatric symptoms | Every visit, especially during dose changes | Steroid-induced psychosis, mood lability, insomnia |
| Blood glucose (home monitoring) | During high-dose pulses and taper | Patient self-monitoring for early detection |
| Serum cortisol (if tapering from prolonged use) | After discontinuation if symptoms of adrenal insufficiency | Adrenal suppression; may persist months after cessation |

#### Safety Monitoring

| Event | Action |
|-------|--------|
| New-onset hyperglycaemia | Initiate glucose monitoring; consider diabetes management; review steroid dose |
| Psychiatric symptoms | Low threshold for psychiatric referral; consider dose reduction |
| Osteoporotic fracture | Bone protection; consider steroid-sparing strategy |
| Avascular necrosis (hips, knees) | MRI if persistent joint pain; consider steroid cessation |
| Severe infection | Consider dose reduction; initiate prophylaxis per protocol |
| Posterior subcapsular cataract | Ophthalmology referral; may progress even after cessation |
| Adrenal crisis (during taper) | Hydrocortisone stress dosing; slow taper protocol |

#### Response Assessment

- **Primary:** Reduction in disease activity (proteinuria, haematuria, complement levels, autoantibody titres as disease-specific)
- **Secondary:** Inflammatory marker reduction (CRP, ESR)
- **Steroid-sparing goal:** Taper to <= 5 mg prednisolone daily or alternate-day dosing within 3-6 months where possible

#### Dose Adjustment Criteria

| Scenario | Action |
|----------|--------|
| Glucose > 11 mmol/L repeatedly | Consider concurrent diabetic management; discuss dose reduction |
| Significant mood disturbance | Dose reduction if disease activity allows; psychiatric input |
| Infection requiring antibiotics | Consider temporary dose reduction |
| Inadequate response at 4-6 weeks | Reassess diagnosis; consider steroid resistance vs. non-adherence |

#### Treatment Discontinuation Criteria

- Disease remission achieved with stable renal parameters
- Severe psychiatric toxicity
- Avascular necrosis
- Severe infection in immunocompromised host
- Uncontrolled diabetes despite maximum glycaemic management
- Goal: discontinue within 6-12 months where disease allows

#### Long-Term Surveillance

- Annual bone density monitoring if on prolonged therapy
- Annual cardiovascular risk assessment
- Metabolic syndrome screening (glucose, lipids, weight, BP)
- Ophthalmological screening annually
- Psychological well-being assessment

### 2.4 IV Methylprednisolone Pulse Monitoring

#### Overview

Intravenous methylprednisolone pulses (typically 500-1000 mg IV daily for 3 consecutive days) deliver high-dose immunosuppression for acute disease flares. The concentrated dosing schedule requires intensive short-term monitoring for cardiac, metabolic, and infectious complications.

#### Baseline Investigations

| Test | Rationale |
|------|-----------|
| Serum electrolytes (especially potassium, sodium) | Risk of hyperkalaemia and sodium/fluid retention |
| Blood glucose | Diabetogenic effect; may precipitate hyperglycaemic crisis |
| Blood pressure | Hypertension exacerbation |
| ECG | Arrhythmia risk, QTc interval; potassium-related changes |
| Serum creatinine, eGFR | Renal function baseline |
| Blood cultures (if febrile) | Exclude active bacteraemia before immunosuppressive pulse |
| Hepatitis B, CMV, EBV serology | Risk of viral reactivation |
| Chest X-ray | Exclude active infection |
| Full blood count | Leucocyte and platelet baseline |

#### Monitoring Schedule

| Parameter | Frequency | Clinical Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|---------------------|
| Blood glucose | Before each pulse and 4-6 hours post-pulse | Acute hyperglycaemia is near-universal; may exceed 20 mmol/L |
| Blood pressure | Before each pulse, during infusion, and 4-6 hours post | Acute hypertension exacerbation |
| Heart rate and rhythm | Continuous telemetry during infusion; pre- and post-pulse | Bradycardia, arrhythmia risk |
| ECG (12-lead) | Pre-first pulse; repeat if electrolyte disturbance | QTc monitoring; potassium-related changes |
| Serum potassium | Before each pulse and 4-6 hours post | Hypokalaemia from redistribution; risk of arrhythmia |
| Serum sodium and fluid balance | Daily during pulse course | Sodium and fluid retention |
| Blood glucose (finger-prick) | 2-hourly during pulse day and post-pulse | Sliding scale insulin may be required |
| Infection surveillance | Daily clinical assessment during and 2 weeks post | Immunosuppression window; viral reactivation |
| Mood / psychiatric symptoms | Daily during pulse and 1 week post | Acute steroid psychosis |

#### Safety Monitoring

| Event | Action |
|-------|--------|
| Blood glucose > 16 mmol/L | Initiate insulin sliding scale; adjust diabetic medications |
| K+ < 3.5 mmol/L | IV potassium replacement; cardiac monitoring |
| New arrhythmia or haemodynamic instability | Discontinue infusion; cardiology review |
| Seizure | Discontinue infusion; benzodiazepines; neurology review |
| Acute psychiatric symptoms | Low-dose antipsychotic; do not repeat pulses without psychiatric clearance |
| Serious infection post-pulse | Treat actively; consider IVIG if profound lymphopenia |

#### Response Assessment

- Clinical improvement within 1-2 weeks of pulse completion
- Reduction in disease activity markers (disease-specific)
- Decision to repeat pulse or transition to oral immunosuppression at 2-4 weeks

#### Dose Adjustment Criteria

| Scenario | Action |
|----------|--------|
| Persistent hyperglycaemia | Reduce or eliminate oral hypoglycaemics on pulse days; sliding scale insulin |
| Electrolyte disturbance | Correct before next pulse; extend monitoring interval |
| Psychiatric symptoms | Do not repeat pulse; switch to alternative immunosuppression |

#### Treatment Discontinuation Criteria

- Maximal planned course completed (typically 3 pulses)
- Serious adverse event during infusion
- Infection requiring systemic treatment
- Psychiatric contraindication to further pulses

#### Long-Term Surveillance

- Monitor for adrenal insufficiency after pulse course
- Blood glucose normalisation typically within 1-2 weeks of final pulse
- Ophthalmological review if recurrent pulse courses
- Bone density assessment if cumulative steroid exposure is high

### 2.5 Cyclophosphamide Monitoring

#### Overview

Cyclophosphamide is an alkylating agent used for severe lupus nephritis, ANCA-associated vasculitis, and other severe autoimmune glomerular diseases. It carries significant toxicity including myelosuppression, gonadal toxicity, haemorrhagic cystitis, and malignancy risk. Monitoring is intensive and protocol-driven.

#### Baseline Investigations

| Test | Rationale |
|------|-----------|
| Full blood count with differential | Baseline for myelosuppression monitoring; ANC must be > 1.5 x 10^9/L |
| Serum creatinine, eGFR | Dose adjustment for renal impairment |
| Hepatic function (ALT, AST, bilirubin, ALP) | Baseline hepatic function |
| Urine dipstick and culture | Exclude haematuria and active UTI before treatment |
| EBV, CMV, hepatitis B and C serology | Viral reactivation risk |
| Fertility counselling and consideration of sperm/oocyte banking | Gonadal toxicity is dose-dependent and often irreversible |
| Pregnancy test (women of childbearing potential) | Teratogenic; must be excluded before each cycle |
| Uric acid | Tumour lysis risk in high-turnover disease |
| ECG | Baseline cardiac assessment |
| Serum magnesium | Baseline; hypomagnesaemia common |
| Immunoglobulins (IgG, IgA, IgM) | Baseline for hypogammaglobulinaemia risk |

#### Monitoring Schedule

| Parameter | Frequency | Clinical Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|---------------------|
| Full blood count | Before each pulse; nadir typically day 10-14 | Severe neutropenia risk (ANC < 0.5 x 10^9/L); infection risk peaks at nadir |
| Urinalysis (dipstick) | Before each pulse and weekly between pulses | Haemorrhagic cystitis; dose-limiting toxicity |
| Serum creatinine, electrolytes | Before each pulse | Nephrotoxicity monitoring; tumour lysis syndrome |
| Hepatic function | Before each pulse | Hepatotoxicity monitoring |
| Infection surveillance | Weekly clinical assessment | Prophylaxis (PJP, antifungal) required during treatment |
| CMV reactivation monitoring | Monthly PCR during treatment and 3 months post | CMV reactivation common with profound immunosuppression |
| Semen analysis (if fertility preservation planned) | Pre-treatment and 12-24 months post-treatment | Gonadal toxicity assessment |
| Menstrual history (women) | Every cycle | Premature ovarian failure screening |
| IgG levels | Every 3 months during treatment and 6 months post | Hypogammaglobulinaemia; risk of serious infection if IgG < 4 g/L |

#### Safety Monitoring

| Event | Action |
|-------|--------|
| ANC < 0.5 x 10^9/L | Delay next cycle; G-CSF if febrile neutropenia risk |
| Haemorrhagic cystitis | Aggressive hydration; MESNA prophylaxis; dose reduction or discontinuation |
| Febrile neutropenia | Emergency department; blood cultures; broad-spectrum antibiotics |
| Macrohaematuria | Urology referral; cystoscopy if persistent; consider discontinuation |
| Veno-occlusive disease (rare) | Supportive care; discontinuation |
| Severe infection (PJP, CMV, fungal) | Treat; modify immunosuppression; consider discontinuation |

#### Response Assessment

- **Primary (Lupus Nephritis):** Partial or complete renal response at 6-12 months per ISN/RPS criteria
- **Primary (ANCA Vasculitis):** Disease remission (BVAS = 0) within 3-6 months
- **Secondary:** Reduction in autoantibody titres; improvement in systemic inflammation

#### Dose Adjustment Criteria

| Scenario | Action |
|----------|--------|
| eGFR < 10 mL/min/1.73m2 | Reduce dose by 25-50%; consider alternative agents |
| WBC nadir < 2.0 x 10^9/L | Reduce dose by 25% |
| WBC nadir < 1.0 x 10^9/L | Reduce dose by 50%; consider G-CSF |
| Macrohaematuria | Withhold until resolved; ensure MESNA prophylaxis; consider dose reduction |
| Hepatic dysfunction (ALT > 3x ULN) | Withhold; reassess when resolved |

#### Treatment Discontinuation Criteria

- Cumulative dose approaching gonadal toxicity threshold (age-dependent)
- Severe myelosuppression despite dose reduction
- Recurrent haemorrhagic cystitis
- Severe opportunistic infection
- Malignancy risk concern

#### Long-Term Surveillance

- Annual CBC for 5+ years post-treatment (myelodysplastic syndrome risk)
- Cancer screening: bladder cancer (annual urinalysis for haematuria for 10+ years)
- Gonadal function monitoring
- Fertility assessment if family planning desired
- Immunoglobulin levels for 12+ months post-treatment

### 2.6 Mycophenolate Mofetil (MMF) Monitoring

#### Overview

Mycophenolate mofetil (or mycophenolic acid enteric-coated) is a selective inhibitor of inosine monophosphate dehydrogenase, suppressing lymphocyte proliferation. It is a cornerstone of maintenance immunosuppression in lupus nephritis and transplant rejection prophylaxis. Monitoring focuses on gastrointestinal tolerance, myelosuppression, and infection risk.

#### Baseline Investigations

| Test | Rationale |
|------|-----------|
| Full blood count with differential | Baseline for myelosuppression monitoring |
| Serum creatinine, eGFR | Renal function baseline |
| Hepatic function (ALT, AST, bilirubin) | Hepatotoxicity baseline |
| Pregnancy test (women of childbearing potential) | Teratogenic; must be negative |
| EBV, CMV, hepatitis B serology | Viral reactivation risk |
| Serum albumin | May affect mycophenolate levels |
| Immunoglobulins (IgG, IgA, IgM) | Baseline for hypogammaglobulinaemia risk |
| UACR / uPCR | Proteinuria baseline for treatment response |

#### Monitoring Schedule

| Parameter | Frequency | Clinical Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|---------------------|
| Full blood count | Weekly for first month; fortnightly for months 2-3; monthly thereafter for first year; every 3 months once stable | Leucopenia occurs in 20-35% of patients; may be delayed |
| Serum creatinine, electrolytes | Monthly for first 3 months; every 1-3 months thereafter | Renal function monitoring; drug interactions |
| Hepatic function | Monthly for first 3 months; every 3 months thereafter | Hepatotoxicity (rare) |
| CMV viral load | Monthly during first 6 months; if symptomatic thereafter | CMV viraemia risk; higher than with azathioprine |
| EBV viral load | If clinical concern (fever, lymphadenopathy) | PTLD risk, especially in children |
| Gastrointestinal symptoms | Every visit for first 3 months | Diarrhoea (most common AE, 25-45%); may require dose reduction |
| Infection surveillance | Every visit | PJP prophylaxis typically for 3-6 months post-transplant |
| Body weight | Every 3 months | Dose adjustment if weight changes significantly |
| Pregnancy test | Before each prescription refill in women of childbearing potential | Effective contraception required |

#### Safety Monitoring

| Event | Action |
|-------|--------|
| WBC < 3.0 x 10^9/L | Reduce dose by 50% or hold |
| WBC < 2.0 x 10^9/L | Withhold; reassess; consider G-CSF if febrile |
| Severe diarrhoea (> 6 loose stools/day for > 7 days) | Reduce dose; switch to enteric-coated form; consider alternatives |
| CMV viraemia > 1000 copies/mL | Consider dose reduction; initiate antiviral therapy |
| Severe infection | Reduce or withhold; prophylactic strategies |

#### Response Assessment

- **Primary (Lupus Nephritis):** Stable or improved renal function; reduction in proteinuria; reduction in disease activity scores
- **Primary (Transplant):** Absence of rejection episodes; stable graft function
- **Therapeutic drug monitoring (if available):** MPA AUC 30-60 mg*h/L; trough levels 1-3.5 mg/L

#### Dose Adjustment Criteria

| Scenario | Action |
|----------|--------|
| GI intolerance (mild-moderate) | Switch to enteric-coated MPA; reduce dose; split dosing |
| WBC 2.5-3.5 x 10^9/L | Reduce dose by 25-50% |
| WBC < 2.5 x 10^9/L | Withhold; reassess |
| Body weight change > 10% | Recalculate dose |

#### Treatment Discontinuation Criteria

- Severe GI intolerance not responsive to dose modification
- Recurrent serious infections
- Persistent severe leucopenia
- Malignancy
- Pregnancy (teratogenic)

#### Long-Term Surveillance

- Annual CBC and renal function
- Annual CMV and EBV monitoring
- Cancer screening per general population and immunosuppression guidelines
- GI symptom reassessment
- Fertility counselling as needed

### 2.7 Azathioprine Monitoring

#### Overview

Azathioprine is a purine analogue prodrug converted to 6-mercaptopurine, inhibiting lymphocyte proliferation. It is used as maintenance immunosuppression in glomerular diseases and transplant rejection prophylaxis. Monitoring includes TPMT/NUDT15 genotyping before initiation and haematological surveillance for myelosuppression.

#### Baseline Investigations

| Test | Rationale |
|------|-----------|
| Full blood count with differential | Baseline for myelosuppression monitoring |
| TPMT and NUDT15 genotype or phenotype | Predicts risk of severe myelosuppression; dose adjustment required |
| Serum creatinine, eGFR | Renal function baseline |
| Hepatic function (ALT, AST, ALP, bilirubin) | Hepatotoxicity baseline |
| Thiopurine methyltransferase (TPMT) activity | Alternative to genotype if genotyping unavailable |
| Pregnancy test (women of childbearing potential) | Teratogenic |
| Hepatitis B and C serology | Viral reactivation risk |
| EBV and CMV serology | Viral reactivation risk |
| Lipase / amylase (if clinically indicated) | Pancreatitis baseline |

#### Monitoring Schedule

| Parameter | Frequency | Clinical Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|---------------------|
| Full blood count | Weekly for first month; fortnightly for months 2-3; monthly for months 4-6; every 3 months thereafter | Myelosuppression is dose-limiting; can be delayed |
| Liver function tests | Monthly for first 3 months; every 3 months thereafter | Hepatotoxicity; veno-occlusive disease (rare) |
| Serum creatinine | Monthly for first 3 months; every 3 months thereafter | Renal function baseline and drug monitoring |
| Infection surveillance | Every visit | Opportunistic infections; immunosuppression-related |
| Lipase / amylase | If abdominal pain occurs | Azathioprine-induced pancreatitis (1-7%) |
| Thiopurine metabolites (6-TGN, 6-MMP) | If non-response, toxicity, or adherence concerns | 6-TGN therapeutic range 235-450 pmol/8x10^8 RBC; helps distinguish non-adherence from resistance |

#### Safety Monitoring

| Event | Action |
|-------|--------|
| WBC < 3.0 x 10^9/L | Reduce dose by 50% or hold |
| WBC < 2.0 x 10^9/L or ANC < 1.0 x 10^9/L | Withhold; G-CSF if febrile; permanent dose reduction or discontinuation |
| Hepatotoxicity (ALT > 3x ULN) | Withhold; hepatic ultrasound; consider alternative |
| Pancreatitis | Discontinue permanently; do not rechallenge |
| Severe infection | Reduce or withhold; prophylaxis review |

#### Response Assessment

- **Primary:** Stable or improved disease activity markers; stable renal function
- **Secondary:** Absence of rejection episodes (transplant); stable proteinuria (glomerular disease)
- **Therapeutic drug monitoring:** 6-TGN 235-450 pmol/8x10^8 RBC (therapeutic range)

#### Dose Adjustment Criteria

| Scenario | Action |
|----------|--------|
| TPMT poor metabolizer | Reduce starting dose by 75%; maximum dose 1 mg/kg/day |
| TPMT intermediate metabolizer | Reduce starting dose by 30-50% |
| NUDT15 variant carrier | Reduce starting dose by 50-75% |
| WBC 3.0-3.5 x 10^9/L | Reduce dose by 25%; recheck in 1-2 weeks |
| Elevated 6-MMP (> 5700 pmol/8x10^8 RBC) | Hepatotoxicity risk; reduce dose or switch |

#### Treatment Discontinuation Criteria

- TPMT poor metabolizer (relative; use alternative agent)
- Severe myelosuppression
- Pancreatitis (permanent discontinuation)
- Severe hepatotoxicity
- Malignancy
- Pregnancy

#### Long-Term Surveillance

- Annual CBC and liver function tests
- Cancer screening (increased lymphoma and non-melanoma skin cancer risk)
- Viral reactivation monitoring if indicated
- Vaccination review (avoid live vaccines)

### 2.8 Tacrolimus Monitoring

#### Overview

Tacrolimus is a calcineurin inhibitor with a narrow therapeutic index, requiring therapeutic drug monitoring (TDM). It is used for transplant rejection prophylaxis and in some glomerular diseases. Monitoring must balance efficacy against nephrotoxicity, neurotoxicity, diabetes, and other toxicities.

#### Baseline Investigations

| Test | Rationale |
|------|-----------|
| Full blood count | Baseline for haematological monitoring |
| Serum creatinine, eGFR, electrolytes | Renal function and electrolyte baseline |
| Hepatic function (ALT, AST, bilirubin) | Hepatotoxicity baseline; may affect tacrolimus metabolism |
| Fasting glucose and HbA1c | Diabetes baseline; tacrolimus is diabetogenic |
| Blood pressure | Baseline for hypertension monitoring |
| Magnesium | Baseline; hypomagnesaemia common |
| Lipid profile | Metabolic baseline |
| CYP3A5 genotype (if available) | Affects tacrolimus metabolism; poor metabolizers need lower doses |
| CMV, EBV, hepatitis B serology | Viral reactivation risk |
| BK virus serology | Post-transplant nephropathy risk |
| ECG (if cardiovascular risk) | Baseline QTc and rhythm |

#### Monitoring Schedule

| Parameter | Frequency | Clinical Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|---------------------|
| Tacrolimus trough (C0) | Daily for first 1-2 weeks; every 2-3 days for weeks 3-4; weekly for months 2-3; every 2-4 weeks thereafter | Narrow therapeutic index; target C0 varies by indication and time post-transplant |
| Serum creatinine, electrolytes | With every trough level | Nephrotoxicity and electrolyte disturbance (hyperkalaemia, hypomagnesaemia) |
| Fasting glucose | Weekly for first month; monthly for months 2-3; every 3 months thereafter | New-onset diabetes after transplant (NODAT); 10-20% incidence |
| Blood pressure | Every visit; home monitoring encouraged | Hypertension (50-70% of transplant recipients) |
| HbA1c | Every 3 months if glucose abnormal; every 6 months if stable | Long-term glycaemic control |
| Lipid profile | Baseline, 3 months, then annually | Dyslipidaemia common |
| Hepatic function | Monthly for first 3 months; every 3 months thereafter | Hepatotoxicity monitoring |
| Magnesium | Monthly for first 3 months; every 3 months thereafter | Hypomagnesaemia; renal wasting |
| BK virus PCR | Monthly for first 12 months; every 3 months months 12-24 (transplant) | BK nephropathy risk; preemptive reduction of immunosuppression |
| CMV viral load | Per protocol (often weekly for 3 months) (transplant) | CMV viraemia monitoring |
| Neurotoxicity symptoms | Every visit | Tremor, headache, posterior reversible encephalopathy syndrome (PRES) |

#### Safety Monitoring

| Event | Action |
|-------|--------|
| C0 above target | Reduce dose by 10-20%; recheck in 2-3 days |
| C0 below target | Increase dose by 10-20%; recheck in 2-3 days; assess adherence |
| Creatinine rise > 20% from baseline | Check C0; reduce dose if elevated; exclude other causes |
| Glucose > 11 mmol/L repeatedly | NODAT management; consider alternative immunosuppression |
| BK viraemia > 10,000 copies/mL | Reduce immunosuppression (typically reduce tacrolimus by 50% and/or MMF) |
| Severe tremor or neurotoxicity | Check C0; reduce dose; consider switch to cyclosporine |
| Hypomagnesaemia | Oral magnesium supplementation; monitor renal wasting |
| Thrombotic microangiopathy | Discontinue tacrolimus; plasma exchange; consider alternative |

#### Response Assessment

- **Primary (Transplant):** Stable graft function; absence of rejection on protocol or indication biopsies
- **Primary (Glomerular disease):** Stable or improved renal function; reduction in proteinuria
- **TDM targets (transplant):**

| Time Post-Transplant | Target C0 (ng/mL) |
|-----------------------|---------------------|
| Days 0-14 | 8-12 |
| Months 1-3 | 6-10 |
| Months 3-12 | 5-8 |
| > 12 months | 4-7 |

- **TDM targets (glomerular disease):** Typically 4-8 ng/mL (varies by protocol)

#### Dose Adjustment Criteria

| Scenario | Action |
|----------|--------|
| C0 above target | Reduce dose by 10-25%; recheck in 2-3 days |
| C0 below target | Increase dose by 10-25%; recheck in 2-3 days |
| Creatinine rise with high C0 | Reduce tacrolimus dose; consider short-term discontinuation if severe |
| Drug interaction (azole antifungals, macrolides, grapefruit) | Proactive dose reduction of 30-50% when starting interacting drug |
| Diarrhoea / vomiting | May increase absorption; check levels; may need temporary dose reduction |

#### Treatment Discontinuation Criteria

- Severe nephrotoxicity not responsive to dose reduction
- Uncontrolled NODAT
- Severe neurotoxicity (PRES, seizures)
- Thrombotic microangiopathy
- Malignancy
- Switch to belatacept or alternative CNI-free protocol if indicated

#### Long-Term Surveillance

- Annual tacrolimus trough levels (stable patients)
- Annual renal function, electrolytes, glucose, lipids
- Cancer screening (increased skin cancer risk; sun protection counselling)
- Annual ophthalmological review

### 2.10 Rituximab Monitoring

#### Overview

Rituximab is a chimeric anti-CD20 monoclonal antibody that depletes B lymphocytes. It is used for ANCA-associated vasculitis, membranous nephropathy (anti-PLA2R-positive), lupus nephritis, and antibody-mediated transplant rejection. Monitoring focuses on infusion reactions, B-cell depletion, hypogammaglobulinaemia, and infection risk (particularly PML).

#### Baseline Investigations

| Test | Rationale |
|------|-----------|
| Full blood count with differential | Baseline for lymphopenia and cytopenia monitoring |
| CD19/CD20 B-cell count | Baseline for response monitoring |
| Serum immunoglobulins (IgG, IgA, IgM) | Baseline for hypogammaglobulinaemia risk |
| Hepatitis B serology (HBsAg, anti-HBc, anti-HBs) | Reactivation risk; rituximab causes profound B-cell depletion |
| Hepatitis C serology | Screening |
| CMV, EBV serology | Viral reactivation risk |
| HIV screening | Risk of PML; contraindication if positive |
| Serum creatinine, eGFR | Renal function baseline |
| Chest X-ray | Baseline cardiac and pulmonary assessment |
| Serum albumin | Baseline for disease assessment |
| Anti-PLA2R antibody (membranous nephropathy) | Baseline titre for treatment response |

#### Monitoring Schedule

| Parameter | Frequency | Clinical Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|---------------------|
| Infusion reaction monitoring | During each infusion; observation for 1-2 hours post-infusion | Infusion reactions in 10-30% of first infusion; typically first 2 hours |
| CD19+ B-cell count | Pre-infusion, 3 months post, 6 months, then every 6-12 months | B-cell depletion (CD19 < 0.01% or < 5 cells/uL) confirms efficacy |
| Serum immunoglobulins | 3 months post, 6 months, then every 6-12 months | Hypogammaglobulinaemia (IgG < 4 g/L) increases infection risk |
| Full blood count | Monthly for first 3 months; then every 3 months | Lymphopenia; cytopenias |
| Hepatitis B viral load | Monthly for first 6 months; then every 3 months if HBsAg+ or anti-HBc+ | HBV reactivation; potentially fatal |
| Serum creatinine | Every 1-3 months | Disease activity and renal function monitoring |
| Infection surveillance | Every visit for first 6 months | PJP prophylaxis; serious bacterial and viral infections |
| Neurological assessment | If new neurological symptoms develop | Progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy (PML); rare but fatal |

#### Safety Monitoring

| Event | Action |
|-------|--------|
| Infusion reaction (grade 1-2) | Slow infusion; antihistamines and paracetamol; may continue |
| Infusion reaction (grade 3-4) | Stop infusion; treat with adrenaline, corticosteroids; consider desensitization protocol for next dose |
| Severe lymphopenia (CD4 < 200 cells/uL) | PJP prophylaxis; infection precautions |
| IgG < 4 g/L with recurrent infections | Consider IVIG replacement |
| HBV reactivation | Entecavir or tenofovir; consider discontinuation of rituximab |
| PML | Discontinue rituximab; neurology referral; no specific treatment |

#### Response Assessment

- **Primary (Membranous Nephropathy):** >= 50% reduction in anti-PLA2R titre at 6 months; >= 50% reduction in proteinuria at 12 months
- **Primary (ANCA Vasculitis):** Disease remission (BVAS = 0); ANCA titre reduction
- **Secondary:** B-cell depletion; improvement in renal function; reduction in complement consumption

#### Dose Adjustment Criteria

| Scenario | Action |
|----------|--------|
| Standard protocol | 1000 mg IV on days 1 and 15; repeat at 6 months if needed |
| Infusion reaction (prior) | Pre-medicate with methylprednisolone 100 mg IV, chlorphenamine, paracetamol |
| Hepatitis B carrier | Antiviral prophylaxis mandatory; rituximab not absolutely contraindicated |
| HIV positive | Contraindicated; increased PML risk |

#### Treatment Discontinuation Criteria

- Sustained complete response (anti-PLA2R negative, complete proteinuria remission)
- Severe recurrent infections
- Hypogammaglobulinaemia requiring ongoing IVIG
- PML or suspected PML
- Patient preference after informed discussion

#### Long-Term Surveillance

- Annual immunoglobulin levels for 5+ years
- Annual B-cell recovery monitoring
- Cancer screening (increased NHL risk)
- Infection history review
- HBV monitoring for 12+ months post-treatment if at-risk

#### Overview

Eculizumab and ravulizumab are terminal complement (C5) inhibitors used for atypical haemolytic uraemic syndrome (aHUS) and C3 glomerulopathy. | Hepatitis B, C serology | Viral screening |
| Serum albumin | Nutritional and disease status |
| Coagulation screen (PT, APTT, fibrinogen) | Baseline before plasma exchange |
| Peripheral blood film | Schistocytes, haemolysis |

#### Monitoring Schedule

| Parameter | Frequency | Clinical Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|---------------------|
| Serum creatinine, eGFR | Weekly for first month; every 2 weeks for months 2-3; monthly thereafter | Renal function response |
| Complete blood count | Weekly for first month; every 2 weeks for months 2-3; monthly thereafter | Haemolysis resolution; haematological response |

#### Safety Monitoring

| Event | Action |
|-------|--------|
| Suspected meningococcal infection | Emergency: IV ceftriaxone; urgent infectious disease input |

| Inadequate complement suppression | Check free C5; consider dose adjustment; check for anti-drug antibodies |
| Serious bacterial infection | Treat actively; consider temporary discontinuation |

#### Response Assessment

- **Primary (aHUS):** Normalisation of platelet count; haemolysis marker normalisation; improvement in renal function
- **Primary (C3G):** Reduction in proteinuria; stabilisation of eGFR; reduction in complement consumption
- **Pharmacodynamic:** Undetectable free C5 and CH50/AP50 at trough

#### Dose Adjustment Criteria

| Scenario | Action |
|----------|--------|

| Breakthrough haemolysis with adequate complement suppression | Evaluate for alternative mechanisms |
| Weight-based dosing (ravulizumab) | Recalculate if weight changes > 10% |

#### Treatment Discontinuation Criteria

- aHUS: Generally lifelong therapy; discontinuation associated with disease relapse
- C3G: May attempt cautious withdrawal if sustained complete remission for >= 2 years; relapse is common
- Life-threatening infection
- Anti-drug antibody formation causing treatment failure

#### Long-Term Surveillance

- Lifelong meningococcal vaccination
- Annual complement function testing
- Infection risk assessment
- Renal function monitoring
- Vaccination for encapsulated organisms (pneumococcal, Hib)
- Prophylactic antibiotics during therapy per local protocol

| Lipid profile | Baseline, 1 month, 3 months, then every 3 months | Severe hypertriglyceridaemia (30-70%); hypercholesterolaemia |
| Fasting glucose / HbA1c | Monthly for first 3 months; every 3 months thereafter | Hyperglycaemia |
| Hepatic function | Monthly for first 3 months; every 3 months thereafter | Hepatotoxicity |
| Serum creatinine | Monthly for first 3 months; every 3 months thereafter | Renal function |
| Chest X-ray / CT chest | If cough, dyspnoea, or fever develops | Non-infectious pneumonitis (5-10%) |
| Wound healing assessment | Weekly for first month post-surgery | Impaired wound healing; lymphocoele risk |
| Proteinuria | Monthly for first 6 months; every 3 months thereafter | Proteinuria may paradoxically worsen |
| Oral mucosal assessment | Every visit | Oral ulcers (aphthous-like) |

#### Safety Monitoring

| Event | Action |
|-------|--------|
| Trough level above target | Reduce dose by 20-30%; recheck in 3-5 days |
| Trough level below target | Increase dose by 20-30%; recheck in 3-5 days |
| Triglycerides > 5.6 mmol/L | Fibrate therapy; consider dose reduction |
| Pneumonitis (new cough, dyspnoea, ground-glass opacities) | Hold drug; chest CT; bronchoscopy if needed; consider steroid therapy |
| Impaired wound healing | Hold drug; local wound care; resume when healed |
| Severe thrombocytopenia (platelets < 50 x 10^9/L) | Hold drug; haematology review |
| Severe oral ulcers | Topical therapy; dose reduction; consider alternative |
| Severe proteinuria (increase > 50%) | Hold drug; reassess |

#### Response Assessment

- **Primary (Transplant):** Stable graft function with CNI minimisation/withdrawal
- **Primary (Glomerular disease):** Reduction in proteinuria; stabilisation of eGFR
- **TDM targets:**

| Indication | Target Trough (ng/mL) |
|------------|----------------------|
| Transplant (de novo) | 8-12 |
| Transplant (conversion) | 5-10 |
| Glomerular disease | 5-10 |

#### Dose Adjustment Criteria

| Scenario | Action |
|----------|--------|
| Elevated trough | Reduce dose; recheck in 3-5 days |
| Low trough | Increase dose; recheck in 3-5 days |
| Drug interactions (CYP3A4 inhibitors/inducers) | Adjust dose accordingly |
| Recent surgery (within 3 months) | Contraindicated; wait for wound healing |
| Severe dyslipidaemia | Lipid-lowering therapy; dose reduction |

#### Treatment Discontinuation Criteria

- Non-infectious pneumonitis (severe or recurrent)
- Severe thrombocytopenia
- Impaired wound healing requiring surgical intervention
- Severe proteinuria
- Uncontrolled dyslipidaemia

#### Long-Term Surveillance

- Annual lipids, glucose, renal function
- Cancer screening (mTOR inhibitors may have antineoplastic properties)
- Wound healing review after any surgery
- Fertility and sexual function assessment
- Pulmonary function if prior pneumonitis

### 2.16 IVIG Monitoring

#### Overview

Intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG) is used for antibody-mediated diseases, complement-mediated diseases, and as adjunctive therapy in glomerulonephritis. Monitoring focuses on infusion reactions, aseptic meningitis, thromboembolic events, and renal function (sucrose-containing preparations).

#### Baseline Investigations

| Test | Rationale |
|------|-----------|
| Serum immunoglobulins (IgG, IgA, IgM) | Baseline for dosing and response |
| Serum creatinine, eGFR | Renal function baseline; sucrose-containing IVIG nephrotoxic |
| Blood pressure | Baseline |
| Full blood count | Baseline for haemolysis monitoring |
| Direct antiglobulin test (DAT) | Baseline for haemolytic anaemia risk |
| Coagulation screen | Baseline for thromboembolic risk |
| IgA level | If IgA-deficient, risk of anaphylaxis with IVIG |
| Hepatitis A, B, C serology | Screening |

#### Monitoring Schedule

| Parameter | Frequency | Clinical Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|---------------------|
| Infusion reaction monitoring | During each infusion; observation 1-2 hours post | Fever, chills, headache, myalgia (10-30%) |
| Blood pressure | During infusion; hourly during infusion | Hypotension or hypertension |
| Vital signs (temperature, pulse, SpO2) | Every 30 minutes during infusion | Infusion reaction detection |
| Serum creatinine | Before each infusion cycle; within 48 hours post | Nephrotoxicity (sucrose-containing IVIG) |
| Full blood count | Before each infusion cycle | Haemolytic anaemia (anti-A/anti-B antibodies) |
| Direct antiglobulin test | If haemolysis suspected | Coombs-positive haemolytic anaemia |
| Infection surveillance | Every visit | Theoretical risk of pathogen transmission |

#### Safety Monitoring

| Event | Action |
|-------|--------|
| Anaphylaxis | Stop infusion; adrenaline; standard resuscitation; contraindicate future IVIG in IgA-deficient patients |
| Severe infusion reaction | Slow rate; pre-medicate (paracetamol, diphenhydramine, hydrocortisone); consider subcutaneous formulation |
| Aseptic meningitis | Stop infusion; CT head to exclude other causes; typically self-limiting |
| Thromboembolic event | Stop infusion; anticoagulation; risk factor assessment |
| Renal dysfunction (sucrose IVIG) | Switch to non-sucrose formulation; ensure adequate hydration |
| Haemolytic anaemia | Stop infusion; transfuse if severe; haematology review |

#### Response Assessment

- **Primary:** Disease-specific markers (proteinuria, complement, autoantibody titres)
- **Trough IgG level:** If used for immunodeficiency, target trough > 5 g/L
- **Clinical improvement:** Typically within 1-4 weeks of infusion

#### Dose Adjustment Criteria

| Scenario | Action |
|----------|--------|
| Standard dosing | 2 g/kg divided over 2-5 days (disease-specific) |
| Recurrent infusion reactions | Slow rate; pre-medicate; switch formulation |
| Haemolysis | Stop; transfuse if severe; avoid future IVIG or use IgA-depleted product |
| Renal dysfunction | Switch to non-sucrose formulation; reduce infusion rate |

#### Treatment Discontinuation Criteria

- Anaphylaxis
- Severe thromboembolic event
- Aseptic meningitis (recurrent)
- Severe haemolytic anaemia
- Renal failure attributable to IVIG

#### Long-Term Surveillance

- Annual immunoglobulin levels
- Renal function monitoring
- Thromboembolic risk assessment
- Vaccination review (avoid live vaccines for 11 months post-IVIG)

### 2.17 Plasmapheresis (PLEX) Monitoring

#### Overview

Plasma exchange (PLEX) removes pathogenic antibodies, immune complexes, and complement components. It is used for anti-GBM disease, TTP, severe ANCA vasculitis with diffuse alveolar haemorrhage, transplant rejection, and antibody-mediated rejection. Monitoring focuses on vascular access, coagulation, electrolyte balance, and replacement fluid complications.

#### Baseline Investigations

| Test | Rationale |
|------|-----------|
| Full blood count with differential | Baseline for cytopenia and platelet monitoring |
| Coagulation screen (PT, APTT, INR, fibrinogen) | Baseline for coagulopathy risk |
| Serum immunoglobulins (IgG, IgA, IgM) | Baseline; immunoglobulins are removed by PLEX |
| Serum creatinine, eGFR | Renal function baseline |
| Serum calcium, magnesium, potassium | Electrolyte baseline; citrate anticoagulation causes hypocalcaemia |
| Albumin | Baseline for replacement fluid selection |
| Blood group and crossmatch | In case of transfusion requirement |
| Viral serology (hepatitis B, C, HIV) | Screening |
| Vascular access assessment | Central line or peripheral; DVT risk |
| Blood pressure | Baseline |
| Body weight | Fluid balance baseline |

#### Monitoring Schedule

| Parameter | Frequency | Clinical Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|---------------------|
| Vital signs (BP, HR, SpO2, temperature) | Every 15-30 minutes during procedure | Hypotension, arrhythmia, allergic reaction |
| Serum ionised calcium | During procedure if symptomatic; post-procedure | Citrate anticoagulation causes hypocalcaemia (tingling, paraesthesia, tetany) |
| Serum potassium | Before and after each session | Hypokalaemia from citrate chelation |
| Coagulation screen (fibrinogen, PT, APTT) | Before each session; 4 hours post | Fibrinogen depletion; coagulopathy |
| Full blood count | Before each session | Platelet depletion; anaemia |
| Serum immunoglobulins | Weekly during active treatment | Immunoglobulin depletion increases infection risk |
| Serum albumin | Before each session | Albumin replacement assessment |
| Body weight | Before and after each session | Fluid balance |
| Vascular access site | After each session | Infection, thrombosis, haematoma |
| Serum creatinine | Before each session | Renal function (may improve with disease control) |

#### Safety Monitoring

| Event | Action |
|-------|--------|
| Hypocalcaemia (symptomatic) | IV calcium gluconate; slow infusion; slow citrate rate |
| Hypotension | Fluid bolus; reduce flow rate; Trendelenburg position |
| Allergic reaction to replacement fluid | Stop procedure; antihistamines; corticosteroids; consider albumin vs. FFP switch |
| Coagulopathy (fibrinogen < 1.0 g/L) | Pause procedure; replace fibrinogen (cryoprecipitate) |
| Severe thrombocytopenia (platelets < 50 x 10^9/L) | Pause; assess bleeding risk; platelet transfusion if active bleeding |
| Line-related complication (infection, thrombosis) | Remove line; manage complication; alternative access |

#### Response Assessment

- **Primary (Anti-GBM):** Rapid reduction in anti-GBM antibody titre
- **Primary (ANCA Vasculitis with DAH):** Clinical improvement in respiratory status
- **Primary (AMR):** Reduction in donor-specific antibodies; improvement in graft function
- **Typical protocol:** 5-7 exchanges over 10-14 days; assess response at midpoint

#### Dose Adjustment Criteria

| Scenario | Action |
|----------|--------|
| Standard volume | 1-1.5x plasma volume per exchange |
| Hypotension during procedure | Reduce flow rate; albumin bolus |
| Coagulopathy | Reduce exchange volume; replace fibrinogen |
| Severe immunoglobulin depletion | Delay next session; consider IVIG supplementation |

#### Treatment Discontinuation Criteria

- Target antibody titre achieved
- Clinical response
- Severe coagulopathy
- Life-threatening complication of PLEX
- Vascular access exhaustion

#### Long-Term Surveillance

- Immunoglobulin levels for 3+ months post-treatment
- Vascular access site assessment
- Infection risk monitoring
- Disease activity monitoring for relapse

### 2.18 Bortezomib Monitoring

#### Overview

Bortezomib is a proteasome inhibitor that targets plasma cells, reducing antibody production. It is used for antibody-mediated transplant rejection, resistant lupus nephritis, and other antibody-driven diseases. Monitoring focuses on neuropathy (dose-limiting), cytopenias, GI toxicity, and infection risk.

#### Baseline Investigations

| Test | Rationale |
|------|-----------|
| Full blood count with differential | Baseline for cytopenia monitoring |
| Serum creatinine, eGFR | Renal function baseline; dose adjustment for renal impairment |
| Hepatic function (ALT, AST, bilirubin) | Hepatotoxicity baseline |
| Nerve conduction studies (if feasible) | Baseline for peripheral neuropathy monitoring |
| Serum immunoglobulins (IgG, IgA, IgM) | Baseline for hypogammaglobulinaemia risk |
| Platelet count | Baseline; thrombocytopenia is common |
| Coagulation screen | Baseline |
| Hepatitis B and C serology | Viral screening |
| Varicella zoster serology | Reactivation risk; antiviral prophylaxis required |
| CMV, EBV serology | Viral reactivation risk |
| ECG | Baseline cardiac assessment |

#### Monitoring Schedule

| Parameter | Frequency | Clinical Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|---------------------|
| Full blood count | Before each dose | Thrombocytopenia (30-50%); leucopenia |
| Platelet count | Before each dose | Dose-limiting if < 25 x 10^9/L |
| Serum creatinine, electrolytes | Before each cycle | Renal function monitoring |
| Hepatic function | Before each cycle | Hepatotoxicity |
| Peripheral neuropathy assessment | Before each dose (use validated scoring e.g., NCI-CTCAE) | Peripheral neuropathy (30-40%); dose-limiting; may be irreversible |
| GI symptoms | Every visit | Nausea, diarrhoea, constipation (50-70%) |
| Infection surveillance | Every visit | PJP prophylaxis; VZV prophylaxis |
| Immunoglobulin levels | Every 3 months | Hypogammaglobulinaemia |
| Tumour lysis markers (if applicable) | Before each cycle | Uric acid, potassium, phosphate |

#### Safety Monitoring

| Event | Action |
|-------|--------|
| Grade 2 peripheral neuropathy | Dose reduction from 1.3 mg/m2 to 1.0 mg/m2; consider weekly schedule |
| Grade 3 peripheral neuropathy | Withhold until resolution; resume at 0.7 mg/m2 if improved to <= Grade 1 |
| Grade 4 peripheral neuropathy | Discontinue permanently |
| Platelets < 25 x 10^9/L | Withhold; resume when > 50 x 10^9/L |
| Neutropenia (ANC < 0.5 x 10^9/L) | Withhold; G-CSF if febrile |
| VZV reactivation | Antiviral treatment; ensure prophylaxis for future cycles |

#### Response Assessment

- **Primary (AMR):** Reduction in donor-specific antibody levels; improvement in graft function
- **Primary (Lupus Nephritis):** Reduction in anti-dsDNA; improvement in renal function; reduction in proteinuria

#### Dose Adjustment Criteria

| Scenario | Action |
|----------|--------|
| Standard dosing | 1.3 mg/m2 subcutaneously twice weekly for 2 weeks, then 1 week off (cycle = 3 weeks) |
| Neuropathy Grade 2 | Reduce to 1.0 mg/m2; switch to once weekly |
| Neuropathy Grade 3 | Withhold; resume at 0.7 mg/m2 when improved |
| Neuropathy Grade 4 | Discontinue |
| Thrombocytopenia (platelets < 25 x 10^9/L) | Withhold; resume when > 50 x 10^9/L |
| eGFR < 25 mL/min/1.73m2 | Reduce dose to 0.7 mg/m2 |

#### Treatment Discontinuation Criteria

- Grade 3 or 4 peripheral neuropathy
- Severe cytopenias despite dose adjustment
- Treatment failure after 4-6 cycles

#### Long-Term Surveillance

- Peripheral neuropathy recovery monitoring (may take months to years)
- Immunoglobulin levels for 12+ months post-treatment
- Cancer screening
- VZV monitoring for 12+ months post-treatment

### 2.19 Belatacept Monitoring

#### Overview

Belatacept is a selective T-cell co-stimulation blocker (CTLA-4-Ig) used as maintenance immunosuppression in kidney transplantation, particularly as an alternative to calcineurin inhibitors. It offers CNI-free immunosuppression with potentially better long-term graft function. Monitoring focuses on rejection risk (especially early post-transplant), infection, and metabolic effects.

#### Baseline Investigations

| Test | Rationale |
|------|-----------|
| Full blood count | Baseline |
| Serum creatinine, eGFR | Renal function baseline |
| Hepatic function (ALT, AST, bilirubin) | Baseline |
| CMV, EBV, hepatitis B and C serology | Viral reactivation risk |
| BK virus serology | Post-transplant nephropathy risk |
| Blood pressure | Baseline |
| Fasting glucose and HbA1c | Metabolic baseline |
| Lipid profile | Metabolic baseline |
| Tuberculosis screening | Immunosuppression baseline |
| HLA typing and donor-specific antibodies | Transplant immunology baseline |

#### Monitoring Schedule

| Parameter | Frequency | Clinical Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|---------------------|
| Serum creatinine, eGFR | Weekly for first month; every 2 weeks months 2-3; monthly months 3-6; every 3 months thereafter | Rejection risk highest in first 6 months |
| Full blood count | Weekly for first month; monthly for months 2-6; every 3 months thereafter | Cytopenia monitoring |
| Hepatic function | Monthly for first 6 months; every 3 months thereafter | Hepatotoxicity |
| CMV viral load | Weekly for first 3 months; monthly for months 3-6; every 3 months thereafter | CMV viraemia; higher risk without CMV prophylaxis |
| EBV viral load | Monthly for first 12 months | PTLD risk; higher with belatacept than CNI-based regimens |
| BK virus PCR | Monthly for first 12 months | BK nephropathy |
| Blood pressure | Every visit | Hypertension (may be lower than CNI-based regimens) |
| Fasting glucose / HbA1c | Monthly for first 3 months; every 3 months thereafter | Diabetes monitoring (lower risk than tacrolimus) |
| Donor-specific antibodies | Every 3 months for first year; then annually | De novo DSA development; AMR risk |
| Protocol biopsy (if indicated) | Protocol biopsies at 3, 12 months per protocol | Subclinical rejection; CNI-free protocols have higher early rejection rates |

#### Safety Monitoring

| Event | Action |
|-------|--------|
| Acute rejection (biopsy-proven) | Pulse steroids; consider adding CNI or switching to CNI-based regimen |
| PTLD (EBV-positive) | Reduce immunosuppression; rituximab +/- chemotherapy; oncology referral |
| CMV disease | Antiviral therapy (valganciclovir) |
| BK nephropathy | Reduce immunosuppression; consider switch to CNI-based regimen |
| DSA development | Risk-stratify; consider desensitisation; protocol biopsy |

#### Response Assessment

- **Primary:** Stable graft function; absence of rejection on protocol biopsies
- **Secondary:** Superior long-term GFR compared to CNI-based regimens; lower rates of NODAT

#### Dose Adjustment Criteria

| Scenario | Action |
|----------|--------|
| Standard dosing | 10 mg/kg IV on days 0-5, then days 14, 28, then every 4 weeks |
| Positive CMV viral load | Antiviral therapy; may continue belatacept |
| Positive EBV viral load (high titre) | Reduce immunosuppression; consider adding CNI |
| BK viraemia > 10,000 copies/mL | Reduce immunosuppression; consider conversion to CNI-based regimen |
| Rejection episode | Pulse methylprednisolone; consider conversion to CNI-based regimen |

#### Treatment Discontinuation Criteria

- Recurrent rejection episodes despite adjunctive immunosuppression
- PTLD or EBV-associated lymphoproliferative disorder
- Severe or recurrent serious infections
- Patient non-adherence to monthly infusions

#### Long-Term Surveillance

- Annual renal function, metabolic monitoring
- Annual DSA monitoring
- Annual cancer screening (PTLD risk)
- Viral surveillance (CMV, EBV, BK) for 2+ years

### 2.20 Hydroxychloroquine Monitoring

#### Overview

Hydroxychloroquine (HCQ) is an antimalarial with immunomodulatory properties, used in lupus nephritis and other autoimmune diseases. It reduces disease flares, thrombotic events, and mortality in lupus. Monitoring focuses on retinal toxicity (cumulative dose-dependent) and cardiac effects.

#### Baseline Investigations

| Test | Rationale |
|------|-----------|
| Ophthalmological examination (including OCT and visual fields) | Baseline retinal assessment before initiation |
| Full blood count | Baseline for cytopenia monitoring |
| Serum creatinine, eGFR | Dose adjustment for renal impairment |
| Fasting glucose and HbA1c | Metabolic baseline |
| ECG (12-lead) | Baseline QTc interval; conduction abnormalities |
| Hepatic function (ALT, AST) | Baseline |
| Body weight | Dosing: <= 5 mg/kg actual body weight |

#### Monitoring Schedule

| Parameter | Frequency | Clinical Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|---------------------|
| Ophthalmological examination (OCT + visual fields) | Baseline; annually from initiation (after 5 years if no risk factors); annually from year 1 if risk factors present | Retinal toxicity (bull's eye maculopathy); cumulative and often irreversible |
| Full blood count | Baseline, 3 months, then annually | Rare cytopenias (leucopenia, aplastic anaemia) |
| Serum creatinine, eGFR | Baseline, 3 months, then annually | Renal impairment may require dose adjustment |
| Fasting glucose / HbA1c | Baseline, 3 months, then annually | HCQ may cause hypoglycaemia or hyperglycaemia |
| ECG | Baseline; repeat if cardiac symptoms develop | QTc prolongation; conduction abnormalities |
| Body weight | Every visit | Dosing accuracy: <= 5 mg/kg |
| Visual symptoms inquiry | Every visit | Blurring, scotomata, colour vision changes |

#### Risk Factors for Retinal Toxicity

| Factor | Threshold |
|--------|-----------|
| Cumulative dose | > 1000 g total |
| Duration | > 5 years (higher risk) |
| Daily dose | > 5 mg/kg actual body weight |
| Renal impairment | eGFR < 60 mL/min/1.73m2 |
| Concomitant tamoxifen | Increased risk |

#### Safety Monitoring

| Event | Action |
|-------|--------|
| Retinal toxicity on OCT/visual fields | Discontinue HCQ immediately; retinal damage is irreversible |
| Visual symptoms (new scotomata, blurred vision) | Urgent ophthalmology review; consider discontinuation |
| QTc prolongation (> 500 ms) | Stop HCQ; cardiology review; correct electrolytes |
| Severe GI intolerance | Take with food; consider split dosing; if intolerable, discontinue |
| Hypoglycaemia | Monitor glucose; adjust diabetic medications |

#### Response Assessment

- **Primary:** Reduction in lupus flare frequency; reduction in anti-dsDNA titre; reduction in proteinuria (lupus nephritis)
- **Secondary:** Reduction in thrombotic events; improved overall survival
- **Time to response:** Typically 3-6 months for full immunomodulatory effect

#### Dose Adjustment Criteria

| Scenario | Action |
|----------|--------|
| Standard dosing | 200-400 mg daily (<= 5 mg/kg actual body weight) |
| eGFR 30-60 mL/min/1.73m2 | Maximum 200 mg daily |
| eGFR < 30 mL/min/1.73m2 | Avoid use; if essential, 200 mg weekly |
| Body weight < 60 kg | Maximum 200 mg daily |

#### Treatment Discontinuation Criteria

- Retinal toxicity (mandatory discontinuation)
- QTc prolongation > 500 ms
- Severe GI intolerance despite dose modification
- Severe cytopenia

#### Long-Term Surveillance

- Annual ophthalmological examination (lifelong if previously taking HCQ)
- Annual metabolic monitoring (glucose, lipids)
- Annual ECG (if prior QTc concern)

## 4. General Renal Monitoring

### 4.1 CKD Progression Monitoring

#### Overview

Chronic kidney disease (CKD) progression monitoring is the foundation of nephrology care. Regardless of aetiology, all patients require systematic assessment of GFR trajectory, proteinuria, and cardiovascular risk.

#### Monitoring Schedule by CKD Stage

| CKD Stage | eGFR (mL/min/1.73m2) | Monitoring Frequency |
|-----------|----------------------|---------------------|
| G1 | >= 90 (with kidney damage markers) | Every 6-12 months |
| G2 | 60-89 | Every 6-12 months |
| G3a | 45-59 | Every 3-6 months |
| G3b | 30-44 | Every 3 months |
| G4 | 15-29 | Every 1-3 months |
| G5 | < 15 | Every 1-2 months |

#### Parameters Monitored

| Parameter | Frequency | Clinical Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|---------------------|
| Serum creatinine / eGFR | Per CKD stage (above) | GFR trajectory; > 5 mL/min/year decline is concerning |
| Proteinuria (uPCR/uACR) | Per proteinuria severity (see 4.2) | Proteinuria is an independent risk factor for progression |
| Blood pressure | Every visit | Target < 130/80 mmHg |
| Serum potassium | Every visit for G3b-G5; every 3-6 months for G1-G3a | Hyperkalaemia risk increases with declining GFR |
| Serum bicarbonate | Every 3-6 months for G3b-G5 | Metabolic acidosis accelerates CKD progression |
| Haemoglobin | Every 3-6 months for G3b-G5 | Anaemia of CKD; erythropoietin deficiency |
| Calcium, phosphate, PTH | Every 6-12 months for G3b-G5 | CKD-mineral bone disorder |
| Vitamin D (25-OH) | Annually for G3b-G5 | Deficiency common; bone metabolism |
| Lipid profile | Annually | Cardiovascular risk |
| HbA1c (diabetic patients) | Every 3 months | Glycaemic control |
| Volume status | Every visit | Fluid overload in advanced CKD |

#### eGFR Trajectory Assessment

| eGFR Decline (mL/min/year) | Interpretation | Action |
|---------------------------|----------------|--------|
| < 1 | Normal age-related decline | Continue current management |
| 1-3 | Mildly accelerated | Optimise BP control; ensure RAAS inhibition |
| 3-5 | Moderately accelerated | Intensify treatment; investigate reversible causes |
| > 5 | Rapidly progressive | Urgent investigation; consider nephrology referral |

### 4.2 Proteinuria Monitoring

#### Overview

Proteinuria is both a marker and mediator of kidney disease progression. Monitoring frequency is determined by baseline proteinuria severity and treatment status.

#### Monitoring Frequency by Severity

| Proteinuria Level | uPCR (mg/mmol) | uACR (mg/mmol) | Monitoring Frequency |
|-------------------|----------------|-----------------|---------------------|
| Normal | < 15 | < 3 | Annual (CKD screening) |
| Mildly increased (microalbuminuria) | 15-50 | 3-30 | Every 6-12 months |
| Moderately increased | 50-300 | 30-300 | Every 3-6 months |
| Severely increased (overt proteinuria) | > 300 | > 300 | Every 1-3 months |
| Nephrotic range | > 3500 | > 3500 | Every 1-4 weeks (during active treatment) |

#### Proteinuria Response Assessment

| Response Category | Definition | Timeframe |
|-------------------|-----------|-----------|
| Complete response | uPCR < 15 mg/mmol (< 150 mg/day) | 6-12 months |
| Partial response | >= 50% reduction in proteinuria | 3-6 months |
| Non-response | < 50% reduction | Reassess at 6-12 months |
| Worsening | > 30% increase from nadir | Immediate reassessment |

#### Factors Affecting Proteinuria Measurement

| Factor | Effect | Management |
|--------|--------|------------|
| Urinary tract infection | False-positive | Treat infection; recheck after resolution |
| Haematuria | False-positive dipstick; uPCR may be elevated | Microscopy; use uACR or 24-hour urine |
| Exercise | Transient increase | Avoid strenuous exercise 24 hours before collection |
| Menstruation | May increase uACR | Collect mid-stream sample |
| Fever | Transient increase | Recheck when afebrile |
| Time of day | Morning urine more concentrated | Standardise collection timing |

### 4.3 eGFR Monitoring

#### Overview

Estimated GFR is the primary measure of kidney function. Monitoring frequency depends on CKD stage, rate of change, and treatment interventions.

#### Monitoring Frequency by CKD Stage

| CKD Stage | eGFR Range | Recommended Monitoring |
|-----------|-----------|----------------------|
| G1 | >= 90 | Annually (if at risk) or with disease flare |
| G2 | 60-89 | Annually to every 6 months |
| G3a | 45-59 | Every 6 months |
| G3b | 30-44 | Every 3-4 months |
| G4 | 15-29 | Every 1-3 months |
| G5 | < 15 | Every 1-2 months |
| G5D (dialysis) | N/A (use Kt/V) | Per dialysis protocol |

#### eGFR Accuracy Considerations

| Factor | Effect on eGFR | Action |
|--------|---------------|--------|
| AKI | Unreliable | Use measured GFR or cystatin C |
| Muscle mass extremes | Overestimates (low muscle) or underestimates (high muscle) | Consider cystatin C-based eGFR |
| Amputation | May overestimate | Adjust for muscle mass |
| Drug interference (trimethoprim, cimetidine) | Transient creatinine rise | Recognise artefact; discontinue offending agent if possible |
| Diet (high protein, creatine supplements) | May elevate creatinine | Dietary counselling |

#### Measured GFR Indications

| Indication | Method |
|-----------|--------|
| Living kidney donor evaluation | Iohexol or Cr-EDTA clearance |
| High-dose chemotherapy dosing | 24-hour urine creatinine clearance or measured GFR |
| eGFR discordant with clinical status | Measured GFR |
| Drug dosing in extremes of body composition | Measured GFR |

---

### 4.4 Blood Pressure Monitoring

#### Overview

Hypertension is both a cause and consequence of kidney disease. Optimal blood pressure control is critical for slowing CKD progression and reducing cardiovascular risk.

#### Targets

| Population | Target BP | Evidence |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| CKD with proteinuria (uPCR > 30 mg/mmol) | < 130/80 mmHg | KDIGO 2021; AASK; REIN |
| CKD without proteinuria | < 130/80 mmHg | KDIGO 2021 |
| Diabetic CKD | < 130/80 mmHg | ACCORD-BP; KDIGO 2021 |
| Post-transplant | < 130/80 mmHg | KDIGO 2021 |
| Elderly (> 80 years) | < 140/90 mmHg | Pragmatic target |

#### Monitoring Schedule

| Context | Frequency | Method |
|---------|-----------|--------|
| Clinic visits | Every visit | Automated office BP (AOBP) minimum 2 readings |
| Home BP monitoring | Daily (morning and evening) for 7 days every month | Validated oscillometric device |
| Ambulatory BP monitoring (ABPM) | If white-coat or masked hypertension suspected | 24-hour ABPM |

#### BP Monitoring Best Practices

| Practice | Rationale |
|----------|-----------|
| Seated, rested 5 minutes before measurement | Reduces measurement artefact |
| Appropriate cuff size | Avoids falsely high readings |
| Arm supported at heart level | Accuracy |
| No caffeine, smoking 30 minutes prior | Avoids transient BP elevation |
| Multiple readings (2-3 minimum) | Reduces variability |
| Morning and evening readings | Detects nocturnal dipping pattern |

---

### 4.5 Cardiovascular Risk Monitoring

#### Overview

CKD is a coronary heart disease risk equivalent. Patients with CKD have 5-10x higher cardiovascular mortality than the general population. Systematic cardiovascular risk monitoring is essential.

#### Risk Assessment Parameters

| Parameter | Frequency | Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Blood pressure | Every visit | Hypertension is the most modifiable CV risk factor in CKD |
| Lipid profile | Annually; more frequently if dyslipidaemia | Dyslipidaemia common in nephrotic syndrome and CKD |
| HbA1c / glucose | Every 3 months (diabetic); annually (non-diabetic) | Diabetes is the leading cause of CKD and CV disease |
| Body weight / BMI | Every visit | Obesity is an independent CV risk factor |
| Smoking status | Every visit | Smoking cessation is the single most impactful lifestyle modification |
| ECG | Baseline; if cardiac symptoms develop | LVH, arrhythmia, ischaemia |
| Echocardiogram | If cardiac symptoms or signs; baseline if advanced CKD | LV function, LVH, valvular disease |
| BNP / NT-proBNP | If heart failure suspected | Heart failure screening in advanced CKD |
| hsCRP | Annually | Inflammation and CV risk |
| Coronary artery calcium score | If intermediate CV risk; pre-transplant evaluation | Risk stratification |

#### CV Risk Reduction in CKD

| Intervention | Target | Evidence |
|-------------|--------|---------|
| ACEi/ARB | BP < 130/80; proteinuria reduction | KDIGO 2021; RENAAL; IDNT |
| SGLT2 inhibitor | eGFR > 20 | DAPA-CKD; EMPA-KIDNEY |
| Statin (if indicated) | LDL < 1.8 mmol/L or >= 50% reduction | SHARP; KDIGO 2013 |
| Smoking cessation | Complete cessation | All-cause mortality reduction |
| Weight management | BMI 20-25 kg/m2 | CV risk reduction |
| Glycaemic control (diabetic) | HbA1c < 53 mmol/mol (if safe) | UKPDS; ADVANCE |
