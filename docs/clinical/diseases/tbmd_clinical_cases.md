## 1. Adolescent with Macroscopic Hematuria During Exercise

**Presentation:** A 14-year-old boy presents with episode of cola-coloured urine after a marathon soccer tournament. No prior renal history. Mother has microscopic hematuria. No sore throat. No dysuria.

**Exam:** BP 125/70. No edema. Throat normal. No rash.

**Diagnosis:** TBMD presenting with exercise-induced macroscopic hematuria

**Management:** No treatment. Reassured: macroscopic hematuria in TBMD can occur with exercise or infections. Spontaneous resolution expected. Advised to reduce extreme exercise during episodes. Annual dipstick.

**Outcome:** Macroscopic hematuria resolved in 3 days. Microscopic hematuria persists. No further gross episodes over 1 year follow-up. eGFR normal. No proteinuria.


## 2. TBMD with Mild Proteinuria Development

**Presentation:** A 45-year-old man with known TBMD (diagnosed age 25, GBM thinning 220 nm, persistent hematuria) presents for routine follow-up. Dipstick now shows 1+ protein (previously negative). He feels well. No hearing symptoms.

**Exam:** BP 126/78. No edema. Fundoscopy normal. Audiometry: mild high-frequency loss (age-appropriate).

**Diagnosis:** TBMD with new mild proteinuria — possible evolving phenotype

**Management:** Ramipril 5 mg started for proteinuria management. Repeat labs in 3 months. Audiometry and ophthalmology to re-exclude Alport. Patient reassured that mild proteinuria may occur in TBMD but needs monitoring.

**Outcome:** Month 3: UPCR 0.4, eGFR 86. Month 6: UPCR 0.3, eGFR 84. Ramipril well tolerated. Hearing and eye exams normal. No Alport features. Proteinuria stable. Continues annual monitoring.


## 3. Discharge After Stable Long-Term Follow-Up

**Presentation:** A 50-year-old woman with TBMD diagnosed at age 30 (confirmed by biopsy: GBM thickness 225 nm) has been followed annually for 20 years. Persistent microscopic hematuria throughout. No proteinuria ever. BP normal. eGFR stable.

**Exam:** BP 124/78. No edema. Hearing normal for age. Urinalysis: 1+ blood.

**Diagnosis:** TBMD — 20-year stable course confirming benign prognosis

**Management:** Discharged from nephrology to primary care. Annual urine dipstick and BP check by GP. Patient letter provided for insurance: TBMD is benign, no treatment needed, normal life expectancy. Advised to return if new symptoms develop.

**Outcome:** Discharged. 5 years post-discharge: hematuria persists, BP and eGFR normal. No complications. Patient fully reassured and living normal life.


## 4. TBMD Misdiagnosed as IgA Nephropathy — Avoided Immunosuppression

**Presentation:** A 32-year-old man was diagnosed with IgA nephropathy 2 years ago at another centre based on hematuria and a biopsy read as mesangial proliferation. He was offered steroids but declined. Second opinion requested. No proteinuria, normal eGFR. Review of original biopsy.

**Exam:** BP 124/76. No edema. Audiometry normal. Ophthalmology normal.

**Diagnosis:** Revised Diagnosis: Thin Basement Membrane Nephropathy (not IgA Nephropathy)

**Management:** NO immunosuppression needed. Previous steroid recommendation rescinded. Patient education: TBMD is benign. Prognosis excellent. Avoid NSAIDs. Annual monitoring. Genetic counseling offered.

**Outcome:** Revised diagnosis led to discontinuation of unnecessary immunosuppression. 2-year follow-up: stable hematuria, normal eGFR, no proteinuria. Patient relieved and reassured.


## 5. Adult with Persistent Hematuria and Family History

**Presentation:** A 35-year-old woman is referred for persistent microscopic hematuria found on insurance physical. She reports that her father and paternal aunt have had hematuria since childhood with normal kidney function. No foamy urine, no hearing loss. One pregnancy uncomplicated.

**Exam:** BP 118/74. No edema. Fundoscopy normal. Audiometry: normal.

**Diagnosis:** Thin Basement Membrane Nephropathy — autosomal dominant pattern

**Management:** No treatment. Reassured of benign prognosis. Genetic counseling offered for family planning (50% transmission risk). Advised normal lifestyle, avoid NSAIDs. Annual dipstick.

**Outcome:** 5-year follow-up: hematuria persists, UPCR <0.1, eGFR stable 88-92. No hypertension. Considered for discharge to primary care.


## 6. Child with Incidental Microscopic Hematuria

**Presentation:** A 9-year-old asymptomatic boy is found to have microscopic hematuria on routine school urinalysis. No history of gross hematuria, edema, or urinary symptoms. Mother reports she had hematuria as a child and was told it was benign. No hearing loss. Normal vision.

**Exam:** BP 100/62 (normal for age). No edema. Normal growth. Fundoscopy normal. Hearing normal to clinical testing.

**Diagnosis:** Thin Basement Membrane Nephropathy — classic childhood presentation

**Management:** No treatment required. Reassurance provided to family. Explanation of benign prognosis. Advise avoid NSAIDs. Annual dipstick and BP check at pediatrician.

**Outcome:** Annual review for 2 years: persistent microscopic hematuria, stable eGFR 108-112, UPCR <0.1. No proteinuria. Discharged to primary care. Normal growth and development.


## 7. Pregnancy in a Woman with TBMD

**Presentation:** A 30-year-old primigravida (12 weeks pregnant) with known TBMD (diagnosed age 16, persistent hematuria, GBM thinning 195 nm). No prior pregnancies. Normal kidney function. No proteinuria. She is concerned about pregnancy risks.

**Exam:** BP 112/68. No edema. Urinalysis: 2+ blood, negative protein.

**Diagnosis:** TBMD — pregnancy in a patient with benign familial hematuria

**Management:** No treatment needed for TBMD during pregnancy. Standard obstetric care. Monitor BP and urine dipstick each trimester. Reassurance: TBMD does not increase pregnancy complication risk. No contraindication to vaginal delivery or epidural.

**Outcome:** Uncomplicated pregnancy. BP 110-120/65-75 throughout. No proteinuria. Term delivery (39 weeks, 3.2 kg healthy infant). Postpartum: hematuria persists, BP normal, eGFR 94. Excellent outcome.


## 8. TBMD Diagnosis Through Family Cascade Screening

**Presentation:** A 28-year-old man is evaluated because his brother was recently diagnosed with Alport syndrome (COL4A5 mutation, X-linked). The patient has no symptoms but urinalysis shows microscopic hematuria. No hearing loss. Normal vision.

**Exam:** BP 120/76. No edema. Fundoscopy normal. Hearing normal.

**Diagnosis:** TBMD — COL4A4 heterozygous carrier (different gene from brother's X-linked Alport)

**Management:** No treatment needed. Genetic counseling: 50% transmission risk. Risk of Alport only if partner also carries COL4A3/COL4A4 mutation (<1% chance). Reassurance: benign prognosis. Annual monitoring advised.

**Outcome:** Stable hematuria, normal function over 3 years follow-up. Patient engaged in family planning discussions with genetic counselor. Reassured about benign course.
