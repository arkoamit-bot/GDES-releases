## 2. Disease Record — Full 21-Field Schema
### 2.1 Definition
IgA Nephropathy (IgAN), also known as Berger's disease, is the most common primary glomerulonephritis worldwide. It is defined by the presence of dominant or co-dominant IgA deposits in the glomerular mesangium on immunofluorescence microscopy. The clinical spectrum ranges from asymptomatic microscopic hematuria to rapidly progressive glomerulonephritis leading to kidney failure. First described by Berger and Hinglais in 1968, IgAN remains the leading cause of glomerulonephritis-related end-stage kidney disease (ESKD) in young adults. The disease is characterized by a highly variable clinical course, with approximately 30-40% of patients progressing to ESKD within 20-30 years of diagnosis. Recent advances in understanding the pathogenesis — particularly the 'four-hit hypothesis' involving galactose-deficient IgA1 (Gd-IgA1), anti-glycan antibodies, immune complex formation, and mesangial activation — have opened new therapeutic avenues including targeted therapies such as iptacopan, narsoplimab, and budesonide targeted-release formulation.
### 2.2 Epidemiology
IgA Nephropathy has a striking geographic and ethnic distribution. It is most prevalent in East Asian populations (particularly Chinese, Japanese, and Korean), where it accounts for 30-50% of all primary glomerulonephritis diagnoses. In Caucasians, prevalence is approximately 15-30%, while it is significantly less common in individuals of African descent (approximately 2-5%). The estimated global incidence ranges from 0.5 to 4.0 per 100,000 person-years. Males are affected 2-3 times more frequently than females in most populations. Peak age of onset is in the second to fourth decades of life (20-40 years), though presentation can occur at any age. The male predominance is less pronounced in Asian populations. Familial clustering occurs in 5-15% of cases, suggesting genetic susceptibility factors. Genome-wide association studies (GWAS) have identified multiple risk loci including MHC region, TNFSF13, and HORMAD2. The variability in geographic distribution likely reflects both genetic predisposition and environmental triggers. The incidence of biopsy-diagnosed IgAN has increased over recent decades, partly due to increased biopsy rates for asymptomatic urinary abnormalities discovered through routine screening programs, particularly in Japan and Korea where school and workplace urinalysis screening is widely practiced. Approximately 30-40% of patients will progress to ESKD within 20 years of diagnosis, making IgAN a significant contributor to the global ESKD burden, particularly in Asia.
### 2.3 Aetiology
The exact etiology of IgA Nephropathy is multifactorial, involving complex interactions between genetic susceptibility, environmental triggers, and immune dysregulation. Genetic factors: GWAS have identified susceptibility loci at 1q32 (CFH/CFHR cluster), 22q12 (HORMAD2/LIF), 8p23 (TNFSF13 — encoding APRIL), 6p21 (MHC region), 16p11, 17p13, and 9q34. Variants in the CFH/CFHR region alter complement regulation. TNFSF13 variants increase APRIL production, promoting IgA class switching. The MHC region associations involve specific HLA alleles including HLA-DRB1*1501 and HLA-DQB1*0601. Environmental triggers include mucosal infections (upper respiratory tract, gastrointestinal), which often trigger episodes of macroscopic hematuria (sympharyngitic hematuria). Mucosal dysregulation at mucosal-associated lymphoid tissue (MALT) sites, particularly tonsillar and intestinal mucosa, leads to overproduction of IgA1. Dietary antigens (gluten, microbial components from gut dysbiosis) may also contribute. Dysregulation of the intestinal mucosal immune system with altered gut microbiota composition has been implicated in recent studies. Aberrant IgA1 glycosylation: The central abnormality is the production of galactose-deficient IgA1 (Gd-IgA1) by mucosal plasma cells. This results from reduced activity of Core 1 beta-1,3-galactosyltransferase (C1GALT1) and its chaperone Cosmc in IgA1-producing cells. Gd-IgA1 is recognized by circulating autoantibodies (IgG, IgA1) directed against the exposed hinge region glycans, forming immune complexes that deposit in the glomerular mesangium.
### 2.4 Pathophysiology
The pathogenesis of IgA Nephropathy is best understood through the 'four-hit hypothesis': Hit 1: Increased production of galactose-deficient IgA1 (Gd-IgA1). In patients with IgAN, a subset of IgA1-producing plasma cells (particularly those in mucosal sites) produce IgA1 with reduced galactose content in the hinge region O-glycans. This results from decreased expression and activity of C1GALT1 and its chaperone Cosmc. Genetic variants in these regulatory pathways contribute to the trait. Elevated circulating levels of Gd-IgA1 are found in 50-80% of IgAN patients and also in some first-degree relatives, indicating this is a heritable susceptibility trait. Hit 2: Formation of autoantibodies directed against Gd-IgA1. The immune system recognizes the aberrantly glycosylated IgA1 as autoantigen, producing IgG and IgA1 autoantibodies specific to the exposed hinge region glycopeptides. These autoantibodies are of restricted clonality and often use specific variable region genes. Hit 3: Formation of pathogenic immune complexes. Circulating Gd-IgA1 complexes with anti-glycan autoantibodies, forming high-molecular-weight immune complexes that resist normal hepatic clearance due to their size and composition. These complexes also activate the alternative pathway of complement through interactions with properdin, factor H, and C3. Hit 4: Mesangial deposition and downstream injury. The immune complexes deposit in the glomerular mesangium, activating mesangial cells through CD89 (Fc-alpha receptor), transferrin receptor (CD71), and Toll-like receptors. Activated mesangial cells proliferate, secrete extracellular matrix, and produce pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-6, TNF-alpha, TGF-beta) and chemokines (MCP-1, IL-8). This leads to mesangial expansion, glomerular inflammation, podocyte injury, and eventually sclerosis. Complement activation via the alternative and lectin pathways amplifies the inflammatory response. The clinical expression and rate of progression depend on the balance between these pathogenic mechanisms and protective regulatory factors.
### 2.5 Clinical Presentation
IgA Nephropathy has a heterogeneous clinical presentation that varies by age, ethnicity, and disease severity. Typical presentation: The most common presentation (40-50% of cases) is persistent microscopic hematuria detected incidentally on routine urinalysis, often accompanied by mild proteinuria (<1 g/day). Episodic macroscopic hematuria: Seen in 30-50% of patients, particularly children and young adults. These episodes occur within 24-48 hours of an upper respiratory tract infection (sympharyngitic hematuria) or, less commonly, gastrointestinal infections. Unlike post-streptococcal GN, there is no latent period. The visible hematuria typically resolves within 3-5 days but may persist longer. Atypical presentation: Nephrotic-range proteinuria occurs in 5-10% of cases, sometimes with nephrotic syndrome. Rapidly progressive glomerulonephritis (RPGN) with crescentic disease occurs in 2-5% and requires urgent treatment. Emergency presentation: Acute kidney injury (AKI) from crescentic disease, severe macroscopic hematuria causing tubular obstruction, or malignant hypertension. Pediatric considerations: Macroscopic hematuria more common; overall prognosis better in children; spontaneous remission more frequent. Elderly considerations: More likely to present with reduced eGFR and hypertension; worse renal survival. Pregnancy considerations: Pre-pregnancy counseling essential; increased risk of gestational hypertension, pre-eclampsia, and eGFR decline in women with proteinuria >1 g/day or eGFR <60 mL/min.

### 2.12 Treatment Overview
The management of IgA Nephropathy follows a stepwise approach based on risk stratification: Level 1 — Supportive therapy (ALL patients): RAAS blockade (ACEi or ARB) titrated to maximally tolerated dose, blood pressure control (<130/80 mmHg), sodium restriction (<5 g/day), cardiovascular risk management, lifestyle modification (weight loss, smoking cessation, exercise). Level 2 — Add immunosuppression for HIGH-RISK patients: Patients with persistent proteinuria >1 g/day after 3-6 months of optimized supportive therapy, despite adequate BP control. Options include corticosteroids (6-month modified PO regimen per TESTING trial), targeted-release budesonide (TRF-budesonide, Nefecon), or mycophenolate mofetil (in select populations). Level 3 — Rapidly progressive disease: Pulse methylprednisolone followed by oral corticosteroids plus cyclophosphamide (or mycophenolate mofetil), similar to ANCA vasculitis protocols. Recent advances: SGLT2 inhibitors (dapagliflozin, empagliflozin) now recommended for patients with eGFR >25 mL/min to reduce proteinuria and slow progression. Hydroxychloroquine shows promise in reducing proteinuria in Chinese cohorts. Targeted therapies including anti-APRIL (sibeprenlimab, zigakibart), complement inhibitors (iptacopan, narsoplimab), and endothelin receptor antagonists (sparsentan, atrasentan) are under investigation or recently approved. KDIGO 2025 guidelines are evolving to incorporate these newer agents as evidence accumulates.

### 2.14 Monitoring Protocol
Baseline: eGFR, UPCR or 24h proteinuria, serum albumin, BP, Oxford MEST-C score on biopsy. Low-risk patients (proteinuria <0.5 g/day, eGFR normal): monitor every 6-12 months with eGFR, UPCR, BP. Moderate-risk patients (proteinuria 0.5-1 g/day, eGFR 30-60): monitor every 3-6 months with same parameters. High-risk patients (proteinuria >1 g/day, eGFR <30): monitor every 1-3 months. During immunosuppression: monitor monthly for drug levels, renal function, blood count, liver enzymes, glucose, and infections. After treatment: monitor every 3 months initially, then every 6 months if stable. Indications for re-biopsy: rapidly progressive course, unexplained eGFR decline, significant proteinuria increase, assessment before immunosuppression in patients with prior non-diagnostic biopsy, or suspicion of transplant recurrence. Immunization: influenza (annual), pneumococcal (every 5 years), COVID-19 (per local schedule). Live vaccines contraindicated during immunosuppression.

### 2.16 Relapse Information
Relapse after immunosuppression occurs in 15-40% of treated patients within 5 years of treatment completion. Risk factors for relapse: younger age at diagnosis, higher proteinuria at presentation, rapid initial response to therapy (may indicate steroid-sensitive disease that re-activates), incomplete Oxford MEST-C resolution. Relapse management: Re-initiate RAASi optimization; consider repeat course of corticosteroids; consider MMF or other steroid-sparing agents for frequent relapsers; ensure BP control and SGLT2i therapy. Transplant recurrence: IgA deposition recurs histologically in 30-60% of allografts; clinical recurrence (proteinuria, hematuria, graft dysfunction) in 5-10%. Risk factors for recurrence: young recipient age, aggressive primary disease, rapid progression to ESKD, HLA matching, living-related donor. Management of recurrence: optimize RAASi, consider corticosteroid therapy for clinically significant recurrence, MMF is part of standard immunosuppression and may be protective.
### 2.17 Long-Term Prognosis
Renal survival is highly variable: approximately 70-80% at 10 years, 50-60% at 20 years, and 30-40% at 30 years from diagnosis. The median time from diagnosis to ESKD is approximately 15-20 years. Predictors of worse prognosis include: proteinuria >1 g/day persisting despite therapy, hypertension, reduced eGFR at diagnosis, T2 score on Oxford classification, C2 crescents, and lack of proteinuria reduction with therapy. The International IgAN Prediction Tool provides validated 5-year risk estimates. Complete remission (proteinuria <0.3 g/day, normal eGFR) is associated with excellent long-term outcomes. Spontaneous remission occurs in 5-15% of patients, more commonly in those with mild disease and hematuria-only presentation. Mortality is increased in IgAN patients compared to the general population, primarily from cardiovascular causes and ESKD-related complications. Recent cohort data suggest improving outcomes over recent decades with better supportive care and appropriate use of immunosuppression.
### 2.18 Evidence Summary
The evidence base for IgA Nephropathy has evolved significantly in recent years. Key landmark trials: (1) TESTING trial (2022) — randomized 503 patients to modified-dose prednisolone (0.5 mg/kg alternate days, max 40 mg, taper over 6-9 months) vs placebo. Results: 40% reduction in 5-year ESKD risk (HR 0.60, p=0.002). Serious adverse events increased (11% vs 4%), mainly infections. (2) STOP-IgAN trial (2015) — 162 patients with proteinuria >0.75 g/day despite 6 months supportive therapy randomized to immunosuppression (steroids ± cyclophosphamide/MMF) vs continued supportive care. No difference in eGFR decline at 3 years; 86% of supportive group did not qualify for immunosuppression due to proteinuria reduction. (3) NefIgArd trial (Phase 3, 2023) — 364 patients randomized to TRF-budesonide (Nefecon) 16 mg/day vs placebo for 9 months. Results: significant proteinuria reduction (27% at 12 months), eGFR benefit of 5.1 mL/min at 24 months. (4) DAPA-CKD (2020) — sub-analysis of patients with IgAN showed dapagliflozin reduced proteinuria by 38% and slowed eGFR decline. (5) Supportive care trials: RAASi benefits well-established from multiple RCTs and meta-analyses. Emerging evidence: Complement inhibitors (iptacopan, narsoplimab), APRIL inhibitors (sibeprenlimab), and endothelin receptor antagonists (sparsentan) show promise in Phase 2/3 trials. The KDIGO 2021 Guideline provides the current standard framework with updates expected in 2025 reflecting new trial data.
