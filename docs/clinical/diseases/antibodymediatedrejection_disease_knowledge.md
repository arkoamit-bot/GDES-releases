## 2. Disease Record — Full 21-Field Schema

### 2.1 Definition

Antibody-Mediated Rejection (ABMR) is a form of renal allograft rejection mediated by donor-specific antibodies (DSA) targeting human leukocyte antigen (HLA) or non-HLA antigens on graft endothelial cells. ABMR is classified into acute/active ABMR (within days to months of transplantation — rising creatinine, graft dysfunction, C4d deposition in peritubular capillaries, microvascular inflammation) and chronic ABMR (slowly progressive graft dysfunction over months to years — transplant glomerulopathy, multilayering of peritubular capillary basement membranes, arterial intimal fibrosis). ABMR is the leading cause of late allograft loss (beyond the first year) and accounts for 50-60% of all rejection episodes in the modern immunosuppression era. Diagnosis requires three criteria: (1) histologic evidence of microvascular injury, (2) C4d deposition or evidence of DSA, and (3) serologic evidence of DSA. Treatment involves anti-humoral therapies including IVIG, plasma exchange (PLEX), rituximab, and proteasome inhibitors (bortezomib/carfilzomib), though outcomes for chronic ABMR remain poor.

### 2.2 Epidemiology

Prevalence: ABMR occurs in 10-30% of kidney transplant recipients. Acute/active ABMR: 5-10% in the first year despite modern immunosuppression. Chronic ABMR: 15-25% by 5 years post-transplant — the leading cause of late graft loss. Risk factors: pre-formed DSA (especially DSA with MFI >3000, C1q-binding DSA, class II DSA), previous transplant (20-30% risk), female gender (pregnancy-sensitised), blood transfusions, non-adherence to immunosuppression (30-50% of late ABMR), under-immunosuppression (tacrolimus trough <5 ng/mL), younger recipient age, African American recipient, delayed graft function. Incidence varies by centre (10-30%) depending on DSA screening protocols and biopsy practice. Chronic ABMR accounts for 50-60% of late (>1 year) graft failures.

### 2.3 Aetiology

Immunologic: DSA directed against donor HLA (class I: HLA-A, -B, -C; class II: HLA-DR, -DQ, -DP — class II DSA more common in chronic ABMR) or non-HLA antigens (AT1R, ETAR, agrin, perlecan, collagen V, K-alpha-1 tubulin). Complement-fixing DSA (C1q+, C3d+) cause more severe injury. Non-adherence: single most common modifiable cause of late ABMR — 30-50% of cases. Under-immunosuppression: sub-therapeutic CNI levels (<5 ng/mL tacrolimus), steroid withdrawal. Infection-driven: CMV infection increases DSA production. Re-transplant: DSA from prior graft. Pregnancy: HLA sensitisation from foetal antigens. Blood transfusion: pre-transplant sensitisation. Subclinical ABMR: DSA-positive without graft dysfunction — progresses to overt ABMR in 40-60% within 2 years.

### 2.4 Pathophysiology

Phase 1 — DSA Binding: DSA binds to HLA/non-HLA antigens on graft endothelial cells (peritubular capillaries, glomerular capillaries). Complement-dependent pathway: DSA (IgG1, IgG3) activates classical complement pathway -> C4d deposition (biomarker) -> C5a (chemotactic) -> C5b-9 membrane attack complex -> endothelial cell activation and injury. Complement-independent pathway: DSA cross-links HLA on endothelium -> intracellular signalling (mTOR, PI3K/Akt, ERK) -> endothelial proliferation and survival -> thickening of capillary walls -> transplant glomerulopathy. Phase 2 — Microvascular Inflammation: Activated endothelium upregulates adhesion molecules (VCAM-1, ICAM-1) -> NK cell and macrophage recruitment -> capillaritis (glomerulitis, peritubular capillaritis). NK cells mediate antibody-dependent cell-mediated cytotoxicity (ADCC) via Fc-gamma receptors. Phase 3 — Chronic Injury: Repeated/persistent injury leads to transplant glomerulopathy (GBM duplication), peritubular capillary basement membrane multilayering, arterial intimal fibrosis, interstitial fibrosis/tubular atrophy (IFTA) -> progressive graft dysfunction -> graft loss.

### 2.5 Clinical Presentation

ACUTE/ACTIVE ABMR: typically within days to months post-transplant. Rising creatinine (50-100% from baseline), oliguria, graft tenderness/swelling, fever, hypertension, reduced urine output. May present with <30% creatinine rise in subclinical ABMR detected on surveillance biopsy. CHRONIC ABMR: insidious onset >6 months (often years). Slowly rising creatinine, new/worsening proteinuria (often subnephrotic: UPCR 0.5-2.0), hypertension. May be asymptomatic until advanced. Proteinuria is a hallmark of transplant glomerulopathy. End-stage: worsening oedema, anaemia of CKD, fatigue, anorexia. SUBCLINICAL ABMR: DSA-positive, normal creatinine, but biopsy shows microvascular inflammation. Detected on surveillance biopsy (protocol biopsy at 3, 6, 12 months). Key: ABMR can be C4d-positive or C4d-negative (the latter is increasingly recognised).

### 2.6 Diagnostic Criteria

| # | Criterion | Essential | Evidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Histologic evidence of microvascular inflammation: glomerulitis (g>0), peritubular capillaritis (ptc>0) on light microscopy — Banff lesion scores | Yes | 1 |
| 2 | C4d deposition in peritubular capillaries (C4d2 or C4d3 by IF or IHC) OR evidence of DSA in the circulation | Yes | 1 |
| 3 | Serologic evidence of DSA (donor-specific antibody) against HLA or non-HLA antigens — tested by single-antigen bead (SAB) assay | Yes | 1 |
| 4 | Transplant glomerulopathy (cg>0) on electron microscopy — GBM double contours, PTCML | No | 1 |
| 5 | Arterial intimal fibrosis (cv>0) — indicator of chronic ABMR | No | 1 |
| 6 | Molecular classifier (MMDx) showing ABMR gene expression signature | No | 2 |
| 7 | Rising DSA MFI or new DSA — evidence of active humoral response | No | 2 |
| 8 | Exclusion of T-cell mediated rejection (Banff Type IA/B), CNI toxicity, BK virus nephropathy, and recurrent GN | Yes | 1 |

### 2.8 Laboratory Findings

  * 
Rising serum creatinine — hallmark of graft dysfunction (acute or insidious)  * Proteinuria (UPCR 0.5-5.0) — common in chronic ABMR with transplant glomerulopathy  * DSA testing: single-antigen bead (SAB) assay — MFI >1000-3000 (threshold varies by centre). C1q-binding and C3d-binding assays identify complement-fixing DSA (worse prognosis)  * C4d staining on biopsy: peritubular capillary deposition (IF or IHC). C4d-negative ABMR is increasingly recognised (30-50%)  * Anti-HLA antibody screening: class I and class II by Luminex. De novo DSA: most commonly anti-DQ (DSA against HLA-DQ)  * Non-HLA antibodies: anti-AT1R, anti-ETAR (if HLA DSA negative but biopsy suspicious)  * BK virus PCR (plasma) — to exclude BK virus nephropathy (biopsy mimics ABMR)  * Tacrolimus trough level — exclude under-immunosuppression as contributor

### 2.9 Biopsy Findings

  * 
ACUTE/ACTIVE ABMR (Banff 2019): g>0 (glomerulitis), ptc>0 (peritubular capillaritis), C4d2/3 in PTC. Optional: intimal arteritis (v>0), arterial fibrinoid necrosis (severe). EM: early GBM changes, PTC basement membrane multilayering not always present in acute  * CHRONIC ABMR (Banff 2019): cg>0 (transplant glomerulopathy — GBM double contours) on EM or LM (PAS/silver stain). PTCML (peritubular capillary basement membrane multilayering) on EM — >5 layers. Arterial intimal fibrosis (cv>0). IFTA. C4d may be negative in late chronic ABMR  * C4d-NEGATIVE ABMR: microvascular inflammation (g+ptc >=2) with DSA but C4d negative. Same poor prognosis as C4d-positive. Increasingly recognised — molecular classifiers helpful  * MIXED REJECTION: features of both ABMR and TCMR (tubulitis, interstitial infiltrate)  * IMMUNOFLUORESCENCE: Linear C4d along peritubular capillaries (bright, circumferential). C3d, C1q may also be positive. IgG, IgM, IgA usually negative (unless recurrent GN)

### 2.11 Risk Stratification

| Factor | Impact | Risk Level |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-formed DSA (MFI >5000) | High risk of acute ABMR — up to 50% without desensitisation | High |
| C1q-binding DSA | Complement-fixing — severe microvascular inflammation, worse graft survival | High |
| De novo DSA (especially anti-DQ) | Chronic ABMR risk — 40-60% graft loss at 5 years | High |
| Non-adherence to immunosuppression | 30-50% of all late ABMR — highest modifiable risk factor | High |
| Tacrolimus trough <5 ng/mL | Suboptimal — increased DSA formation risk | Moderate |
| Previous transplant with ABMR history | High risk of recurrent ABMR — 20-30% | High |
| CMV infection/DNAemia | Promotes DSA production — 2-3x increased ABMR risk | Moderate |

### 2.12 Treatment Overview

ACUTE/ACTIVE ABMR FIRST-LINE: Pulse corticosteroids (methylprednisolone 500-1000 mg IV x3 days) + IVIG (2 g/kg divided over 2-5 days) + Plasma exchange (PLEX: 3-5 sessions, every other day). Rituximab (375 mg/m2 x1) is added if severe or PLEX-refractory. SECOND-LINE (for severe/refractory): Bortezomib (1.3 mg/m2 x4 doses, Day 1, 4, 8, 11) — targets plasma cells. Carfilzomib (second-generation proteasome inhibitor). Eculizumab (anti-C5) — limited evidence (complement blockade). Splenectomy (historical — rarely used). CHRONIC ABMR: No proven effective therapy. Optimise immunosuppression (tacrolimus target 5-8 ng/mL, MMF 2 g/day). IVIG + Rituximab may stabilise (but rarely reverses). Bortezomib limited benefit for chronic ABMR. Supportive: BP control, RAASi for proteinuria, management of CKD complications. Prevent/slow progression by optimising immunosuppression, ensuring adherence, and treating active rejection promptly. SUPPORTIVE: BP <130/80, RAASi for proteinuria, statin, vitamin D, management of anaemia and mineral bone disorder as CKD progresses. Dialysis and re-transplantation if graft fails.

### 2.13 Treatment Algorithm

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | Confirm active ABMR by Banff criteria: biopsy-proven microvascular inflammation + C4d and/or DSA |
| 2 | Assess severity: creatinine rise (%), DSA MFI and C1q-binding, biopsy lesion scores (g, ptc, v) |
| 3 | Mild-moderate ABMR: Methylprednisolone 500 mg IV x3 + IVIG 2 g/kg + PLEX x3-5 sessions. Optimise tacrolimus (target 5-8 ng/mL) and MMF |
| 4 | Severe/refractory ABMR: Add Rituximab 375 mg/m2. Consider Bortezomib 1.3 mg/m2 x4 doses |
| 5 | Chronic ABMR: Optimise baseline immunosuppression. IVIG 2 g/kg monthly x3-6 months. RAASi for proteinuria. BP <130/80 |
| 6 | Monitor response: weekly creatinine, DSA MFI at 1 month. Repeat biopsy if no improvement at 3 months |
| 7 | Non-responders: Counselling about graft loss. Prepare for dialysis or re-transplantation |
| 8 | Prevention of recurrent ABMR in re-transplant: desensitisation + aggressive peri-transplant PLEX/IVIG + rituximab |

### 2.14 Monitoring Protocol

DSA monitoring: pre-transplant (baseline), then at 3, 6, 12 months post-transplant, and annually thereafter. If DSA detected: monthly MFI trend, C1q/C3d binding assays. Graft biopsy: for-cause biopsy when creatinine rises OR DSA detected (even with stable creatinine — subclinical ABMR). Protocol biopsy: 3 months, 12 months in high-risk patients. After ABMR treatment: weekly creatinine x1 month, monthly DSA x3 months, repeat biopsy at 3-6 months to assess histologic response. Non-adherence monitoring: tacrolimus trough levels (variability >30% = non-adherence), medication event monitoring (MEMS caps), self-report. Long-term: annual eGFR, UPCR, DSA, tacrolimus trough. Bio-banking: store sera for retrospective DSA testing.

### 2.15 Complications

  * 
Graft loss: 30-50% at 5 years after chronic ABMR diagnosis — leading cause of late graft failure  * Infections from intensified immunosuppression: CMV, BK virus, EBV (PTLD), opportunistic infections  * Treatment toxicity: PLEX (line infections, bleeding), IVIG (thrombosis, AKI, haemolysis), Rituximab (infusion reactions, PML), Bortezomib (neuropathy, GI toxicity)  * Transplant glomerulopathy: irreversible structural damage — proteinuria, progressive CKD  * Sensitisation from PLEX/IVIG: may complicate future transplantation

### 2.16 Relapse Information

ABMR can recur in subsequent transplants, especially if pre-formed DSA persists or if non-adherence continues. Risk of recurrent ABMR in re-transplant: 20-30% (higher if prior graft loss due to ABMR). Prevention: pre-transplant desensitisation (PLEX/IVIG/Rituximab), aggressive peri-transplant immunosuppression, avoidance of DSA at transplant. Within the same graft: recurrent/persistent ABMR occurs in 30-50% after treatment — defined as ongoing microvascular inflammation on repeat biopsy despite therapy. Chronic ABMR does not resolve once transplant glomerulopathy has developed — treatment aims to slow progression.

### 2.17 Long-Term Prognosis

ACUTE ABMR WITH COMPLETE RESPONSE: 85-90% 1-year graft survival if creatinine returns to baseline and DSA clears. Partial response: 50-60% 5-year survival. CHRONIC ABMR: 50-70% 5-yr graft survival if transplant glomerulopathy present. Without cg: 80% 5-yr. Overall: ABMR accounts for 50-60% of late graft failures. Poor prognostic factors: C1q-binding DSA, high MFI (>5000), class II DSA (vs class I), severe histologic lesions (g+ptc >3), transplant glomerulopathy at diagnosis, IFTA >25%, failure to clear DSA after treatment. Better prognosis: early detection (subclinical), reversible DSA, acute presentation (vs chronic), response to PLEX/IVIG. Re-transplant: feasible after ABMR graft loss but requires careful desensitisation.

### 2.18 Evidence Summary

RCTs: RCT of IVIG + Rituximab vs IVIG alone for ABMR (n=20) — no difference in graft survival but trend toward benefit (Ahn et al 2014). RCT of PLEX + IVIG + Rituximab vs PLEX + IVIG (n=40) — no difference in GFR at 12 months (Macklin et al 2020). BIG study: IVIG vs IVIG + Rituximab + PLEX — no significant difference (Macklin 2023). Bortezomib for ABMR: case series (n=20-40) show DSA reduction and histologic improvement. Eculizumab for ABMR: small series (n=10-20) suggest benefit for complement-fixing ABMR. Cohort studies (n=100+): DSA detection + protocol biopsy improves ABMR diagnosis and graft survival. Banff classification validated by molecular studies (INTERCOM, BFC) — molecular ABMR score correlates with histology and outcomes. Key: Chronic ABMR remains an area of major unmet need — no RCT has shown a proven therapy to reverse or reliably slow chronic ABMR.
