## 2. Disease Record — Full 21-Field Schema
### 2.1 Definition
Calcineurin Inhibitor Nephrotoxicity (CNI Toxicity) refers to structural and functional renal injury caused by calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus and cyclosporine), manifesting as both acute (reversible, haemodynamically mediated) and chronic (irreversible, structural) forms. Acute CNI toxicity is a dose-dependent, reversible decrease in glomerular filtration rate (GFR) caused by afferent arteriolar vasoconstriction mediated by endothelin-1 and thromboxane A2, with relative sparing of efferent arterioles, leading to reduced renal blood flow. Chronic CNI toxicity is characterised by progressive, irreversible histologic changes: arteriolar hyalinosis (nodular hyaline deposits in afferent arterioles), striped tubulointerstitial fibrosis and tubular atrophy (IFTA), tubular microcalcification, and ultimately global glomerulosclerosis. CNI toxicity is distinct from rejection by the absence of microvascular inflammation (glomerulitis, peritubular capillaritis), absence of tubulitis (or minimal), negative C4d staining, and negative DSA. Management involves minimising CNI exposure through dose reduction, switching to alternative immunosuppression (belatacept, mTOR inhibitor), or CNI withdrawal/minimisation protocols.
### 2.2 Epidemiology
Prevalence: Universal histologic finding in kidney transplant recipients on long-term CNI therapy. Acute CNI toxicity: up to 20-30% of patients at some point post-transplant (especially with high trough levels). Chronic CNI toxicity: histologic changes visible in >50% of biopsies by 2 years and >90% by 10 years post-transplant. Risk factors: high CNI trough levels (tacrolimus >10 ng/mL, cyclosporine >300 ng/mL), prolonged CNI therapy, African American recipient, delayed graft function, ischaemia-reperfusion injury, pre-existing cardiovascular disease, hypertension, diabetes, donor age >60, reduced nephron mass. Impact: CNI toxicity contributes to chronic graft dysfunction and late graft loss — but the relative contribution vs other causes (ABMR, BK, recurrent GN) is debated. In modern immunosuppression: prevalence of chronic CNI toxicity has not declined despite lower target levels — suggesting subclinical injury is ongoing even at therapeutic doses.
### 2.3 Aetiology
ACUTE CNI TOXICITY: Afferent arteriolar vasoconstriction due to CNI-mediated imbalance in vasoactive substances. CNIs (tacrolimus and cyclosporine) bind to immunophilins (FKBP12 and cyclophilin, respectively), inhibiting calcineurin — reducing NFAT-mediated cytokine transcription (immunosuppressive effect). Calcineurin inhibition in vascular smooth muscle cells and endothelium causes: (a) increased endothelin-1 (potent vasoconstrictor); (b) increased thromboxane A2; (c) decreased prostacyclin and nitric oxide (vasodilators); (d) activation of the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system; (e) sympathetic nervous system activation. Net effect: afferent arteriolar constriction -> reduced renal blood flow -> reduced GFR (reversible upon dose reduction). CHRONIC CNI TOXICITY: Repeated/continuous vasoconstriction -> chronic ischaemia -> microvascular and tubulointerstitial injury. Non-haemodynamic mechanisms: (a) TGF-beta upregulation -> fibroblast activation -> interstitial fibrosis; (b) apoptosis of tubular epithelial cells; (c) direct podocyte injury (proteinuria); (d) impaired tubular repair/regeneration; (e) oxidative stress. Additional: CNIs also cause hypertension (via vasoconstriction and sodium retention) and diabetes (tacrolimus — impaired insulin secretion), which further contribute to CKD progression.
### 2.4 Pathophysiology
ACUTE PHASE (days-weeks): CNI -> afferent arteriolar vasoconstriction (endothelin-1 up, NO/prostacyclin down) -> decreased renal blood flow (20-40% reduction) -> decreased GFR (10-30% reduction). Reversible: dose reduction restores vasodilatory balance within days. Tubular function preserved (no acute tubular necrosis unless very high levels). CHRONIC PHASE (months-years): Chronic intermittent ischaemia -> endothelial injury -> arteriolar hyalinosis (nodular hyaline material replaces smooth muscle in afferent arteriolar walls). Luminal narrowing -> downstream ischaemia -> striped tubulointerstitial fibrosis (fibrotic bands alternating with spared areas — pathognomonic). Tubular microcalcification (dystrophic calcification of necrotic tubular cells). Glomerular: ischaemic glomerular collapse -> focal segmental or global glomerulosclerosis. Podocyte: direct CNI toxicity causes foot process effacement -> proteinuria. PROGRESSION: Chronic CNI toxicity + hypertension + diabetes + recurrent rejection episodes -> cumulative nephron loss -> progressive CKD -> ESKD. Histologic triad: arteriolar hyalinosis + striped IFTA + tubular microcalcification. C4d negative, DSA negative (distinguishes from ABMR). No tubulitis or minimal (distinguishes from TCMR).
### 2.5 Clinical Presentation
ACUTE CNI TOXICITY: Rapid rise in creatinine (10-30% above baseline) within days to weeks of CNI initiation or dose increase. Associated with high trough levels (tacrolimus >10-15, cyclosporine >300-400). Usually asymptomatic — detected on routine lab monitoring. May present with hypertension (worsening control) or hyperkalaemia (CNI inhibits K+ excretion). Reversible within days of dose reduction. CHRONIC CNI TOXICITY: Slow, insidious rise in creatinine over months to years (eGFR decline 1-3 mL/min/year). Hypertension (often worsening). Subnephrotic proteinuria (UPCR 0.3-1.0) — from podocyte injury. Progressive CKD manifestations as eGFR declines. No acute symptoms. Often discovered on surveillance biopsy or when evaluating slowly declining graft function. Differentiating CNI toxicity from other causes of chronic graft dysfunction (ABMR, BKVN, recurrent GN) requires biopsy — clinical features alone are insufficient.

### 2.8 Laboratory Findings
  * 
Rising creatinine: acute rise (10-30% above baseline) with high CNI levels; or slow insidious rise over months-years in chronic toxicity  * CNI trough levels: tacrolimus >10-15 ng/mL (acute toxicity). Cyclosporine >300-400 ng/mL (C0) or >800-1000 (C2). Chronic toxicity can occur at therapeutic levels  * Hyperkalaemia: CNI inhibits renal K+ secretion (30-50% of patients) — mild to moderate  * Hyponatraemia: CNI impairs water excretion (dilutional)  * Metabolic acidosis: CNI impairs urinary acidification (distal RTA type 4 — hypoaldosteronism-like)  * Proteinuria (UPCR 0.3-1.0): chronic podocyte injury. Usually subnephrotic; nephrotic-range suggests other aetiologies  * DSA: negative (essential to exclude ABMR — DSA+ would indicate mixed pathology)  * C4d: negative on biopsy (essential)  * Urinalysis: bland — no haematuria, no WBC casts (unless concurrent pathology)
### 2.9 Biopsy Findings
  * 
ARTERIOLAR HYALINOSIS (hallmark): Eosinophilic, PAS-positive, nodular hyaline deposits in the media of afferent arterioles. Replaces smooth muscle cells. Can be focal or circumferential. Cyclosporine-associated: more pronounced afferent arteriole hyalinosis. Tacrolimus-associated: milder but still present. Distinguish from hypertensive arteriolar hyalinosis: CNI-associated is nodular (vs diffuse in hypertension). Severe: complete luminal obliteration  * STRIPED FIBROSIS (characteristic): Bands of interstitial fibrosis and tubular atrophy alternating with spared parenchyma. Result of chronic ischaemia from arteriolar narrowing. Most prominent in the medullary ray region. Severity correlates with cumulative CNI exposure  * TUBULAR MICROCALCIFICATION: Dystrophic calcification in tubular lumina or epithelial cells — due to cell necrosis and dystrophic calcification. Not pathognomonic but supportive  * GLOMERULAR: Ischaemic changes — GBM wrinkling and collapse, periglomerular fibrosis. Focal segmental glomerulosclerosis (secondary) in advanced disease. No immune complex deposits  * IMMUNOFLUORESCENCE: Complete negative — no C4d, no IgG, no complement. Essential negative finding (excludes ABMR, recurrent GN)  * ELECTRON MICROSCOPY: Non-specific — GBM wrinkling, foot process effacement (focal), no electron-dense deposits. Arteriolar hyaline material is electron-dense, granular

### 2.11 Risk Stratification
| Factor | Impact | Risk Level |
|---|---|---|
| High CNI trough levels (tac >10, cyclosporine >300) | Direct dose-dependent acute toxicity — reversible | High |
| Cumulative CNI exposure (duration x dose) | Directly correlates with chronic CNI toxicity severity — every year on CNI increases risk | High |
| Donor age >60 / reduced nephron mass | Lower nephron reserve — less tolerance for CNI-induced ischaemia | High |
| Delayed graft function / ischaemia-reperfusion | Primed for CNI toxicity — injured tubules more susceptible | Moderate |
| Hypertension / diabetes / cardiovascular disease | Synergistic injury with CNI vasoconstriction and metabolic effects | Moderate |
| African American recipient | Higher CNI requirements and increased toxicity risk | Moderate |
### 2.12 Treatment Overview
NO SPECIFIC THERAPY — management is preventive and supportive. ACUTE CNI TOXICITY: Reduce CNI dose (lower target trough: tacrolimus 4-6 ng/mL, cyclosporine 100-200 ng/mL). Improvement expected within days-weeks. If recurrent: consider switching to CNI-free regimen. CHRONIC CNI TOXICITY: Minimise CNI exposure — the only strategy to slow or prevent progression. Options: (1) CNI withdrawal/minimisation: reduce CNI to lowest effective dose (tacrolimus 3-5 ng/mL); (2) CNI-free immunosuppression — switch to belatacept (FDA-approved, improved GFR vs CNI). Belatacept is the ONLY immunosuppressant proven to slow CNI-toxicity-related GFR decline (BENEFIT/BENEFIT-EXT trials). (3) Switch from CNI to mTOR inhibitor (sirolimus/everolimus) — but mTOR inhibitors also have nephrotoxic potential (proteinuria, delayed wound healing). SUPPORTIVE: Strict BP control (<130/80) — RAASi first-line (ACEi/ARB). Manage diabetes (tacrolimus worsens). Control hyperkalaemia (patiromer, fludrocortisone if needed). Monitor for proteinuria. Avoid additional nephrotoxins (NSAIDs, contrast). Optimal: belatacept conversion for suitable recipients (preferably before eGFR <30 or severe IFTA, as advanced injury is irreversible).
### 2.13 Treatment Algorithm
| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | Confirm diagnosis: biopsy to exclude rejection (C4d, DSA, g, ptc, t scores). Typical: arteriolar hyalinosis, striped fibrosis, microcalcification |
| 2 | Acute CNI toxicity (Cr rise + high trough): reduce CNI dose by 25-50%. Target tacrolimus 4-6, cyclosporine 100-200. Recheck Cr in 1-2 weeks |
| 3 | Chronic CNI toxicity (biopsy-proven with arteriolar hyalinosis/fibrosis): minimise CNI exposure. Reduce target level or consider conversion |
| 4 | Conversion to belatacept: best option for preserving GFR. Avoid if EBV seronegative (risk of PTLD). Contraindicated in EBV D+/R- |
| 5 | Conversion to mTOR inhibitor (sirolimus/everolimus): alternative. Monitor for proteinuria (can worsen). Avoid if UPCR >0.5 or eGFR <30 |
| 6 | Supportive: RAASi for proteinuria and BP. Target <130/80. Manage hyperkalaemia. Avoid NSAIDs. Control diabetes (switch tacrolimus to cyclosporine if new-onset diabetes) |
| 7 | Advanced CNI toxicity (eGFR <30, IFTA >50%): accept that changes are irreversible. Manage as CKD. Prepare for RRT if needed |
### 2.14 Monitoring Protocol
CNI trough levels: tacrolimus (C0) — weekly for first month after initiation/change, then monthly until stable, then Q3-6 months. Cyclosporine: C0 and/or C2 (2-hour post-dose). Creatinine: monthly. UPCR: Q3-6 months (screen for proteinuria from mTOR inhibitor conversion or podocyte injury). BP: every visit. Glucose/HbA1c: Q3-6 months (tacrolimus-induced diabetes). Potassium: monthly (hyperkalaemia common). Magnesium: supplement if low (CNI causes Mg wasting). Protocol biopsy: consider at 12 months after conversion to assess histologic improvement. DSA monitoring: especially after CNI reduction/withdrawal — de novo DSA risk increases.
### 2.15 Complications
  * 
Progressive graft dysfunction: leading cause of late graft function decline in many transplant recipients  * End-stage kidney disease: cumulative nephron loss from CNI toxicity contributes to late graft failure  * New-onset diabetes after transplant (NODAT): tacrolimus impairs insulin secretion — 10-30% risk  * Hypertension: CNI-mediated vasoconstriction and sodium retention — difficult to control  * Hyperkalaemia: CNI inhibits renal K+ excretion — may require potassium binders or fludrocortisone  * Proteinuria: podocyte injury from chronic CNI toxicity or mTOR inhibitor conversion  * Thrombotic microangiopathy (TMA): rare but severe — CNI-mediated endothelial injury -> microangiopathic haemolytic anaemia, thrombocytopenia, AKI  * Need for re-transplantation: progressive graft loss from CNI toxicity
### 2.16 Relapse Information
CNI toxicity is not a relapsing condition — it is a cumulative, dose-dependent, ongoing process. If CNI dose is reduced, acute haemodynamic changes reverse but chronic structural changes (arteriolar hyalinosis, IFTA) are irreversible and do not regress. If CNI is resumed at higher doses after a period of minimisation, acute toxicity can recur. Conversion to belatacept or mTOR inhibitor removes ongoing CNI-mediated injury but does not reverse established fibrosis. The goal is to slow/prevent further progression, not to cure. Patients with advanced CNI toxicity who undergo re-transplantation: should receive CNI-free or CNI-minimised immunosuppression in the new graft to prevent recurrence.
### 2.17 Long-Term Prognosis
ACUTE CNI TOXICITY: Excellent — reversible within days-weeks of dose reduction. No permanent damage if promptly addressed. CHRONIC CNI TOXICITY: Progressive, dose-dependent. GFR decline averages 1-3 mL/min/year on CNI-based therapy. CONVERSION TO BELATACEPT: BENEFIT trial showed 50% improvement in eGFR trajectory — mean eGFR 10-15 mL/min higher than CNI-based group at 7 years. CONVERSION TO mTOR INHIBITOR: Variable — may stabilise GFR but proteinuria can worsen. NO INTERVENTION: Progressive CKD — 30-50% of long-term graft loss is attributable at least in part to CNI toxicity (though often mixed with ABMR/other causes). POOR PROGNOSTIC FACTORS: Severe arteriolar hyalinosis (>50% arterioles), IFTA >50% at diagnosis, proteinuria >1 g/g, eGFR <30 at diagnosis, donor age >60, pre-existing hypertension/diabetes. BEST OUTCOME: Early detection (protocol biopsy at 12 months) + early conversion to belatacept (before eGFR <30 or severe IFTA) — can stabilise or improve GFR.
### 2.18 Evidence Summary
RCTs: BENEFIT trial (n=1200, NEJM 2010): belatacept vs cyclosporine at 12 months — belatacept had higher GFR (mean 65 vs 50 mL/min) but higher acute rejection rate (17% vs 7%). BENEFIT-EXT (n=543, 2016): extended criteria donors — same benefit. 7-year follow-up (AJT 2019): belatacept group maintained eGFR advantage of 10-15 mL/min. TRANSFORM trial (n=2000, 2017): everolimus + reduced CNI vs MMF + standard CNI — similar GFR at 12 months. Meta-analyses: CNI withdrawal/ minimisation improves GFR (by 5-10 mL/min) but increases rejection risk (OR 1.5-2.0). Cohort studies: Protocol biopsies show arteriolar hyalinosis in 30-50% at 1 year, 60-80% at 5 years, >90% at 10 years. Observational: Belatacept conversion in CNI toxicity (n=100-200) — GFR improvement or stabilisation in 60-70%. KEY: Belatacept is the only drug proven to slow CNI-toxicity-related progression. CNI withdrawal/minimisation helps but risks rejection.
