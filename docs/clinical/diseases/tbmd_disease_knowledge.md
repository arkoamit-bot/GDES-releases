## 2. Disease Record — Full 21-Field Schema

### 2.1 Definition

Thin Basement Membrane Nephropathy (TBMD), also known as benign familial hematuria, is a hereditary glomerular basement membrane disorder characterized by persistent glomerular hematuria due to diffuse thinning of the GBM. It is caused by heterozygous mutations in COL4A3 or COL4A4 genes encoding type IV collagen alpha-3 and alpha-4 chains. TBMD is considered a carrier state for Alport syndrome and carries an excellent prognosis with normal life expectancy and minimal risk of ESKD.

### 2.2 Epidemiology

Most common cause of persistent microscopic hematuria worldwide. Prevalence estimated at 1-5% of the general population. Both sexes equally affected. All ages, but typically presents in childhood (3-15 years). Autosomal dominant inheritance pattern (COL4A3/COL4A4 heterozygous mutations). Family history of hematuria in 60-80%. Underdiagnosed in the community — many never seek medical attention. Accounts for 3-10% of native renal biopsies performed for isolated hematuria.

### 2.3 Etiology

Heterozygous mutation in COL4A3 (chromosome 2q36.3) or COL4A4 (chromosome 2q36.3) genes. These encode the alpha-3 and alpha-4 chains of type IV collagen, a major component of the GBM. Haploinsufficiency leads to reduced production of normal collagen IV alpha-3/alpha-4 chains. The remaining wild-type allele produces sufficient collagen for GBM function, but structural integrity is compromised. TBMD represents a carrier state — COL4A3/COL4A4 compound heterozygotes or homozygotes develop autosomal recessive Alport syndrome. De novo mutations account for a minority of cases. Genetic heterogeneity: some families without identifiable COL4A3/COL4A4 mutations suggest additional loci.

### 2.4 Pathophysiology

Reduced expression of collagen IV alpha-3 and alpha-4 chains in the GBM due to heterozygous COL4A3/COL4A4 mutations leads to a structurally weakened GBM that is diffusely thin (adult: <250 nm, child: <200 nm). The thin GBM is mechanically fragile and prone to micro-tears under normal hemodynamic stress, resulting in glomerular basement membrane barrier disruption and passage of red blood cells into the urinary space (hematuria). Unlike Alport syndrome, there is no progressive thickening, splitting, or lamellation of the GBM. Podocyte structure is normal. The process is non-inflammatory — no immune complex deposition or complement activation. Proteinuria is minimal or absent because the podocyte slit diaphragm remains intact.

### 2.5 Clinical Presentation

Persistent microscopic hematuria (the cardinal feature — often incidental finding on routine urinalysis). Episodic macroscopic hematuria (10-20%, typically during upper respiratory infections or exercise). Proteinuria is usually ABSENT or <1 g/day (if >1 g/day, consider Alport syndrome or superimposed GN). Normal blood pressure. Normal kidney function (eGFR normal for age). Normal hearing and vision (NO extra-renal manifestations — distinguishes from Alport). Family history of hematuria in 60-80% (autosomal dominant). Often discovered during screening of relatives of Alport syndrome patients. Completely asymptomatic in most cases.

### 2.8 Laboratory Findings

- Hematuria: persistent microscopic (dysmorphic RBCs, RBC casts)
- Proteinuria: absent or mild (<1 g/day)
- eGFR: normal for age and sex
- Serum creatinine: normal
- C3/C4: normal (no complement activation)
- Blood pressure: normal
- Urine culture: negative (no infection)
- Genetic testing: heterozygous COL4A3 or COL4A4 mutation
- Hearing test: normal (audiometry)
- Ophthalmology: normal (no lenticonus, no retinopathy)

### 2.9 Biopsy Findings

- LM: Normal light microscopy (no mesangial hypercellularity, no proliferation)
- LM: Normal glomerular architecture
- LM: Normal tubules, interstitium, and vessels
- IF: Negative for immunoglobulins and complement (NO immune deposits)
- IF: Normal staining for collagen IV alpha-5 chain in GBM (positive in males with X-linked Alport)
- EM: Diffuse thinning of GBM (adult <250 nm, child <200 nm) — DIAGNOSTIC
- EM: Normal lamina densa (no splitting, no lamellation — distinguishes from Alport)
- EM: Normal podocyte foot processes
- EM: No electron-dense deposits

### 2.12 Treatment Overview

NO TREATMENT REQUIRED for isolated hematuria with normal kidney function. This is the most important management message: TBMD is benign and does not require immunosuppression, anticoagulation, or other interventions. RAASi (ACEi/ARB) if proteinuria develops (>0.5 g/day). Annual monitoring: BP, eGFR, urine dipstick. Avoid nephrotoxic drugs (NSAIDs, aminoglycosides) where possible. Genetic counseling for family planning (explain 50% transmission risk, and that homozygous/compound heterozygous offspring may develop Alport syndrome). Reassurance and education are the mainstays. Normal lifestyle, no restrictions, no special diet. Insurance counseling: TBMD should not affect life insurance or employment. Pregnancy: no contraindication; monitor BP and proteinuria during pregnancy.

### 2.13 Treatment Algorithm

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | Confirm diagnosis: persistent hematuria + GBM thinning + normal function + no proteinuria |
| 2 | Reassure patient: TBMD is BENIGN — no treatment needed, normal life expectancy |
| 3 | Baseline assessment: BP, eGFR, UPCR, hearing, ophthalmology, urinalysis |
| 4 | Annual monitoring: BP, eGFR, urine dipstick. If proteinuria develops, add UPCR and consider RAASi |
| 5 | Consider genetic testing: COL4A3/COL4A4 sequencing for family planning and Alport carrier status |
| 6 | Genetic counseling: explain autosomal dominant inheritance, 50% transmission risk |
| 7 | Patient education: avoid nephrotoxins, normal lifestyle, no diet restrictions, insurance advice |
| 8 | If proteinuria >1 g/day or eGFR declining: re-evaluate for Alport syndrome or superimposed GN |

### 2.14 Monitoring Protocol

Annual: BP, eGFR (CKD-EPI), urine dipstick for protein. If proteinuria detected: UPCR, serum albumin, consider RAASi. If proteinuria >1 g/day or eGFR declining: consider re-biopsy and genetic testing for Alport syndrome. No need for routine imaging or renal biopsy follow-up. Pregnancy: monitor BP and proteinuria each trimester. Children: annual follow-up until adulthood; reassure parents about benign nature.

### 2.15 Complications

- Progressive CKD: VERY RARE (<1%) — only if misdiagnosed Alport syndrome
- Proteinuria development: 5-10% over lifetime — usually mild
- Hypertension: RARE — not directly caused by TBMD
- Misdiagnosis as IgA nephropathy leading to unnecessary immunosuppression
- Psychosocial: anxiety from hematuria discovery, insurance discrimination concerns

### 2.16 Relapse Information

Persistent condition (hematuria is continuous), not relapsing-remitting. Hematuria intensity may vary (increased during infections/exercise). No true relapses. No treatment response evaluation needed. Prognosis is uniformly excellent.

### 2.17 Long-Term Prognosis

EXCELLENT. Normal life expectancy. ESKD risk: <1% (similar to general population). No increased cardiovascular risk. No increased pregnancy risk. Proteinuria develops in <10% (usually mild). Hypertension rates similar to general population. The critical clinical task is to distinguish TBMD from Alport syndrome — misdiagnosis leads to unnecessary anxiety and potential insurance discrimination. Most patients can be discharged from nephrology follow-up after initial diagnosis confirmation if stable.

### 2.18 Evidence Summary

TBMD is well-described in cohort studies and family studies. Natural history defined by multiple longitudinal cohorts (N>1000 over 20+ years): ESKD rate <1%, no difference in life expectancy vs general population. GBM thickness established by EM morphometry studies (normal ~350 nm, TBMD <250 nm). COL4A3/COL4A4 discovered in 1990s. Large next-generation sequencing studies show COL4A3/COL4A4 heterozygotes in 1% of general population. No RCTs are needed — TBMD requires no intervention. KDIGO 2021 provides guidance on differential diagnosis from Alport syndrome.
