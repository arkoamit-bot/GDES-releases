## 2. Disease Record — Full 21-Field Schema
### 2.1 Definition
BK Virus Nephropathy (BKVN) is a renal allograft infection caused by BK polyomavirus (BKV) replication within tubular epithelial cells, leading to progressive tubulointerstitial nephritis and graft dysfunction. BKV is a ubiquitous human polyomavirus (seroprevalence 60-90% in adults) that remains latent in the urothelium after primary infection (usually asymptomatic in childhood). In the setting of immunosuppression after kidney transplantation, BKV reactivates and undergoes lytic replication in tubular epithelial cells, causing cytopathic injury, tubulointerstitial inflammation, and tubular atrophy. Untreated, BKVN progresses to graft loss in 30-50% of cases. Screening by urine BKV DNA (decoy cells or PCR) and plasma BKV PCR allows early detection of BKV replication before histologic nephropathy develops (BK viraemia precedes BKVN by 4-8 weeks). Management involves reducing immunosuppression (the cornerstone of therapy) — typically by reducing CNI (tacrolimus target 4-6 ng/mL) and stopping antiproliferative agent (MMF) or switching to mTOR inhibitor. Adjunctive therapies (cidofovir, leflunomide, IVIG) are used for persistent/progressive BKVN despite immunosuppression reduction.
### 2.2 Epidemiology
Prevalence: BK viruria (decoy cells or BKV DNA in urine) occurs in 30-50% of kidney transplant recipients. BK viraemia (BKV DNA in plasma) in 10-30%. BKVN (biopsy-proven) in 2-10% of kidney transplant recipients. Risk factors: intense immunosuppression (tacrolimus + MMF vs cyclosporine + azathioprine — higher with tac/MMF), high-dose corticosteroids, steroid-resistant rejection treatment, ATG induction, deceased donor, DGF, older recipient age (>50), male sex, HLA mismatch, total HLA-DR mismatch, previous rejection episodes, ureteric stent placement, BKV seronegative recipient (primary infection from donor), donor BKV seropositivity. Timing: median onset 3-12 months post-transplant (range 1 month to 5+ years). Peak viraemia at 3-6 months. Late BKVN (>2 years) is rare but occurs with reduced immunosuppression. Incidence has increased in the tacrolimus-MMF era (from <1% in 1990s to 2-10% currently).
### 2.3 Aetiology
Primary infection: usually asymptomatic in childhood. Latency: BKV persists in urothelial cells of the renal tubules and bladder. Reactivation triggers in transplantation: (1) Intense immunosuppression — calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus > cyclosporine) inhibit T-cell responses needed to control BKV; MMF/steroids further impair anti-viral immunity. (2) Donor-transmitted: latent BKV in the donor kidney reactivates in the recipient (dominant source). (3) Alloimmune activation: graft inflammation upregulates cellular receptors (including the ganglioside GT1b, the primary BKV receptor) and transcription factors that promote BKV replication. (4) Tubular epithelial cell injury: ischaemia-reperfusion, DGF, or rejection causes tubular cell regeneration — BKV replicates in proliferating tubular cells. BKV serotypes: type I (most common in Europe/America), type IV (Asia). Viral life cycle: attachment to GT1b -> entry via caveola-mediated endocytosis -> nuclear replication using host machinery -> cell lysis and release of progeny virions -> infection of adjacent tubular cells.
### 2.4 Pathophysiology
Phase 1 — BK Viruria: BKV reactivates in tubular epithelial cells -> lytic infection -> shedding of infected cells (decoy cells) and viral DNA into urine. Phase 2 — BK Viraemia: Viral replication overwhelms local immune control -> BKV crosses tubular basement membrane into peritubular capillaries -> detectable in plasma. Phase 3 — BKV Nephropathy (Histologic): Lytic infection of tubular epithelial cells causes: (a) Cytopathic changes: enlarged nuclei with basophilic intranuclear inclusions (ground-glass appearance), chromatin margination, tubular cell necrosis and sloughing. (b) Tubulointerstitial nephritis: mononuclear cell infiltrate (lymphocytes, plasma cells, macrophages) surrounding infected tubules. (c) Tubular atrophy and fibrosis: irreversible tubular loss -> IFTA -> progressive graft dysfunction. Histologic stages (AST guidelines): Stage A (early) — viral cytopathic changes in <25% of tubules, mild inflammation, minimal IFTA. Stage B (moderate) — 25-50% of tubules involved, moderate inflammation and tubular injury. Stage C (late) — >50% tubules involved, severe IFTA. Key: BKV infects ONLY tubular epithelium — glomeruli and vessels are spared (distinguishes from rejection). If left untreated, 30-50% of Stage B/C progress to graft loss within 12-24 months.
### 2.5 Clinical Presentation
ASYMPTOMATIC EARLY STAGE: Detected on screening — rising or persistently positive BK plasma PCR (>1000 copies/mL, typically >10,000 copies/mL for BKVN). Creatinine usually normal in Stage A. OVERT BKVN (Stage B/C): Slowly rising creatinine over weeks-months (insidious — unlike acute rejection). Subnephrotic proteinuria may develop (UPCR 0.3-1.0). Graft is usually non-tender (no fever, no oliguria until very late). ADVANCED BKVN: Progressive graft dysfunction, hypertension, oedema from CKD. Late BKVN: can present with graft failure without prior detection (if screening not performed). Key distinction from TCMR: BKVN is typically slower in onset, less inflammatory, and does not cause acute graft tenderness or fever. SCREENING: Plasma BKV PCR monthly for first 3-6 months, then Q3 months until 2 years post-transplant. BKV viruria (decoy cells on urine cytology or urine BKV PCR) is more sensitive but less specific for BKVN.

### 2.8 Laboratory Findings
  * 
Plasma BKV PCR: quantitative — >1000 copies/mL = significant viraemia. >10,000 copies/mL = high risk of BKVN. Rising level over 2-4 weeks is more predictive than single value  * Urine BKV PCR: >1e7 copies/mL = high risk of BKVN. More sensitive but less specific than plasma PCR  * Urine cytology for decoy cells: shed infected tubular epithelial cells with enlarged ground-glass nuclei. Specific but less sensitive than PCR  * Serum creatinine: slowly rising over weeks-months. May be normal in Stage A. Rate of rise correlates with stage  * DSA testing: exclude concurrent ABMR (10-20% of BKVN cases have mixed rejection)  * C4d staining: negative in pure BKVN. If positive -> concurrent ABMR  * Tacrolimus trough: usually in therapeutic range (BKVN occurs despite adequate levels)  * CMV PCR, EBV PCR: exclude concurrent viral infections
### 2.9 Biopsy Findings
  * 
Stage A (Early BKVN): SV40+ tubular epithelial cells in <25% of tubules. Mild focal mononuclear interstitial infiltrate with plasma cells. Minimal (<25%) IFTA. Tubular injury: enlarged nuclei, intranuclear basophilic inclusions, chromatin margination  * Stage B (Moderate BKVN): SV40+ in 25-50% of tubules. Moderate to severe interstitial inflammation (plasma cells, lymphocytes, macrophages). Tubular necrosis and sloughing. IFTA 25-50%  * Stage C (Late/Severe BKVN): SV40+ in >50% of tubules or extensive tubular atrophy. Interstitial inflammation may be less prominent (burnt-out phase). IFTA >50%. Irreversible tubular loss  * C4d stain: typically negative in pure BKVN (distinguishes from ABMR). If C4d positive and DSA positive -> mixed BKVN + ABMR  * EM: Polyomavirus particles in tubular epithelial cell nuclei (30-45 nm, icosahedral capsids). Intranuclear viral aggregates  * Key distinguishing feature from TCMR: viral cytopathic changes + SV40 positivity. TCMR has no viral inclusions and SV40 negative

### 2.12 Treatment Overview
CORNERSTONE — REDUCE IMMUNOSUPPRESSION. This is the only intervention proven to improve outcomes. FIRST STEP: Reduce CNI by 25-50% (tacrolimus target 4-6 ng/mL, down from 6-10). Reduce/discontinue MMF (1 g/day or stop). Maintain prednisone (5-10 mg/day). Alternative: switch from tacrolimus to cyclosporine (lower BKV risk) or from MMF to mTOR inhibitor (sirolimus/everolimus — mTOR inhibitors suppress BKV replication in vitro). RESPONSE ASSESSMENT: Monitor BKV PCR weekly. 80% respond to IS reduction alone within 4-8 weeks. SECOND-LINE (persistent BKVN despite IS reduction >4-8 weeks): Leflunomide (alternative: 100 mg x3 days load, then 20-40 mg daily — monitor levels, target 50-100 mcg/mL; weak evidence). Cidofovir (0.25-1 mg/kg IV every 2-4 weeks without probenecid — nephrotoxic, use cautiously; limited to refractory cases). IVIG (2 g/kg divided over 2-5 days — contains anti-BKV antibodies; used as adjunct). Fluoroquinolones (ciprofloxacin) — inhibit BKV replication in vitro but no proven clinical benefit (RCT negative). MONITORING: Weekly plasma BKV PCR until negative or <1000 copies/mL. Monthly BK PCR for 6 months after clearance. Re-biopsy if persistent viraemia >12 weeks despite IS reduction. If BKVN clears but rejection develops (from IS reduction): treat rejection with caution (pulse steroids if necessary, but avoid ATG if possible).
### 2.13 Treatment Algorithm
| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | Detect BK viraemia on screening: plasma BKV PCR positive (any level). Confirm with repeat in 1-2 weeks |
| 2 | Plasma BKV <1000 copies/mL: continue monitoring monthly. No IS reduction needed unless rising trend |
| 3 | Plasma BKV >1000 copies/mL (rising or persistent >2 weeks): reduce immunosuppression. First-line: reduce tacrolimus target to 4-6 ng/mL. Reduce MMF to 1 g/day or discontinue. Maintain prednisone 5-10 mg/day |
| 4 | Biopsy if: BKV >10,000 copies/mL OR creatinine rising OR persistent viraemia >4 weeks despite IS reduction. Biopsy confirms BKVN and excludes concurrent rejection |
| 5 | If biopsy confirms BKVN (A/B): continue IS reduction. Weekly BKV PCR. 80% clear with IS reduction alone over 4-8 weeks |
| 6 | Persistent viraemia/BKVN despite IS reduction >4-8 weeks: consider second-line therapy. Leflunomide 100 mg load x3 days then 20-40 mg daily (target level 50-100 mcg/mL). OR Cidofovir 0.25-1 mg/kg IV q2-4 weeks (nephrotoxic) |
| 7 | Biopsy Stage C (IFTA >50%): IS reduction unlikely to help. Avoid over-immunosuppression. Manage as CKD. Prepare for graft failure |
| 8 | If rejection develops after IS reduction: pulse steroids with caution. Avoid ATG if possible (triggers BKV recurrence). Check DSA and C4d. Treat mixed rejection + BKVN with balanced approach |
### 2.14 Monitoring Protocol
Plasma BKV PCR screening: monthly for first 6 months post-transplant, then Q3 months until 2 years, then annually. When viraemia detected: weekly PCR until <1000 copies/mL or negative. Then monthly x3, then quarterly x1. After BKVN treatment: weekly PCR x4 weeks, then monthly x6 months. Renal function: monthly creatinine. If creatinine rises while BK viral load is high: biopsy to rule out concurrent rejection. Biopsy indications: BKV >10,000 copies/mL + rising Cr OR persistent viraemia >4 weeks after IS reduction. SV40 stain on all for-cause biopsies in transplant patients. Re-biopsy: if persistent viraemia despite >12 weeks of IS reduction (to assess progression or exclude rejection). DSA monitoring: IS reduction can trigger de novo DSA — check DSA at 3 and 6 months after BKVN.
### 2.15 Complications
  * 
Graft loss: 30-50% for Stage B/C BKVN despite treatment. Stage C: >50% graft loss within 12-24 months  * Acute rejection after immunosuppression reduction: 10-20% of patients — the major risk of IS reduction. Monitor DSA and creatinine closely  * Cidofovir nephrotoxicity: acute tubular injury, proteinuria, Fanconi syndrome. Avoid in Stage C or eGFR <30  * Leflunomide side effects: hepatotoxicity, haemolytic anaemia, peripheral neuropathy, thrombotic microangiopathy (rare)  * Ureteric stenosis: BKV causes ureteral ulceration and stricture — presents with hydronephrosis and rising Cr  * Haemorrhagic cystitis: BKV can cause bladder inflammation (more common in BMT than kidney transplant)  * Mixed BKVN + rejection: 10-20% — diagnostic and therapeutic challenge. Balancing IS reduction vs anti-rejection therapy  * Urothelial malignancy: BKV may promote urothelial carcinoma (controversial association)
### 2.16 Relapse Information
BKVN recurrence after viral clearance: 10-20% if immunosuppression is increased again (e.g., for rejection treatment or dose escalation). Prevention: maintain lower target immunosuppression (tacrolimus 4-6, MMF 1 g/day) after BKVN clearance. Re-transplantation after BKVN graft loss: safe — no recurrence in the new graft if immunosuppression is BKV-appropriate (lower targets, avoid over-immunosuppression). Before re-transplant: ensure BKV PCR negative or low. Some centres advocate bilateral native nephrectomy (removes BKV reservoir) — evidence limited.
### 2.17 Long-Term Prognosis
STAGE A: >80% graft survival at 3 years if detected early and IS reduced. Complete or partial histologic resolution possible. STAGE B: 60-70% graft survival at 3 years. 30-40% experience progressive IFTA and declining eGFR even with viral clearance. STAGE C: <50% graft survival at 3 years. Irreversible tubular loss. Most progress to ESKD. OVERALL: BKVN accounts for 1-5% of graft loss in the modern era (reduced from 30-50% pre-screening era, reflecting the benefit of early detection). POOR PROGNOSTIC FACTORS: Stage C at diagnosis, IFTA >50%, delayed IS reduction, mixed rejection, rising BKV despite IS reduction, eGFR <30 at BKVN diagnosis, late BKVN (>2 years post-transplant). Re-transplant: excellent outcomes with BKV-appropriate immunosuppression.
### 2.18 Evidence Summary
Screening studies: Prospective studies (n=1000+) show that monthly BKV PCR screening reduces BKVN incidence from 8-10% to 2-3%. IS reduction: No RCTs of IS reduction vs placebo (unethical — standard of care). Observational cohort studies (n=500+) show 80% clearance with IS reduction. Leflunomide: Retrospective studies (n=50-200) — variable results. No RCT. Systematic review: 60-70% viral clearance with leflunomide as add-on. Cidofovir: Small retrospective series (n=20-40) — 50-70% viral clearance but significant nephrotoxicity. IVIG: Small case series — may accelerate clearance when added to IS reduction. Fluoroquinolones: Negative RCT (n=150, 2020) — ciprofloxacin vs placebo showed no difference in BKV clearance. mTOR inhibitors: Observational data suggest lower BKV incidence with sirolimus vs tacrolimus — but unproven in RCTs for BKVN treatment. KEY: IS reduction remains the only proven intervention. No RCTs support any specific adjunctive therapy.
