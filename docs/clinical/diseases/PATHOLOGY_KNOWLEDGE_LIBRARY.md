
### LM-01: Mesangial Proliferation/Hypercellularity

**Document Reference:** GDES-V4.2-PATH-LM-01

#### Definition

An increase in the number of mesangial cells and/or mesangial matrix within glomerular tufts, exceeding the normal expectation of no more than two cell nuclei per mesangial area in peripheral (non-hilar) mesangial zones on a 3-micron tissue section. This finding reflects mesangial cell activation, proliferation, and/or matrix expansion.

#### Histological Appearance

- **Cellular component**: Increased mesangial cell nuclei, typically assessed by counting nuclei per mesangial area. Normal is ≤2 nuclei per peripheral mesangial area; >2 nuclei per area in ≥2 glomeruli is generally considered abnormal
- **Matrix component**: Expanded mesangial matrix material that may be pale, eosinophilic, or slightly fibrillar, surrounding and separating mesangial cells
- **Distribution**: May be focal (involving <50% of glomeruli) or diffuse (involving ≥50% of glomeruli); may be segmental (involving part of a glomerular tuft) or global (involving the entire tuft)
- **Severity grading**:
  - Mild: 3-4 nuclei per mesangial area
  - Moderate: 5-6 nuclei per mesangial area
  - Severe: ≥7 nuclei per mesangial area (often with matrix expansion)
- **Oxford MEST-C classification** (for IgA nephropathy): M0 = no mesangial hypercellularity; M1 = >50% of glomeruli with >3 mesangial cells per area (defined in the Oxford Classification of IgA Nephropathy)
- **Caveat**: Must distinguish true mesangial proliferation from mesangial matrix expansion (acellular) and from artifacts of sectioning tangentially through mesangial areas
- **Special stains**: PAS stain highlights mesangial matrix; silver (Jones) stain outlines basement membranes and distinguishes matrix from cells
- **Differential**: Tangential sectioning of glomeruli, mesangial matrix expansion without hypercellularity (as in diabetic nephropathy), nodular glomerulosclerosis

#### Clinical Significance

Mesangial proliferation is one of the most common histological findings in glomerular disease and reflects active immune-mediated inflammation within the mesangium. It is the histological hallmark of IgA nephropathy but is non-specific and occurs in numerous immune and non-immune glomerular diseases. The degree of mesangial proliferation correlates with proteinuria levels and may reflect ongoing disease activity. In transplant settings, de novo mesangial proliferation may indicate recurrent or de novo disease.

#### Associated Diseases

| Disease ID | Frequency | Diagnostic Significance |
|---|---|---|
| `iga` | Constant (>95%) | Pathognomonic — defining feature; diffuse mesangial proliferation with IgA-dominant IF |
| `lupus` | Frequent (50-95%) | Supportive — Class II (mesangial proliferative) lupus nephritis by ISN/RPS |
| `irgn` | Common (20-50%) | Supportive — often mild mesangial expansion during resolution phase |
| `diabeticNephropathy` | Frequent (50-95%) | Non-specific — matrix expansion predominates; true cellular proliferation is variable |
| `c3` | Common (20-50%) | Supportive — mesangial proliferation with C3-dominant deposits |
| `mpgn` | Common (20-50%) | Supportive — part of membranoproliferative pattern |
| `fsgs` | Occasional (5-20%) | Non-specific — may be present in collapsing or cellular variants |
| `hivan` | Frequent (50-95%) | Strongly Suggestive — exuberant mesangial proliferation in HIVAN |
| `drugInducedGn` | Common (20-50%) | Non-specific — variable mesangial response to immune complex deposition |
| `cryoglobulinemic` | Common (20-50%) | Supportive — often with endocapillary proliferation and pseudothrombi |

#### Prognostic Value

- In IgA nephropathy, extent of mesangial proliferation correlates with Oxford M classification and independently predicts progression to ESKD
- Diffuse mesangial proliferation in lupus nephritis (Class II) carries a better prognosis than proliferative classes (III/IV)
- In diabetic nephropathy, the degree of matrix expansion parallels severity of diabetic control
- Increasing mesangial proliferation over sequential biopsies suggests active/persistent disease

#### Treatment Implications

- IgA nephropathy with significant mesangial proliferation: SGLT2 inhibitors as first-line; immunosuppression (corticosteroids, MMF) for persistent proteinuria despite maximized supportive care
- Lupus Class II: Generally does not require aggressive immunosuppression unless significant proteinuria
- Drug-induced: Discontinuation of offending agent
- HIVAN: Antiretroviral therapy; immunosuppression generally contraindicated

2. Working Group of the International IgA Nephropathy Network. An Oxford Clinical Practice Guideline for the management of IgA nephropathy. *Kidney Int.* 2024;105(1S):S1-S25
3. Weening JJ, et al. The classification of glomerulonephritis in systemic lupus erythematosus revisited. *J Am Soc Nephrol.* 2004;15(2):241-250
4. Tervaert TW, et al. Pathologic classification of diabetic nephropathy. *J Am Soc Nephrol.* 2010;21(4):556-563

#### Diagnostic Pearls

- Always count mesangial cells in peripheral (non-hilar) mesangial areas; hilar areas normally have more nuclei
- A PAS stain is essential to distinguish matrix from cells and to assess true mesangial expansion
- The finding of >5 mesangial nuclei per area in multiple glomeruli is highly abnormal regardless of the clinical context
- In pediatric biopsies, a slightly higher mesangial cell count may be within normal limits
- If mesangial proliferation is accompanied by endocapillary proliferation, consider active proliferative GN (lupus III/IV, post-infectious, pauci-immune)
- In transplant biopsies, mesangial proliferation may represent recurrent IgA nephropathy — correlate with IF for IgA

### LM-02: Endocapillary Hypercellularity

**Document Reference:** GDES-V4.2-PATH-LM-02

#### Definition

An increase in cells within the capillary lumina of glomerular tufts, resulting from inflammatory cell influx (neutrophils, monocytes/macrophages) and/or proliferation of resident glomerular cells (endothelial cells, mesangial cells migrating into capillary loops). This finding indicates active glomerular inflammation and is a key marker of endocapillary proliferative glomerulonephritis.

#### Histological Appearance

- **Cellular composition**: Neutrophils (polymorphonuclear leukocytes) and monocytes/macrophages within capillary lumina, accompanied by swollen endothelial cells that may obliterate capillary spaces
- **Distribution**: May be focal (<50% of glomeruli) or diffuse (≥50% of glomeruli); may be segmental or global within affected glomeruli
- **Severity**: Graded by the Oxford Classification (for IgA nephropathy): E0 = no endocapillary hypercellularity; E1 = any glomerulus with endocapillary hypercellularity (fewer than 25% of glomeruli globally sclerosed)
- **Crescents**: When endocapillary hypercellularity is severe, it may extend to form cellular crescents — this should be reported separately
- **Accompanying features**: Often associated with capillary loop necrosis, fibrinoid necrosis, and luminal fibrin deposition
- **Differential from mesangial proliferation**: Endocapillary hypercellularity involves cells within capillary lumina (obliterating them), whereas mesangial proliferation involves cells within the mesangium (expanding mesangial areas)
- **Special stains**: Fite or other special stains to exclude infectious organisms (e.g., leishmaniasis in endemic areas) when endocapillary proliferation is prominent and of uncertain etiology
- **Silver stain**: May reveal associated GBM interruptions if necrotizing injury is present

#### Clinical Significance

Endocapillary hypercellularity represents active inflammatory injury to the glomerular capillaries. It is the histological hallmark of active endocapillary proliferative glomerulonephritis, including post-infectious GN, lupus nephritis Classes III and IV, and cryoglobulinemic GN. Its presence generally indicates an active, potentially reversible phase of disease that may respond to immunosuppressive therapy. Conversely, its absence suggests chronicity or low disease activity.

#### Associated Diseases

| Disease ID | Frequency | Diagnostic Significance |
|---|---|---|
| `lupus` | Frequent (50-95%) | Strongly Suggestive — defining feature of ISN/RPS Class III and IV lupus nephritis |
| `irgn` | Constant (>95%) | Pathognomonic — endocapillary proliferative GN is the defining pattern |
| `cryoglobulinemic` | Frequent (50-95%) | Strongly Suggestive — endocapillary proliferation with pseudothrombi |
| `anca` | Common (20-50%) | Supportive — may be present in active pauci-immune GN |
| `iga` | Common (20-50%) | Supportive — Oxford E1 classification |
| `antiGbm` | Common (20-50%) | Supportive — often with concurrent necrosis and crescents |
| `c3` | Occasional (5-20%) | Non-specific — endocapillary proliferation in acute C3 glomerulopathy |
| `drugInducedGn` | Common (20-50%) | Supportive — immune complex-mediated endocapillary GN |
| `mpgn` | Common (20-50%) | Supportive — part of membranoproliferative pattern |

#### Prognostic Value

- Oxford E1 in IgA nephropathy independently predicts renal outcome; its presence (along with M1, S1, T1/2, C1) increases risk of 50% GFR decline or ESKD
- In lupus nephritis, endocapillary hypercellularity (Class III/IV) indicates active disease requiring prompt treatment
- Absence of endocapillary hypercellularity on follow-up biopsy suggests resolution of active inflammation
- Diffuse endocapillary hypercellularity in post-infectious GN generally resolves, but severe cases may progress

#### Treatment Implications

- Active endocapillary proliferation with necrosis/crescents generally warrants immunosuppression (corticosteroids ± cyclophosphamide or MMF)
- Lupus III/IV: Induction with mycophenolate or cyclophosphamide + corticosteroids; maintenance with MMF or azathioprine
- Post-infectious GN: Usually supportive; immunosuppression only for severe/crescentic presentations
- Cryoglobulinemic GN: Rituximab-based regimens; treat underlying HCV if present

1. Working Group of the International IgA Nephropathy Network. The Oxford Classification of IgA nephropathy. *Kidney Int.* 2009;76(5):534-546
2. Weening JJ, et al. The classification of glomerulonephritis in systemic lupus erythematosus revisited. *J Am Soc Nephrol.* 2004;15(2):241-250
3. Jennette JC, et al. 2012 revised international consensus classification criteria for anti-neutrophil cytoplasmic antibody-associated vasculitis. *Arthritis Rheum.* 2013;65(12):3154-3159
4. Couser WG. Rapidly progressive glomerulonephritis: classification, pathogenesis, and treatment. *Am J Kidney Dis.* 1988;11(5):449-464

#### Diagnostic Pearls

- The presence of neutrophils within capillary loops (not just mesangium) defines endocapillary hypercellularity
- In IgA nephropathy, endocapillary hypercellularity (E1) is less common than in lupus or post-infectious GN but carries significant prognostic weight
- Always look for accompanying necrosis, as pure endocapillary proliferation without necrosis may represent a different phase of injury
- In lupus nephritis, endocapillary hypercellularity must be present along with immune complex deposits (IF/EM) to classify as active Class III/IV rather than Class II
- When prominent neutrophilic endocapillary proliferation is seen in a "starry sky" pattern, consider post-infectious GN or cryoglobulinemic GN

### LM-03: Crescents

**Document Reference:** GDES-V4.2-PATH-LM-03

#### Definition

Proliferation of parietal epithelial cells and infiltration of inflammatory cells (monocytes/macrophages, T cells) within Bowman's space, forming layers that compress the glomerular tuft. Crescents form in response to disruption of the glomerular capillary wall (GBM rupture), allowing plasma proteins (fibrinogen) and inflammatory cells to enter Bowman's space. The presence of crescents in ≥1 glomerulus defines crescentic glomerulonephritis, a medical emergency.

#### Histological Appearance

**Classification by composition (temporal evolution):**

1. **Cellular crescent** (early/acute):
   - Composed predominantly of proliferating parietal epithelial cells and monocytes/macrophages
   - Fibrin strands interspersed among cells
   - No significant collagen deposition
   - Indicates active, acute injury (<2-3 weeks)

2. **Fibrocellular crescent** (intermediate/subacute):
   - Mix of cells and collagen (fibrosis)
   - Approximately equal proportions of cellular and fibrous components
   - Parietal epithelial cells undergoing transition to myofibroblasts
   - Indicates injury of intermediate age (2-8 weeks)
   - Collagen types I and III begin to appear alongside normal type IV collagen of Bowman's capsule

3. **Fibrous crescent** (late/chronic):
   - Predominantly acellular collagenous tissue
   - Few residual cells
   - Complete obliteration of Bowman's space
   - Indicates old, irreversible injury (>8 weeks)
   - Represents end-stage scarring of the glomerulus

**Additional morphological features:**
- Crescents may be circumferential (encircling >50% of the glomerular tuft) or segmental (<50%)
- Associated GBM breaks (gaps) may be visible on silver stain
- Fibrinoid necrosis of the tuft periphery is often present adjacent to cellular crescents
- Bowman's capsule disruption may be evident (especially in severe cases)
- Glomerular tuft compression and ischemic changes of the tuft periphery

#### Clinical Significance

Crescents represent severe glomerular injury and define rapidly progressive glomerulonephritis (RPGN), a syndrome of rapid loss of renal function over days to weeks. The number and type of crescents are the most important histological predictors of renal outcome. The percentage of crescents on biopsy, their composition (cellular vs fibrous), and the presence or absence of intact glomeruli are critical for treatment decisions. Cellular crescents are potentially reversible with aggressive immunosuppression; fibrous crescents indicate irreversible scarring.

#### Associated Diseases

| Disease ID | Frequency | Diagnostic Significance |
|---|---|---|
| `anca` | Frequent (50-95%) | Strongly Suggestive — pauci-immune crescentic GN is hallmark of ANCA vasculitis |
| `antiGbm` | Constant (>95%) | Pathognomonic — diffuse crescents with linear IgG on IF; ± pulmonary hemorrhage |
| `lupus` | Common (20-50%) | Strongly Suggestive — ISN/RPS Class III/IV with crescents (particularly IV-G(A/C)) |
| `iga` | Occasional (5-20%) | Supportive — crescents in IgA nephropathy indicate severe disease |
| `cryoglobulinemic` | Occasional (5-20%) | Supportive — crescents in severe cryoglobulinemic GN |
| `irgn` | Rare (<5%) | Non-specific — crescents possible in severe post-infectious GN |
| `c3` | Rare (<5%) | Non-specific — severe C3 glomerulopathy may show crescents |
| `drugInducedGn` | Rare (<5%) | Non-specific — severe drug-induced GN may rarely show crescents |
| `fsgs` | Rare (<5%) | Non-specific — collapsing glomerulopathy may show crescents |
| `membranous` | Rare (<5%) | Non-specific — superimposed crescents indicate complication |

#### Prognostic Value

- **Number of crescents**: >50% crescents (especially cellular) indicate severe disease requiring urgent treatment; <50% crescents with many preserved glomeruli has a better prognosis
- **Composition**: Cellular crescents are potentially reversible; fibrous crescents are irreversible scars
- **Presence of cellular crescents**: Suggests a window of therapeutic opportunity
- **Absent normal glomeruli**: Very poor prognosis for renal recovery regardless of treatment
- **Percentage of glomeruli with any crescents**: Correlates with likelihood of ESKD; >25% crescents generally associated with poor outcomes
- In ANCA vasculitis: Presence of cellular crescents + absent chronic damage = good response to rituximab/cyclophosphamide
- In anti-GBM disease: Crescent percentage and creatinine at presentation predict outcome

#### Treatment Implications

- **Cellular crescents**: Aggressive immunosuppression (corticosteroids + cyclophosphamide or rituximab for ANCA; plasma exchange + immunosuppression for anti-GBM)
- **Fibrocellular crescents**: May still respond to treatment but with diminishing returns
- **Fibrous crescents**: Immunosuppression unlikely to be beneficial for the scarred glomeruli; focus on preventing further damage
- **Plasma exchange**: Consider in severe anti-GBM disease and ANCA vasculitis with severe renal failure or pulmonary hemorrhage
- **Timing**: Urgent treatment of cellular crescents is critical — delay leads to irreversible fibrosis

2. Walsh M, et al. Plasma exchange and glucocorticoids in severe ANCA-associated vasculitis. *N Engl J Med.* 2020;382(7):622-631
3. Loupy A, et al. The Banff 2019 Kidney Meeting Report. *Am J Transplant.* 2020;20(3):586-615
4. Couser WG. Rapidly progressive glomerulonephritis. *Am J Kidney Dis.* 1988;11(5):449-464
5. Jayne DR, et al. Rituximab versus cyclophosphamide for ANCA-associated vasculitis (RAVE). *N Engl J Med.* 2010;363(3):221-232

#### Diagnostic Pearls

- A biopsy showing predominantly cellular crescents with fibrinoid necrosis and few normal glomeruli demands urgent treatment — do not wait for additional testing
- The IF pattern distinguishes the three causes of crescentic GN: linear IgG (anti-GBM), granular immune complexes (lupus, IgA, post-infectious), pauci-immune (ANCA vasculitis)
- Crescents that disrupt Bowman's capsule (extracapillary extension) indicate very severe injury and worse prognosis
- When fibrous crescents predominate, the disease has likely been smoldering for weeks to months — look for clinical clues of insidious onset
- In transplant biopsies, crescents may indicate recurrent ANCA vasculitis, anti-GBM disease, or severe rejection

### LM-04: Segmental Glomerulosclerosis

**Document Reference:** GDES-V4.2-PATH-LM-04

#### Definition

Sclerosis (increased matrix/collagen deposition) involving a portion (segment) of the glomerular tuft, while the remainder of the tuft appears relatively preserved. This finding may represent a primary podocytopathy (e.g., FSGS), a secondary scarring response to prior injury, or a manifestation of various systemic and inherited diseases. Classification of FSGS variants is essential as they carry different prognoses and treatment implications.

#### Histological Appearance

**Columbia Classification of FSGS Variants:**

1. **Not otherwise specified (NOS)**:
   - Sclerosis involves the tubular pole or arbitrary segment
   - The sclerotic segment shows increased matrix and collapsed capillary loops
   - Adjacent podocytes may be hypertrophied
   - Most common variant (~50% of FSGS cases)

2. **Tip lesion**:
   - Sclerosis involves the tubular pole (near the tubuloglomerular junction)
   - Podocyte hyperplasia/hypertrophy at the tip
   - May have intratubular protrusion of synechiae or podocyte clusters
   - Generally best prognosis among FSGS variants

3. **Perihilar**:
   - Sclerosis involves the hilar region (vascular pole)
   - Often associated with glomerular hypertrophy
   - Most commonly associated with secondary FSGS (e.g., obesity, reduced nephron mass)
   - May have hyalinosis at the vascular pole

4. **Cellular**:
   - Endocapillary hypercellularity with obliteration of capillary lumina
   - Podocyte hyperplasia/hypertrophy on the surface
   - Microlesions (intraglomerular foam cells, matrix increase)
   - May represent an early/active phase of FSGS

5. **Collapsing**:
   - Collapse (wrinkling and retraction) of the glomerular tuft
   - Marked podocyte hypertrophy and hyperplasia with loss of foot processes
   - Podocytes often show mitotic figures and endomitosis (so-called "pseudocrescents")
   - Most severe variant; worst prognosis
   - Characteristic of HIV-associated nephropathy (HIVAN) and other collapsing glomerulopathies

**Additional features to assess:**
- Presence or absence of podocyte hypertrophy/hyperplasia (active injury marker)
- Mesangial matrix expansion
- Tubular atrophy and interstitial fibrosis (chronicity indices)
- Global glomerulosclerosis (secondary to segmental)
- Arteriosclerosis/hyaline arteriolopathy
- Podocyte foot process effacement on EM (diffuse >80% supports primary FSGS)

#### Clinical Significance

Segmental glomerulosclerosis is the defining feature of focal segmental glomerulosclerosis (FSGS), one of the most common causes of nephrotic syndrome worldwide. However, segmental sclerosis is not specific to FSGS — it can be seen as a secondary finding in many glomerular diseases (as a "tip of the iceberg" phenomenon), in adaptive responses to reduced nephron mass, and in toxic/ischemic injury. Distinguishing primary (idiopathic) FSGS from secondary causes is critical as treatment differs dramatically. Collapsing FSGS carries the worst prognosis and requires aggressive investigation for underlying causes (HIV, parvovirus B19, medications).

#### Associated Diseases

| Disease ID | Frequency | Diagnostic Significance |
|---|---|---|
| `fsgs` | Constant (>95%) | Pathognomonic — defining feature; primary FSGS requires exclusion of secondary causes |
| `hivan` | Frequent (50-95%) | Strongly Suggestive — collapsing variant with tubuloreticular inclusions |
| `diabeticNephropathy` | Common (20-50%) | Non-specific — secondary segmental sclerosis as part of advanced diabetic glomerulopathy |
| `lupus` | Occasional (5-20%) | Non-specific — segmental sclerosis may be seen in Class V or as chronic change |
| `membranous` | Occasional (5-20%) | Non-specific — segmental sclerosis may develop in advanced membranous nephropathy |
| `iga` | Occasional (5-20%) | Non-specific — chronic segmental scarring in longstanding IgA nephropathy |
| `anca` | Occasional (5-20%) | Non-specific — segmental sclerosis as healed necrotizing lesion |
| `cniToxicity` | Occasional (5-20%) | Supportive — segmental sclerosis with collapsing features in CNI toxicity |
| `transplantGlomerulopathy` | Occasional (5-20%) | Non-specific — segmental sclerosis may develop in chronic transplant glomerulopathy |
| `alport` | Rare (<5%) | Non-specific — segmental sclerosis in advanced Alport syndrome |
| `mcd` | Rare (<5%) | Non-specific — very rare progression of MCD to FSGS |

#### Prognostic Value

- **Variant matters**: Collapsing > NOS > perihilar > tip (worst to best prognosis)
- **Tip lesion**: Generally favorable prognosis; may be a variant of MCD; often responds to corticosteroids
- **Collapsing**: Very poor prognosis; rapid progression to ESKD; often resistant to immunosuppression
- **Extent of sclerosis**: >20% global sclerosis or >2 segmental lesions indicates more advanced disease
- **Podocyte hypertrophy/hyperplasia**: Marker of active disease; may indicate ongoing injury
- **Interstitial fibrosis/tubular atrophy**: Best single predictor of long-term renal survival in FSGS
- In transplant setting: Recurrent FSGS (especially collapsing) carries poor allograft outcome

#### Treatment Implications

- **Primary FSGS (NOS, tip, cellular)**: Corticosteroid-responsive; second-line calcineurin inhibitors (CNIs) for steroid-resistant cases; rituximab as third-line
- **Collapsing FSGS**: Aggressive investigation for HIV, medications, parvovirus B19; CNIs, rituximab; limited response to treatment
- **Perihilar FSGS**: Secondary to hemodynamic factors — treat underlying cause (BP control, reduce proteinuria, address obesity)
- **All FSGS variants**: RAAS inhibition, SGLT2 inhibitors, dietary protein/blood pressure management; avoid nephrotoxins
- **Transplant recurrence**: Plasma exchange + rituximab for severe recurrent FSGS; consider rituximab prophylaxis in high-risk patients

#### Diagnostic Pearls

- At least 20 glomeruli should be sampled for accurate focal assessment; <10 glomeruli may miss focal lesions entirely
- If tip lesion is found in isolation with <50% foot process effacement, consider secondary FSGS (obesity, reduced nephron mass, genetic)
- Collapsing FSGS should always prompt HIV testing and medication review (especially bisphosphonates, interferon, sirolimus)
- Segmental sclerosis with hyalinosis at the hilum in a patient with obesity strongly suggests adaptive/secondary FSGS
- In transplant biopsies, segmental sclerosis alone is non-specific — consider chronic antibody-mediated rejection, recurrent disease, and CNI toxicity in the differential

### LM-05: Global Glomerulosclerosis

**Document Reference:** GDES-V4.2-PATH-LM-05

#### Definition

Complete obliteration of the entire glomerular tuft by sclerotic (collagenous) material, with loss of all normal glomerular architecture. The glomerulus appears as an acellular or paucicellular ball of PAS-positive, silver-positive matrix material. Global glomerulosclerosis may result from progressive segmental sclerosis, ischemic obliterative change, or other mechanisms of glomerular destruction.

#### Histological Appearance

- **Morphology**: The entire glomerular tuft is replaced by dense, homogeneous matrix material; capillary loops are completely obliterated; Bowman's space may be obliterated or widened (adhesion to Bowman's capsule)
- **Cellularity**: Typically paucicellular (few residual cells); may have entrained foam cells (diabetic nephropathy) or cholesterol clefts
- **Types**:
  - **Solidified sclerosis**: Tuft collapsed with increased matrix but still recognizable as a glomerular tuft; often due to ischemic injury or chronic GN
  - **Obsolescent (hyalinized) sclerosis**: Tuft completely replaced by hyaline material; often associated with arteriosclerosis and ischemic nephropathy
- **Bowman's capsule**: May show thickening, periglomerular fibrosis, and tubulization of Bowman's capsule (pseudotubular transformation)
- **Associated findings**: Often present alongside tubulointerstitial fibrosis, tubular atrophy, and arteriosclerosis
- **Counting**: The percentage of globally sclerosed glomeruli is reported as part of chronicity assessment in most classification systems
- **Banff classification**: glomerulitis and chronicity scoring includes assessment of global sclerosis

#### Clinical Significance

Global glomerulosclerosis represents irreversible nephron loss. The percentage of globally sclerosed glomeruli is one of the most powerful predictors of long-term renal outcome, as it directly reflects the number of functioning nephrons that have been permanently destroyed. In the setting of declining GFR, a high percentage of global sclerosis indicates that the kidney's functional reserve is severely depleted and further insults may precipitate ESKD.

#### Associated Diseases

| Disease ID | Frequency | Diagnostic Significance |
|---|---|---|
| `diabeticNephropathy` | Constant (>95%) | Strongly Suggestive — progressive global sclerosis with Kimmelstiel-Wilson nodules in advanced disease |
| `fsgs` | Frequent (50-95%) | Supportive — segmental lesions progress to global sclerosis |
| `iga` | Frequent (50-95%) | Non-specific — chronic global sclerosis indicates longstanding disease |
| `lupus` | Frequent (50-95%) | Non-specific — end-stage scarring from prior lupus nephritis |
| `anca` | Frequent (50-95%) | Non-specific — healed crescents and prior necrotizing lesions |
| `alport` | Constant (>95%) | Strongly Suggestive — progressive global sclerosis with characteristic GBM changes |
| `transplantGlomerulopathy` | Frequent (50-95%) | Non-specific — end-stage chronic transplant glomerulopathy |
| `membranous` | Frequent (50-95%) | Non-specific — chronic advanced membranous nephropathy |
| `thinBasementMembrane` | Occasional (5-20%) | Non-specific — rarely progresses to global sclerosis |
| `cniToxicity` | Occasional (5-20%) | Non-specific — chronic CNI nephrotoxicity with global sclerosis |

#### Prognostic Value

- The percentage of globally sclerosed glomeruli is one of the strongest independent predictors of future GFR decline
- **Thresholds**: >25% global sclerosis generally associated with significantly increased risk of ESKD; >50% indicates advanced chronic kidney disease
- In IgA nephropathy, the Oxford C (chronicity) classification includes global sclerosis assessment
- In transplant biopsies, >10-15% global sclerosis is considered abnormal and may indicate chronic allograft nephropathy
- Serial biopsies showing increasing global sclerosis despite treatment indicate treatment failure or ongoing subclinical injury

#### Treatment Implications

- High percentage of global sclerosis: Focus on nephroprotective measures (RAAS inhibition, SGLT2 inhibitors, BP control) rather than immunosuppression
- If global sclerosis >50% with declining GFR: Prepare for renal replacement therapy (dialysis or transplant)
- In transplant setting: Global sclerosis >10-15% may warrant adjustment of immunosuppression and assessment of donor quality
- Global sclerosis is irreversible — treatment aims to prevent progression of remaining viable glomeruli

2. Loupy A, et al. The Banff 2019 Kidney Meeting Report. *Am J Transplant.* 2020;20(3):586-615
3. KDIGO 2021 Clinical Practice Guideline for the Management of Glomerular Diseases. *Kidney Int.* 2021;100(4S):S1-S276
4. Rule AD, et al. A scoring system for predicting CKD progression. *Clin J Am Soc Nephrol.* 2009;4(10):1620-1626

#### Diagnostic Pearls

- Always report the percentage of globally sclerosed glomeruli and the total number of glomeruli sampled
- Periglomerular fibrosis with tubular atrophy surrounding globally sclerosed glomeruli indicates chronic ischemia — check the vasculature for arteriosclerosis
- Nodular PAS-positive material within globally sclerosed glomeruli (Kimmelstiel-Wilson nodules) is diagnostic of diabetic nephropathy
- If global sclerosis is present in a young patient without hypertension or diabetes, consider hereditary nephropathies (Alport, thin basement membrane, podocytopathies)
- In a transplant biopsy, global sclerosis with peritubular capillary C4d and donor-specific antibodies indicates chronic active antibody-mediated rejection

### LM-06: Focal Necrosis

**Document Reference:** GDES-V4.2-PATH-LM-06

#### Definition

Necrosis of a portion (focal) of the glomerular tuft, characterized by destruction of glomerular cells with loss of nuclear detail (karyolysis, karyorrhexis, pyknosis), disruption of the GBM, and extravasation of plasma proteins including fibrin. This finding indicates acute, severe focal injury to the glomerular capillary wall and is a hallmark of necrotizing glomerulonephritis.

#### Histological Appearance

- **Necrotic segment**: Eosinophilic, amorphous material with loss of cellular detail (smudgy appearance); disrupted or absent GBM on silver stain
- **Fibrin deposition**: Brightly eosinophilic (H&E) and positive for fibrinogen/fibrin on IF within the necrotic area
- **Inflammatory cells**: Neutrophils and nuclear dust (leukocytoclasis) within and adjacent to the necrotic zone
- **GBM disruption**: Silver stain reveals gaps or discontinuities in the GBM at the site of necrosis
- **Karyorrhexis**: Fragmented nuclear debris (nuclear dust) within the necrotic area
- **Adjacent areas**: May show endocapillary hypercellularity, mesangiolysis, or crescent formation
- **Location**: Typically involves the peripheral capillary loops (away from the hilum)
- **Distinction from sclerosis**: Necrosis is characterized by loss of nuclear detail, fibrin deposition, and inflammatory cell infiltration; sclerosis shows increased matrix without inflammation

#### Clinical Significance

Focal necrosis represents the most acute and severe form of glomerular capillary injury. It indicates active, ongoing disease that may be rapidly progressive if untreated. The presence of necrosis is a critical component of the activity index in lupus nephritis and the necrosis score in ANCA vasculitis. Necrotizing lesions are potentially reversible if treated promptly, but they may heal as segmental scars (sclerotic lesions) if treatment is delayed.

#### Associated Diseases

| Disease ID | Frequency | Diagnostic Significance |
|---|---|---|
| `anca` | Frequent (50-95%) | Strongly Suggestive — focal necrosis with pauci-immune IF is hallmark of ANCA vasculitis |
| `lupus` | Common (20-50%) | Strongly Suggestive — necrosis contributes to ISN/RPS activity index |
| `antiGbm` | Frequent (50-95%) | Strongly Suggestive — necrosis with crescents and linear IgG |
| `cryoglobulinemic` | Occasional (5-20%) | Supportive — necrotizing GN in severe cryoglobulinemia |
| `iga` | Occasional (5-20%) | Supportive — focal necrosis indicates severe IgA nephropathy |
| `drugInducedGn` | Occasional (5-20%) | Supportive — immune complex-mediated necrotizing GN |

#### Prognostic Value

- Focal necrosis is a strong predictor of rapid renal function decline if not treated
- In ANCA vasculitis, necrotizing lesions are associated with higher relapse rates
- In lupus nephritis, necrosis contributes to the activity index; higher activity index predicts better response to treatment but also higher relapse risk
- Healing of necrotic lesions may result in segmental scars, contributing to chronic damage

#### Treatment Implications

- Necrotizing GN generally requires urgent immunosuppression
- ANCA vasculitis: Cyclophosphamide or rituximab + corticosteroids; consider plasma exchange
- Anti-GBM: Plasma exchange + cyclophosphamide + corticosteroids
- Lupus: Induction immunosuppression per Class III/IV protocol

#### Guideline References

1. Jennette JC, et al. 2012 revised international consensus classification criteria for ANCA-associated vasculitis. *Arthritis Rheum.* 2013;65(12):3154-3159
2. Weening JJ, et al. The classification of glomerulonephritis in systemic lupus erythematosus revisited. *J Am Soc Nephrol.* 2004;15(2):241-250
3. Bajema IM, et al. Revision of the histopathologic classification of ANCA-associated GN. *J Am Soc Nephrol.* 2020;31(8):1789-1796

#### Diagnostic Pearls

- Necrosis with fibrinoid change on H&E and bright fibrinogen on IF is distinct from sclerotic lesions (which show matrix deposition without fibrinogen)
- Focal necrosis should prompt an urgent search for the cause — the IF pattern (pauci-immune vs immune complex vs linear IgG) guides diagnosis
- Small necrotizing lesions may be subtle on H&E; silver stain highlights GBM disruption and helps identify focal lesions
- In ANCA vasculitis, focal necrosis may be the earliest detectable lesion — early identification enables timely treatment
- Always assess for associated crescents, as focal necrosis frequently progresses to crescentic GN

### LM-07: GBM Duplication/Double Contours

**Document Reference:** GDES-V4.2-PATH-LM-07

#### Definition

The presence of duplicated or double-contoured glomerular basement membranes (GBM), visible as two parallel silver-positive lines separated by a pale space or containing cellular elements within the GBM. This finding results from interposition of mesangial cell processes or monocyte/macrophage processes into the GBM, or from deposition of new basement membrane material beneath the endothelium.

#### Histological Appearance

- **Silver stain (Jones/PAM)**: Most sensitive — reveals two parallel silver-positive lines with a pale or cellular space between them, giving a "tram-track" or "double contour" appearance
- **Location**: Typically most prominent in the peripheral capillary loops; may be segmental or diffuse
- **Mechanism**:
  - Interposition of mesangial cell cytoplasm and/or monocyte processes between the endothelium and the GBM (mesangio-interposition)
  - Deposition of new basement membrane-like material (lamina rara interna duplication)
- **Degree**: May be focal (in some loops) or diffuse (in most loops); segmental or global
- **Associated features**: Often accompanies mesangial expansion, endocapillary proliferation, and subendothelial deposits
- **Distinction from true GBM splitting**: In Alport syndrome, GBM splitting (lamellation) is intramembranous and affects the full thickness of the GBM; double contours from mesangio-interposition are subendothelial

#### Clinical Significance

Double contours are a key histological feature of the membranoproliferative glomerulonephritis (MPGN) pattern, reflecting chronic endothelial injury and reparative GBM remodeling. This finding indicates ongoing immune-mediated or non-immune-mediated injury to the glomerular capillary wall and is associated with a range of immune complex, complement-mediated, and thrombotic diseases.

#### Associated Diseases

| Disease ID | Frequency | Diagnostic Significance |
|---|---|---|
| `mpgn` | Constant (>95%) | Pathognomonic — defining histological feature of MPGN pattern |
| `c3` | Frequent (50-95%) | Strongly Suggestive — C3 glomerulopathy with MPGN pattern |
| `denseDepositDisease` | Frequent (50-95%) | Strongly Suggestive — dense intramembranous deposits with GBM remodeling |
| `cryoglobulinemic` | Frequent (50-95%) | Strongly Suggestive — cryoglobulinemic GN with MPGN pattern |
| `lupus` | Common (20-50%) | Supportive — lupus nephritis Class IV with mesangio-interposition |
| `antibodyMediatedRejection` | Common (20-50%) | Supportive — chronic transplant glomerulopathy with double contours |
| `transplantGlomerulopathy` | Frequent (50-95%) | Strongly Suggestive — chronic transplant glomerulopathy shows double contours |
| `iga` | Occasional (5-20%) | Non-specific — chronic IgA nephropathy may develop MPGN pattern |
| `alport` | Rare (<5%) | Non-specific — GBM splitting in Alport is intramembranous, not subendothelial |

#### Prognostic Value

- In C3 glomerulopathy, the degree of GBM duplication correlates with severity of complement dysregulation
- In transplant glomerulopathy, presence of double contours indicates chronic antibody-mediated injury and is associated with progressive graft loss
- Diffuse GBM duplication carries a worse prognosis than segmental
- Presence of GBM duplication in IgA nephropathy indicates chronic, advanced disease

#### Treatment Implications

- C3 glomerulopathy with MPGN pattern: Complement inhibition (eculizumab, iptacopan) in selected cases
- Cryoglobulinemic GN: Treat underlying cause (HCV antiviral therapy, rituximab)
- Transplant glomerulopathy: Optimize immunosuppression, consider donor-specific antibody management
- ANCA with MPGN pattern: Standard ANCA vasculitis treatment

#### Guideline References

1. Sethi S, et al. C3 glomerulopathy: clinicopathologic features and spectrum of disease. *Clin J Am Soc Nephrol.* 2019;14(1):24-35
2. Haas M. Chronic active antibody-mediated rejection. *Am J Transplant.* 2018;18(12):2907-2913
3. Fogo AB, et al. Membranoproliferative glomerulonephritis. *Kidney Int.* 2019;95(3):537-548

#### Diagnostic Pearls

- Silver stain (Jones methenamine silver or PAM) is essential — double contours may be invisible on H&E
- Distinguish subendothelial double contours (MPGN pattern) from intramembranous splitting (Alport syndrome) by location within the GBM
- In transplant biopsies, double contours should prompt C4d staining and donor-specific antibody testing to evaluate for chronic active AMR
- The finding of double contours with subendothelial deposits on EM and C3/Ig on IF should prompt comprehensive complement evaluation

### LM-08: Membranoproliferative Pattern

**Document Reference:** GDES-V4.2-PATH-LM-08

#### Definition

A histological pattern characterized by the combination of increased mesangial cellularity (mesangial hypercellularity), capillary wall thickening, and duplication of the GBM (double contours), resulting in a lobulated or accentuated glomerular tuft architecture. This is a pattern descriptor, not a specific diagnosis — the underlying etiology must be determined by IF and EM studies.

#### Histological Appearance

- **Lobular accentuation**: The glomerular tuft shows exaggerated lobulation due to mesangial matrix expansion and cellular proliferation within mesangial areas
- **Mesangial hypercellularity**: Increased mesangial cells (≥3 per peripheral mesangial area) distributed diffusely
- **Capillary wall thickening**: Due to GBM duplication, subendothelial deposits, and/or mesangio-interposition
- **Double contours**: Visible on silver stain as "tram-tracking" of peripheral capillary walls
- **Endocapillary proliferation**: May be present in active phases; neutrophils and monocytes may be seen
- **Subendothelial deposits**: Pale eosinophilic material on H&E; best seen on IF and EM
- **Mild glomerular enlargement**: Due to increased cellular and matrix volume
- **Severity assessment**: The pattern may be focal or diffuse, segmental or global

#### Clinical Significance

The membranoproliferative pattern is a reaction pattern of the glomerulus that can be caused by immune complex deposition, complement dysregulation, or chronic endothelial injury. Historically called "membranoproliferative glomerulonephritis" (MPGN), this is now recognized as a pattern rather than a single disease. Identifying the underlying mechanism is critical because treatment varies dramatically: immune complex-mediated MPGN requires treatment of the underlying disease (e.g., HCV, autoimmune disease), C3 glomerulopathy requires complement-targeted therapy, and chronic TMA requires management of the underlying thrombotic condition.

#### Associated Diseases

| Disease ID | Frequency | Diagnostic Significance |
|---|---|---|
| `cryoglobulinemic` | Constant (>95%) | Pathognomonic — MPGN pattern with cryoglobulin-specific features |
| `c3` | Constant (>95%) | Strongly Suggestive — MPGN pattern with C3-dominant IF |
| `denseDepositDisease` | Constant (>95%) | Strongly Suggestive — MPGN pattern with dense intramembranous deposits |
| `mpgn` | Constant (>95%) | Pathognomonic — defining pattern for this diagnosis |
| `lupus` | Common (20-50%) | Supportive — MPGN pattern in chronic lupus nephritis |
| `transplantGlomerulopathy` | Frequent (50-95%) | Supportive — chronic AMR with MPGN-like features |
| `antibodyMediatedRejection` | Common (20-50%) | Supportive — chronic AMR with double contours |
| `iga` | Occasional (5-20%) | Non-specific — chronic IgA nephropathy may develop MPGN pattern |
| `irgn` | Occasional (5-20%) | Non-specific — MPGN pattern in chronic post-infectious GN |

#### Prognostic Value

- MPGN pattern with C3-dominant IF and dense intramembranous deposits: Dense deposit disease carries a poor prognosis for renal survival (50% ESKD at 10 years)
- MPGN pattern with immune complex deposits: Prognosis depends on underlying etiology (treatable causes have better outcomes)
- MPGN pattern with chronic TMA features: May indicate atypical HUS or chronic AMR; prognosis depends on etiology
- Degree of mesangial hypercellularity and GBM duplication correlates with chronicity

#### Treatment Implications

- **C3 glomerulopathy**: Complement inhibitors (eculizumab, iptacopan, ravulizumab); MMF + corticosteroids; consider rituximab
- **Cryoglobulinemic GN**: Antiviral therapy (HCV if present); rituximab + plasma exchange for severe cases
- **Immune complex MPGN**: Treat the underlying disease (autoimmune, infection-related)
- **Chronic TMA**: Manage underlying condition (aHUS: complement inhibition; TMA due to other causes: remove trigger)

#### Guideline References

1. Fogo AB, et al. Membranoproliferative glomerulonephritis. *Kidney Int.* 2019;95(3):537-548
2. Sethi S, et al. C3 glomerulopathy: clinicopathologic features and spectrum of disease. *Clin J Am Soc Nephrol.* 2019;14(1):24-35
3. Cook HT, Pickering MC. Histopathology of MPGN and C3 glomerulopathies. *Nat Rev Nephrol.* 2015;11(1):29-38
4. Haas M. Chronic active antibody-mediated rejection. *Am J Transplant.* 2018;18(12):2907-2913

#### Diagnostic Pearls

- The MPGN pattern is a histological description, not a diagnosis — always investigate the underlying mechanism with IF and EM
- IF pattern determines the category: C3-dominant → C3 glomerulopathy; IgG-dominant → immune complex; pauci-immune → chronic TMA or other
- When MPGN pattern is seen in a transplant, consider chronic AMR before assuming recurrent disease
- Cryoglobulinemic GN often shows characteristic "wire loop" lesions and intraluminal pseudothrombi alongside the MPGN pattern
- In post-infectious GN, the MPGN pattern may develop in the subacute phase — correlate with clinical history

### LM-09: Mesangiolysis

**Document Reference:** GDES-V4.2-PATH-LM-09

#### Definition

Dissolution or destruction of the mesangial matrix and mesangial cells, resulting in acellular, expanded mesangial areas. This finding reflects acute endothelial and mesangial injury, typically from ischemia, toxic injury, or direct endothelial damage, and is most characteristically seen in thrombotic microangiopathy.

#### Histological Appearance

- **PAS stain**: Most apparent — loss of PAS-positive mesangial matrix with pale, washed-out mesangial areas
- **H&E**: Mesangial areas appear expanded but acellular, with pale or edematous matrix
- **Silver stain**: Loss of silver-positive mesangial matrix
- **Distribution**: May be focal or diffuse; often involves multiple glomeruli
- **Associated features**: May be accompanied by endothelial cell swelling, subendothelial edema, and fibrin thrombi in capillary loops
- **Progression**: Mesangiolysis may evolve to mesangial matrix expansion and segmental sclerosis as the lesion heals
- **Distinct from mesangial matrix expansion**: Mesangiolysis represents active dissolution (loss of PAS staining); matrix expansion represents deposition (gain of PAS staining)

#### Clinical Significance

Mesangiolysis is a marker of acute endothelial injury within the glomerulus. It is most commonly associated with thrombotic microangiopathy (TMA) and calcineurin inhibitor nephrotoxicity. The finding indicates ongoing vascular injury that may progress to thrombosis and capillary necrosis if the underlying cause is not addressed.

#### Associated Diseases

| Disease ID | Frequency | Diagnostic Significance |
|---|---|---|
| `cniToxicity` | Frequent (50-95%) | Strongly Suggestive — mesangiolysis with nodular hyalinosis in CNI nephrotoxicity |
| `antibodyMediatedRejection` | Occasional (5-20%) | Supportive — endothelial injury in AMR may cause mesangiolysis |
| `diabeticNephropathy` | Occasional (5-20%) | Non-specific — mesangiolysis may occur in advanced diabetic glomerulopathy |
| `transplantGlomerulopathy` | Occasional (5-20%) | Non-specific — endothelial injury in chronic transplant glomerulopathy |
| `cryoglobulinemic` | Rare (<5%) | Non-specific — severe endothelial injury may cause mesangiolysis |

#### Prognostic Value

- Mesangiolysis in CNI toxicity indicates dose-related nephrotoxicity that may be reversible with CNI reduction
- In transplant biopsies, mesangiolysis associated with endothelialitis and C4d deposition indicates active AMR
- Persistent mesangiolysis with progressive matrix expansion indicates chronic endothelial injury

#### Treatment Implications

- CNI toxicity: Reduce or discontinue CNI; consider conversion to mTOR inhibitor or belatacept
- AMR: Increase immunosuppression, consider plasmapheresis, treat donor-specific antibodies
- TMA: Identify and treat underlying cause; consider complement inhibitors for aHUS

#### Guideline References

1. Snanoudj R, et al. Calcineurin inhibitor nephrotoxicity. *Nat Rev Nephrol.* 2016;12(12):720-734
2. Roufosse C, et al. A working pathology classification for kidney transplant biopsies. *Am J Transplant.* 2018;18(12):2907-2913
3. Herzenberg AM, et al. CNI nephrotoxicity. *J Am Soc Nephrol.* 2003;14(10):2662-2671

#### Diagnostic Pearls

- Mesangiolysis is best appreciated on PAS stain — the mesangium appears pale and expanded rather than darkly staining
- In CNI toxicity, look for the combination of mesangiolysis, isometric vacuolization of tubular epithelial cells, and arteriolar hyalinosis
- If mesangiolysis is accompanied by fibrin thrombi and endothelial swelling, suspect TMA
- In transplant biopsies, mesangiolysis without other features of AMR may indicate CNI nephrotoxicity

### LM-10: Thrombotic Microangiopathy Pattern

**Document Reference:** GDES-V4.2-PATH-LM-10

#### Definition

A histological pattern characterized by the triad of endothelial cell injury, thrombosis (fibrin thrombi in glomerular capillaries and/or arterioles), and fragmentation of red blood cells (schistocytes) within the microvasculature. This pattern indicates acute microvascular thrombosis and can be caused by numerous underlying conditions including complement dysregulation, drug toxicity, infection, and malignancy.

#### Histological Appearance

**Glomerular changes:**
- **Fibrin thrombi**: Eosinophilic, PAS-negative material within glomerular capillary lumina; may be segmental or global
- **Endothelial swelling**: Enlarged endothelial cells that narrow or occlude capillary lumina
- **Subendothelial edema**: Pale, expanded space between endothelium and GBM
- **Mesangiolysis**: Dissolution of mesangial matrix (see LM-09)
- **GBM corrugation**: Wrinkling of GBM in ischemic areas
- **Glomerular ischemia**: Shrunken, collapsed glomerular tufts with wrinkled GBM

**Arteriolar/arterial changes:**
- **Mucoid intimal edema**: Pale, myxoid expansion of the intima of interlobular arteries
- **Fibrinoid necrosis of arterioles**: Brightly eosinophilic smudgy material in arteriolar walls
- **Onion-skin intimal proliferation**: Concentric laminated intimal fibrosis in chronic/recurrent TMA
- **Fibrin thrombi in arterioles**: Eosinophilic occlusive material within arteriolar lumina

**Chronic changes (if longstanding):**
- **Glomerular ischemic collapse**: Collapsed, solidified glomerular tufts
- **Fibrocellular intimal thickening**: Arterial wall thickening with recanalization
- **Cortex scarring**: Interstitial fibrosis and tubular atrophy with loss of peritubular capillaries

#### Clinical Significance

TMA represents a medical emergency requiring rapid identification and treatment of the underlying cause. The finding of TMA on renal biopsy mandates immediate clinical correlation with peripheral blood smear (for schistocytes), platelet count, LDH, haptoglobin, and coagulation studies. The etiology determines treatment: complement inhibition for atypical HUS, plasma exchange for TTP, withdrawal of offending drug for drug-induced TMA, and treatment of underlying infection.

#### Associated Diseases

| Disease ID | Frequency | Diagnostic Significance |
|---|---|---|
| `cniToxicity` | Frequent (50-95%) | Supportive — CNI-induced endothelial injury with TMA features |
| `antibodyMediatedRejection` | Common (20-50%) | Supportive — endothelial injury in AMR may manifest as TMA |
| `transplantGlomerulopathy` | Occasional (5-20%) | Non-specific — chronic endothelial injury may show TMA features |
| `diabeticNephropathy` | Occasional (5-20%) | Non-specific — TMA may occur in severe diabetic nephropathy |

#### Prognostic Value

- Acute TMA with extensive fibrin thrombi and absent chronic changes: Potentially reversible with appropriate treatment
- Chronic TMA with arterial intimal fibrosis and glomerular ischemia: Irreversible damage; poor renal prognosis
- In aHUS, early complement inhibition dramatically improves outcomes
- TMA in transplant setting may indicate AMR, CNI toxicity, or recurrent aHUS — each with different prognosis

#### Treatment Implications

- **aHUS**: Eculizumab (complement inhibitor) — immediate initiation
- **Drug-induced TMA**: Discontinue offending agent (CNIs, antibiotics, oral contraceptives)
- **TTP**: Plasma exchange + corticosteroids
- **AMR with TMA**: Plasmapheresis + increased immunosuppression + complement inhibition
- **All TMA**: Supportive care, blood pressure management, avoid further endothelial injury

#### Guideline References

1. Noris M, Remuzzi G. Atypical hemolytic uremic syndrome. *N Engl J Med.* 2009;361(17):1675-1687
2. George JN, Nester CM. Syndromes of thrombotic microangiopathy. *N Engl J Med.* 2014;371(7):654-666
3. Lusco MA, et al. Banff 2019 Kidney Meeting Report. *Am J Transplant.* 2020;20(10):2518-2531

#### Diagnostic Pearls

- TMA on biopsy is a pattern, not a diagnosis — always investigate the underlying cause
- In transplant biopsies, the differential for TMA includes AMR, CNI toxicity, and recurrent aHUS — use C4d, DSA, and clinical context to differentiate
- Chronic TMA (arterial onion-skinning) should prompt evaluation for scleroderma renal crisis, malignant hypertension, and chronic AMR
- Glomerular TMA (fibrin thrombi) differs from arteriolar TMA (arteriolar fibrinoid necrosis) — both represent the same pathological process at different vascular levels
- When TMA is present, correlate with peripheral smear for schistocytes, platelet count, and LDH to confirm microangiopathic hemolytic anemia

### LM-11: Interstitial Fibrosis and Tubular Atrophy (IFTA)

**Document Reference:** GDES-V4.2-PATH-LM-11

#### Definition

Chronic irreversible injury to the renal tubulointerstitium characterized by the replacement of normal tubular structures with fibrous (collagenous) tissue and the corresponding atrophy (reduction in size and number) of tubular epithelial cells. IFTA is the final common pathway of progressive renal disease regardless of etiology and is one of the strongest predictors of long-term renal outcome.

#### Histological Appearance

- **Tubular atrophy**: Thickened tubular basement membranes (TBM), simplified (flattened) tubular epithelium with loss of brush border, reduced tubular diameter, and loss of tubular nuclei in severely atrophic tubules
- **Interstitial fibrosis**: Increased collagen deposition in the interstitium, visible as blue on trichrome stain, pale pink on H&E, and silver-positive on reticulin stain
- **Pattern of distribution**: Typically patchy initially, becoming more diffuse with progression; may be perivascular, periglomerular, or diffuse
- **Severity grading**:
  - Mild IFTA: <25% of cortical area
  - Moderate IFTA: 25-50% of cortical area
  - Severe IFTA: >50% of cortical area
- **Banff classification**: ci (interstitial fibrosis) and ct (tubular atrophy) scored 0-3:
  - ci0/ct0: No fibrosis/atrophy
  - ci1/ct1: Mild (<25%)
  - ci2/ct2: Moderate (26-50%)
  - ci3/ct3: Severe (>50%)
- **Associated features**: Periglomerular fibrosis, peritubular capillary rarefaction, scattered chronic inflammatory cells (lymphocytes, plasma cells)
- **Differential**: Interstitial nephritis (active, with inflammatory cell infiltrate) vs IFTA (chronic, predominantly fibrosis with minimal inflammation)

#### Clinical Significance

IFTA is the single best histological predictor of renal outcome across all kidney diseases. It represents irreversible parenchymal loss and directly correlates with declining GFR. The extent of IFTA on biopsy is more predictive of long-term outcome than the primary glomerular lesion. In transplant biopsies, progressive IFTA is the hallmark of chronic allograft nephropathy and is associated with graft loss.

#### Associated Diseases

| Disease ID | Frequency | Diagnostic Significance |
|---|---|---|
| `diabeticNephropathy` | Constant (>95%) | Non-specific — progressive IFTA is universal in advanced DN |
| `alport` | Constant (>95%) | Non-specific — progressive IFTA accompanies glomerular sclerosis |
| `fsgs` | Frequent (50-95%) | Non-specific — IFTA correlates with declining renal function |
| `iga` | Frequent (50-95%) | Non-specific — Oxford T1/T2 classification |
| `lupus` | Frequent (50-95%) | Non-specific — chronic damage from prior nephritis |
| `membranous` | Frequent (50-95%) | Non-specific — IFTA in advanced membranous nephropathy |
| `anca` | Frequent (50-95%) | Non-specific — IFTA from prior necrotizing/crescentic GN |
| `transplantGlomerulopathy` | Constant (>95%) | Strongly Suggestive — progressive IFTA with chronic AMR |
| `cniToxicity` | Frequent (50-95%) | Supportive — CNI nephrotoxicity with IFTA |
| `tCellMediatedRejection` | Common (20-50%) | Non-specific — chronic TCMR with IFTA |
| `bkVirusNephropathy` | Frequent (50-95%) | Non-specific — IFTA from chronic viral injury |

#### Prognostic Value

- IFTA percentage is the strongest independent predictor of ESKD across virtually all kidney diseases
- **Banff ci/ct scoring**:
  - ci0/ct0: Excellent prognosis for graft survival
  - ci1/ct1: Moderate risk of progression
  - ci2/ct2: Significant risk of graft loss
  - ci3/ct3: Very high risk; many grafts lost within 5 years
- IFTA >50% on native kidney biopsy: Very limited potential for renal recovery
- In IgA nephropathy, Oxford T1 (26-50%) and T2 (>50%) independently predict progression to ESKD
- Serial biopsies showing increasing IFTA despite treatment indicate treatment failure

#### Treatment Implications

- IFTA is irreversible — treatment focuses on preventing further progression
- Nephroprotective measures: RAAS inhibition, SGLT2 inhibitors, BP control, dietary sodium restriction
- In transplant: Optimize immunosuppression, address modifiable risk factors (CMV, BK virus, drug levels)
- IFTA >50%: Prepare for renal replacement therapy
- Avoid nephrotoxins in kidneys with significant IFTA

#### Guideline References

1. Loupy A, et al. The Banff 2019 Kidney Meeting Report. *Am J Transplant.* 2020;20(3):586-615
2. Working Group of the International IgA Nephropathy Network. The Oxford Classification of IgA nephropathy. *Kidney Int.* 2009;76(5):534-546
3. KDIGO 2021 Clinical Practice Guideline for the Management of Glomerular Diseases. *Kidney Int.* 2021;100(4S):S1-S276
4. Nankivell BJ, Borrows RJ. Calcineurin inhibitor nephrotoxicity. *Am J Transplant.* 2004;4(7):1103-1113

#### Diagnostic Pearls

- Always report IFTA percentage or Banff ci/ct score — this is as important as (or more important than) the primary glomerular diagnosis
- Trichrome stain is essential for accurate assessment of interstitial fibrosis
- Distinguish IFTA from acute interstitial nephritis: IFTA shows fibrosis with atrophic tubules and minimal inflammation; acute IN shows inflammation with relatively preserved tubules
- In transplant biopsies, IFTA with tubulitis (T) and arteritis (v) indicates ongoing rejection superimposed on chronic damage — treatable if caught early
- Periglomerular fibrosis surrounding globally sclerosed glomeruli with adjacent IFTA suggests chronic ischemia as the predominant mechanism

### LM-12: Acute Tubular Injury/Necrosis

**Document Reference:** GDES-V4.2-PATH-LM-12

#### Definition

Acute damage to renal tubular epithelial cells ranging from sublethal injury (acute tubular injury, ATI) to cell death (acute tubular necrosis, ATN). This is the most common cause of acute kidney injury (AKI) and results from ischemic or nephrotoxic insults. The finding is characterized by loss of the tubular brush border, simplification and detachment of tubular epithelium, and intratubular casts.

#### Histological Appearance

- **Loss of brush border**: Particularly evident in proximal tubules; loss of the normally prominent apical brush border on H&E and PAS stain
- **Simplification of tubular epithelium**: Flattened, cuboidal to squamous tubular lining cells with loss of normal cytoplasmic volume
- **Cell detachment**: Sloughing of dead or injured epithelial cells into the tubular lumen
- **Nuclear changes**: Pyknosis, karyorrhexis, and karyolysis in necrotic cells; mitotic figures in regenerating cells
- **Intratubular casts**: Granular (muddy brown) casts, pigmented casts (myoglobin, hemoglobin), or hyaline casts
- **Interstitial edema**: Pale, expanded interstitium between tubules
- **Backleak**: Contrast material or other markers may leak from injured tubules into the interstitium
- **Regenerative changes**: If surviving cells are present, tubular epithelial cells may show enlarged nuclei, prominent nucleoli, and mitotic figures (regeneration)
- **Distribution**: May be focal (patchy) or diffuse; proximal tubules are more susceptible to ischemic injury; distal tubules and collecting ducts may be more affected by certain toxins
- **Distinguishing ATI from normal**: Normal proximal tubules have abundant eosinophilic cytoplasm, round nuclei, and prominent brush border; ATI shows simplified, flattened epithelium with loss of brush border

#### Clinical Significance

ATI/ATN is the most common cause of AKI in hospitalized patients. The histological finding confirms the clinical diagnosis and may provide clues to the etiology (ischemic vs toxic). While ATN is potentially reversible (unlike chronic interstitial fibrosis), severe or prolonged ATN may lead to persistent tubular dysfunction and slow recovery. The severity of tubular injury correlates with the degree and duration of AKI.

#### Associated Diseases

| Disease ID | Frequency | Diagnostic Significance |
|---|---|---|
| `cniToxicity` | Frequent (50-95%) | Supportive — CNI nephrotoxicity with tubular injury |
| `diabeticNephropathy` | Occasional (5-20%) | Non-specific — ATN may complicate advanced DN |
| `lupus` | Occasional (5-20%) | Non-specific — ischemic ATN in active lupus nephritis |
| `tCellMediatedRejection` | Occasional (5-20%) | Non-specific — ischemic ATN from vascular compromise in rejection |
| `bkVirusNephropathy` | Occasional (5-20%) | Non-specific — viral cytopathic effect may resemble ATI |

#### Prognostic Value

- Severity of ATI/ATN correlates with the degree of AKI and duration of renal replacement therapy
- Extensive ATI with diffuse tubular necrosis: Poor prognosis; may not recover fully
- ATI with regenerative changes (mitotic figures, nuclear atypia): Indicates recovery phase
- Persistent ATI on serial biopsies: Suggests ongoing injury (ischemia, drug toxicity, ongoing disease)

#### Treatment Implications

- Identify and treat underlying cause (sepsis, hypotension, nephrotoxins)
- Supportive care: Volume management, avoid further nephrotoxins, renal replacement therapy if needed
- CNI toxicity: Reduce CNI levels or convert to alternative immunosuppression
- Recovery phase: Careful monitoring for diuresis and GFR recovery

#### Guideline References

1. Basile DP, et al. Acute kidney injury. *Nat Rev Dis Primers.* 2021;7(1):82
2. Bonventre JV, Yang L. Cellular pathophysiology of ischemic acute kidney injury. *J Clin Invest.* 2011;121(11):4210-4220
3. Kellum JA, et al. Acute kidney injury. *Nat Rev Dis Primers.* 2021;7(1):82

#### Diagnostic Pearls

- ATI/ATN is a common incidental finding on biopsies performed for other indications — always note its presence as it may explain AKI
- Loss of brush border on PAS stain is the earliest and most sensitive indicator of proximal tubular injury
- "Muddy brown" granular casts on urinalysis correlate with sloughed necrotic tubular cells — they are the clinical hallmark of ATN
- In transplant biopsies, ATI may be due to preservation injury, rejection, CNI toxicity, or BK virus — use all available data to determine etiology
- Regenerative changes (mitotic figures, nuclear enlargement, prominent nucleoli) indicate recovery from ATN — this is a reassuring finding

### LM-13: Interstitial Nephritis

**Document Reference:** GDES-V4.2-PATH-LM-13

#### Definition

Inflammatory cell infiltration of the renal interstitium, typically composed of lymphocytes, monocytes/macrophages, eosinophils, and/or plasma cells, with associated tubulitis (inflammation of tubular epithelium). This finding indicates immune-mediated injury to the tubulointerstitium and may be caused by drugs, infections, autoimmune diseases, or may be secondary to glomerular or vascular disease.

#### Histological Appearance

- **Inflammatory infiltrate**: Mononuclear cells (lymphocytes, plasma cells, macrophages) ± eosinophils within the interstitium
- **Distribution**: May be focal or diffuse, patchy or uniform; corticomedullary junction often most severely affected in drug-induced cases
- **Composition**:
  - Predominantly lymphocytes: Suggests autoimmune, viral, or drug-induced
  - Eosinophils: Suggests drug-induced or allergic etiology
  - Neutrophils: Suggests bacterial infection (ascending pyelonephritis) or acute phase of disease
  - Plasma cells: Suggests autoimmune disease (IgG4-related disease, lupus)
- **Tubulitis**: Inflammatory cells within tubular epithelium; graded by Banff classification:
  - t0: No tubulitis
  - t1: Mild tubulitis (<10% of tubules affected)
  - t2: Moderate tubulitis (10-25% of tubules affected)
  - t3: Severe tubulitis (>25% of tubules affected)
- **Tubular injury**: Secondary tubular epithelial damage with simplification, loss of brush border
- **Edema**: Pale, expanded interstitium with separation of tubules
- **Granulomas**: If present, consider drug-induced, sarcoidosis, tuberculosis, or other granulomatous diseases
- **Fibrosis**: If interstitial fibrosis accompanies inflammation, suggests chronicity or chronic active process

#### Clinical Significance

Interstitial nephritis is a common cause of AKI, particularly drug-induced acute interstitial nephritis (AIN). The finding of inflammatory cells within the interstitium with tubulitis confirms immune-mediated tubulointerstitial injury. In transplant biopsies, tubulitis (t score) is a key component of the Banff classification of T-cell-mediated rejection (TCMR). In native kidneys, interstitial nephritis may be the presenting manifestation of systemic autoimmune disease, drug hypersensitivity, or infection.

#### Associated Diseases

| Disease ID | Frequency | Diagnostic Significance |
|---|---|---|
| `tCellMediatedRejection` | Frequent (50-95%) | Strongly Suggestive — tubulitis with mononuclear cell infiltrate defines TCMR |
| `lupus` | Occasional (5-20%) | Supportive — lupus interstitial nephritis may occur without glomerular disease |
| `bkVirusNephropathy` | Frequent (50-95%) | Supportive — tubulitis with viral inclusions may mimic rejection |
| `cniToxicity` | Occasional (5-20%) | Non-specific — chronic CNI nephrotoxicity with interstitial fibrosis |
| `drugInducedGn` | Frequent (50-95%) | Strongly Suggestive — drug-induced AIN is the most common cause |
| `diabeticNephropathy` | Occasional (5-20%) | Non-specific — chronic inflammatory cells in advanced DN |

#### Prognostic Value

- Drug-induced AIN: Generally good prognosis if drug is withdrawn early; may be irreversible if delayed
- TCMR in transplant: Banff t score correlates with severity and response to treatment
- Interstitial nephritis with granulomas: May indicate sarcoidosis or drug reaction; generally responds well to corticosteroids
- Interstitial nephritis with fibrosis: Indicates chronicity; less likely to respond fully to treatment

#### Treatment Implications

- Drug-induced AIN: Discontinue offending drug; corticosteroids if not improving within 1-2 weeks
- TCMR: Pulse corticosteroids; steroid-resistant: OKT3, anti-thymocyte globulin
- Autoimmune interstitial nephritis: Immunosuppression based on underlying etiology
- BK virus nephropathy: Reduce immunosuppression; antiviral therapy (cidofovir, leflunomide)

#### Guideline References

1. Loupy A, et al. The Banff 2019 Kidney Meeting Report. *Am J Transplant.* 2020;20(3):586-615
2. Rossert J. Drug-induced acute interstitial nephritis. *Kidney Int.* 2001;60(2):804-817
3. Miettinen M, et al. Tubulitis in renal transplant biopsies. *Am J Transplant.* 2005;5(11):2710-2717

#### Diagnostic Pearls

- In transplant biopsies, tubulitis with inflammatory cells infiltrating tubular epithelium is the key feature of TCMR — distinguish from BK virus nephropathy by SV40 staining
- Eosinophils in the interstitium are highly suggestive of drug-induced AIN but are not pathognomonic
- The combination of glomerular disease + interstitial nephritis should prompt consideration of lupus nephritis, IgG4-related disease, or sarcoidosis
- Always assess the degree of interstitial fibrosis alongside inflammation — fibrosis indicates chronicity
- In a biopsy showing predominantly interstitial inflammation without glomerular disease, consider drug reaction, autoimmune disease, and infection

### LM-14: Arteriolar Hyalinosis

**Document Reference:** GDES-V4.2-PATH-LM-14

#### Definition

Deposition of homogenous, eosinophilic, PAS-positive hyaline material within arteriolar walls, resulting in narrowing of the arteriolar lumen. Hyaline consists of plasma proteins (including immunoglobulins and complement) that have leaked through injured endothelium, along with increased basement membrane material. This finding indicates chronic vascular injury and is most commonly associated with hypertension and diabetes.

#### Histological Appearance

- **H&E**: Homogenous, pink, glassy (hyaline) material within arteriolar walls, causing concentric or eccentric luminal narrowing
- **PAS stain**: Hyaline material is strongly PAS-positive (bright magenta)
- **Distribution**: May be afferent only, efferent only, or both (afferent > efferent); both afferent and efferent hyalinosis strongly suggests diabetes
- **Severity**: Mild (minimal luminal narrowing) to severe (near-complete luminal occlusion)
- **Differential from fibrinoid necrosis**: Hyalinosis is smooth, homogenous, and acellular; fibrinoid necrosis shows smudgy, bright eosinophilic material with associated inflammation
- **Associated findings**: Arteriosclerosis (intimal fibrosis) of larger vessels, glomerulosclerosis, tubulointerstitial fibrosis

#### Clinical Significance

Arteriolar hyalinosis is a marker of chronic vascular injury and is strongly associated with systemic hypertension and diabetes mellitus. It indicates long-standing damage to the arteriolar wall, leading to reduced renal perfusion and ischemic nephron loss. The finding is usually irreversible and contributes to progressive chronic kidney disease.

#### Associated Diseases

| Disease ID | Frequency | Diagnostic Significance |
|---|---|---|
| `diabeticNephropathy` | Constant (>95%) | Strongly Suggestive — both afferent and efferent hyalinosis is highly characteristic |
| `fsgs` | Frequent (50-95%) | Non-specific — hypertensive arteriolopathy in secondary FSGS |
| `iga` | Frequent (50-95%) | Non-specific — hypertensive vascular damage in chronic IgA nephropathy |
| `lupus` | Frequent (50-95%) | Non-specific — hyalinosis from hypertension in chronic lupus nephritis |
| `membranous` | Frequent (50-95%) | Non-specific — vascular hyalinosis from nephrotic syndrome and hypertension |
| `cniToxicity` | Frequent (50-95%) | Strongly Suggestive — nodular hyalinosis in CNI nephrotoxicity |
| `alport` | Frequent (50-95%) | Non-specific — arteriolar hyalinosis in advanced Alport syndrome |

#### Prognostic Value

- Severity of arteriolar hyalinosis correlates with the degree of hypertension and diabetic control
- Efferent arteriolar hyalinosis is virtually specific for diabetic nephropathy
- Severe afferent hyalinosis with luminal occlusion: Indicates significant ischemic injury to downstream nephrons
- Arteriolar hyalinosis with global glomerulosclerosis and IFTA: Indicates advanced hypertensive nephrosclerosis

#### Treatment Implications

- Strict blood pressure control to prevent progression
- Optimize glycemic control in diabetic nephropathy
- RAAS inhibition for renoprotection
- Arteriolar hyalinosis is irreversible — focus on preventing further vascular injury

#### Guideline References

1. Tervaert TW, et al. Pathologic classification of diabetic nephropathy. *J Am Soc Nephrol.* 2010;21(4):556-563
2. Marcantoni C, et al. Arteriolar hyalinosis in hypertension. *Am J Kidney Dis.* 2013;61(3):453-462
3. Snanoudj R, et al. Calcineurin inhibitor nephrotoxicity. *Nat Rev Nephrol.* 2016;12(12):720-734

#### Diagnostic Pearls

- Afferent AND efferent arteriolar hyalinosis in the same kidney biopsy is highly specific for diabetic nephropathy
- In CNI toxicity, hyalinosis may be nodular and more prominent in arterioles than in diabetic nephropathy
- Hyalinosis should be distinguished from fibrinoid necrosis (acute vascular injury) — both appear eosinophilic but have different clinical implications
- Arteriolar hyalinosis with global glomerulosclerosis and IFTA suggests hypertensive nephrosclerosis as the predominant cause of CKD
- In transplant biopsies, CNI-related hyalinosis typically affects efferent arterioles — a useful distinguishing feature from hypertensive hyalinosis

### LM-15: Vascular Intimal Fibrosis

**Document Reference:** GDES-V4.2-PATH-LM-15

#### Definition

Fibrous thickening of the intimal layer of interlobular and arcuate arteries, resulting in luminal narrowing. This finding represents chronic vascular injury and remodeling and is distinct from arteriolar hyalinosis, which affects smaller vessels. Vascular intimal fibrosis indicates longstanding hypertension, aging, or chronic vascular injury (e.g., chronic AMR, CNI toxicity).

#### Histological Appearance

- **H&E**: Concentric or eccentric thickening of the intima by collagenous tissue, narrowing the arterial lumen
- **Trichrome stain**: Blue-staining collagen in the thickened intima
- **Elastic stain (EVG/Verhoeff-Van Gieson)**: Shows the internal elastic lamina; helps distinguish intimal fibrosis from medial hypertrophy
- **Onion-skin pattern**: Concentric, laminated intimal fibrosis — seen in chronic TMA, scleroderma renal crisis, malignant hypertension
- **Recanalization**: Some chronically thrombosed arteries may show recanalized lumina within fibrotic intima
- **Distribution**: May affect interlobular arteries (most commonly), arcuate arteries, or both
- **Associated features**: Luminal narrowing leads to downstream ischemic injury (global glomerulosclerosis, IFTA)
- **Differential**: Distinguish from arterial intimal hyperplasia (smooth muscle cell proliferation) and arteriosclerosis (elastic lamina fragmentation with intimal fibrosis)

#### Clinical Significance

Vascular intimal fibrosis is a marker of chronic vascular injury and is associated with long-standing hypertension, aging, and chronic vascular diseases. It is a component of chronic allograft vasculopathy in transplant kidneys and may be seen in chronic AMR. The degree of arterial fibrosis contributes to ischemic nephron loss and progressive CKD.

#### Associated Diseases

| Disease ID | Frequency | Diagnostic Significance |
|---|---|---|
| `diabeticNephropathy` | Frequent (50-95%) | Non-specific — hypertensive and diabetic vascular disease |
| `iga` | Frequent (50-95%) | Non-specific — chronic vascular damage in IgA nephropathy |
| `alport` | Frequent (50-95%) | Non-specific — chronic vascular injury in advanced disease |
| `transplantGlomerulopathy` | Frequent (50-95%) | Strongly Suggestive — chronic allograft vasculopathy with intimal fibrosis |
| `antibodyMediatedRejection` | Frequent (50-95%) | Strongly Suggestive — chronic vascular rejection with intimal fibrosis |
| `lupus` | Frequent (50-95%) | Non-specific — chronic vascular damage in longstanding lupus nephritis |
| `membranous` | Common (20-50%) | Non-specific — vascular changes in advanced membranous nephropathy |
| `cniToxicity` | Occasional (5-20%) | Supportive — CNI-related vascular remodeling |

#### Prognostic Value

- Severity of intimal fibrosis correlates with degree of ischemic nephron loss
- In transplant biopsies, progressive intimal fibrosis is a hallmark of chronic allograft vasculopathy and is associated with graft loss
- Severe arterial fibrosis with >50% luminal narrowing: Indicates significant ischemic injury

#### Treatment Implications

- Treat underlying cause (BP control, immunosuppression, remove offending agents)
- In transplant: Optimize immunosuppression; consider complement inhibition for chronic AMR
- Arterial fibrosis is irreversible — focus on preventing further vascular injury

#### Guideline References

1. Lusco MA, et al. Banff 2019 Kidney Meeting Report. *Am J Transplant.* 2020;20(10):2518-2531
2. Marcantoni C, et al. Arterial intimal fibrosis in kidney disease. *Am J Kidney Dis.* 2013;61(3):453-462
3. Haas M. Chronic active antibody-mediated rejection. *Am J Transplant.* 2018;18(12):2907-2913

#### Diagnostic Pearls

- Elastic stain is essential to evaluate arterial architecture — it distinguishes intimal fibrosis from medial hypertrophy
- "Onion-skin" intimal fibrosis in interlobular arteries is characteristic of chronic TMA (aHUS, scleroderma, malignant HTN)
- In transplant biopsies, intimal fibrosis with inflammation within the intima suggests active chronic vascular rejection (Banff cv score)
- Arterial intimal fibrosis with downstream global glomerulosclerosis and IFTA indicates chronic ischemic nephropathy
- Distinguish arterial intimal fibrosis from arteriolar hyalinosis — they affect different-sized vessels and have different pathophysiology

### IF-01: IgA Dominant Mesangial Deposits

**Document Reference:** GDES-V4.2-PATH-IF-01

#### Definition

Granular immunofluorescence staining for IgA within the mesangial regions of glomeruli, with IgA intensity at least 2 orders of magnitude greater than any other immunoreactant (IgG, IgM, C3, C1q, kappa, lambda). This finding is the hallmark of IgA-associated glomerular diseases, most commonly IgA nephropathy.

#### Histological Appearance

- **Pattern**: Granular, sausage-shaped deposits within mesangial areas, following the distribution of mesangial matrix
- **Intensity**: Typically 2+ to 4+ on a semiquantitative scale; IgA must be the dominant or co-dominant immunoreactant
- **Distribution**: Diffuse (all glomeruli) in primary IgA nephropathy; may be focal in early or mild disease
- **Associated immunoreactants**: C3 is commonly co-dominant with IgA; IgG and/or IgM may be present at lower intensity; C1q is typically absent or weak
- **Capillary wall deposits**: If present, suggest secondary IgA disease (e.g., IgA vasculitis, liver disease)
- **Distinguishing from other mesangial deposits**: IgA must be clearly dominant over IgG and IgM to distinguish from non-specific mesangial trapping
- **Kappa/lambda ratio**: Polyclonal (normal kappa:lambda ratio) — monoclonal IgA should raise concern for monoclonal gammopathy-associated GN

#### Clinical Significance

IgA-dominant mesangial deposits are the defining immunofluorescence finding of IgA nephropathy (when deposits are in the kidney) and IgA vasculitis (when deposits affect skin, joints, and kidneys). The presence of IgA-dominant deposits in a renal biopsy, combined with the appropriate clinical context, is diagnostic of an IgA-associated glomerular disease. Secondary causes of IgA deposition (liver disease, celiac disease, HIV) must be excluded.

#### Associated Diseases

| Disease ID | Frequency | Diagnostic Significance |
|---|---|---|
| `iga` | Constant (>95%) | Pathognomonic — defining feature of IgA nephropathy |
| `hivan` | Occasional (5-20%) | Supportive — IgA deposits may be seen in HIV-associated GN |
| `irgn` | Occasional (5-20%) | Non-specific — IgA may be present in post-infectious GN |
| `drugInducedGn` | Rare (<5%) | Non-specific — secondary IgA deposition reported in drug-induced GN |

#### Prognostic Value

- Intensity of IgA deposition does not reliably predict clinical severity or prognosis
- Presence of IgG co-deposits may indicate a more inflammatory phenotype and worse prognosis (IgA nephropathy)
- C3 co-dominance with IgA suggests complement activation via the alternative pathway
- IgA deposits with crescents and necrosis indicate severe disease

#### Treatment Implications

- IgA nephropathy with IgA-dominant deposits: SGLT2 inhibitors as first-line; immunosuppression for proteinuria >0.5-1 g/day despite maximized supportive care
- Secondary IgA deposition: Treat underlying cause (liver disease, celiac disease, HIV)
- IgA vasculitis: Generally self-limited in children; immunosuppression for severe renal or extrarenal involvement

#### Guideline References

1. Working Group of the International IgA Nephropathy Network. The Oxford Classification of IgA nephropathy. *Kidney Int.* 2009;76(5):534-546
2. Haas M, et al. IgA nephropathy: the Banff 2018 working proposal. *Kidney Int.* 2018;94(3):477-480
3. KDIGO 2021 Clinical Practice Guideline for the Management of Glomerular Diseases. *Kidney Int.* 2021;100(4S):S1-S276

#### Diagnostic Pearls

- IgA must be the dominant immunoreactant — if IgG > IgA, consider other diagnoses (lupus, post-infectious GN)
- Polyclonal kappa/lambda ratio is essential to exclude monoclonal IgA deposition disease
- IgA deposits in a transplant biopsy may represent recurrent IgA nephropathy
- The finding of IgA-dominant mesangial deposits with mesangial proliferation on LM is diagnostic of IgA nephropathy — no further IF criteria are needed
- In children with IgA-dominant deposits and palpable purpura, consider IgA vasculitis (Henoch-Schönlein purpura)

### IF-02: Full-House Immunofluorescence

**Document Reference:** GDES-V4.2-PATH-IF-02

#### Definition

Immunofluorescence pattern showing positivity for all major immunoreactants: IgG, IgA, IgM, C3, and C1q in a glomerular distribution. This pattern is most characteristically associated with lupus nephritis, where immune complex deposition is widespread and involves multiple complement pathways. Full-house IF is also known as the "full-house pattern" or "all-positive IF."

#### Histological Appearance

- **Pattern**: Granular deposits of IgG, IgA, IgM, C3, and C1q within mesangial and/or capillary wall (subendothelial, subepithelial) distributions
- **Intensity**: May be 1+ to 4+ for each immunoreactant; the intensity does not need to be uniform across all immunoreactants
- **Distribution**: Mesangial (in Class II), mesangial + capillary wall (Class III/IV), or capillary wall predominant (Class V)
- **Mesangial vs capillary wall**: Mesangial deposits suggest mesangial lupus (Class II); capillary wall (subendothelial) deposits suggest proliferative lupus (Class III/IV)
- **"Wire loop" deposits**: Heavy subendothelial deposits visible on H&E as thickened capillary walls
- **C1q**: Strong C1q staining is particularly characteristic of lupus and helps distinguish from other immune complex diseases
- **Granularity**: Deposits are typically coarse and granular, reflecting immune complex deposition
- **Kappa/lambda**: Polyclonal staining pattern

#### Clinical Significance

Full-house IF is strongly suggestive of lupus nephritis and should prompt thorough clinical evaluation for systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE). While the pattern is not absolutely pathognomonic — it can be seen in other conditions — in the appropriate clinical context, it is essentially diagnostic. The distribution and intensity of deposits correlate with the ISN/RPS class of lupus nephritis and guide treatment decisions.

#### Associated Diseases

| Disease ID | Frequency | Diagnostic Significance |
|---|---|---|
| `lupus` | Constant (>95%) | Pathognomonic — defining IF feature of lupus nephritis |
| `cryoglobulinemic` | Occasional (5-20%) | Supportive — mixed cryoglobulinemia may show full-house pattern |
| `drugInducedGn` | Rare (<5%) | Non-specific — rare cases of drug-induced GN with full-house pattern |
| `iga` | Rare (<5%) | Non-specific — rare IgA nephropathy with multiple immunoreactant deposits |

#### Prognostic Value

- Full-house IF with strong C1q and capillary wall distribution: Indicates active proliferative lupus nephritis (Class III/IV) with potential for renal damage
- Full-house IF with mesangial distribution only: Indicates milder Class II lupus nephritis with better prognosis
- Intensity of immunoreactants generally correlates with the activity of lupus nephritis

#### Treatment Implications

- Lupus nephritis with full-house IF: Treatment per ISN/RPS class
  - Class II: Supportive; immunosuppression only for significant proteinuria
  - Class III/IV: Induction with MMF or cyclophosphamide + corticosteroids; maintenance with MMF
  - Class V: ACE inhibitors/ARBs; immunosuppression for nephrotic-range proteinuria

#### Guideline References

1. Weening JJ, et al. The classification of glomerulonephritis in systemic lupus erythematosus revisited. *J Am Soc Nephrol.* 2004;15(2):241-250
2. KDIGO 2021 Clinical Practice Guideline for the Management of Glomerular Diseases. *Kidney Int.* 2021;100(4S):S1-S276
3. Austin HA, et al. Renal biopsy in lupus nephritis. *Arthritis Rheum.* 2004;50(3):725-733

#### Diagnostic Pearls

- "Full-house until proven otherwise" — always consider lupus nephritis first when full-house IF is seen
- C1q is particularly helpful — strong C1q staining strongly favors lupus over other immune complex diseases
- If full-house IF is seen in a male patient, evaluate carefully for lupus (males can have SLE, though less commonly)
- In a transplant biopsy, full-house IF may indicate recurrent lupus nephritis
- The presence of all five immunoreactants does not require equal intensity — the pattern is considered full-house if all five are positive at any intensity

### IF-03: C3 Dominant (With/Without Immune Complexes)

**Document Reference:** GDES-V4.2-PATH-IF-03

#### Definition

Immunofluorescence pattern showing C3 as the dominant or sole immunoreactant in glomerular deposits, with C3 intensity at least 2 orders of magnitude greater than any immunoglobulin (IgG, IgA, IgM). This finding indicates activation of the complement system, typically via the alternative or lectin pathways, and is the hallmark of C3 glomerulopathy (C3G) when immune complex deposits are absent or minimal.

#### Histological Appearance

**Two categories:**

1. **C3 glomerulopathy (C3G) — immune complex poor:**
   - C3 dominant with trace or absent immunoglobulins (IgG, IgA, IgM all ≤1+ if present)
   - Two subtypes by EM:
     - **Dense Deposit Disease (DDD)**: Dense, ribbon-like intramembranous deposits on EM
     - **C3 Glomerulonephritis (C3GN)**: Subendothelial, mesangial, and/or subepithelial electron-dense deposits
   - LM may show MPGN pattern, mesangial proliferative pattern, or diffuse proliferative GN

2. **C3 glomerulopathy — immune complex associated:**
   - C3 dominant but with trace-to-mild immunoglobulin co-deposits
   - May indicate complement activation superimposed on immune complex disease
   - Requires EM to characterize deposit location

- **Granularity**: Granular deposits in mesangial and/or capillary wall distribution
- **Distribution**: May be mesangial only, capillary wall only, or both
- **Intensity**: C3 typically 2+ to 4+; immunoglobulins <2+ if present
- **Associated features**: C3 may be accompanied by C3c and C3d (split products); properdin and factor H may be positive

#### Clinical Significance

C3-dominant deposits without significant immunoglobulins indicate dysregulation of the alternative complement pathway. This finding should trigger a comprehensive complement evaluation including serum C3, C4, factor H, factor I, anti-factor H antibodies, and genetic testing for complement gene mutations. C3 glomerulopathy is a relatively rare but potentially aggressive disease that may progress rapidly to ESKD.

#### Associated Diseases

| Disease ID | Frequency | Diagnostic Significance |
|---|---|---|
| `c3` | Constant (>95%) | Pathognomonic — defining IF feature of C3 glomerulopathy |
| `denseDepositDisease` | Constant (>95%) | Pathognomonic — C3-dominant with dense intramembranous deposits on EM |
| `irgn` | Common (20-50%) | Non-specific — C3 may dominate in resolving post-infectious GN |
| `membranous` | Occasional (5-20%) | Non-specific — C3 may be co-dominant in membranous nephropathy |
| `cryoglobulinemic` | Occasional (5-20%) | Non-specific — C3 may be prominent in cryoglobulinemic GN |

#### Prognostic Value

- C3GN generally carries a better prognosis than DDD, though both can be aggressive
- DDD: ~50% progress to ESKD within 10 years
- C3GN: Variable outcome; some cases respond to treatment, others progress
- Presence of monoclonal immunoglobulin + C3: Consider monoclonal gammopathy-associated C3 glomerulopathy

#### Treatment Implications

- Complement inhibition: Eculizumab (terminal complement inhibitor); newer agents (iptacopan, ravulizumab) targeting proximal complement
- MMF + corticosteroids for inflammatory C3G
- Rituximab for monoclonal gammopathy-associated C3G
- Plasma infusion for factor H deficiency
- Underlying monoclonal gammopathy: Hematologic treatment (bortezomib, daratumumab)

#### Guideline References

1. Cook HT, Pickering MC. Histopathology of MPGN and C3 glomerulopathies. *Nat Rev Nephrol.* 2015;11(1):29-38
2. Sethi S, et al. C3 glomerulopathy: clinicopathologic features and spectrum of disease. *Clin J Am Soc Nephrol.* 2019;14(1):24-35
3. Pickering MC, et al. C3 glomerulopathy: consensus report. *Kidney Int.* 2013;84(6):1079-1089

#### Diagnostic Pearls

- C3 must be clearly dominant — if IgG > 2+ or approximately equal to C3, reconsider immune complex GN
- Distinguished from post-infectious GN (which typically has C3 + immunoglobulins) by the absence of significant immunoglobulin deposits
- In transplant biopsies, C3-dominant deposits may represent recurrent C3G or de novo disease
- Always correlate with EM — C3GN and DDD are distinguished by the pattern of electron-dense deposits (subendothelial/mesangial vs intramembranous)
- Complement gene testing is essential for all patients with C3-dominant GN to guide treatment and family screening

### IF-04: C1q Deposits

**Document Reference:** GDES-V4.2-PATH-IF-04

#### Definition

Immunofluorescence staining for complement component C1q within glomerular deposits. C1q is the first component of the classical complement pathway and its presence in glomerular deposits indicates activation of the classical pathway, most commonly by immune complexes containing IgG or IgM. C1q is most characteristically seen in lupus nephritis but can occur in other immune complex diseases.

#### Histological Appearance

- **Pattern**: Granular deposits in mesangial and/or capillary wall distribution, similar to other immune complex deposits
- **Intensity**: Variable, from trace to 4+; strong C1q (>2+) is particularly associated with lupus nephritis
- **Distribution**: Mesangial (Class II), capillary wall (Class III/IV), or both
- **Associated immunoreactants**: Typically co-stains with IgG, IgA, IgM, C3 (full-house pattern)
- **"C1q nephropathy"**: Rare entity with isolated C1q deposits and mesangial proliferation; relationship to lupus is debated
- **Differential**: Weak/trace C1q may be non-specific; strong C1q (>2+) with other immunoreactants strongly favors lupus

#### Clinical Significance

Strong C1q staining is one of the most characteristic features of lupus nephritis and has high specificity for the diagnosis. Its presence in the context of other immunoreactant deposits (full-house pattern) is virtually diagnostic of lupus nephritis. C1q activation via the classical pathway distinguishes lupus from other complement-mediated diseases (e.g., C3 glomerulopathy, which involves the alternative pathway).

#### Associated Diseases

| Disease ID | Frequency | Diagnostic Significance |
|---|---|---|
| `lupus` | Constant (>95%) | Pathognomonic — strong C1q is a hallmark of lupus nephritis |
| `cryoglobulinemic` | Occasional (5-20%) | Supportive — classical pathway activation by cryoglobulin immune complexes |
| `drugInducedGn` | Rare (<5%) | Non-specific — rare reports of drug-induced GN with C1q |

#### Prognostic Value

- Strong C1q (>2+) in lupus nephritis correlates with active disease
- C1q nephropathy: May be a forme fruste of lupus; long-term outcome uncertain
- Absence of C1q in a biopsy suspected to be lupus: Consider alternative diagnoses or early/mild disease

#### Treatment Implications

- C1q-positive lupus nephritis: Treat per ISN/RPS classification
- C1q nephropathy: Corticosteroids for proteinuria; monitor for evolution to overt lupus

#### Guideline References

1. Weening JJ, et al. The classification of glomerulonephritis in systemic lupus erythematosus revisited. *J Am Soc Nephrol.* 2004;15(2):241-250
2. Jennette JC, et al. C1q nephropathy. *Am J Kidney Dis.* 1993;22(4):553-560
3. Austin HA, et al. Renal biopsy in lupus nephritis. *Arthritis Rheum.* 2004;50(3):725-733

#### Diagnostic Pearls

- Strong C1q (>2+) is highly specific for lupus nephritis — consider lupus until proven otherwise
- If C1q is present with full-house pattern (IgG, IgA, IgM, C3), lupus nephritis is the diagnosis until excluded
- Weak/trace C1q may be non-specific and should not be over-interpreted
- C1q nephropathy with isolated C1q deposits is controversial — close follow-up is recommended
- In transplant biopsies, C1q deposits may indicate recurrent lupus nephritis

### IF-05: Linear IgG along GBM

**Document Reference:** GDES-V4.2-PATH-IF-05

#### Definition

Smooth, continuous, linear immunofluorescence staining for IgG along the glomerular basement membrane (GBM), in a ribbon-like pattern that outlines the entire GBM contour. This finding is diagnostic of anti-GBM disease (Goodpasture syndrome when pulmonary hemorrhage is present) and represents autoantibodies directed against the alpha-3 chain of type IV collagen in the GBM.

#### Histological Appearance

- **Pattern**: Smooth, unbroken, linear staining of IgG along the GBM — as if someone drew a continuous line along the GBM
- **Distribution**: Global (all glomeruli) and circumferential (entire GBM of each glomerulus)
- **Intensity**: Typically 2+ to 4+; uniformly distributed along the GBM
- **Associated immunoreactants**: IgG is typically the sole immunoreactant; C3 may be weakly positive along the GBM; IgA, IgM, and C1q are typically negative
- **Kappa/lambda**: Polyclonal (though anti-GBM antibodies are typically IgG1 and IgG3 subclasses)
- **Distinction from linear nonspecific staining**: True anti-GBM linear IgG is smooth and uniform; nonspecific linear staining may be weak, irregular, or patchy
- **Distinction from diabetic nephropathy linear IgG**: In diabetic nephropathy, weak linear IgG along the GBM may be seen due to nonspecific trapping — it is typically weak (trace to 1+) and may be accompanied by other immunoreactants

#### Clinical Significance

Linear IgG along the GBM is the diagnostic hallmark of anti-GBM disease, a severe and potentially fatal autoimmune disease that causes rapidly progressive glomerulonephritis and, in ~50% of cases, diffuse alveolar hemorrhage (pulmonary-renal syndrome). Immediate treatment with plasma exchange, corticosteroids, and cyclophosphamide is required. The finding mandates urgent clinical evaluation for pulmonary hemorrhage and measurement of serum anti-GBM antibodies.

#### Associated Diseases

| Disease ID | Frequency | Diagnostic Significance |
|---|---|---|
| `antiGbm` | Constant (>95%) | Pathognomonic — defining IF feature of anti-GBM disease |
| `anca` | Rare (<5%) | Supportive — dual-positive (anti-GBM + ANCA) disease exists in ~10-30% of anti-GBM cases |
| `lupus` | Rare (<5%) | Non-specific — weak linear IgG may rarely be seen in lupus nephritis |

#### Prognostic Value

- Strong, diffuse linear IgG with crescents and severe AKI: Poor prognosis if treatment is delayed
- Presence of linear IgG in oligoanuric patients: Very poor prognosis for renal recovery
- Linear IgG without crescents: Better prognosis; may respond to treatment
- Dual-positive (anti-GBM + ANCA): May have a slightly better renal prognosis than isolated anti-GBM disease

#### Treatment Implications

- Immediate: Plasma exchange (5-7 sessions over 14 days) + cyclophosphamide + corticosteroids
- Pulmonary hemorrhage: Urgent plasma exchange is life-saving
- Severe AKI with dialysis dependence at presentation: Very poor prognosis; some centers may withhold immunosuppression if bilateral renal biopsy shows >100% crescents and no normal glomeruli
- Long-term: Monitor for relapse (rare); screen for anti-GBM antibodies periodically

#### Guideline References

1. Savage CO, et al. Anti-GBM disease. *Lancet.* 2006;368(9548):1731-1739
2. KDIGO 2021 Clinical Practice Guideline for the Management of Glomerular Diseases. *Kidney Int.* 2021;100(4S):S1-S276
3. Walsh M, et al. Plasma exchange and glucocorticoids in severe ANCA-associated vasculitis. *N Engl J Med.* 2020;382(7):622-631

#### Diagnostic Pearls

- Linear IgG along the GBM is one of the most diagnostically specific IF patterns in renal pathology — it is virtually diagnostic of anti-GBM disease
- Weak linear IgG may be seen in diabetic nephropathy (nonspecific trapping) — always correlate with clinical context
- If linear IgG is seen with crescents and severe AKI, treat immediately — do not wait for serum anti-GBM antibody results
- Dual-positive disease (anti-GBM + ANCA) may have a better prognosis — the ANCA component may respond to treatment even if the anti-GBM component is severe
- In transplant biopsies, linear IgG may be non-specific — correlate with clinical history and donor-specific antibodies

### IF-06: Fibrinogen in Crescents

**Document Reference:** GDES-V4.2-PATH-IF-06

#### Definition

Positive immunofluorescence staining for fibrinogen (and/or fibrin) within crescentic lesions of glomeruli. This finding confirms that crescents contain fibrin, which has leaked through disrupted GBM into Bowman's space, indicating acute, active glomerular injury with capillary wall rupture.

#### Histological Appearance

- **Pattern**: Bright, somewhat amorphous staining for fibrinogen within the crescentic proliferation
- **Distribution**: Localized to crescents; may also be present in areas of fibrinoid necrosis
- **Intensity**: Typically 2+ to 4+ within the crescent
- **Associated immunoreactants**: Fibrinogen is typically the predominant IF finding within crescents; other immunoreactants (IgG, IgA, C3) may be present in the glomerular tuft depending on the underlying disease
- **Significance of fibrinogen positivity**: Confirms that the crescent is acute/active (contains fibrin) rather than chronic/fibrous (collagen-dominant)

#### Clinical Significance

Fibrinogen-positive crescents confirm the presence of acute, active glomerular injury with GBM disruption and fibrin leakage into Bowman's space. This finding indicates that the disease is in an active phase and may be amenable to immunosuppressive treatment. Fibrous crescents (fibrinogen-negative) represent old, irreversible scarring.

#### Associated Diseases

| Disease ID | Frequency | Diagnostic Significance |
|---|---|---|
| `anca` | Frequent (50-95%) | Strongly Suggestive — fibrinogen in pauci-immune crescents |
| `antiGbm` | Constant (>95%) | Strongly Suggestive — fibrinogen in crescents with linear IgG |
| `lupus` | Common (20-50%) | Supportive — fibrinogen in crescents of proliferative lupus nephritis |
| `iga` | Occasional (5-20%) | Supportive — fibrinogen in crescents of severe IgA nephropathy |
| `cryoglobulinemic` | Occasional (5-20%) | Supportive — fibrinogen in crescents of severe cryoglobulinemic GN |

#### Prognostic Value

- Fibrinogen-positive crescents indicate active disease that may respond to treatment
- Absence of fibrinogen within crescents suggests fibrous (chronic) crescents — less likely to respond to treatment
- The number of fibrinogen-positive crescents correlates with disease activity

#### Treatment Implications

- Fibrinogen-positive crescents: Urgent immunosuppression is warranted
- Treatment depends on the underlying disease (ANCA vasculitis, anti-GBM, lupus)

#### Guideline References

1. Jennette JC, et al. 2012 revised international consensus classification criteria for ANCA-associated vasculitis. *Arthritis Rheum.* 2013;65(12):3154-3159
2. Bajema IM, et al. Revision of the histopathologic classification of ANCA-associated GN. *J Am Soc Nephrol.* 2020;31(8):1789-1796

#### Diagnostic Pearls

- Fibrinogen staining within crescents is a simple but powerful marker of disease activity
- Always correlate fibrinogen positivity with the underlying IF pattern (pauci-immune vs immune complex vs linear IgG)
- Fibrous crescents will be fibrinogen-negative — this helps distinguish acute from chronic crescents
- Fibrinogen may also be positive in areas of fibrinoid necrosis outside of crescents

### IF-07: C4d in Peritubular Capillaries

**Document Reference:** GDES-V4.2-PATH-IF-07

#### Definition

Linear or circumferential immunofluorescence staining for C4d (a degradation product of complement component C4) within peritubular capillaries (PTCs), indicating activation of the complement classical pathway within the allograft microvasculature. This finding is a marker of antibody-mediated complement activation in the transplanted kidney and is a key diagnostic criterion for antibody-mediated rejection (AMR).

#### Histological Appearance

- **Pattern**: Linear staining of C4d along the walls of peritubular capillaries, outlining the capillary circumference
- **Distribution**: May be focal (in some areas of the cortex) or diffuse (in all areas of the cortex)
- **Intensity**: Graded as negative, trace, or positive (>0); C4d >0 is considered positive by Banff criteria
- **Staining method**: Both immunofluorescence and immunohistochemistry (IHC) are acceptable; IF is more sensitive
- **Artifact considerations**: Tubular basement membrane staining is non-specific; true C4d staining should be within peritubular capillary lumina, not in tubular basement membranes
- **Differential**: Non-specific peritubular capillary staining, artifact from tissue processing
- **Association with AMR**: C4d deposition in PTCs indicates complement activation by donor-specific antibodies (DSA) binding to donor HLA on peritubular capillary endothelium

#### Clinical Significance

C4d deposition in PTCs is one of the diagnostic hallmarks of antibody-mediated rejection in the transplanted kidney. It indicates that donor-specific antibodies are activating the complement cascade within the allograft, leading to endothelial injury and potentially graft dysfunction. The presence of C4d, combined with evidence of tissue injury (microvascular inflammation, transplant glomerulopathy) and circulating DSA, defines AMR by Banff criteria.

#### Associated Diseases

| Disease ID | Frequency | Diagnostic Significance |
|---|---|---|
| `antibodyMediatedRejection` | Frequent (50-95%) | Strongly Suggestive — C4d in PTCs is a diagnostic criterion for AMR |
| `transplantGlomerulopathy` | Frequent (50-95%) | Strongly Suggestive — C4d + transplant glomerulopathy indicates chronic AMR |
| `lupus` | Rare (<5%) | Non-specific — C4d may be present in PTCs in lupus nephritis due to complement activation |

#### Prognostic Value

- Diffuse C4d deposition (>50% of PTCs): Stronger association with AMR and worse graft outcome
- Focal C4d deposition (<50% of PTCs): May indicate subclinical or early AMR
- C4d-negative AMR: Exists — C4d deposition is not required for the diagnosis of AMR; microvascular inflammation and DSA are also diagnostic
- C4d + DSA + tissue injury = active AMR (poor graft prognosis if untreated)
- C4d-negative AMR may have a better prognosis than C4d-positive AMR

#### Treatment Implications

- Acute AMR with C4d: Plasmapheresis + IVIG + rituximab; optimize immunosuppression
- Chronic AMR with C4d: Consider complement inhibition (eculizumab); adjust immunosuppression; evaluate for desensitization
- C4d-negative AMR: Treat similarly based on DSA and tissue injury

#### Guideline References

1. Loupy A, et al. The Banff 2019 Kidney Meeting Report. *Am J Transplant.* 2020;20(3):586-615
2. Haas M, et al. The Banff 2017 Kidney Meeting Report. *Am J Transplant.* 2018;18(2):293-307
3. Solez K, et al. Banff 07 classification of renal allograft pathology. *Am J Transplant.* 2008;8(4):753-760

#### Diagnostic Pearls

- C4d in PTCs should be interpreted with clinical context — the finding alone does not diagnose AMR without evidence of tissue injury and DSA
- C4d-negative AMR is increasingly recognized — absence of C4d does not exclude AMR
- Distinguish true PTC C4d from tubular basement membrane staining artifact
- In transplant biopsies with transplant glomerulopathy (double contours), always check C4d in PTCs
- C4d may also be positive in glomerular capillaries in AMR — report this as well

### IF-08: Kappa/Lambda Light Chain Restriction

**Document Reference:** GDES-V4.2-PATH-IF-08

#### Definition

Restriction of immunoglobulin light chain staining to kappa or lambda (monoclonal) rather than the normal polyclonal mixture of both kappa and lambda light chains. This finding indicates a monoclonal immunoglobulin process and raises concern for hematologic malignancy (multiple myeloma, lymphoma, CLL) or monoclonal gammopathy of renal significance (MGRS).

#### Histological Appearance

- **Normal (polyclonal)**: Both kappa and lambda light chains stain glomerular and tubular deposits; normal kappa:lambda ratio is approximately 2:1 to 3:1
- **Monoclonal (restricted)**: Only kappa OR lambda light chains stain the deposits; the other light chain is absent or significantly diminished
- **Pattern of staining**: May be glomerular (mesangial, capillary wall), tubular (cast formation), or both
- **Associated findings**:
  - Kappa restriction with C3-dominant deposits: Consider monoclonal gammopathy-associated C3 glomerulopathy
  - Lambda restriction with organized deposits: Consider amyloidosis (AL type)
  - Isolated light chain restriction without other immunoreactants: Consider light chain deposition disease (LCDD) or light chain proximal tubulopathy
- **Distinction from nonspecific**: Trace kappa or lambda staining in the absence of restriction is non-specific; true restriction requires clear predominance of one light chain over the other

#### Clinical Significance

Light chain restriction on renal biopsy mandates evaluation for an underlying monoclonal gammopathy. This may range from a clinically significant malignancy (myeloma, lymphoma) to a small B-cell clone producing a nephrotoxic monoclonal immunoglobulin (MGRS). Hematologic evaluation including serum protein electrophoresis, free light chain assay, urine protein electrophoresis, and bone marrow biopsy is essential. Treatment targets the underlying clone to prevent further renal damage.

#### Associated Diseases

| Disease ID | Frequency | Diagnostic Significance |
|---|---|---|
| `membranous` | Occasional (5-20%) | Supportive — membranous nephropathy may be associated with monoclonal gammopathy |
| `c3` | Occasional (5-20%) | Supportive — monoclonal gammopathy-associated C3 glomerulopathy |
| `fsgs` | Rare (<5%) | Non-specific — monoclonal immunoglobulin may cause FSGS-like lesions |
| `lupus` | Rare (<5%) | Non-specific — rarely, monoclonal gammopathy may coexist with lupus |

#### Prognostic Value

- Light chain restriction with progressive renal disease: Poor prognosis if underlying clone is not identified and treated
- MGRS-related GN: Renal prognosis depends on identifying and treating the clone; delay leads to irreversible damage
- AL amyloidosis with light chain restriction: Prognosis depends on organ involvement and hematologic response

#### Treatment Implications

- Identify underlying clone: Hematologic evaluation (SPEP, FLC, UPEP, bone marrow biopsy)
- Treat underlying malignancy: Chemotherapy targeting the clone (bortezomib, lenalidomide, rituximab)
- MGRS: Treat the clone even if it does not meet criteria for overt malignancy
- Consider renal transplant if clone is controlled and renal function continues to decline

#### Guideline References

1. Leung N, et al. Monoclonal gammopathy of renal significance. *Kidney Int.* 2019;95(6):1299-1311
2. Nasr SH, et al. Renal involvement in monoclonal gammopathy. *Kidney Int.* 2019;95(6):1312-1324
3. KDIGO 2021 Clinical Practice Guideline for the Management of Glomerular Diseases. *Kidney Int.* 2021;100(4S):S1-S276

#### Diagnostic Pearls

- Light chain restriction on renal biopsy is a critical finding that should never be ignored — always recommend hematologic evaluation
- The kappa:lambda ratio should be approximately 2-3:1; a ratio outside this range suggests restriction
- In membranous nephropathy with light chain restriction, PLA2R may be negative — consider monoclonal gammopathy-associated membranous nephropathy
- Light chain restriction with C3-dominant deposits: Consider complement-activating monoclonal immunoglobulin
- Always correlate with serum and urine light chain levels to confirm the monoclonal process

### EM-01: Foot Process Effacement (Diffuse vs Focal)

**Document Reference:** GDES-V4.2-PATH-EM-01

#### Definition

Flattening, broadening, and fusion of podocyte foot processes, resulting in loss of the normal interdigitating foot process architecture along the urinary surface of the glomerular basement membrane (GBM). Foot process effacement is a non-specific ultrastructural finding that reflects podocyte injury or activation and is the hallmark of podocytopathies such as minimal change disease and focal segmental glomerulosclerosis.

#### Histological Appearance

- **Normal foot processes**: Thin (200-400 nm width), finger-like projections from podocyte cell bodies that interdigitate along the GBM, separated by slit diaphragms
- **Effaced foot processes**: Wide, flattened, plate-like extensions that cover the GBM surface; slit diaphragms are lost or distorted
- **Diffuse effacement**: >80% of the GBM surface shows effaced foot processes; typical of primary FSGS and MCD
- **Focal effacement**: <80% of the GBM surface shows effacement; may indicate early or resolving disease, or secondary FSGS
- **Associated findings**: Podocyte microvillus transformation (see EM-14), protein reabsorption droplets in tubular epithelial cells, electron-dense deposits (if immune complex disease), GBM changes
- **Quantification**: Percentage of GBM with effaced foot processes is estimated by the pathologist; >80% is considered "diffuse" and is more suggestive of primary podocytopathy
- **Distinction from sectioning artifact**: Tangential sectioning through podocytes may mimic effacement; true effacement shows plate-like foot processes covering the GBM

#### Clinical Significance

Foot process effacement correlates directly with proteinuria — loss of the slit diaphragm filtration barrier allows protein to leak into the urinary space. Diffuse foot process effacement (>80%) without other ultrastructural abnormalities is the hallmark of minimal change disease. In FSGS, foot process effacement is typically diffuse even in areas without sclerosis, supporting the concept that FSGS is a diffuse podocyte disease even when segmental sclerosis is focal. The degree of foot process effacement correlates with the severity of nephrotic syndrome.

#### Associated Diseases

| Disease ID | Frequency | Diagnostic Significance |
|---|---|---|
| `mcd` | Constant (>95%) | Pathognomonic — diffuse foot process effacement (>80%) with normal GBM is diagnostic of MCD |
| `fsgs` | Constant (>95%) | Strongly Suggestive — diffuse foot process effacement with segmental sclerosis |
| `membranous` | Constant (>95%) | Supportive — foot process effacement overlying subepithelial deposits |
| `lupus` | Frequent (50-95%) | Non-specific — variable foot process effacement in lupus nephritis |
| `iga` | Frequent (50-95%) | Non-specific — variable foot process effacement |
| `diabeticNephropathy` | Frequent (50-95%) | Non-specific — foot process effacement in advanced DN |
| `hivan` | Constant (>95%) | Strongly Suggestive — diffuse effacement with podocyte microvillus transformation |
| `drugInducedGn` | Occasional (5-20%) | Non-specific — foot process effacement may be seen in drug-induced GN |

#### Prognostic Value

- Diffuse foot process effacement (>80%) with normal GBM: Excellent prognosis if MCD (corticosteroid-responsive)
- Diffuse foot process effacement with segmental sclerosis: Primary FSGS — variable prognosis depending on variant and treatment response
- Focal foot process effacement (<80%): May indicate secondary FSGS (obesity, reduced nephron mass, genetic) — prognosis depends on underlying cause
- Foot process effacement resolving on follow-up biopsy: Indicates treatment response

#### Treatment Implications

- MCD with diffuse effacement: Corticosteroids (excellent response rate)
- Primary FSGS with diffuse effacement: Corticosteroids; CNI for steroid-resistant cases
- Secondary FSGS with focal effacement: Treat underlying cause; immunosuppression generally not indicated
- Foot process effacement with subepithelial deposits: Consider membranous nephropathy — PLA2R testing

#### Guideline References

1. D'Agati VD, et al. Pathologic classification of focal segmental glomerulosclerosis. *J Am Soc Nephrol.* 2014;25(5):908-917
2. KDIGO 2021 Clinical Practice Guideline for the Management of Glomerular Diseases. *Kidney Int.* 2021;100(4S):S1-S276
3. Barisoni L, et al. The Columbia classification of FSGS. *J Am Soc Nephrol.* 2004;15(9):2450-2460

#### Diagnostic Pearls

- Diffuse foot process effacement (>80%) with normal LM and IF is diagnostic of MCD — it is the only ultrastructural abnormality
- In FSGS, foot process effacement is typically diffuse even in non-sclerotic segments — this reflects the global nature of podocyte injury despite focal scarring
- Focal foot process effacement in an adult with FSGS should prompt evaluation for secondary causes (obesity, reduced nephron mass, genetic mutations)
- If foot process effacement is accompanied by electron-dense deposits, consider immune complex disease (membranous, lupus, IgA)
- In pediatric nephrotic syndrome, the combination of MCD on LM + diffuse effacement on EM is the classic presentation

### EM-02: Subepithelial Electron-Dense Deposits

**Document Reference:** GDES-V4.2-PATH-EM-02

#### Definition

Electron-dense (dark) deposits located between the glomerular basement membrane (GBM) and the podocyte foot processes, on the urinary side of the GBM. These deposits represent immune complexes that have deposited from the circulating blood or have formed in situ. The subepithelial location is characteristic of membranous nephropathy and several other immune complex diseases.

#### Histological Appearance

- **Location**: Between the outer aspect of the GBM and the podocyte foot processes (urinary side of the GBM)
- **Appearance**: Dark (electron-dense), rounded or dome-shaped deposits that protrude into the urinary space
- **Size and shape**: Variable — may be small and scattered (early membranous) or large and confluent (advanced membranous)
- **GBM reaction**: In membranous nephropathy, the GBM grows around and eventually envelops the deposits, forming spike-like projections (Stage II-III of Ehrenreich-Churg classification)
- **Ehrenreich-Churg stages of membranous nephropathy**:
  - Stage I: Small subepithelial deposits with no GBM reaction; may be missed by LM
  - Stage II: GBM spike formation around deposits (visible on silver stain)
  - Stage III: GBM material envelops deposits (intramembranous deposits)
  - Stage IV: Deposits are resorbed, leaving irregular GBM with lucent areas
  - Stage V: Complete resolution with normal-appearing GBM
- **Distribution**: May be focal (<50% of glomeruli) or diffuse (≥50%); may be segmental or global within affected glomeruli
- **Associated features**: Foot process effacement overlying deposits; may be accompanied by mesangial or subendothelial deposits in lupus nephritis

#### Clinical Significance

Subepithelial deposits are the defining ultrastructural feature of membranous nephropathy, one of the most common causes of nephrotic syndrome in adults. The deposits represent autoantibodies (most commonly anti-PLA2R or anti-THSD7A) that bind to antigens on the podocyte surface, forming in situ immune complexes. The subepithelial location makes these deposits inaccessible to conventional immunosuppression, which may explain the chronic nature of membranous nephropathy. In lupus nephritis, subepithelial deposits define Class V (membranous) lupus nephritis.

#### Associated Diseases

| Disease ID | Frequency | Diagnostic Significance |
|---|---|---|
| `membranous` | Constant (>95%) | Pathognomonic — subepithelial deposits are the defining feature of membranous nephropathy |
| `lupus` | Frequent (50-95%) | Strongly Suggestive — subepithelial deposits define Class V lupus nephritis |
| `irgn` | Occasional (5-20%) | Supportive — subepithelial "humps" in post-infectious GN (larger, dome-shaped) |
| `c3` | Occasional (5-20%) | Supportive — subepithelial deposits in C3GN or DDD |
| `drugInducedGn` | Occasional (5-20%) | Non-specific — subepithelial deposits in drug-induced membranous-like GN |

#### Prognostic Value

- Extent of subepithelial deposits correlates with proteinuria severity
- Stage of GBM reaction (Ehrenreich-Churg) correlates with chronicity — Stage IV indicates longstanding disease
- PLA2R-positive membranous nephropathy carries a higher risk of progression to ESKD than PLA2R-negative
- Subepithelial deposits in lupus nephritis (Class V) carry variable prognosis depending on extent and response to treatment

#### Treatment Implications

- Primary membranous nephropathy: Anti-CD20 (rituximab) as first-line; calcineurin inhibitors for second-line; corticosteroids for mild cases
- Lupus Class V: ACE inhibitors/ARBs for proteinuria; immunosuppression (MMF, CNIs) for nephrotic-range proteinuria
- Post-infectious GN with subepithelial humps: Usually self-limited; supportive care

#### Guideline References

1. KDIGO 2021 Clinical Practice Guideline for the Management of Glomerular Diseases. *Kidney Int.* 2021;100(4S):S1-S276
2. Ronco P, Debiec H. Pathophysiological advances in membranous nephropathy. *Nat Rev Nephrol.* 2021;17(6):362-375
3. Ehrenreich T, Churg J. Pathology of membranous nephropathy. *Pathol Annu.* 1968;3:145-186

#### Diagnostic Pearls

- Small subepithelial deposits (Stage I membranous) may be missed on LM — EM is essential for diagnosis
- Large, dome-shaped subepithelial deposits with neutrophils ("humps") suggest post-infectious GN rather than membranous nephropathy
- In lupus nephritis, subepithelial deposits without subendothelial deposits define Class V; with subendothelial deposits, it is Class V superimposed on Class III/IV
- PLA2R and THSD7A antibody testing should be performed on all membranous nephropathy cases — positive results confirm primary disease
- In pediatric membranous nephropathy, consider secondary causes (hepatitis B, lupus) more strongly than in adults

### EM-03: Subendothelial Electron-Dense Deposits

**Document Reference:** GDES-V4.2-PATH-EM-03

#### Definition

Electron-dense (dark) deposits located between the endothelium and the glomerular basement membrane (GBM), on the vascular side of the GBM. These deposits represent immune complexes that have deposited from the circulating blood within the glomerular capillary wall. The subendothelial location is characteristic of active proliferative glomerulonephritis, particularly lupus nephritis Classes III and IV.

#### Histological Appearance

- **Location**: Between the endothelial cell and the inner aspect of the GBM (vascular side)
- **Appearance**: Dark (electron-dense), often large and confluent deposits that widen the subendothelial space
- **GBM reaction**: The GBM may appear thickened due to the deposits; mesangio-interposition may be present
- **Distribution**: May be focal (<50% of glomeruli) or diffuse (≥50%); often segmental or global within affected glomeruli
- **Associated features**:
  - Mesangial deposits are often present concurrently
  - GBM duplication/double contours may develop as a reparative response
  - Endocapillary hypercellularity and neutrophils may be present
  - Fibrinoid necrosis may be present in severe cases
- **"Wire loop" lesions**: Heavy subendothelial deposits visible on H&E as thickened, eosinophilic capillary walls
- **Distinction from subepithelial deposits**: Subendothelial deposits are on the inner (vascular) side of the GBM; subepithelial deposits are on the outer (urinary) side

#### Clinical Significance

Subendothelial deposits are the defining ultrastructural feature of active proliferative lupus nephritis (Classes III and IV). They represent circulating immune complexes that have deposited within the capillary wall, triggering local inflammation, complement activation, and endocapillary proliferation. The presence and extent of subendothelial deposits correlate with disease activity and guide treatment decisions.

#### Associated Diseases

| Disease ID | Frequency | Diagnostic Significance |
|---|---|---|
| `lupus` | Frequent (50-95%) | Pathognomonic — subendothelial deposits define Classes III and IV lupus nephritis |
| `mpgn` | Frequent (50-95%) | Strongly Suggestive — subendothelial deposits in immune complex-mediated MPGN |
| `cryoglobulinemic` | Frequent (50-95%) | Strongly Suggestive — subendothelial deposits with organized structures |
| `irgn` | Occasional (5-20%) | Supportive — subendothelial deposits may be present in post-infectious GN |
| `drugInducedGn` | Occasional (5-20%) | Non-specific — subendothelial deposits in drug-induced immune complex GN |
| `iga` | Rare (<5%) | Non-specific — rare subendothelial deposits in severe IgA nephropathy |

#### Prognostic Value

- Extent of subendothelial deposits correlates with disease activity in lupus nephritis
- Diffuse subendothelial deposits indicate active, widespread disease requiring prompt treatment
- Subendothelial deposits with necrosis and crescents: Severe disease with risk of rapid progression
- Resolution of subendothelial deposits on follow-up biopsy indicates treatment response

#### Treatment Implications

- Lupus nephritis with subendothelial deposits (Classes III/IV): Induction with MMF or cyclophosphamide + corticosteroids; maintenance with MMF
- Immune complex MPGN: Treat underlying cause (HCV, autoimmune disease)
- Cryoglobulinemic GN: Antiviral therapy + rituximab for severe cases

#### Guideline References

1. Weening JJ, et al. The classification of glomerulonephritis in systemic lupus erythematosus revisited. *J Am Soc Nephrol.* 2004;15(2):241-250
2. KDIGO 2021 Clinical Practice Guideline for the Management of Glomerular Diseases. *Kidney Int.* 2021;100(4S):S1-S276
3. Austin HA, et al. Renal biopsy in lupus nephritis. *Arthritis Rheum.* 2004;50(3):725-733

#### Diagnostic Pearls

- Subendothelial deposits are the ultrastructural correlate of "wire loop" lesions seen on LM in lupus nephritis
- When subendothelial deposits are present with mesangial deposits, consider lupus nephritis (Classes III/IV superimposed on Class II)
- In MPGN pattern, subendothelial deposits with immunoglobulin deposits on IF suggest immune complex-mediated disease
- Subendothelial deposits without significant immunoglobulins on IF may indicate organized deposits (cryoglobulins, fibrillary GN)
- The presence of subendothelial deposits in a transplant biopsy may indicate recurrent lupus nephritis

### EM-04: Mesangial Electron-Dense Deposits

**Document Reference:** GDES-V4.2-PATH-EM-04

#### Definition

Electron-dense (dark) deposits located within the mesangial matrix of glomeruli. These deposits represent immune complexes that have deposited in the mesangium, typically from circulating immune complexes that are trapped by mesangial cells or from local immune complex formation. Mesangial deposits are the earliest and most common location of immune complex deposition in many glomerular diseases.

#### Histological Appearance

- **Location**: Within the mesangial matrix, between mesangial cell processes
- **Appearance**: Dark (electron-dense), often rounded or irregular deposits within the mesangium
- **Size and shape**: Variable — may be small and scattered or large and confluent
- **Distribution**: May be focal (<50% of glomeruli) or diffuse (≥50%); may involve some or all mesangial areas within a glomerulus
- **Associated features**:
  - Mesangial matrix expansion and hypercellularity may be present
  - May be accompanied by capillary wall deposits (subendothelial or subepithelial) depending on the disease
  - Foot process effacement may be present if proteinuria is significant
- **Distinction from normal mesangial matrix**: Normal mesangial matrix is moderately electron-dense but relatively homogeneous; immune complex deposits are darker (more electron-dense) and more discrete

#### Clinical Significance

Mesangial deposits are the most common location of immune complex deposition and are seen in numerous glomerular diseases. In IgA nephropathy, mesangial deposits of IgA are the defining ultrastructural feature. In lupus nephritis, mesangial deposits define Class II (mesangial proliferative) lupus nephritis. The presence of mesangial deposits alone (without capillary wall deposits) generally indicates milder disease than deposits in other locations.

#### Associated Diseases

| Disease ID | Frequency | Diagnostic Significance |
|---|---|---|
| `iga` | Constant (>95%) | Pathognomonic — mesangial IgA deposits are the defining feature of IgA nephropathy |
| `lupus` | Frequent (50-95%) | Strongly Suggestive — mesangial deposits define Class II lupus nephritis |
| `irgn` | Frequent (50-95%) | Supportive — mesangial deposits in post-infectious GN |
| `c3` | Common (20-50%) | Supportive — mesangial deposits in C3 glomerulopathy |
| `membranous` | Occasional (5-20%) | Non-specific — mesangial deposits may accompany subepithelial deposits |
| `drugInducedGn` | Occasional (5-20%) | Non-specific — mesangial deposits in drug-induced GN |

#### Prognostic Value

- Isolated mesangial deposits (without capillary wall deposits): Generally milder disease with better prognosis
- Mesangial deposits with mesangial hypercellularity on LM: Active disease that may respond to treatment
- Increasing mesangial deposits on follow-up biopsy: Suggests persistent or worsening immune complex deposition
- In IgA nephropathy, the extent of mesangial deposits correlates with the degree of mesangial proliferation

#### Treatment Implications

- IgA nephropathy with mesangial deposits: SGLT2 inhibitors; immunosuppression for persistent proteinuria
- Lupus Class II with mesangial deposits: Generally supportive; immunosuppression only for significant proteinuria
- Post-infectious GN with mesangial deposits: Usually self-limited

#### Guideline References

1. Working Group of the International IgA Nephropathy Network. The Oxford Classification of IgA nephropathy. *Kidney Int.* 2009;76(5):534-546
2. Weening JJ, et al. The classification of glomerulonephritis in systemic lupus erythematosus revisited. *J Am Soc Nephrol.* 2004;15(2):241-250
3. KDIGO 2021 Clinical Practice Guideline for the Management of Glomerular Diseases. *Kidney Int.* 2021;100(4S):S1-S276

#### Diagnostic Pearls

- Mesangial deposits are the most common location of immune complex deposition — they are seen in virtually all immune complex-mediated glomerular diseases
- In IgA nephropathy, mesangial deposits of IgA on EM correlate with IgA-dominant deposits on IF
- Isolated mesangial deposits without capillary wall deposits indicate mild disease (e.g., IgA nephropathy, Class II lupus)
- If mesangial deposits are present with capillary wall deposits, the more severe location determines the classification
- In transplant biopsies, mesangial deposits may indicate recurrent IgA nephropathy or lupus nephritis

### EM-05: Intramembranous Electron-Dense Deposits

**Document Reference:** GDES-V4.2-PATH-EM-05

#### Definition

Electron-dense (dark) deposits located within the substance of the glomerular basement membrane (GBM) itself, embedded within the lamina densa. This location is characteristic of dense deposit disease (DDD), a subtype of C3 glomerulopathy, and represents complement-mediated injury to the GBM with dense, ribbon-like deposits of complement components and altered basement membrane material.

#### Histological Appearance

- **Location**: Within the lamina densa of the GBM, appearing as continuous or interrupted ribbon-like deposits
- **Appearance**: Very dark (extremely electron-dense), ribbon-like or band-like deposits that follow the contour of the GBM
- **Distribution**: May be segmental or global; continuous or interrupted along the GBM
- **Associated features**:
  - GBM may be thickened or have irregular contours
  - Subendothelial or mesangial deposits may be present concurrently
  - Foot process effacement is typically present
  - Mesangial matrix expansion and interposition may be present
- **Distinct from Stage III membranous nephropathy**: In Stage III membranous, intramembranous deposits result from GBM enveloping subepithelial deposits and appear as discrete rounded deposits within a thickened GBM; in DDD, intramembranous deposits are continuous and ribbon-like

#### Clinical Significance

Intramembranous electron-dense deposits are the defining ultrastructural feature of dense deposit disease (DDD), a subtype of C3 glomerulopathy. DDD is caused by dysregulation of the alternative complement pathway, leading to complement-mediated injury to the GBM. The finding of intramembranous deposits on EM, combined with C3-dominant deposits on IF, confirms the diagnosis of DDD. DDD is a relatively rare but potentially aggressive disease that may progress rapidly to ESKD.

#### Associated Diseases

| Disease ID | Frequency | Diagnostic Significance |
|---|---|---|
| `denseDepositDisease` | Constant (>95%) | Pathognomonic — intramembranous ribbon-like deposits are the defining feature of DDD |
| `c3` | Frequent (50-95%) | Strongly Suggestive — C3 glomerulopathy with intramembranous deposits |

#### Prognostic Value

- DDD carries a poor prognosis for renal survival (~50% ESKD at 10 years)
- Presence of intramembranous deposits indicates advanced complement-mediated GBM injury
- C3 glomerulopathy with intramembranous deposits may progress more rapidly than C3GN with subendothelial/mesangial deposits
- C5b-9 (membrane attack complex) deposition within the GBM correlates with disease activity

#### Treatment Implications

- Complement inhibition: Eculizumab, iptacopan, or ravulizumab
- MMF + corticosteroids for inflammatory C3G
- Plasma infusion for factor H deficiency
- Hematologic evaluation for monoclonal gammopathy-associated C3G

#### Guideline References

1. Cook HT, Pickering MC. Histopathology of MPGN and C3 glomerulopathies. *Nat Rev Nephrol.* 2015;11(1):29-38
2. Sethi S, et al. C3 glomerulopathy: clinicopathologic features and spectrum of disease. *Clin J Am Soc Nephrol.* 2019;14(1):24-35
3. Pickering MC, et al. C3 glomerulopathy: consensus report. *Kidney Int.* 2013;84(6):1079-1089

#### Diagnostic Pearls

- Intramembranous ribbon-like deposits on EM are essentially diagnostic of dense deposit disease when combined with C3-dominant IF
- Distinguish from Stage III membranous nephropathy (discrete rounded deposits within thickened GBM) and from GBM splitting in Alport syndrome
- In transplant biopsies, intramembranous deposits may indicate recurrent DDD
- Complement gene testing is essential — many DDD patients have identified complement pathway mutations
- The C3d degradation product may be positive on IF — this reflects ongoing complement activation

### EM-06: GBM Thinning

**Document Reference:** GDES-V4.2-PATH-EM-06

#### Definition

Reduction in the thickness of the glomerular basement membrane (GBM) below the normal range for age and sex. Normal adult male GBM thickness is approximately 300-350 nm; normal adult female GBM thickness is approximately 270-320 nm. GBM thinning is the defining ultrastructural feature of thin basement membrane lesion (thin basement membrane nephropathy) and may also be seen in early Alport syndrome, minimal change disease, and other conditions.

#### Histological Appearance

- **GBM measurement**: Electron microscopy measurement of GBM width at multiple points along peripheral capillary loops
- **Normal ranges**:
  - Adult male: 300-350 nm (mean ~325 nm)
  - Adult female: 270-320 nm (mean ~295 nm)
  - Children: May be thinner than adults
- **GBM thinning**: GBM width <250 nm is generally considered abnormally thin
- **Distribution**: May be diffuse (all glomeruli) or focal; may be segmental or global within affected capillary loops
- **Associated features**:
  - Foot process effacement may be present if proteinuria is significant
  - GBM may appear uniformly thin or may show irregular thinning
  - No splitting, lamellation, or electron-dense deposits (distinguishes from Alport syndrome and immune complex diseases)
- **Measurement technique**: The pathologist measures GBM width at multiple points and calculates the mean; <250 nm is considered thin

#### Clinical Significance

GBM thinning is the defining feature of thin basement membrane lesion (thin basement membrane nephropathy), a common inherited condition affecting type IV collagen. It is the most common cause of persistent microscopic hematuria in adults. While typically benign, GBM thinning may be a carrier state for Alport syndrome (COL4A3/COL4A4 mutations) and rarely may progress to CKD. In children, GBM thinning should raise concern for early Alport syndrome.

#### Associated Diseases

| Disease ID | Frequency | Diagnostic Significance |
|---|---|---|
| `thinBasementMembrane` | Constant (>95%) | Pathognomonic — GBM thinning <250 nm is the defining feature |
| `alport` | Common (20-50%) | Supportive — GBM thinning may be seen in early or carrier Alport syndrome |
| `mcd` | Occasional (5-20%) | Non-specific — GBM may be thin in minimal change disease |
| `fsgs` | Occasional (5-20%) | Non-specific — GBM thinning may be seen in early FSGS |

#### Prognostic Value

- Thin basement membrane lesion: Generally benign prognosis; most patients maintain normal renal function
- GBM thinning as a carrier state for Alport syndrome: Carriers may develop progressive CKD (rare)
- GBM thinning with proteinuria: May indicate coexisting disease or progression to FSGS
- In children, GBM thinning with family history of ESKD: Concern for Alport syndrome

#### Treatment Implications

- Thin basement membrane lesion: Reassurance; ACE inhibition if proteinuria develops; monitoring for progression
- Alport syndrome carriers: Monitor for proteinuria and GFR decline; ACE inhibition if proteinuria develops
- All patients with GBM thinning: Avoid nephrotoxins; monitor blood pressure and renal function

#### Guideline References

1. Kashtan CE, et al. Alport syndrome. *Nat Rev Dis Primers.* 2018;4(1):23
2. Scolari F, et al. Thin basement membrane nephropathy. *Clin J Am Soc Nephrol.* 2007;2(5):981-990
3. KDIGO 2021 Clinical Practice Guideline for the Management of Glomerular Diseases. *Kidney Int.* 2021;100(4S):S1-S276

#### Diagnostic Pearls

- GBM thinning should be measured by electron microscopy — it cannot be reliably assessed by light microscopy
- A GBM width <250 nm is considered abnormally thin in adults
- In a young patient with hematuria and GBM thinning, consider genetic testing for COL4A3/A4/A5 mutations
- GBM thinning with hematuria and proteinuria may indicate a more severe phenotype or coexisting disease
- Distinguish GBM thinning from GBM lamellation/splitting (Alport syndrome) — thinning alone without splitting suggests thin basement membrane lesion

### EM-07: GBM Lamellation/Splitting

**Document Reference:** GDES-V4.2-PATH-EM-07

#### Definition

Irregular thickening and splitting of the glomerular basement membrane (GBM) with a laminated, "basket-weave" or "moth-eaten" appearance, resulting from disruption and disorganization of the type IV collagen network within the GBM. This finding is the defining ultrastructural feature of Alport syndrome (hereditary nephritis) and results from mutations in genes encoding type IV collagen chains (COL4A3, COL4A4, COL4A5).

#### Histological Appearance

- **GBM architecture**: The GBM appears irregularly thickened with alternating areas of thickening and thinning
- **Splitting/lamellation**: The GBM shows a laminated, "basket-weave" appearance with splitting of the lamina densa into multiple thin layers
- **Electron-lucent areas**: Interposed between the lamellated layers, giving a "moth-eaten" appearance
- **Distribution**: Typically diffuse (all glomeruli) and global (all capillary loops within a glomerulus)
- **Severity**: May be focal early in the disease; becomes diffuse with progression
- **Associated features**:
  - GBM thinning may be present in early or carrier states
  - Foot process effacement is typically present
  - Mesangial matrix expansion and global glomerulosclerosis may be present in advanced disease
- **Distinction from GBM duplication**: GBM splitting/lamellation in Alport syndrome involves the full thickness of the GBM and produces a "basket-weave" pattern; GBM duplication (double contours) from mesangio-interposition is subendothelial and produces a "tram-track" pattern

#### Clinical Significance

GBM lamellation/splitting is the defining ultrastructural feature of Alport syndrome, a hereditary nephropathy caused by mutations in type IV collagen genes. The finding of GBM splitting in a patient with hematuria, proteinuria, and a family history of CKD or ESKD is diagnostic. Alport syndrome is one of the most common inherited causes of ESKD in young adults. The GBM splitting reflects progressive disruption of the type IV collagen network, leading to GBM fragility, hematuria, and eventual glomerulosclerosis.

#### Associated Diseases

| Disease ID | Frequency | Diagnostic Significance |
|---|---|---|
| `alport` | Constant (>95%) | Pathognomonic — GBM splitting/lamellation is the defining feature of Alport syndrome |
| `thinBasementMembrane` | Occasional (5-20%) | Non-specific — early Alport may show GBM thinning before splitting develops |

#### Prognostic Value

- GBM splitting indicates established Alport syndrome — progressive CKD and ESKD are expected
- X-linked Alport syndrome (COL4A5): Males more severely affected; most progress to ESKD by age 30-40
- Autosomal recessive Alport syndrome: Severe; ESKD in childhood or adolescence
- Autosomal dominant Alport syndrome: Variable; may progress to ESKD in middle age
- GBM splitting with global glomerulosclerosis and IFTA: Advanced disease with limited treatment options

#### Treatment Implications

- ACE inhibition: Early initiation to delay progression (ramipril, losartan)
- Avoid nephrotoxins and ototoxins
- Blood pressure control to <130/80 mmHg
- Hearing and ophthalmologic screening
- Renal transplant evaluation when GFR declines
- Genetic counseling for family members
- Emerging: Anti-fibrotic and complement-targeted therapies under investigation

#### Guideline References

1. Kashtan CE, et al. Alport syndrome. *Nat Rev Dis Primers.* 2018;4(1):23
2. KDIGO 2021 Clinical Practice Guideline for the Management of Glomerular Diseases. *Kidney Int.* 2021;100(4S):S1-S276
3. Gross O, et al. Early angiotensin receptor blockade delays ESKD in Alport syndrome. *J Am Soc Nephrol.* 2013;24(3):451-459

#### Diagnostic Pearls

- GBM splitting with a "basket-weave" appearance is essentially diagnostic of Alport syndrome when combined with the appropriate clinical context
- In early Alport syndrome, GBM thinning may be the only finding — genetic testing is essential for diagnosis
- Distinguish GBM splitting from GBM duplication (MPGN pattern) — splitting involves the full GBM thickness; duplication is subendothelial
- In female carriers of X-linked Alport syndrome, GBM splitting may be segmental and focal — measure multiple GBM segments
- Anti-GBM disease may develop after renal transplant in ~3-5% of Alport patients — monitor for anti-GBM antibodies

### EM-08: Tubuloreticular Inclusions

**Document Reference:** GDES-V4.2-PATH-EM-08

#### Definition

Aggregate of branching, anastomosing tubular structures within the cytoplasm of endothelial cells, measuring approximately 20-30 nm in diameter. These structures represent remnants of endoplasmic reticulum and are induced by interferon-alpha. They are an ultrastructural marker of interferon stimulation and are most commonly seen in lupus nephritis, HIV-associated nephropathy, and other interferon-driven diseases.

#### Histological Appearance

- **Location**: Within the cytoplasm of endothelial cells, particularly in glomerular and peritubular capillary endothelium
- **Appearance**: Branching, anastomosing tubular structures, approximately 20-30 nm in diameter
- **Distribution**: May be focal or diffuse; may involve endothelial cells of glomerular capillaries, peritubular capillaries, or both
- **Associated features**: May be accompanied by endothelial cell swelling, mesangial expansion, or other ultrastructural changes depending on the underlying disease
- **Interpretation**: Tubuloreticular inclusions (TRIs) indicate interferon-alpha stimulation and are an indirect marker of immune activation

#### Clinical Significance

Tubuloreticular inclusions are an ultrastructural marker of interferon-alpha stimulation. In the renal biopsy setting, they are most commonly associated with lupus nephritis (where interferon-alpha plays a central pathogenic role) and HIV-associated nephropathy (where HIV infection induces interferon production). The finding of TRIs should prompt evaluation for these conditions if not already suspected. In transplant biopsies, TRIs may indicate interferon-driven immune activation.

#### Associated Diseases

| Disease ID | Frequency | Diagnostic Significance |
|---|---|---|
| `lupus` | Frequent (50-95%) | Strongly Suggestive — TRIs are a marker of interferon stimulation in lupus nephritis |
| `hivan` | Frequent (50-95%) | Strongly Suggestive — TRIs are commonly seen in HIVAN |
| `drugInducedGn` | Occasional (5-20%) | Non-specific — interferon therapy may induce TRIs |
| `tCellMediatedRejection` | Occasional (5-20%) | Non-specific — immune activation in rejection may induce TRIs |
| `antibodyMediatedRejection` | Occasional (5-20%) | Non-specific — immune activation may induce TRIs |

#### Prognostic Value

- TRIs do not independently predict outcome but serve as a marker of interferon-driven immune activation
- In lupus nephritis, TRIs correlate with disease activity and interferon signature
- In HIVAN, TRIs are a marker of active HIV infection in the kidney

#### Treatment Implications

- Lupus nephritis with TRIs: Immunosuppression per ISN/RPS classification; interferon-targeted therapies under investigation
- HIVAN with TRIs: Antiretroviral therapy
- Drug-induced TRIs: Discontinuation of interferon-containing medications

#### Guideline References

1. Weening JJ, et al. The classification of glomerulonephritis in systemic lupus erythematosus revisited. *J Am Soc Nephrol.* 2004;15(2):241-250
2. D'Agati V, Appel GB. HIV infection and the kidney. *J Am Soc Nephrol.* 1997;8(1):131-140
3. Bhatt S, et al. Tubuloreticular inclusions in renal biopsies. *Am J Surg Pathol.* 2008;32(10):1508-1514

#### Diagnostic Pearls

- TRIs are an indirect marker of interferon stimulation — they do not diagnose a specific disease but indicate interferon-driven immune activation
- In lupus nephritis, TRIs are commonly present and correlate with the interferon gene signature
- In HIVAN, TRIs are frequently present and reflect active HIV infection in renal endothelial cells
- TRIs may be seen in other conditions associated with interferon elevation (viral infections, interferon therapy, autoimmune diseases)
- The finding of TRIs should prompt evaluation for lupus nephritis, HIV-associated nephropathy, and other interferon-driven conditions

### EM-09: Endothelial Swelling (TMA)

**Document Reference:** GDES-V4.2-PATH-EM-09

#### Definition

Enlargement and swelling of glomerular capillary endothelial cells, resulting in narrowing or occlusion of capillary lumina. This finding is a hallmark of acute endothelial injury and is most characteristically seen in thrombotic microangiopathy (TMA). The swelling represents acute endothelial cell activation and damage, often preceding or accompanying fibrin thrombi formation.

#### Histological Appearance

- **Endothelial cells**: Enlarged, swollen endothelial cells that protrude into capillary lumina
- **Capillary lumina**: Narrowed or occluded by swollen endothelial cells
- **Subendothelial space**: May show edema (pale, expanded space between endothelium and GBM)
- **Fibrin thrombi**: May be present within capillary lumina alongside swollen endothelial cells
- **Mesangiolysis**: May be present (see LM-09/EM findings)
- **GBM**: May appear wrinkled or corrugated in ischemic areas
- **Distribution**: May be focal or diffuse; involves multiple glomeruli in TMA
- **Associated features**: Subendothelial electron-lucent areas, mesangial expansion, and fibrin strands within capillary lumina

#### Clinical Significance

Endothelial swelling on EM is a sensitive marker of acute endothelial injury and is one of the earliest ultrastructural changes in TMA. It may precede the development of visible fibrin thrombi on LM, making EM particularly valuable for early detection of TMA. The finding mandates investigation for underlying causes of endothelial injury including aHUS, drug toxicity, infection, and malignancy.

#### Associated Diseases

| Disease ID | Frequency | Diagnostic Significance |
|---|---|---|
| `cniToxicity` | Frequent (50-95%) | Strongly Suggestive — endothelial swelling is a hallmark of CNI-induced TMA |
| `antibodyMediatedRejection` | Common (20-50%) | Supportive — endothelial injury in AMR may manifest as endothelial swelling |
| `transplantGlomerulopathy` | Occasional (5-20%) | Non-specific — chronic endothelial injury may show endothelial swelling |
| `diabeticNephropathy` | Occasional (5-20%) | Non-specific — endothelial swelling may occur in severe diabetic nephropathy |

#### Prognostic Value

- Acute endothelial swelling without chronic changes: Potentially reversible with appropriate treatment
- Endothelial swelling with fibrin thrombi: Established TMA requiring urgent intervention
- Persistent endothelial swelling on follow-up biopsy: Indicates ongoing endothelial injury

#### Treatment Implications

- CNI toxicity: Reduce or discontinue CNI
- aHUS: Eculizumab (complement inhibitor)
- AMR with endothelial swelling: Plasmapheresis + immunosuppression
- All TMA: Treat underlying cause; supportive care

#### Guideline References

1. Noris M, Remuzzi G. Atypical hemolytic uremic syndrome. *N Engl J Med.* 2009;361(17):1675-1687
2. Snanoudj R, et al. Calcineurin inhibitor nephrotoxicity. *Nat Rev Nephrol.* 2016;12(12):720-734
3. Lusco MA, et al. Banff 2019 Kidney Meeting Report. *Am J Transplant.* 2020;20(10):2518-2531

#### Diagnostic Pearls

- Endothelial swelling on EM may be the earliest detectable change in TMA — it may precede fibrin thrombi on LM
- In transplant biopsies, endothelial swelling with subendothelial edema and mesangiolysis suggests AMR or CNI toxicity
- Endothelial swelling is non-specific — always correlate with clinical context and other findings
- When endothelial swelling is accompanied by fibrin thrombi, the diagnosis of TMA is established
- EM is more sensitive than LM for detecting early endothelial injury

### EM-10: Fibrillary Deposits (10-20nm)

**Document Reference:** GDES-V4.2-PATH-EM-10

#### Definition

Non-organized, randomly arranged fibrillary deposits measuring 10-20 nm in diameter, found within the glomerular mesangium and/or along the glomerular basement membrane. These deposits are characteristic of fibrillary glomerulonephritis (FGN), a rare glomerular disease that mimics membranous nephropathy clinically but shows distinctive ultrastructural features.

#### Histological Appearance

- **Fibril diameter**: 10-20 nm (typically ~15 nm); measured directly on EM
- **Arrangement**: Random, non-oriented (unlike immunotactoid deposits, which are organized and microtubular)
- **Distribution**: May be within the mesangium, along the GBM, or both; may be focal or diffuse
- **Associated features**:
  - Foot process effacement is typically present
  - Mesangial matrix expansion may be present
  - GBM may be thickened
  - No electron-dense deposits (distinguishes from immune complex diseases)
- **Distinguishing from amyloid**: Amyloid fibrils are typically 8-12 nm (slightly smaller); FGN fibrils are 10-20 nm (slightly larger); both are randomly arranged; Congo red stain is positive in amyloid and negative in FGN
- **Distinguishing from immunotactoid**: Immunotactoid deposits are organized, hollow, and microtubular (20-40 nm); FGN fibrils are non-organized and solid (10-20 nm)
- **Distinguishing from cryoglobulin**: Cryoglobulin deposits are organized, curved, and often show substructure; FGN fibrils are randomly arranged

#### Clinical Significance

Fibrillary glomerulonephritis is a rare but important cause of proteinuria and progressive CKD. The finding of randomly arranged fibrillary deposits (10-20 nm) on EM, with negative Congo red stain and negative for immunoglobulin and complement on IF, is diagnostic of FGN. The disease may be primary or associated with monoclonal gammopathy, and some cases show genetic associations (particularly variants in the GALECTIN-3 gene). FGN has a guarded prognosis, with ~50% of patients progressing to ESKD within 10 years.

#### Associated Diseases

| Disease ID | Frequency | Diagnostic Significance |
|---|---|---|
| `fibrillaryGlomerulonephritis` | Constant (>95%) | Pathognomonic — randomly arranged fibrillary deposits (10-20 nm) define FGN |
| `membranous` | Rare (<5%) | Non-specific — may rarely coexist with membranous nephropathy |
| `membranous` | Rare (<5%) | Non-specific — membranous-like features may be present |

#### Prognostic Value

- FGN generally carries a guarded prognosis (~50% ESKD at 10 years)
- Genetic FGN (GALECTIN-3 variants) may have a more favorable prognosis
- FGN associated with monoclonal gammopathy may respond to treatment of the underlying clone
- No effective targeted therapy is currently available for most cases of FGN

#### Treatment Implications

- No established targeted therapy for FGN
- Supportive care: ACE inhibition, SGLT2 inhibitors, BP control
- If monoclonal gammopathy is present: Treat the underlying clone
- Renal transplant: FGN may recur in the allograft; rituximab may reduce recurrence risk
- Emerging therapies: Anti-fibrotic and immunomodulatory agents under investigation

#### Guideline References

1. Nasr SH, et al. Fibrillary glomerulonephritis and immunotactoid glomerulopathy. *J Am Soc Nephrol.* 2011;22(3):429-436
2. Collins M, et al. Fibrillary glomerulonephritis: a clinicopathologic study. *Kidney Int.* 2018;94(1):141-150
3. KDIGO 2021 Clinical Practice Guideline for the Management of Glomerular Diseases. *Kidney Int.* 2021;100(4S):S1-S276

#### Diagnostic Pearls

- The key distinguishing feature of FGN fibrils is their non-organized, random arrangement and 10-20 nm diameter
- Congo red stain must be negative to exclude amyloidosis — this is essential for diagnosis
- FGN fibrils do not stain with immunohistochemistry for amyloid (SAP, kappa, lambda) or electron microscopy immunolabeling
- Distinguish from immunotactoid deposits (organized, microtubular, 20-40 nm) and cryoglobulin (organized, curved, substructure)
- In transplant biopsies, FGN may recur — consider rituximab prophylaxis

### EM-11: Microtubular Deposits (20-40nm)

**Document Reference:** GDES-V4.2-PATH-EM-11

#### Definition

Organized, microtubular deposits measuring 20-40 nm in diameter, found within the glomerular mesangium and/or along the GBM. These deposits may represent immunotactoid glomerulopathy, cryoglobulinemic glomerulonephritis, or other organized deposit diseases. The organized, hollow, microtubular structure distinguishes these deposits from the randomly arranged fibrils of fibrillary glomerulonephritis.

#### Histological Appearance

- **Fibril diameter**: 20-40 nm (typically ~30 nm); larger than FGN fibrils
- **Arrangement**: Organized, parallel or curving microtubules; may show hollow centers
- **Distribution**: May be within the mesangium, along the GBM, or both; may be focal or diffuse
- **Associated features**:
  - Foot process effacement is typically present
  - Mesangial matrix expansion and hypercellularity may be present
  - GBM may be thickened or show double contours
  - May be accompanied by subendothelial or mesangial electron-dense deposits
- **Distinguishing from FGN**: FGN fibrils are non-organized (random) and smaller (10-20 nm); microtubular deposits are organized and larger (20-40 nm)
- **Distinguishing from cryoglobulin**: Cryoglobulin deposits are typically curved, organized, and may show substructure; microtubular deposits are straighter and more uniform

#### Clinical Significance

Microtubular deposits on EM indicate an organized deposit disease that requires correlation with IF and clinical context to determine the specific diagnosis. The most important differential considerations are immunotactoid glomerulopathy, cryoglobulinemic glomerulonephritis, and monoclonal immunoglobulin deposition disease. Identifying the underlying cause is essential because treatment varies dramatically.

#### Associated Diseases

| Disease ID | Frequency | Diagnostic Significance |
|---|---|---|
| `fibrillaryGlomerulonephritis` | Common (20-50%) | Supportive — some cases of FGN show microtubular rather than purely fibrillary deposits |
| `cryoglobulinemic` | Frequent (50-95%) | Strongly Suggestive — organized microtubular deposits are characteristic of cryoglobulinemic GN |
| `membranous` | Rare (<5%) | Non-specific — rare microtubular deposits in membranous nephropathy |

#### Prognostic Value

- Microtubular deposits associated with cryoglobulinemic GN: Prognosis depends on underlying cause (HCV, autoimmune disease)
- Microtubular deposits associated with monoclonal gammopathy: May respond to treatment of the underlying clone
- Microtubular deposits of uncertain etiology: Guarded prognosis; may progress to ESKD

#### Treatment Implications

- Cryoglobulinemic GN with microtubular deposits: Antiviral therapy (HCV if present); rituximab for severe cases
- Monoclonal gammopathy-associated: Treat the underlying clone (bortezomib, rituximab)
- All organized deposit diseases: Supportive care with ACE inhibition, BP control

#### Guideline References

1. Nasr SH, et al. Fibrillary glomerulonephritis and immunotactoid glomerulopathy. *J Am Soc Nephrol.* 2011;22(3):429-436
2. Sethi S, et al. Glomerulonephritis with organized deposits. *Kidney Int.* 2013;84(2):234-243
3. KDIGO 2021 Clinical Practice Guideline for the Management of Glomerular Diseases. *Kidney Int.* 2021;100(4S):S1-S276

#### Diagnostic Pearls

- The organized, microtubular structure of these deposits distinguishes them from the randomly arranged fibrils of FGN
- Always correlate with IF — immunotactoid deposits often show monoclonal immunoglobulin restriction
- If microtubular deposits are present with cryoglobulin-specific features (curved, organized, subendothelial), suspect cryoglobulinemic GN
- Serum cryoglobulin testing should be performed in all patients with organized deposits on EM
- In transplant biopsies, organized deposits may indicate recurrent cryoglobulinemic GN or immunotactoid glomerulopathy

### EM-12: Immunotactoid Deposits

**Document Reference:** GDES-V4.2-PATH-EM-12

#### Definition

Organized, hollow, microtubular deposits measuring 20-40 nm in diameter, typically arranged in parallel arrays or random orientation, found within the glomerular mesangium and/or along the GBM. Immunotactoid glomerulopathy is characterized by these deposits, which are composed of monoclonal immunoglobulin (typically IgG with kappa or lambda light chain restriction) and are often associated with monoclonal gammopathy or lymphoproliferative disorders.

#### Histological Appearance

- **Fibril diameter**: 20-40 nm (typically ~30 nm)
- **Arrangement**: Organized, parallel or random microtubules; may show hollow centers
- **Distribution**: May be within the mesangium, along the GBM, or both; may be focal or diffuse
- **Associated features**:
  - Foot process effacement is typically present
  - Mesangial matrix expansion and hypercellularity may be present
  - GBM may be thickened or show double contours
  - May be accompanied by subendothelial or mesangial electron-dense deposits
- **IF findings**: Typically show monoclonal immunoglobulin (IgG with kappa or lambda light chain restriction)
- **Distinguishing from FGN**: FGN fibrils are non-organized and smaller (10-20 nm); immunotactoid deposits are organized and larger (20-40 nm)
- **Distinguishing from cryoglobulin**: Cryoglobulin deposits are typically curved and may show substructure; immunotactoid deposits are straighter and more uniform

#### Clinical Significance

Immunotactoid glomerulopathy is a rare organized deposit disease that is often associated with monoclonal gammopathy or lymphoproliferative disorders. The finding of organized, monoclonal microtubular deposits on EM, combined with monoclonal immunoglobulin restriction on IF, is diagnostic. The association with underlying hematologic disease mandates thorough evaluation and treatment of the clone.

#### Associated Diseases

| Disease ID | Frequency | Diagnostic Significance |
|---|---|---|
| `fibrillaryGlomerulonephritis` | Occasional (5-20%) | Supportive — some cases previously classified as FGN may represent immunotactoid glomerulopathy |
| `membranous` | Rare (<5%) | Non-specific — monoclonal immunoglobulin-associated membranous may show immunotactoid deposits |

#### Prognostic Value

- Immunotactoid glomerulopathy associated with monoclonal gammopathy: Prognosis depends on underlying hematologic disease
- No established targeted therapy for most cases
- Treatment of the underlying clone may improve renal outcome

#### Treatment Implications

- Identify and treat underlying monoclonal gammopathy or lymphoproliferative disorder
- Hematologic evaluation: SPEP, FLC, UPEP, bone marrow biopsy
- Chemotherapy targeting the clone (bortezomib, rituximab, lenalidomide)
- Supportive care with ACE inhibition, BP control

#### Guideline References

1. Nasr SH, et al. Fibrillary glomerulonephritis and immunotactoid glomerulopathy. *J Am Soc Nephrol.* 2011;22(3):429-436
2. Leung N, et al. Monoclonal gammopathy of renal significance. *Kidney Int.* 2019;95(6):1299-1311
3. KDIGO 2021 Clinical Practice Guideline for the Management of Glomerular Diseases. *Kidney Int.* 2021;100(4S):S1-S276

#### Diagnostic Pearls

- The key distinguishing feature of immunotactoid deposits is their organized, microtubular structure and monoclonal immunoglobulin composition
- Always check for light chain restriction on IF — monoclonal restriction supports immunotactoid glomerulopathy over FGN
- Serum and urine immunoelectrophoresis should be performed in all patients with organized deposits
- If immunotactoid deposits are present with monoclonal gammopathy, treat the underlying hematologic disease
- Distinguish from cryoglobulinemic GN — cryoglobulins are typically curved and associated with HCV or autoimmune disease

### EM-13: Organized Deposits (Cryoglobulin)

**Document Reference:** GDES-V4.2-PATH-EM-13

#### Definition

Organized, electron-dense deposits with characteristic substructure, typically showing curved, cylindrical, or fingerprint-like configurations, found within the glomerular mesangium, subendothelial space, and/or capillary lumina. These deposits are characteristic of cryoglobulinemic glomerulonephritis and represent precipitation of cryoglobulin immune complexes within the glomerular microvasculature.

#### Histological Appearance

- **Arrangement**: Curved, cylindrical, or fingerprint-like organized structures
- **Diameter**: Variable (20-80 nm depending on the type of cryoglobulin)
- **Distribution**: Typically within the mesangium, subendothelial space, and capillary lumina; may form intraluminal "thrombi" (pseudothrombi)
- **Substructure**: May show parallel array, curved tubular, or fingerprint-like patterns
- **Associated features**:
  - Subendothelial deposits may be present
  - Mesangial matrix expansion and hypercellularity
  - GBM duplication/double contours
  - Endocapillary proliferation with neutrophils
  - Foot process effacement
- **Distinguishing from other organized deposits**: Cryoglobulin deposits are typically curved and may show characteristic substructure; immunotactoid deposits are straighter and more uniform; FGN fibrils are randomly arranged

#### Clinical Significance

Organized deposits with cryoglobulin-like substructure on EM are diagnostic of cryoglobulinemic glomerulonephritis when combined with the appropriate clinical context (cryoglobulinemia, hypocomplementemia, and characteristic LM findings). Cryoglobulinemic GN is most commonly associated with hepatitis C virus (HCV) infection but may also occur in the setting of autoimmune disease, lymphoproliferative disorders, or idiopathically. The finding mandates evaluation for underlying causes and initiation of appropriate treatment.

#### Associated Diseases

| Disease ID | Frequency | Diagnostic Significance |
|---|---|---|
| `cryoglobulinemic` | Constant (>95%) | Pathognomonic — organized deposits with cryoglobulin substructure define cryoglobulinemic GN |
| `lupus` | Rare (<5%) | Non-specific — rare cryoglobulin-like deposits in lupus nephritis |

#### Prognostic Value

- Cryoglobulinemic GN associated with HCV: Prognosis depends on HCV treatment response
- Cryoglobulinemic GN associated with lymphoproliferative disorder: Prognosis depends on underlying malignancy
- Severe cryoglobulinemic GN with crescents and necrosis: Poor prognosis if not treated promptly
- Response to treatment: Renal function may improve with treatment of underlying cause

#### Treatment Implications

- HCV-associated: Direct-acting antiviral therapy (sofosbuvir-based regimens)
- Severe/fulminant: Rituximab + plasma exchange
- Autoimmune-associated: Treat underlying autoimmune disease
- Lymphoproliferative-associated: Chemotherapy targeting the clone
- Supportive care: ACE inhibition, BP control

#### Guideline References

1. Ferri C, et al. Hepatitis C virus-related cryoglobulinemic vasculitis. *J Hepatol.* 2005;43(3):373-382
2. KDIGO 2021 Clinical Practice Guideline for the Management of Glomerular Diseases. *Kidney Int.* 2021;100(4S):S1-S276
3. Cacoub P, et al. Mixed cryoglobulinemia. *N Engl J Med.* 1994;330(24):1728-1733

#### Diagnostic Pearls

- Organized deposits with curved, cylindrical, or fingerprint-like substructure are highly suggestive of cryoglobulinemia
- Serum cryoglobulin testing should be performed in all patients with organized deposits on EM — however, cryoglobulins are often transient and may be missed
- HCV testing is essential in all patients with suspected cryoglobulinemic GN
- In transplant biopsies, organized deposits may indicate recurrent cryoglobulinemic GN
- The combination of organized deposits on EM + mixed cryoglobulins + hypocomplementemia is diagnostic of cryoglobulinemic GN

### EM-14: Podocyte Microvillus Transformation

**Document Reference:** GDES-V4.2-PATH-EM-14

#### Definition

Transformation of podocyte foot processes into elongated, microvillus-like projections that extend into the urinary space. This finding indicates severe podocyte injury and is a marker of active podocyte damage, most characteristically seen in collapsing glomerulopathy (HIV-associated nephropathy) and other severe podocytopathies.

#### Histological Appearance

- **Podocyte surface**: Elongated, microvillus-like projections extending from the podocyte cell body into the urinary space
- **Foot processes**: Effaced (flattened) along the GBM, with microvilli projecting apically
- **Distribution**: May be focal or diffuse; typically involves podocytes overlying affected glomerular tufts
- **Associated features**:
  - Diffuse foot process effacement (see EM-01)
  - Podocyte hypertrophy and hypercellularity (on LM)
  - Glomerular tuft collapse (in collapsing glomerulopathy)
  - Protein reabsorption droplets in tubular epithelial cells
- **Distinction from normal podocyte morphology**: Normal podocytes have discrete foot processes along the GBM; microvillus transformation shows elongated projections extending into the urinary space

#### Clinical Significance

Podocyte microvillus transformation is a marker of severe podocyte injury and is most characteristically seen in collapsing glomerulopathy (HIV-associated nephropathy). It indicates active podocyte damage with loss of normal foot process architecture and is associated with severe proteinuria and rapid progression to ESKD. The finding should prompt evaluation for HIV infection, medications (bisphosphonates, interferon), and other causes of collapsing glomerulopathy.

#### Associated Diseases

| Disease ID | Frequency | Diagnostic Significance |
|---|---|---|
| `hivan` | Frequent (50-95%) | Strongly Suggestive — podocyte microvillus transformation with collapsing glomerulopathy |
| `fsgs` | Occasional (5-20%) | Supportive — microvillus transformation may be seen in severe FSGS variants |
| `fsgs` | Occasional (5-20%) | Supportive — collapsing FSGS may show microvillus transformation |
| `drugInducedGn` | Rare (<5%) | Non-specific — certain drugs may induce podocyte microvillus transformation |

#### Prognostic Value

- Podocyte microvillus transformation indicates severe podocyte injury with poor prognosis
- In HIV-associated nephropathy, microvillus transformation with collapsing glomerulopathy carries a very poor prognosis for renal survival
- Response to antiretroviral therapy may be limited if microvillus transformation is extensive

#### Treatment Implications

- HIV-associated: Antiretroviral therapy (immediate initiation)
- Drug-induced: Discontinue offending agent (bisphosphonates, interferon)
- Collapsing FSGS: CNIs, rituximab; limited response to treatment
- Supportive care: ACE inhibition, BP control, dietary management

#### Guideline References

1. D'Agati V, Appel GB. HIV infection and the kidney. *J Am Soc Nephrol.* 1997;8(1):131-140
2. Barisoni L, et al. Podocyte pathobiology in HIV-associated nephropathy. *Kidney Int.* 2003;64(4):1221-1231
3. KDIGO 2021 Clinical Practice Guideline for the Management of Glomerular Diseases. *Kidney Int.* 2021;100(4S):S1-S276

#### Diagnostic Pearls

- Podocyte microvillus transformation is a sensitive marker of severe podocyte injury — it is most commonly seen in collapsing glomerulopathy
- In HIV-associated nephropathy, microvillus transformation is often accompanied by tubuloreticular inclusions (see EM-08)
- If microvillus transformation is present with collapsing glomerulopathy, always test for HIV
- The finding of microvillus transformation should prompt evaluation for other causes of severe podocyte injury (drugs, genetic mutations)
- In transplant biopsies, microvillus transformation may indicate recurrent collapsing glomerulopathy

## Appendix A: Combined Biopsy Pattern Recognition

The following tables integrate findings across all three microscopy modalities (LM, IF, EM) to guide diagnostic reasoning. Each pattern lists the key combination of findings and the most likely diagnoses, ordered by probability.

### Pattern 1: Minimal Change Pattern

**Key Findings:**
- LM: Normal glomeruli (or only mild mesangial expansion)
- IF: Negative or non-specific (trace immunoreactants may be present)
- EM: Diffuse foot process effacement (>80%) with no electron-dense deposits

| Rank | Disease ID | Diagnostic Criteria |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | `mcd` | Diffuse foot process effacement (>80%) + normal LM + negative IF = diagnostic of MCD |
| 2 | `fsgs` | Diffuse foot process effacement with early segmental sclerosis (tip lesion variant may have minimal LM changes) |
| 3 | `drugInducedGn` | Diffuse foot process effacement + history of offending drug (NSAIDs, lithium, etc.) |
| 4 | `lupus` | Diffuse foot process effacement with trace immune complex deposits on IF/EM (early lupus nephritis) |
| 5 | `iga` | Diffuse foot process effacement with mild mesangial IgA deposits (early IgA nephropathy) |

**Clinical Pearl:** In a child with nephrotic syndrome, the combination of normal LM + negative IF + diffuse foot process effacement is diagnostic of minimal change disease and predicts excellent response to corticosteroids. In adults, evaluate for secondary causes (malignancy, drugs) before diagnosing primary MCD.

---

### Pattern 2: Membranous Pattern

**Key Findings:**
- LM: Diffuse GBM thickening (PAS and silver stain); spike formation on silver stain (Stage II)
- IF: Granular capillary wall deposits (IgG, C3, ± kappa/lambda)
- EM: Subepithelial electron-dense deposits

| Rank | Disease ID | Distinguishing Features |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | `membranous` | PLA2R or THSD7A antibody-positive; IgG-dominant subepithelial deposits; normal mesangium |
| 2 | `lupus` | Full-house IF (IgG, IgA, IgM, C3, C1q); mesangial deposits on EM; clinical SLE |
| 3 | `drugInducedGn` | Drug history (NSAIDs, penicillamine, gold); resolution with drug withdrawal |
| 4 | `c3` | C3-dominant IF with subepithelial deposits; evaluate for complement dysregulation |
| 5 | `irgn` | Large dome-shaped subepithelial "humps" with neutrophils; clinical context of infection |

**Clinical Pearl:** PLA2R antibody testing should be performed on all adult membranous nephropathy cases. PLA2R-positive cases are primary membranous; PLA2R-negative cases require evaluation for secondary causes (lupus, hepatitis B, malignancy, monoclonal gammopathy).

---

### Pattern 3: FSGS Pattern

**Key Findings:**
- LM: Segmental sclerosis in some glomeruli; focal distribution (<50% of glomeruli)
- IF: Non-specific (may show trapping in sclerotic segments)
- EM: Diffuse foot process effacement (>80%); no electron-dense deposits (primary); may have deposits if secondary

| Rank | Disease ID | Columbia Variant / Distinguishing Features |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | `fsgs` (primary) | NOS or tip lesion variant; diffuse foot process effacement; PLA2R-negative; no secondary causes |
| 2 | `fsgs` (secondary/adaptive) | Perihilar variant; obesity, reduced nephron mass; focal foot process effacement (<80%) |
| 3 | `hivan` | Collapsing variant; tubuloreticular inclusions on EM; HIV-positive |
| 4 | `drugInducedGn` | Collapsing or NOS variant; drug history (bisphosphonates, interferon, sirolimus) |
| 5 | `cniToxicity` | NOS variant; isometric vacuolization on LM; CNI use |
| 6 | `diabeticNephropathy` | NOS or perihilar variant; Kimmelstiel-Wilson nodules; diabetic history |
| 7 | `lupus` | NOS variant; immune complex deposits on IF/EM; clinical SLE |
| 8 | `membranous` | NOS variant superimposed on membranous; subepithelial deposits on EM |

**Clinical Pearl:** Collapsing FSGS always warrants HIV testing and medication review. Tip lesion FSGS generally has a better prognosis and higher corticosteroid response rate than NOS or collapsing variants.

---

### Pattern 4: MPGN Pattern

**Key Findings:**
- LM: Mesangial hypercellularity, capillary wall thickening, GBM duplication (double contours)
- IF: C3 dominant (C3G/DDD) or IgG-dominant (immune complex) or cryoglobulin-specific
- EM: Subendothelial and/or mesangial electron-dense deposits; dense intramembranous deposits (DDD)

| Rank | Disease ID | IF/EM Distinguishing Features |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | `c3` | C3-dominant IF; subendothelial/mesangial deposits on EM (C3GN) |
| 2 | `denseDepositDisease` | C3-dominant IF; dense ribbon-like intramembranous deposits on EM |
| 3 | `cryoglobulinemic` | Mixed cryoglobulin IF; organized deposits on EM; hypocomplementemia |
| 4 | `lupus` | Full-house IF; subendothelial + mesangial deposits on EM; clinical SLE |
| 5 | `mpgn` (immune complex) | IgG-dominant IF; subendothelial deposits on EM; identify underlying cause |
| 6 | `transplantGlomerulopathy` | C4d+; double contours; DSA-positive; transplant context |
| 7 | `antibodyMediatedRejection` | C4d+ in PTCs; DSA-positive; transplant context |
| 8 | `iga` | IgA-dominant IF; mesangial deposits; may show MPGN pattern in chronic cases |

**Clinical Pearl:** The MPGN pattern is a histological description, not a diagnosis. The IF pattern is the key to determining the underlying mechanism: C3-dominant → C3G/DDD; IgG-dominant → immune complex; cryoglobulin-specific → cryoglobulinemic GN.

---

### Pattern 5: Crescentic GN Pattern

**Key Findings:**
- LM: Crescents in ≥1 glomeruli; may be cellular, fibrocellular, or fibrous; ± necrosis
- IF: Pauci-immune (ANCA), immune complex (lupus, IgA), or linear IgG (anti-GBM)
- EM: Varies by underlying disease

| Rank | Disease ID | IF Pattern / Distinguishing Features |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | `anca` | Pauci-immune IF (absent or minimal immunoreactants); MPO or PR3 antibody-positive |
| 2 | `antiGbm` | Linear IgG along GBM; anti-GBM antibody-positive; ± pulmonary hemorrhage |
| 3 | `lupus` | Full-house IF; subendothelial deposits on EM; clinical SLE |
| 4 | `iga` | IgA-dominant IF; mesangial deposits; crescents indicate severe IgA nephropathy |
| 5 | `cryoglobulinemic` | Cryoglobulin-specific IF; organized deposits on EM; hypocomplementemia |
| 6 | `irgn` | C3 + immunoglobulin IF; subepithelial humps on EM; post-infectious context |
| 7 | `c3` | C3-dominant IF; evaluate for complement dysregulation |
| 8 | `drugInducedGn` | Variable IF; drug history; resolution with drug withdrawal |

**Clinical Pearl:** Crescentic GN is a medical emergency. The IF pattern determines the cause: pauci-immune → ANCA vasculitis; linear IgG → anti-GBM disease; immune complex → lupus, IgA, post-infectious. Treatment differs dramatically by cause.

---

### Pattern 6: IgA-Dominant Pattern

**Key Findings:**
- LM: Mesangial proliferation ± endocapillary hypercellularity; may show crescents
- IF: IgA-dominant mesangial deposits (IgA >> IgG, IgM, C3)
- EM: Mesangial electron-dense deposits; diffuse foot process effacement

| Rank | Disease ID | Distinguishing Features |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | `iga` | Primary IgA nephropathy; polyclonal IgA; no secondary causes identified |
| 2 | `lupus` | IgA deposits + full-house pattern (C1q, IgG, IgM, C3); clinical SLE |
| 3 | `hivan` | IgA deposits + HIV-associated features; collapsing glomerulopathy |
| 4 | `irgn` | IgA deposits + post-infectious context; C3 co-dominant; resolving endocapillary proliferation |
| 5 | `drugInducedGn` | IgA deposits + drug history; resolution with drug withdrawal |
| 6 | Secondary IgA | IgA deposits + liver disease, celiac disease, or HIV; treat underlying cause |

**Clinical Pearl:** Primary IgA nephropathy shows IgA-dominant deposits with polyclonal kappa/lambda ratio. Secondary IgA deposition (liver disease, celiac disease) must be excluded. Monoclonal IgA deposition disease (restricted kappa or lambda) requires evaluation for monoclonal gammopathy.

---

### Pattern 7: Full-House Pattern

**Key Findings:**
- LM: Variable (mesangial proliferation, endocapillary proliferation, MPGN pattern)
- IF: IgG, IgA, IgM, C3, and C1q all positive (full-house)
- EM: Mesangial and/or subendothelial electron-dense deposits

| Rank | Disease ID | Distinguishing Features |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | `lupus` | Full-house IF is virtually diagnostic of lupus nephritis; C1q strongly positive |
| 2 | `cryoglobulinemic` | Full-house pattern may be seen; organized deposits on EM; cryoglobulinemia |
| 3 | `drugInducedGn` | Rare full-house pattern; drug history; resolution with drug withdrawal |
| 4 | `iga` | Rare full-house pattern; IgA should be dominant over other immunoreactants |

**Clinical Pearl:** Full-house IF is lupus nephritis until proven otherwise. Strong C1q staining is particularly characteristic of lupus. Always evaluate for clinical SLE if full-house IF is found on renal biopsy.

---

### Pattern 8: Endothelial Injury Pattern

**Key Findings:**
- LM: Fibrin thrombi in glomerular capillaries/arterioles; endothelial swelling; mesangiolysis; arterial onion-skinning
- IF: Fibrinogen in thrombi; C4d in PTCs (if AMR)
- EM: Endothelial swelling; subendothelial edema; mesangiolysis

| Rank | Disease ID | Distinguishing Features |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | `cniToxicity` | CNI use; isometric vacuolization; efferent arteriolar hyalinosis; mesangiolysis |
| 2 | `antibodyMediatedRejection` | C4d+ in PTCs; DSA-positive; transplant context; microvascular inflammation |
| 3 | `transplantGlomerulopathy` | Double contours; C4d+; DSA-positive; chronic endothelial injury |
| 4 | `diabeticNephropathy` | Afferent AND efferent arteriolar hyalinosis; Kimmelstiel-Wilson nodules |
| 5 | `cryoglobulinemic` | Organized deposits on EM; cryoglobulinemia; hypocomplementemia |
| 6 | Scleroderma renal crisis | Onion-skin arterial intimal fibrosis; malignant hypertension; scleroderma history |
| 7 | Malignant hypertension | Onion-skin arterial changes; severe hypertension; emergency |
| 8 | aHUS | Complement gene mutations; fibrin thrombi; no AMR features |

**Clinical Pearl:** Endothelial injury on biopsy mandates immediate investigation for the underlying cause. In transplant biopsies, the differential includes AMR, CNI toxicity, and recurrent aHUS. In native kidney biopsies, consider aHUS, scleroderma renal crisis, and malignant hypertension.
