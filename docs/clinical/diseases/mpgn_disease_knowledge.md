## 2. Disease Record — Full 21-Field Schema

### 2.1 Definition

Membranoproliferative Glomerulonephritis (MPGN) is a histologic pattern of glomerular injury defined by mesangial hypercellularity, endocapillary proliferation, and thickening of the glomerular basement membrane with double contour (tram-track) formation. It is classified by immunofluorescence into immune complex-mediated MPGN (IC-MPGN, IgG-positive) and complement-mediated MPGN (C3 glomerulopathy, C3-dominant). MPGN can be idiopathic or secondary to infections (HCV, HBV, endocarditis), autoimmune diseases (SLE, Sjogren), monoclonal gammopathies, or complement dysregulation.

### 2.2 Epidemiology

Incidence declining due to reclassification; now <5% of native GN biopsies. All ages affected, with bimodal peaks: children/young adults (idiopathic, complement-mediated) and older adults (secondary to infections, MGUS). No clear sex predominance. Geographic variation: higher rates in developing countries due to infectious causes (HCV, schistosomiasis). In developed countries, incidence of idiopathic MPGN is falling while secondary causes (MGUS, HCV) are increasingly recognized with better classification.

### 2.3 Aetiology

IMMUNE COMPLEX-MEDIATED (IC-MPGN): Chronic infections — HCV (cryoglobulinemic MPGN), HBV, hepatitis B/C, endocarditis, shunt nephritis, visceral abscesses, malaria, schistosomiasis. Autoimmune diseases — SLE (Class III/IV), Sjogren syndrome, rheumatoid arthritis, mixed connective tissue disease. Monoclonal gammopathies — MGUS, multiple myeloma, lymphoproliferative disorders. COMPLEMENT-MEDIATED (C3G): C3 nephritic factor (C3NeF), complement factor H/ factor I mutations, CFHR gene rearrangements, anti-CFH autoantibodies. IDIOPATHIC: No identifiable cause after thorough evaluation (increasingly rare with modern diagnostic techniques).

### 2.4 Pathophysiology

IC-MPGN: Subendothelial immune complex deposition (IgG, IgM, C3) triggers classical complement pathway activation → C3 and C5b-9 generation. Inflammatory mediators recruit neutrophils and monocytes → mesangial cell proliferation, matrix expansion, and endothelial injury. GBM remodeling occurs as new basement membrane is deposited around immune deposits, creating characteristic double contours (tram tracks). C3G: Uncontrolled alternative complement pathway activation leads to continuous C3 cleavage and C3b deposition along the GBM and mesangium without significant immunoglobulin deposition. In both types, progressive mesangial interposition, capillary loop occlusion, and glomerulosclerosis develop over time. Subendothelial deposits are characteristic, with immune complex or electron-dense material visible on EM.

### 2.5 Clinical Presentation

Nephrotic syndrome (40-60%): peripheral edema, foamy urine, hypoalbuminemia, hyperlipidemia. Hematuria (microscopic in most, macroscopic in 10-20%). Hypertension (30-50%). Reduced eGFR at presentation (30-50%). Subnephrotic proteinuria with hematuria (20-30%). Acute nephritic syndrome (10-15%): oliguria, hypertension, hematuria, renal impairment. Rapidly progressive GN (5-10%) with crescents. Underlying disease features: HCV rash/arthralgia/neuropathy, SLE malar rash/arthritis, endocarditis fever/murmur, MGUS neuropathy/anemia.

### 2.8. Lab Findings

- Proteinuria (nephrotic or subnephrotic)
- Hematuria (dysmorphic RBCs, RBC casts)
- Low C3 (both IC-MPGN and C3G)
- Low C4 (IC-MPGN only; normal in C3G)
- C3 nephritic factor (C3NeF) — positive in C3G (50-80%)
- eGFR: variable (normal to dialysis-dependent)
- Serum albumin: low (nephrotic syndrome)
- Lipids: elevated (nephrotic syndrome)
- UPCR/ACR: elevated, often >3 g/g
- Cryoglobulins: positive if HCV-associated
- HCV/HBV serology: positive if infection-related
- ANA, anti-dsDNA: positive if lupus
- SPEP, IFE, sFLC: monoclonal gammopathy workup
- Anti-CFH antibodies: positive in subset of C3G
- CFH, CFI, CFHR gene testing for genetic C3G

### 2.9. Biopsy Findings

- LM: Mesangial hypercellularity (diffuse, global)
- LM: Endocapillary proliferation (capillary lumen narrowing)
- LM: GBM double contours (tram tracks) — PATHOGNOMONIC
- LM: Lobular accentuation of glomerular tufts
- LM: Mesangial interposition (mesangial cells/ matrix between GBM and endothelium)
- LM: Crescents in active/progressive cases (cellular, fibrocellular)
- LM: Tubulointerstitial fibrosis and tubular atrophy (proportional to chronicity)
- IF (IC-MPGN): Granular IgG (dominant or codominant) + C3, ± IgM, ± C1q, ± kappa/lambda
- IF (C3G): C3 dominant >2 orders stronger than any immunoglobulin; C5b-9 present
- IF (C3G): Properdin and factor H may be present; C1q minimal or absent
- EM (IC-MPGN): Large subendothelial electron-dense deposits (continuous or discontinuous)
- EM (IC-MPGN): Mesangial deposits present; subepithelial deposits rare
- EM (C3G): Subendothelial, mesangial, and intramembranous dense deposits
- EM (C3G): No organized immune complex structures (vs cryoglobulinemic GN)

### 2.10. Classification Systems

- MPGN Classification by IF Pattern: IC-MPGN (IgG + C3) vs C3 Glomerulopathy (C3 dominant) (KDIGO 2021 Ch 8)
- Pathologic Chronicity Index: Active / Chronic / Mixed features based on crescents, sclerosis, IFTA (ISN 2023)
- Clinical Severity Staging: Mild (Cr <1.5, UPCR <3.5) / Moderate (Cr 1.5-3.0, UPCR 3.5-8) / Severe (Cr >3.0, UPCR >8 or RPGN) (ERA/EDTA 2022)
- Aetiologic Classification: Infection-associated / Autoimmune / Monoclonal gammopathy / Complement-mediated / Idiopathic (KDIGO 2021)

### 2.11. Risk Stratification

- Nephrotic-range proteinuria at presentation: 2-3x risk of ESKD vs subnephrotic (High)
- Reduced eGFR (<60) at diagnosis: Strongest predictor of ESKD; 50% 10-year kidney survival (High)
- Crescents >30% on biopsy: High risk of RPGN and rapid progression (High)
- IC-MPGN vs C3G: C3G has higher recurrence risk post-transplant (50-80%) (Moderate)
- Monoclonal gammopathy as underlying cause: Better response to clone-directed therapy; may improve renal outcomes (Moderate)
- Poor response to initial immunosuppression: Persistent proteinuria >3g at 6 months predicts ESKD (High)
- Tubulointerstitial fibrosis >25% on biopsy: Irreversible chronic damage; poor renal recovery (High)
- Hypertension at presentation: Accelerates CKD progression (Moderate)

### 2.12 Treatment Overview

TREAT UNDERLYING CAUSE (most important): Antivirals for HCV (DAAs) or HBV. Antibiotics for endocarditis/shunt nephritis. Immunosuppression for SLE (steroids + MMF or cyclophosphamide). Clone-directed therapy for monoclonal gammopathy (bortezomib, daratumumab, rituximab). IC-MPGN (Idiopathic): Corticosteroids (prednisone 1 mg/kg/day taper over 6-12 months) + MMF (1-2 g/day) or cyclophosphamide (severe). Rituximab (anti-CD20) for relapsed/refractory. C3 GLOMERULOPATHY: Supportive care (RAASi for proteinuria). Eculizumab (anti-C5) for progressive disease despite conservative therapy (limited evidence, case series). Complement inhibitors in trials (iptacopan — factor B inhibitor, vemircopan — factor D inhibitor). Plasma exchange for CFH autoantibody-associated C3G. SUPPORTIVE: RAASi (ACEi/ARB), BP control, diuretics, statins for all patients. ESKD: dialysis (HD/PD) or kidney transplant. NOTE: C3G has high recurrence rate in transplant (50-80%).

### 2.14 Monitoring Protocol

During active treatment: monthly Cr, UPCR, C3/C4, albumin, BP. Post-treatment remission: Q3 months: eGFR, UPCR, C3, complement levels, BP. Long-term stable: Q6 months: eGFR, UPCR, BP. C3G specific: C3NeF monitoring, eculizumab dose adjustment if used, meningococcal vaccination (required before eculizumab). IC-MPGN specific: re-screen for underlying cause if relapse. Transplant: monitor for recurrence; protocol biopsies may be considered (C3G high risk).

### 2.15 Complications

ESKD requiring RRT: 50% at 10 years (idiopathic); variable with treated underlying cause
Nephrotic syndrome complications: thromboembolism, infection, malnutrition
Hypertension and accelerated CVD
Infection risk from immunosuppression (steroids, MMF, CYC, rituximab)
Eculizumab: meningococcal infection risk (vaccination mandatory)
Post-transplant recurrence: C3G 50-80%, IC-MPGN 30-50%
Malignancy related to underlying MGUS/lymphoma
Progressive tubulointerstitial fibrosis and irreversible CKD

### 2.16 Relapse Information

IC-MPGN: relapse common after steroid/MMF taper (30-50% at 5 years). Predictors: persistent proteinuria at 6 months, nephrotic syndrome at presentation. Treatment of underlying HCV prevents relapse. Rituximab may reduce relapse rate. C3G: chronic progressive disease with frequent relapses; relapse rate >50% after treatment withdrawal. Complement levels (C3, C3NeF) guide disease activity. Post-transplant recurrence: C3G 50-80% (often within first year), IC-MPGN 30-50%. Recurrence risk lower if underlying cause is treated pre-transplant (e.g., HCV cure).

### 2.17 Long Term Prognosis

Idiopathic IC-MPGN: 10-year kidney survival ~50%. Responders to immunosuppression: 70-80% at 10 years. Non-responders: 20-30% at 10 years. C3G: 10-year kidney survival 40-60%. Predictors of poor outcome: Cr >2.0 at presentation, nephrotic-range proteinuria, crescents >30%, IFTA >25%. With treated underlying cause (HCV, SLE): kidney survival 70-90% at 10 years. Post-transplant: C3G recurrence 50-80% with graft loss in 30-50% of those; IC-MPGN recurrence 30-50% with lower graft loss. Overall mortality: 10-20% at 10 years (infection, CVD, underlying disease).

### 2.18 Evidence Summary

No large RCTs for idiopathic MPGN. Corticosteroids + MMF based on observational studies and expert consensus (GRADE 2C). Rituximab: case series in refractory IC-MPGN (N=20-50) show proteinuria reduction in 50-60%. Eculizumab in C3G: open-label trials and case series; benefit in ~30% of patients with progressive disease; no RCT completed. Iptacopan (factor B inhibitor) in C3G: Phase 2 (N=12) showed proteinuria reduction; Phase 3 ongoing. Underlying cause treatment is the strongest evidence (DAAs for HCV: SVR >95%, renal remission 50-70%). KDIGO 2021 Chapter 8 provides the current framework. All treatment is off-label.
