## 1. Document Purpose

This document presents all 8 validated clinical cases for Minimal Change Disease covering the full spectrum of presentation types, including typical childhood presentation, frequently relapsing disease, steroid-dependent disease, adult onset with AKI, secondary causes (NSAID-induced, Hodgkin lymphoma-associated), steroid-resistant transition to FSGS, and long-term remission after rituximab. Each case includes presentation, diagnostic workup, management, and teaching points with emphasis on steroid sensitivity as the defining diagnostic and therapeutic feature.

## 3. Case 1: Classic Childhood MCD — Steroid Response

### 3.1 Presentation

A 4-year-old boy presents with 5-day history of periorbital oedema progressing to facial swelling and bilateral lower extremity oedema. Mother reports foamy urine for 1 week. No preceding illness, but his sister had an upper respiratory tract infection 2 weeks ago.

### 3.2 Examination

- Weight: 17 kg (2 kg above baseline)
- BP: 95/60 mmHg (normal for age)
- Periorbital oedema, 2+ pitting oedema in lower extremities, mild ascites
- No skin rash, no joint swelling

### 3.3 Laboratory Findings

| Parameter | Result | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| Urine dipstick | 4+ protein | Nephrotic range |
| UPCR | 8.2 g/g | Massive proteinuria |
| Serum albumin | 1.8 g/dL | Nephrotic |
| Serum creatinine | 0.3 mg/dL | Normal |
| C3/C4 | Normal | No complement activation |
| ANA | Negative | Excludes lupus |
| Lipid profile | Cholesterol 320 mg/dL, LDL 210 mg/dL | Hyperlipidemia |

### 3.4 Biopsy

Not performed (empiric steroid trial in typical childhood presentation is standard of care per KDIGO 2021 5.1.1).

### 3.5 Diagnosis

Minimal Change Disease, childhood-onset, presenting with acute nephrotic syndrome.

### 3.6 Management

- Prednisone 60 mg/m2/day (20 mg) for 4 weeks
- Sodium restriction, fluid balance monitoring
- Daily urine dipstick at home

### 3.7 Outcome

- **Day 10:** Urine dipstick negative for protein
- **Day 14:** Complete remission (UPCR 0.2 g/g, albumin 3.2 g/dL)
- **Week 4:** Begin alternate-day taper over 3 months
- **6 months:** Remission maintained off steroids

### 3.8 Teaching Points

1. Classic presentation: acute nephrotic syndrome in a toddler with preserved GFR and normal BP
2. Steroid trial without biopsy is standard in children 1-10 years with typical presentation (KDIGO 2021)
3. Rapid steroid response confirms the diagnosis — this is the key therapeutic test
4. Relapse risk remains 60-70%, requiring ongoing surveillance
5. Excellent prognosis: >95% long-term renal survival

## 4. Case 2: Frequently Relapsing MCD in a Child

### 4.1 Presentation

A 6-year-old girl with known MCD diagnosed at age 3. She has experienced 4 relapses in the past 12 months, each treated with prednisone 60 mg/m2/day. Current presentation: 2+ proteinuria on dipstick for 3 consecutive days, periorbital oedema.

### 4.2 Examination

- Weight: 20 kg (1.5 kg above baseline)
- BP: 100/65 mmHg
- Periorbital oedema, trace pedal oedema
- Cushingoid facies from repeated steroid courses

### 4.3 Classification

Frequently Relapsing MCD (FR): 4 relapses in 12 months.

### 4.4 Management

1. Prednisone 60 mg/m2/day (25 mg) restarted — remission achieved by Day 7
2. Initiate steroid-sparing therapy: MMF 600 mg/m2/dose q12h (250 mg BID)
3. Prednisone taper over 8 weeks (faster taper due to FR risk)
4. MMF maintained for 12 months

### 4.5 Outcome

- 1 relapse in 12 months (reduced from 4)
- Median steroid dose reduced by 60%
- Growth velocity stabilised after MMF initiation

### 4.6 Teaching Points

1. FR is defined as 2 relapses in 6 months or 4 in 12 months
2. Steroid-sparing therapy should be initiated after FR pattern established
3. MMF is first-line for FR in children (non-nephrotoxic, well-tolerated)
4. Cumulative steroid toxicity (cushingoid features, growth delay) is the main morbidity
5. Rituximab is second-line if MMF fails to control relapses

## 5. Case 3: Steroid-Dependent MCD in a Young Adult

### 5.1 Presentation

A 22-year-old male presents with nephrotic syndrome. He was initially diagnosed with MCD at age 18. Each attempt to taper prednisone below 15 mg/day results in relapse. He has had 6 relapses over 4 years, all during steroid taper.

### 5.2 Examination

- Weight: 72 kg
- BP: 115/75 mmHg
- 1+ lower extremity oedema
- Acne, striae from chronic steroid use

### 5.3 Classification

Steroid-Dependent MCD.

### 5.4 Previous Therapy

- Cyclosporine 4 mg/kg/day: partial effect but nephrotoxicity (Cr rise from 0.8 to 1.3 mg/dL)
- MMF 2 g/day: inadequate control

### 5.5 Management

- Prednisone 1 mg/kg/day (70 mg) to regain remission
- Rituximab 375 mg/m2 IV weekly x 4 doses
- CD19 monitoring: undetectable at 1 month
- Prednisone taper over 12 weeks

### 5.6 Outcome

- Remission maintained at 12 months off prednisone
- CD19 recovery at 9 months; no relapse
- No B-cell depleting retreatment needed

### 5.7 Teaching Points

1. Steroid dependence implies relapse during taper or within 2 weeks of cessation
2. CNI toxicity limits long-term use; monitor Cr vigilantly
3. Rituximab is the preferred agent for SD (RITURNS: 85% remission at 12 months)
4. B-cell monitoring helps guide retreatment decisions
5. Cumulative steroid burden in young adults accelerates osteoporosis risk

## 6. Case 4: Adult-Onset MCD with AKI

### 6.1 Presentation

A 45-year-old man presents with 2-week history of rapidly worsening oedema, ascites, and decreased urine output. No previous renal history. He reports ibuprofen use 3 times weekly for chronic back pain.

### 6.2 Examination

- Weight: 88 kg (+8 kg from baseline)
- BP: 145/90 mmHg (hypertensive)
- Ascites, 3+ pitting oedema to thighs, scrotal oedema
- Bibasilar crackles (mild pulmonary oedema)

### 6.3 Laboratory Findings

| Parameter | Result | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| Serum Cr | 2.8 mg/dL (baseline 0.9) | Acute kidney injury |
| eGFR | 24 mL/min/1.73m2 | Stage 4 AKI |
| UPCR | 12.5 g/g | Nephrotic range |
| Albumin | 1.5 g/dL | Severely low |
| K+ | 5.1 mEq/L | Mild hyperkalemia |
| C3/C4 | Normal | — |
| ANA, ANCA | Negative | — |
| SPEP | No monoclonal protein | — |

### 6.4 Biopsy

- **LM:** Normal glomeruli, mild acute tubular necrosis
- **IF:** Negative
- **EM:** Diffuse foot process effacement, no deposits
- **Diagnosis:** MCD with superimposed acute tubular necrosis

### 6.5 Management

1. Prednisone 1 mg/kg/day (80 mg)
2. Diuresis: furosemide 40 mg IV BID (titrated to response)
3. Temporary haemodialysis for hyperkalemia and volume overload (3 sessions)
4. NSAID discontinuation strongly advised
5. BP management: amlodipine 5 mg/day

### 6.6 Outcome

- **Week 2:** Urine output improved, Cr 1.8 mg/dL
- **Week 4:** Complete remission (UPCR 0.3 g/g, albumin 3.0 g/dL)
- **Week 8:** Cr 0.9 mg/dL (full renal recovery)
- **Month 6:** Off steroids, remission maintained

### 6.7 Teaching Points

1. Adult-onset MCD frequently presents with AKI (5-10%), especially with NSAID exposure
2. AKI does not preclude excellent renal outcomes; full recovery is expected
3. NSAIDs should be avoided in nephrotic syndrome (reduce renal blood flow, worsen AKI)
4. Temporary dialysis may be required for AKI complications
5. Steroid responsiveness is preserved in adult-onset MCD, though slightly lower than children (75-85%)

## 7. Case 5: NSAID-Induced Secondary MCD

### 7.1 Presentation

A 55-year-old woman with chronic knee osteoarthritis presents with 4-week history of progressive oedema. She has been taking diclofenac 150 mg daily for 2 months. No prior renal disease.

### 7.2 Examination

- BP: 130/80 mmHg
- 2+ pitting oedema to knees
- No ascites

### 7.3 Laboratory Findings

- UPCR: 6.8 g/g
- Albumin: 2.1 g/dL
- Cr: 1.2 mg/dL (eGFR 52)
- Urine sediment: fatty casts, oval fat bodies

### 7.4 Biopsy

- **LM:** Minimal mesangial hypercellularity
- **IF:** Negative
- **EM:** Diffuse foot process effacement, no deposits

### 7.5 Diagnosis

NSAID-induced secondary MCD.

### 7.6 Management

1. Diclofenac discontinued
2. Prednisone 0.5 mg/kg/day (25 mg) — lower dose due to drug-induced aetiology
3. Supportive: low-dose RAAS inhibition (ramipril 2.5 mg)

### 7.7 Outcome

- **Week 3:** Dipstick 1+ protein
- **Week 6:** Complete remission (UPCR 0.2 g/g)
- **Month 3:** Steroids tapered off
- **Month 6:** Remission maintained; no further treatment required

### 7.8 Teaching Points

1. NSAIDs are the most common cause of drug-induced MCD; mechanism involves inhibition of COX-2 leading to altered podocyte prostaglandin metabolism
2. Drug withdrawal alone may not suffice; short steroid course accelerates remission
3. Lower steroid doses are often adequate for secondary MCD
4. Prognosis is excellent once the offending agent is removed
5. Always perform a thorough medication history in adult-onset nephrotic syndrome

## 8. Case 6: MCD Secondary to Hodgkin Lymphoma

### 8.1 Presentation

A 17-year-old male presents with 3-week history of cervical lymphadenopathy, night sweats, weight loss (5 kg), and 1 week of periorbital oedema.

### 8.2 Examination

- Multiple firm, non-tender cervical and supraclavicular lymph nodes (2-3 cm)
- Splenomegaly
- Periorbital oedema, 1+ pedal oedema
- BP: 110/70 mmHg

### 8.3 Laboratory Findings

- UPCR: 9.5 g/g
- Albumin: 1.9 g/dL
- Cr: 0.7 mg/dL (normal)
- LDH: 450 U/L (elevated)
- Lymph node biopsy: Classic Hodgkin lymphoma, mixed cellularity type

### 8.4 Diagnosis

MCD secondary to Hodgkin lymphoma — a paraneoplastic phenomenon. The renal biopsy (LM normal, IF negative, EM diffuse foot process effacement) confirms MCD.

### 8.5 Management

1. ABVD chemotherapy (doxorubicin, bleomycin, vinblastine, dacarbazine) for Hodgkin lymphoma
2. Prednisone 0.5 mg/kg/day (20 mg) as part of chemotherapy regimen
3. Supportive care: hydration, tumour lysis monitoring

### 8.6 Outcome

- **Week 3:** Complete remission of nephrotic syndrome (proteinuria resolved)
- **Month 4:** Hodgkin lymphoma in complete remission (PET-CT negative)
- **Month 12:** No MCD relapse; normal renal function

### 8.7 Teaching Points

1. Hodgkin lymphoma is the classic malignancy associated with MCD (paraneoplastic)
2. Pathophysiology: T-cell dysregulation from the lymphoma (IL-13 secreted by Reed-Sternberg cells) directly induces podocyte injury
3. Treatment of the underlying malignancy is the primary therapy; steroids alone are insufficient
4. MCD remission parallels lymphoma response
5. MCD can be the presenting feature of occult Hodgkin lymphoma; maintain high index of suspicion in adolescents with B symptoms

## 9. Case 7: Steroid-Resistant MCD Transition to FSGS

### 9.1 Presentation

A 35-year-old man with presumed MCD (diagnosed at age 32 on initial biopsy) who has failed three 16-week courses of high-dose prednisone and two courses of cyclosporine (adequately dosed, therapeutic levels). He remains nephrotic.

### 9.2 Examination

- BP: 145/95 mmHg
- 2+ pitting oedema
- No cushingoid features (minimal steroid exposure due to resistance)

### 9.3 Laboratory Findings

- UPCR: 10.2 g/g (persistently nephrotic)
- Albumin: 2.0 g/dL
- Cr: 1.5 mg/dL (eGFR 52) — declining from baseline 0.9
- Persistent steroid resistance after 16+ weeks

### 9.4 Re-Biopsy

- **LM:** 3 of 15 glomeruli show segmental sclerosis; mild interstitial fibrosis and tubular atrophy
- **IF:** Negative
- **EM:** Foot process effacement, glomerular basement membrane collapse in sclerotic segments
- **Diagnosis:** FSGS (transitioned from initial pattern of minimal change)

### 9.5 Management

1. Transition to FSGS treatment pathway
2. Higher-dose cyclosporine 5 mg/kg/day (targeting 150-200 ng/mL)
3. RAAS inhibition maximisation (lisinopril 20 mg titrated)
4. BP control target <125/75 mmHg
5. Rituximab 375 mg/m2 x 4 doses attempted (no response)
6. Listed for genetic testing (APOL1, podocin mutations)

### 9.6 Outcome

- Partial remission at 6 months (UPCR 3.5 g/g, Cr 1.6 mg/dL)
- Progressive CKD over 5 years (Cr 3.0 mg/dL at last follow-up)
- No complete remission achieved

### 9.7 Teaching Points

1. Steroid resistance in presumed MCD warrants re-biopsy to exclude FSGS
2. FSGS transition occurs in approximately 20% of initially steroid-resistant cases
3. Distinguishing MCD from FSGS on initial biopsy can be challenging due to sampling error
4. FSGS carries a worse prognosis with potential progression to ESKD
5. Genetic testing should be considered in steroid-resistant cases
6. Early identification and re-biopsy are critical for appropriate management

## 10. Case 8: Long-Term Remission After Rituximab

### 10.1 Presentation

A 19-year-old woman with steroid-dependent MCD diagnosed at age 10. She has been on prednisone 5-20 mg daily for 9 years. Multiple attempts at steroid-sparing agents failed: cyclophosphamide (cystitis), cyclosporine (nephrotoxicity, Cr 1.4 mg/dL), MMF (poor tolerability due to diarrhoea).

### 10.2 Examination

- BP: 130/85 mmHg
- Cushingoid habitus, growth retardation (height <3rd percentile)
- Osteoporosis (DEXA Z-score -2.5)

### 10.3 Management

1. Rituximab 375 mg/m2 IV weekly x 4 doses
2. CD19 monitored monthly
3. Prednisone slowly tapered from 20 mg to 0 over 16 weeks
4. Bone health: calcium, vitamin D; bisphosphonate therapy initiated

### 10.4 Outcome

- **1 month:** Remission achieved
- **6 months:** Off all immunosuppression for first time in 9 years
- **12 months:** CD19 returned; no relapse
- **24 months:** Remission maintained; steroid toxicity sequelae improving
- **36 months:** Spontaneous remission continues; no retreatment required
- **60 months:** Normal renal function, sustained remission; first pregnancy achieved

### 10.5 Teaching Points

1. Rituximab can induce durable, long-term remission in heavily pre-treated SD patients
2. Complete withdrawal of immunosuppression after rituximab is achievable
3. Prior treatment failures do not predict rituximab response
4. CD19 monitoring guides need for retreatment
5. Steroid toxicity (growth, bone density) requires active management even after remission
6. Pregnancy can be successful after sustained remission
7. This case demonstrates that even severe, protracted SD-MCD can ultimately achieve treatment-free remission
