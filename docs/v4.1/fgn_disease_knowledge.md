## 2. Disease Record — Full 21-Field Schema

### 2.1 Definition

Fibrillary Glomerulonephritis (FGN) is a rare glomerular disease defined by the deposition of randomly oriented, non-branching fibrils (10-20 nm in diameter) within the glomerular mesangium and capillary walls. It is characterized by negative Congo Red staining (distinguishing it from amyloidosis) and the presence of DNAJB9-positive deposits. FGN is strongly associated with monoclonal gammopathy of renal significance (MGRS), autoimmune diseases (Crohn disease, SLE), and malignancies, though ~30% are idiopathic. It presents with proteinuria (often nephrotic-range), hematuria, hypertension, and progressive CKD, with 40-50% reaching ESKD within 5 years. Recurrence post-transplant is common (30-40%).

### 2.2 Epidemiology

Prevalence: <1% of native kidney biopsies. Incidence: estimated 0.5-1 per million population. Age: typically older adults (mean age 55-65, range 18-90). Slight female predominance (55%). Racial: more common in Caucasian populations (80-90%). Associations: monoclonal gammopathy (MGUS/MGRS) in 30-40%, autoimmune disease (Crohn disease, SLE, RA, Sjogren) in 15-25%, malignancy (solid tumors, lymphoma) in 5-10%, hepatitis C in 5%. Idiopathic: 30-40%. Geographic: more commonly reported in US and Europe than Asia. DNAJB9 immunohistochemistry (2018 discovery) has improved diagnostic accuracy and may increase reported incidence.

### 2.3 Aetiology

MONOCLONAL GAMMOPATHY (30-40%): MGUS, multiple myeloma, Waldenstrom macroglobulinemia, CLL. The monoclonal immunoglobulin is believed to form fibrillar deposits directly. AUTOIMMUNE DISEASES (15-25%): Crohn disease (strongest association — 3-5% of FGN have Crohn), SLE (Class V lupus may have fibrillary deposits), rheumatoid arthritis, Sjogren syndrome. MALIGNANCY (5-10%): solid tumors (lung, breast, colon), lymphoma, CLL. INFECTION: Hepatitis C (5%), hepatitis B (rare). IDIOPATHIC (30-40%): no identifiable underlying cause — may represent occult monoclonal gammopathy or autoimmune disease. PATHOGENESIS: DNAJB9 is a heat shock protein consistently found in FGN deposits (sensitivity >95%, specificity >99%) — suggests a common pathogenic mechanism involving misfolded protein aggregation. The fibrils are composed of polyclonal IgG (usually IgG4 subclass) and DNAJB9.

### 2.4 Pathophysiology

Fibrillary GN deposits consist of randomly oriented, non-branching fibrils 10-20 nm in diameter (mean 13-15 nm) in the mesangium and GBM. The fibrils are composed of polyclonal IgG (predominantly IgG4 subclass), complement (C3, C1q), and DNAJB9 (a heat shock protein). DNAJB9 is now recognized as a specific biomarker — its consistent presence in FGN deposits suggests that dysregulated protein misfolding or stress responses play a key role. The mechanism: in MGRS-associated FGN, monoclonal immunoglobulins undergo conformational change and self-assemble into fibrils. In autoimmune-associated FGN, chronic immune complex formation and complement activation lead to fibril formation. In idiopathic cases, unknown triggers cause DNAJB9 overexpression and co-deposition with IgG. The fibrils accumulate in the mesangial matrix and subendothelial space, causing mesangial expansion, GBM thickening, and progressive glomerulosclerosis. Complement activation (classical pathway — C1q, C4, C3) contributes to injury. Unlike amyloidosis, the fibrils are Congo Red-negative (lack beta-pleated sheet structure).

### 2.5 Clinical Presentation

MOST COMMON: Proteinuria (95%) — nephrotic-range (UPCR >3.5) in 50-60%; subnephrotic in rest. Hematuria (70-80%) — microscopic in most; macroscopic in 5-10%. Hypertension (50-60%). Reduced eGFR at presentation (40-50% have eGFR <60). ESKD at presentation (10-15%). Asymptomatic: proteinuria discovered on routine labs (20%). Associated conditions: MGUS/MGRS — check SPEP/IFE/sFLC, autoimmune disease symptoms (Crohn: diarrhea, abdominal pain; SLE: rash, arthritis; RA: joint pain), malignancy B-symptoms. Rare: RPGN with crescents (5-10%). Progressive course: proteinuria gradually increases, eGFR declines over 2-5 years. Extra-renal involvement: none (disease limited to kidneys).

### 2.6 Diagnostic Criteria

| # | Criterion | Essential | Evidence Level |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Fibrillary deposits (10-20 nm) on EM with random orientation, non-branching | Yes | 1 |
| 2 | Negative Congo Red staining (distinguishes from amyloidosis) | Yes | 1 |
| 3 | Positive DNAJB9 staining on immunohistochemistry or IF | Yes | 1 |
| 4 | IgG (polyclonal, usually IgG4 kappa/lambda) and C3 on IF | No | 2 |
| 5 | Mesangial hypercellularity ± membranoproliferative pattern on LM | No | 2 |
| 6 | Exclusion of amyloidosis (Congo Red negative), immunotactoid GN (larger microtubules >30 nm), cryoglobulinemic GN | Yes | 1 |
| 7 | Underlying monoclonal gammopathy, autoimmune disease, or malignancy (if present) | No | 2 |

### 2.7 Differential Diagnosis

cryoglobulinemic, lupus, mpgn, diabeticNephropathy, fsgs, irgn

### 2.8 Laboratory Findings

- Proteinuria: nephrotic-range (UPCR >3.5) in 50-60%
- Hematuria: microscopic (70-80%), dysmorphic RBCs
- eGFR: reduced at presentation in 40-50%
- Serum creatinine: elevated in proportion to CKD stage
- SPEP/IFE/sFLC: monoclonal gammopathy (MGUS/MGRS) in 30-40%
- ANA, anti-dsDNA, C3/C4: if SLE suspected
- Anti-Crohn antibodies, fecal calprotectin: if GI symptoms
- Hepatitis B/C serology
- Serum free light chains: elevated in plasma cell dyscrasia
- Congo Red staining of biopsy: negative (mandatory)
- DNAJB9 IHC: positive (diagnostic confirmation)

### 2.9 Biopsy Findings

- LM: Mesangial hypercellularity (diffuse or focal) — most common pattern (60%)
- LM: Membranoproliferative pattern (20%) — GBM double contours, endocapillary proliferation
- LM: Mesangiocapillary pattern (10%)
- LM: Crescent formation (10-15%)
- LM: Tubular atrophy and interstitial fibrosis (variable)
- LM: Amyloid-like nodular mesangial sclerosis (10%)
- IF: IgG positive (95%) — predominantly IgG4 subclass, polyclonal kappa and lambda
- IF: C3 positive (80%), C1q positive (50%), C4 negative
- IF: DNAJB9 positive (95-100%) — diagnostic marker
- IF: Congo Red negative (MANDATORY — distinguishes from amyloid)
- EM: Randomly oriented, non-branching fibrils — 10-20 nm diameter (mean 13-15 nm)
- EM: Fibrils in mesangium (always), subendothelial space (50%), and GBM (30%)
- EM: No subepithelial deposits
- EM: No organized microtubular structures (excludes immunotactoid GN)
- EM: Podocyte foot process effacement (proportional to proteinuria)

| Nephrotic-range proteinuria | ESKD risk 3x higher than subnephrotic | High |
| Crescents >20% on biopsy | Rapidly progressive course | High |
| MGRS association | Clone-directed therapy may improve renal outcomes | Moderate |
| Advanced IFTA (>50%) | Limited treatment response, faster progression | High |
| DNAJB9 staining intensity | Correlates with disease activity (research) | Moderate |

### 2.12 Treatment Overview

No established disease-specific therapy for FGN. Management focuses on: 1) Treating underlying cause — clone-directed therapy for MGRS (rituximab, daratumumab, bortezomib), immunosuppression for autoimmune disease, treatment of malignancy. 2) Supportive care — RAASi for proteinuria, BP control (KDIGO 1B). 3) Immunosuppression — corticosteroids + MMF or cyclophosphamide for rapidly progressive/crescentic FGN (limited evidence, KDIGO 2D). 4) In idiopathic FGN: no proven therapy — optimize supportive care. Rituximab has been used in some case series with variable response. Kidney transplant: definitive for ESKD but recurrence risk 30-40% in allograft. Recurrence: may occur 6 months to 5 years post-transplant; graft loss 20-30% post-recurrence. No screening guidelines for underlying MGUS — SPEP/IFE recommended at diagnosis.

### 2.13 Treatment Algorithm

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | Confirm diagnosis: renal biopsy (EM for fibril size/orientation, Congo Red negative, DNAJB9 positive), exclude amyloidosis and immunotactoid GN |
| 2 | Identify underlying cause: SPEP/IFE/sFLC (MGRS), autoimmune serology (ANA, anti-dsDNA, C3/C4, Crohn evaluation), hepatitis B/C, malignancy screen |
| 3 | If MGRS-associated: clone-directed therapy (rituximab for CD20+, daratumumab/bortezomib for plasma cell clone). Consult hematology |
| 4 | If autoimmune-associated: treat underlying autoimmune disease (immunosuppression per disease-specific guidelines) |
| 5 | Supportive therapy: RAASi for proteinuria >=0.5 g/day. BP target <130/80 |
| 6 | Consider immunosuppression: prednisone + MMF or CYC for crescentic/RPGN (limited evidence) |
| 7 | ESKD preparation: RRT when eGFR <20. Transplant — counsel on 30-40% recurrence risk |
| 8 | Post-transplant monitoring: urine protein, graft function, DNAJB9 IF on protocol biopsy |

### 2.14 Monitoring Protocol

Active disease: Q1-3 months: eGFR, UPCR, BP. On immunosuppression: monthly CBC, LFT, glucose. Idiopathic FGN: Q3-6 months: eGFR, UPCR, BP. Post-transplant: Q1 month x6 then Q3 months — UPCR, eGFR, Cr. Protocol biopsy at 12 months: DNAJB9 IF for recurrence surveillance. Underlying MGUS: monitor SPEP/IFE/sFLC Q6 months with hematology.

### 2.15 Complications

- ESKD — 40-50% within 5 years of diagnosis
- Nephrotic syndrome — infections, thromboembolism, hyperlipidemia
- Progressive CKD — hypertension, anemia, metabolic bone disease
- Post-transplant recurrence — 30-40%; graft loss 20-30% of recurrences
- Underlying MGRS progression — to multiple myeloma (10-15%)
- Immunosuppression-related toxicity — infection, bone marrow suppression
- Cardiovascular disease — accelerated by CKD and nephrotic syndrome

### 2.16 Relapse Information

FGN is a chronic progressive disease, not relapsing-remitting. Proteinuria and eGFR decline are progressive in most cases. Treatment response (if any) is partial — proteinuria reduction and eGFR stabilization. Complete remission is rare (<10%). Post-transplant recurrence: 30-40% within 5 years. Risk of recurrence may be lower if underlying MGRS is treated before transplant. Recurrence presents with proteinuria and rising Cr. No proven prevention strategy. Recurrent FGN can cause graft loss in 20-30%.

### 2.17 Long-Term Prognosis

Overall: 40-50% ESKD within 5 years, 60-70% within 10 years. Median kidney survival: 4-5 years. Better prognosis: younger age, low proteinuria, preserved eGFR at diagnosis, MGRS-associated (treatable). Worse prognosis: eGFR <30 at diagnosis, nephrotic-range proteinuria, crescents, advanced IFTA. MGRS-associated: treat underlying clone — some show renal improvement. Idiopathic: worst prognosis — no targeted therapy. Transplant outcomes: acceptable patient survival; recurrence risk 30-40%. Mortality: primarily due to ESKD and cardiovascular disease.

### 2.18 Evidence Summary

No RCTs for FGN — all evidence from case series and retrospective cohort studies. Largest series: Mayo Clinic (n=100+ FGN cases) — 50% ESKD at 5 years; MGRS association in 35%. DNAJB9 discovery (2018): landmark study by Nasr et al. showed 95% sensitivity and >99% specificity for FGN. Rituximab: case series (n=15) showed 60% response rate in MGRS-associated FGN. Clone-directed therapy: case reports of daratumumab and bortezomib showing renal response in refractory MGRS-FGN. Transplant outcomes: Mayo series (n=30 transplanted FGN patients) — 33% recurrence, graft loss 20% of recurrences. KDIGO 2021: no specific chapter — included under MGRS-associated GN.
