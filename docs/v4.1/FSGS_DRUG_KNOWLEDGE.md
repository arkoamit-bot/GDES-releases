# FSGS Drug Knowledge

**Document ID**: FSGS_DK  
**Version**: 1.0  
**Date**: 2026-07-10  
**Domain**: Focal Segmental Glomerulosclerosis (FSGS)  
**Classification**: Drug Knowledge — Therapeutic Agents  

---

## 1. Overview

FSGS has no FDA-approved specific therapies. All immunosuppressive agents used are off-label, and treatment is based on clinical trial evidence and expert consensus. Corticosteroids remain the cornerstone of initial therapy for primary FSGS, making it unique among the glomerulonephritides where steroid-first approaches are standard. Supportive care with RAAS blockade and SGLT2 inhibitors forms the foundation of management for all forms of FSGS, including genetic and secondary types.

## 2. Therapeutic Agent Framework

### 2.1 Drug Categories

| Category | Agents | Role in FSGS |
|----------|--------|--------------|
| Corticosteroids | Prednisone, Prednisolone | First-line for primary FSGS |
| Calcineurin Inhibitors | Cyclosporine, Tacrolimus | Second-line for steroid-resistant/dependent |
| Antiproliferative Agents | Mycophenolate mofetil, Cyclophosphamide | Steroid-dependent, steroid-sparing |
| Biologics | Rituximab | Refractory cases, emerging evidence |
| RAAS Blockade | Ramipril (ACEi), ARBs | Supportive, all forms |
| SGLT2 Inhibitors | Empagliflozin | Supportive, all forms |
| Lipid-lowering | Atorvastatin | Nephrotic hyperlipidaemia |
| Diuretics | Spironolactone | Oedema, proteinuria reduction |

## 3. Corticosteroids

### 3.1 Prednisone/Prednisolone

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Drug class** | Glucocorticoid |
| **Mechanism** | Anti-inflammatory; immunosuppressive; direct podocyte stabilisation; reduced T-cell activation |
| **Indication** | First-line for primary FSGS (all Columbia variants except perihilar adaptive) |
| **Dose** | 1 mg/kg/day (max 80 mg) for 16 weeks |
| **Route** | Oral |
| **Key evidence** | FSGS CT Consortium; KDIGO 2021 Ch 4 |
| **Remission rate** | 40–60% in primary FSGS |
| **Unique in FSGS** | Steroid-first approach is unique among GNs; most other GNs do not use steroids as first-line |

**Steroid Protocol**
| Phase | Duration | Dose |
|-------|----------|------|
| Induction | 16 weeks | 1 mg/kg/day (max 80 mg) |
| Taper | 16–24 weeks | Reduce by 5–10 mg every 2–4 weeks |
| Maintenance | Variable | 5–10 mg/day or off if possible |

**Monitoring During Steroid Therapy**
| Parameter | Frequency |
|-----------|-----------|
| Blood pressure | Weekly initially |
| Fasting glucose | Weekly for 4 weeks, then monthly |
| Lipid profile | Baseline and at 3 months |
| Bone density | Baseline and annually |
| Ophthalmological exam | Baseline and annually |
| Infection screening | Baseline (TB, hepatitis) |
| Weight and Cushing features | Monthly |

**Adverse Effects**
- Hyperglycaemia/diabetes (10–20%)
- Hypertension (10–15%)
- Osteoporosis (long-term)
- Cataracts (cumulative dose-dependent)
- Avascular necrosis (rare, cumulative)
- Myopathy
- Psychosis (rare)
- Weight gain/Cushingoid features
- Increased infection risk

### 3.2 Prednisolone

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Drug class** | Glucocorticoid (active metabolite of prednisone) |
| **Mechanism** | Same as prednisone |
| **Indication** | Alternative to prednisone; may be preferred in hepatic impairment |
| **Dose** | Equivalent to prednisone (0.8:1 ratio) |
| **Route** | Oral |
| **Key difference** | Does not require hepatic conversion; more reliable in liver disease |

## 4. Calcineurin Inhibitors

### 4.1 Cyclosporine

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Drug class** | Calcineurin inhibitor |
| **Mechanism** | Inhibits T-cell activation via calcineurin; stabilises podocyte cytoskeleton through synaptopodin preservation |
| **Indication** | Steroid-resistant primary FSGS; steroid-dependent FSGS |
| **Dose** | 3–5 mg/kg/day in 2 divided doses |
| **Target trough** | 100–200 ng/mL (whole blood) |
| **Route** | Oral |
| **Key evidence** | FSGS CT Consortium; 60–70% response rate in resistant FSGS |
| **Duration** | 12–24 months minimum; slow taper |

**Monitoring**
| Parameter | Frequency |
|-----------|-----------|
| Trough levels | Weekly for 4 weeks, then q2–4 weeks |
| Serum creatinine | Weekly for 4 weeks, then monthly |
| Blood pressure | Weekly initially |
| Potassium | Monthly |
| Magnesium | Monthly |
| Lipid profile | Baseline and at 3 months |
| Liver function | Monthly |
| CBC | Monthly |

**Adverse Effects**
- Nephrotoxicity (acute reversible + chronic irreversible)
- Hypertension (20–30%)
- Hyperkalaemia
- Hypomagnesaemia
- Hyperlipidaemia
- Gingival hyperplasia
- Hypertrichosis
- Tremor
- Infection risk
- Malignancy (long-term)

### 4.2 Tacrolimus

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Drug class** | Calcineurin inhibitor |
| **Mechanism** | Similar to cyclosporine; binds FKBP12; inhibits calcineurin |
| **Indication** | Steroid-resistant FSGS; alternative to cyclosporine |
| **Dose** | 0.05–0.1 mg/kg/day in 2 divided doses |
| **Target trough** | 5–10 ng/mL |
| **Route** | Oral |
| **Key evidence** | Comparable efficacy to cyclosporine; less cosmetic side effects |
| **Duration** | 12–24 months minimum; slow taper |

**Monitoring**
| Parameter | Frequency |
|-----------|-----------|
| Trough levels | Weekly for 4 weeks, then q2–4 weeks |
| Serum creatinine | Weekly for 4 weeks, then monthly |
| Blood glucose | Weekly for 4 weeks (higher diabetes risk than cyclosporine) |
| Blood pressure | Weekly initially |
| Potassium | Monthly |
| Liver function | Monthly |
| CBC | Monthly |

**Adverse Effects**
- Nephrotoxicity (similar to cyclosporine)
- New-onset diabetes after transplantation (NODAT) — higher risk than cyclosporine
- Hypertension
- Hyperkalaemia
- Tremor
- Alopecia (more common than cyclosporine)
- Infection risk
- Malignancy (long-term)

**Cyclosporine vs Tacrolimus in FSGS**

| Feature | Cyclosporine | Tacrolimus |
|---------|-------------|-----------|
| Trough target | 100–200 ng/mL | 5–10 ng/mL |
| Diabetes risk | Lower | Higher |
| Cosmetic side effects | Gingival hyperplasia, hypertrichosis | Alopecia |
| Efficacy | Comparable | Comparable |
| Preferred in | Patients with diabetes risk | Patients concerned about cosmetic effects |

## 5. Antiproliferative Agents

### 5.1 Mycophenolate Mofetil (MMF)

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Drug class** | Antiproliferative (inosine monophosphate dehydrogenase inhibitor) |
| **Mechanism** | Selectively inhibits lymphocyte proliferation; reduces antibody production |
| **Indication** | Steroid-dependent FSGS (steroid-sparing agent); add-on for resistant FSGS |
| **Dose** | 1–1.5 g twice daily (2–3 g/day total) |
| **Route** | Oral |
| **Key evidence** | Observational studies; not first-line for resistant disease |
| **Duration** | 12–24 months; taper if in sustained remission |

**Monitoring**
| Parameter | Frequency |
|-----------|-----------|
| CBC with differential | Weekly for 4 weeks, then monthly |
| Liver function tests | Monthly |
| Pregnancy test (women of childbearing potential) | Before initiation |
| GI symptoms | Ongoing |

**Adverse Effects**
- Gastroactive (nausea, diarrhoea, abdominal pain) — most common
- Leukopenia/pancytopenia
- Hepatotoxicity
- Infection risk (CMV, BK virus)
- Teratogenicity (Category D)
- Increased malignancy risk (long-term)

### 5.2 Cyclophosphamide

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Drug class** | Alkylating agent |
| **Mechanism** | Cross-links DNA; inhibits cell division; immunosuppressive |
| **Indication** | Rarely used in FSGS; historical use for steroid-resistant cases |
| **Dose** | 2–3 mg/kg/day oral or IV pulse (0.5–1 g/m² monthly) |
| **Route** | Oral or intravenous |
| **Key evidence** | Limited evidence in FSGS; historical use only |
| **Duration** | 2–6 months maximum due to toxicity |

**Adverse Effects**
- Myelosuppression (dose-limiting)
- Haemorrhagic cystitis (prevent with mesna)
- Gonadal toxicity (infertility)
- Increased malignancy risk (bladder cancer, lymphoma)
- Teratogenicity
- Infection risk
- Alopecia

## 6. Biologics

### 6.1 Rituximab

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Drug class** | Anti-CD20 monoclonal antibody |
| **Mechanism** | Depletes B lymphocytes; may reduce circulating permeability factors |
| **Indication** | Refractory FSGS; steroid-dependent; post-transplant recurrence (investigational) |
| **Dose** | 375 mg/m² weekly x 4 doses OR 1g x 2 doses (2 weeks apart) |
| **Route** | Intravenous |
| **Key evidence** | Small studies; mixed results; KDIGO 2021 notes possible role |
| **Duration** | Course-based; re-dosing if relapse |

**Monitoring**
| Parameter | Frequency |
|-----------|-----------|
| CD19/CD20 counts | Before each infusion and q3 months |
| Immunoglobulin levels | Baseline and q6 months |
| Infusion reactions | During infusion |
| Infection screening | Before each course |
| Hepatitis B reactivation | Before each course |

**Adverse Effects**
- Infusion reactions (first dose)
- Infection risk (especially hepatitis B reactivation)
- Progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy (rare)
- Hypogammaglobulinaemia (with repeated courses)
- Late-onset neutropenia
- PML (JC virus reactivation)

### 6.2 Abatacept

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Drug class** | CTLA-4 Ig (co-stimulation blocker) |
| **Mechanism** | Blocks B7-1/CD28 co-stimulation; may reduce podocyte injury |
| **Indication** | Investigational for FSGS |
| **Dose** | 10 mg/kg IV q2 weeks for 4 doses, then monthly |
| **Route** | Intravenous |
| **Key evidence** | FONT trial; limited efficacy; not approved |
| **Status** | Investigational only |

### 6.3 Adalimumab

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Drug class** | Anti-TNF monoclonal antibody |
| **Mechanism** | TNF-alpha inhibition; may reduce podocyte inflammation |
| **Indication** | Investigational for FSGS |
| **Dose** | 40 mg SC weekly |
| **Route** | Subcutaneous |
| **Key evidence** | Case reports; limited data |
| **Status** | Investigational only |

## 7. Supportive Care Agents

### 7.1 Ramipril (ACE Inhibitor)

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Drug class** | ACE inhibitor |
| **Mechanism** | Reduces intraglomerular pressure; antiproteinuric; renoprotective |
| **Indication** | All forms of FSGS (genetic, primary, secondary) as renoprotective therapy |
| **Dose** | 2.5–10 mg/day |
| **Route** | Oral |
| **Key evidence** | RAAS blockade is standard supportive care in all proteinuric kidney diseases |
| **Duration** | Long-term; often lifelong |

**Monitoring**
| Parameter | Frequency |
|-----------|-----------|
| Serum creatinine | 1–2 weeks after initiation |
| Potassium | 1–2 weeks after initiation |
| Blood pressure | Ongoing |

**Adverse Effects**
- Hyperkalaemia
- Acute kidney injury (volume depletion)
- Cough (class effect)
- Angioedema (rare)

### 7.2 Empagliflozin (SGLT2 Inhibitor)

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Drug class** | Sodium-glucose cotransporter 2 inhibitor |
| **Mechanism** | Reduces intraglomerular pressure via tubuloglomerular feedback; anti-inflammatory; antifibrotic |
| **Indication** | All forms of FSGS with proteinuria; renoprotective adjunct |
| **Dose** | 10 mg/day |
| **Route** | Oral |
| **Key evidence** | DAPA-CKD, EMPA-KIDNEY trials (CKD broadly); emerging FSGS-specific data |
| **Duration** | Long-term; often lifelong |

**Monitoring**
| Parameter | Frequency |
|-----------|-----------|
| eGFR | 2–4 weeks after initiation (expected initial dip) |
| Blood glucose (if diabetic) | Ongoing |
| Genital infection symptoms | Ongoing |
| Volume status | Ongoing |

**Adverse Effects**
- Genital mycotic infections
- Volume depletion
- Initial eGFR dip (expected, not harmful)
- Diabetic ketoacidosis (rare, mainly in type 1 diabetes)

### 7.3 Atorvastatin

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Drug class** | HMG-CoA reductase inhibitor (statin) |
| **Mechanism** | Reduces cholesterol synthesis; pleiotropic anti-inflammatory effects |
| **Indication** | Nephrotic hyperlipidaemia in FSGS |
| **Dose** | 10–40 mg/day |
| **Route** | Oral |
| **Key evidence** | KDIGO recommendations for nephrotic syndrome dyslipidaemia |
| **Duration** | Long-term while proteinuric |

**Monitoring**
| Parameter | Frequency |
|-----------|-----------|
| Lipid profile | 6–12 weeks after initiation, then annually |
| Liver function tests | Baseline, then as clinically indicated |
| Muscle symptoms | Ongoing |

**Adverse Effects**
- Myopathy/rhabdomyolysis (rare)
- Hepatotoxicity
- GI symptoms

### 7.4 Spironolactone

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Drug class** | Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist |
| **Mechanism** | Reduces proteinuria; antifibrotic; diuretic effect for oedema |
| **Indication** | Proteinuria reduction; oedema management in FSGS |
| **Dose** | 25–50 mg/day |
| **Route** | Oral |
| **Key evidence** | Secondary analyses from RALES, EMPHASIS-HF; proteinuria reduction data |
| **Duration** | Long-term if tolerated |

**Monitoring**
| Parameter | Frequency |
|-----------|-----------|
| Potassium | 1–2 weeks after initiation, then monthly |
| Serum creatinine | 1–2 weeks after initiation |
| Blood pressure | Ongoing |
| Gynaecomastia (men) | Ongoing |

**Adverse Effects**
- Hyperkalaemia
- Gynaecomastia (dose-dependent, men)
- Menstrual irregularities (women)
- Acute kidney injury (volume depletion)

## 8. Novel/Investigational Therapies

### 8.1 Sparsentan (DUET Trial)

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Drug class** | Dual endothelin receptor antagonist/angiotensin receptor blocker |
| **Mechanism** | Endothelin-1 receptor blockade + AT1 receptor blockade |
| **Indication** | FSGS (investigational) |
| **Dose** | 200–400 mg/day |
| **Route** | Oral |
| **Key evidence** | DUET trial: significant proteinuria reduction vs irbesartan; FDA status uncertain |
| **Status** | Phase 3 completed; regulatory outcome pending |

### 8.2 TRAF-1 Inhibitor

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Drug class** | TNF receptor-associated factor 1 inhibitor |
| **Mechanism** | Inhibits NF-kB signalling; reduces podocyte inflammation |
| **Indication** | Investigational for FSGS |
| **Status** | Preclinical/early clinical |

### 8.3 APOL1 Inhibitors

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Drug class** | APOL1 protein inhibitors |
| **Mechanism** | Blocks APOL1 channel activity; reduces podocyte toxicity |
| **Indication** | APOL1-associated FSGS (high-risk genotype) |
| **Status** | Phase 1 clinical trials |
| **Notes** | Potential game-changer for APOL1-associated FSGS; targeted therapy |

### 8.4 Complement Inhibitors

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Drug class** | Complement pathway inhibitors (anti-C5, anti-factor B, etc.) |
| **Mechanism** | Reduces complement-mediated podocyte injury |
| **Indication** | Investigational for complement-mediated FSGS |
| **Status** | Preclinical/early clinical |
| **Notes** | May have role in specific FSGS subtypes with complement activation |

## 9. Drug Interaction Considerations

### 9.1 Key Interactions in FSGS

| Combination | Interaction | Management |
|-------------|-------------|------------|
| CNI + NSAIDs | Additive nephrotoxicity | Avoid NSAIDs in FSGS |
| CNI + ACEi/ARB | Additive renal impairment; hyperkalaemia | Monitor creatinine and potassium closely |
| CNI + statins | Increased statin levels; rhabdomyolysis risk | Use lower statin doses; monitor CK |
| MMF + antacids | Reduced MMF absorption | Separate administration by 2 hours |
| Steroids + NSAIDs | Increased GI bleeding risk | Use PPI prophylaxis |
| CNI + grapefruit | Increased CNI levels | Avoid grapefruit |
| Steroids + fluoroquinolones | Increased tendon rupture risk | Avoid combination if possible |

### 9.2 Vaccination Considerations

| Timing | Vaccine | Recommendation |
|--------|---------|----------------|
| Before immunosuppression | Influenza (annual) | Administer |
| Before immunosuppression | Pneumococcal | Administer |
| Before immunosuppression | Hepatitis B | Check immunity; vaccinate if non-immune |
| Before immunosuppression | Shingles (Shingrix) | Administer (if age >50) |
| During immunosuppression | Live vaccines | CONTRAINDICATED |
| During immunosuppression | Inactivated vaccines | Safe to administer |
| Post-transplant | All vaccines | Per transplant protocol |
