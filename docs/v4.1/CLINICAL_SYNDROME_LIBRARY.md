## 1. Nephrotic Syndrome

### Definition

Nephrotic syndrome is a clinical complex defined by the presence of heavy proteinuria (>3.5 g/24h or >3.5 g/g creatinine in adults) accompanied by hypoalbuminaemia (<30 g/L), oedema, and hyperlipidaemia. It results from disruption of the glomerular filtration barrier, particularly podocyte injury.

**Diagnostic Criteria:**
- Urine protein:creatinine ratio (uPCR) >=300 mg/mmol (>=3.5 g/g) OR 24-hour urine protein >=3.5 g
- Serum albumin <30 g/L (typically <25 g/L in full-blown syndrome)
- Generalised oedema (peripheral, periorbital, or ascites)
- Hyperlipidaemia (total cholesterol >5.2 mmol/L, LDL elevated)
- Lipiduria (oval fat bodies, "Maltese crosses" on urine microscopy)

> **Clinical Pearl:** The tetrad of heavy proteinuria, hypoalbuminaemia, oedema, and hyperlipidaemia does not need to be complete at presentation. Heavy proteinuria + hypoalbuminaemia is sufficient to diagnose nephrotic-range disease.

### Common Causes

| Disease | Typical Frequency | Key Clues |
|---|---|---|
| Minimal Change Disease (`mcd`) | Very Common (adults: 20-30%) | Young age (<40), sudden onset, steroid-responsive, normal light microscopy |
| Focal Segmental Glomerulosclerosis (`fsgs`) | Common (25-35% in adults) | Obesity, Black/African heritage, haematuria, HTN, steroid resistance common |
| Membranous Nephropathy (`membranous`) | Very Common (30-40% in Caucasians) | Middle-aged/older adults, anti-PLA2R positive, venous thromboembolism risk, subepithelial deposits |
| Diabetic Nephropathy (`diabeticNephropathy`) | Very Common (overall most common cause globally) | Diabetes >10 years, gradual onset, retinopathy, Kimmelstiel-Wilson nodules, gradual proteinuria increase |
| Lupus Nephritis (`lupus`) | Common (Class V) | Young women, ANA/anti-dsDNA positive, multi-system disease, low complements |
| IgA Nephropathy (`iga`) | Uncommon as nephrotic (10-15%) | Macroscopic haematuria (synpharyngitic), young adults, mesangial IgA |
| Amyloidosis | Uncommon | Age >60, chronic inflammation/myeloma, Congo red positive, apple-green birefringence |
| C3 Glomerulopathy (`c3`) | Uncommon | Low C3, dense deposits or C3-dominant IF, alternative pathway dysregulation |

### Rare Causes

| Disease | Typical Frequency | Key Clues |
|---|---|---|
| Fibrillary Glomerulonephritis (`fibrillaryGlomerulonephritis`) | Rare | Non-amyloid fibrils 12-20 nm, Congo red negative, anti-DNAJB9 positive |
| Membranoproliferative Glomerulonephritis (`mpgn`) | Rare | MPGN pattern, C3/C4 consumption, hepatitis C, paraproteinaemia |
| Dense Deposit Disease (`denseDepositDisease`) | Rare | C3 glomerulopathy subtype, "ribbon-like" GBM on EM, very low C3 |
| Cryoglobulinaemic Glomerulonephritis (`cryoglobulinemic`) | Rare | Hepatitis C, purpura, arthralgia, mixed cryoglobulins, C4 low |
| Drug-Induced GN (`drugInducedGn`) | Rare | NSAIDs, lithium, gold - temporal association with drug exposure |

### Clinical Clues

- **Age <40 years:** Consider `mcd` (children/young adults), `fsgs` (young adults), `iga` (young adults)
- **Age 40-65 years:** Think `membranous` (most common primary GN in this range), `fsgs`, early `diabeticNephropathy`
- **Age >65 years:** Consider `diabeticNephropathy`, amyloidosis, paraproteinaemia-related
- **Sudden onset oedema:** `mcd`, `membranous` (rapid onset)
- **Gradual onset:** `diabeticNephropathy`, `fsgs`, amyloid
- **Haematuria accompanying proteinuria:** `fsgs`, `membranous`, `lupus`, `iga` (less commonly nephrotic)
- **Obesity or BMI >30:** `fsgs` (obesity-associated or primary)
- **Black/African heritage:** `fsgs` (genetic and idiopathic more common)
- **Venous thromboembolism history:** `membranous` (high risk), `mcd` (lower but real risk)
- **Systemic symptoms:** `lupus` (arthralgia, rash), amyloid (organomegaly)
- **Chronic NSAID use:** Consider drug-induced `mcd` or `membranous`

### Laboratory Clues

- **Serum albumin <20 g/L:** Severe - consider `mcd`, `membranous`, or `fsgs`
- **Total cholesterol >10 mmol/L:** More common in `mcd` and `membranous`
- **Anti-PLA2R antibody positive:** Highly specific for primary `membranous`
- **ANA/anti-dsDNA positive, low C3/C4:** `lupus`
- **HbA1c >6.5%, retinopathy:** `diabeticNephropathy`
- **Low C3 with normal C4:** membranoproliferative, C3 glomerulopathy, post-infectious
- **Low C3 and low C4:** `cryoglobulinemic`, `lupus`
- **Serum free light chains abnormal:** Amyloid, myeloma
- **Hepatitis C positive:** `cryoglobulinemic`, `membranous`, `mpgn`
- **Urinalysis:** Oval fat bodies, fatty casts, Maltese crosses under polarised light
- **Renal ultrasound:** Normal kidney size early; enlarged kidneys in `mcd`; may be small in chronic `diabeticNephropathy`
- **Urine sediment:** Active sediment (RBC casts, dysmorphic RBCs) suggests concurrent nephritic component - consider `lupus`, `fsgs` with collapsing variant, `membranous` with superimposed crescents

### Biopsy Clues

- **Light microscopy - Normal:** `mcd`
- **Light microscopy - Focal segmental sclerosis:** `fsgs` (tip, perihilar, NOS, collapsing, cellular variants)
- **Light microscopy - Diffuse capillary wall thickening, "spikes" on silver stain:** `membranous`
- **Light microscopy - Diffuse mesangial expansion, nodules:** `diabeticNephropathy`
- **Light microscopy - Mesangial hypercellularity, crescents:** `lupus` (Class III/IV)
- **Light microscopy - Mesangial IgA deposits on IF:** `iga`
- **Light microscopy - Congo red positive, apple-green birefringence:** Amyloid
- **Immunofluorescence - Granular IgG along capillary walls:** `membranous`
- **Immunofluorescence - IgA dominant mesangial:** `iga`
- **Immunofluorescence - Negative or minimal:** `mcd`
- **Immunofluorescence - Full house (IgG, IgA, IgM, C3, C1q):** `lupus`
- **Electron microscopy - Effacement of podocyte foot processes only:** `mcd`
- **Electron microscopy - Subepithelial electron-dense deposits with spikes:** `membranous`
- **Electron microscopy - Electron-dense deposits in mesangium:** `iga`, `lupus`, `mpgn`
- **Electron microscopy - Non-amyloid microfibrils (12-20 nm):** `fibrillaryGlomerulonephritis`

### Recommended Investigations

1. Urine protein:creatinine ratio (uPCR) - confirm nephrotic-range
2. 24-hour urine protein collection if uPCR equivocal
3. Serum albumin, total protein
4. Fasting lipid profile
5. Renal function (eGFR, creatinine)
6. Urine microscopy (sediment - RBC casts, oval fat bodies, fatty casts)
7. Immunological screen: ANA, anti-dsDNA, C3, C4, IgA
8. Hepatitis B and C serology
9. Serum protein electrophoresis and immunoglobulins
10. Anti-PLA2R antibody (if `membranous` suspected)
11. HbA1c, fasting glucose (if diabetic suspected)
12. Renal ultrasound (kidney size, echogenicity, obstruction)
13. **Second-line:** Serum free light chains, kappa/lambda ratio
14. **Second-line:** Anti-THSD7A antibody (if PLA2R-negative `membranous`)
15. **Second-line:** ASO titre, C3 nephritic factor (if hypocomplementaemia)
16. **Second-line:** Cryoglobulins (if vasculitic features)
17. Renal biopsy with light microscopy, immunofluorescence, and electron microscopy

### Differential Diagnosis

| Disease | Supporting Evidence | Evidence Against | Likelihood | Urgency |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| `membranous` | PLA2R+, 40-70 y/o, VTE risk | Young age, ANA+ | High (most common primary GN) | Moderate - biopsy needed within weeks |
| `mcd` | Young, sudden onset, steroid-sensitive | Older age, HTN, haematuria | High | Moderate |
| `fsgs` | Black ancestry, obesity, HTN, haematuria, steroid resistance | Young Caucasian, sudden onset | High | Moderate |
| `diabeticNephropathy` | DM >10 y, retinopathy, gradual onset | No DM | High (if DM present) | Low - planned workup |
| `lupus` | Young female, ANA+, low C3/C4 | Male, no systemic features | Moderate | Moderate-High - assess for nephritis class |
| Amyloidosis | Age >60, organomegaly, paraprotein | Young, no systemic features | Low-Moderate | Moderate - Congo red stain critical |
| `iga` | Macroscopic haematuria, young adult | Nephrotic-range uncommon, normal C3 | Low | Low-Moderate |

### Immediate Management

- **Fluid and sodium restriction:** 1-2 g sodium/day, fluid balance to target euvolaemia
- **Diuretics:** Loop diuretics (furosemide 40-80 mg PO/IV titrated); may need combination with thiazide for diuretic resistance
- **ACEi/ARB:** Start or uptitrate for proteinuria reduction (if BP allows); aim for BP <130/80 mmHg
- **Anticoagulation:** Consider if albumin <20-25 g/L (higher risk `membranous`) - assess individual VTE risk
- **Statin:** Initiate for hyperlipidaemia (atorvastatin 20-40 mg)
- **Albumin infusion:** Only for severe symptomatic oedema with diuretic resistance - do not use routinely
- **Treat underlying cause:** Refer for renal biopsy if not yet done; if already known, initiate disease-specific therapy
- **Infection prophylaxis:** Pneumococcal vaccination, consider prophylactic antibiotics in high-risk patients with severe hypoalbuminaemia
- **Monitor:** Daily weight, strict I&O, electrolytes (risk of AKI with diuretics)

### Long-Term Evaluation

- **Biopsy confirmation:** Essential for all adult nephrotic syndrome unless clearly diabetic with established retinopathy
- **Proteinuria monitoring:** Monthly uPCR until stable; target >=50% reduction from baseline
- **Renal function:** eGFR every 1-3 months during active disease, every 3-6 months in remission
- **Serum albumin:** Monthly until >30 g/L
- **VTE prophylaxis monitoring:** If anticoagulated, monitor INR/anti-Xa
- **Steroid-sparing agent monitoring:** For those on immunosuppression - FBC, LFTs, glucose as per drug protocol
- **Bone health:** DEXA scan if prolonged corticosteroid use
- **Cardiovascular risk:** Annual lipid assessment, BP monitoring
- **Relapse monitoring:** Monthly uPCR for first year, then quarterly
- **Long-term complication screening:** Infection, thrombosis, AKI, CKD progression

### Linked GDES Diseases

- `mcd`
- `fsgs`
- `membranous`
- `diabeticNephropathy`
- `lupus`
- `iga`
- `mpgn`
- `c3`
- `denseDepositDisease`
- `fibrillaryGlomerulonephritis`
- `cryoglobulinemic`
- `drugInducedGn`

### Expected Outcomes

- **`mcd`:** Excellent prognosis; >90% achieve complete remission with corticosteroids. Relapse rate 50-70% but steroid-dependent disease manageable with calcineurin inhibitors or rituximab. Rare progression to CKD.
- **`fsgs`:** Variable; 30-50% achieve remission with immunosuppression. Genetic forms have poorer response to steroids. 30-40% progress to ESKD within 10 years. Post-transplant recurrence risk 30% for primary FSGS.
- **`membranous`:** 30-40% spontaneous remission; 70% respond to immunosuppression (rituximab or cyclophosphamide/steroids). 20-30% progress to ESKD over 10 years. VTE risk persists during active nephrotic range.
- **`diabeticNephropathy`:** Progressive with 5% per year decline in eGFR once proteinuria established. SGLT2 inhibitors, ACEi/ARB slow progression. Median time to ESKD 10-20 years from onset of proteinuria.
- **`lupus` (Class V):** Variable; 50-70% respond to immunosuppression. Relapse common. 20-30% progress to ESKD at 10 years.
- **`iga`:** 25% develop nephrotic syndrome; usually concurrent nephritic features. 20-30% progress to ESKD at 20 years if severe.
- **Amyloid:** Poor prognosis; median survival 2-3 years from diagnosis without treatment of underlying cause.

## 2. Nephritic Syndrome

### Definition

Nephritic syndrome is a clinical complex characterised by glomerular inflammation resulting in haematuria (typically dysmorphic RBCs or RBC casts), hypertension, oliguria, and a variable degree of proteinuria (usually sub-nephrotic but can reach nephrotic range). Oedema is typically periorbital and due to salt and water retention rather than hypoalbuminaemia.

**Diagnostic Criteria:**
- Haematuria with dysmorphic RBCs or RBC casts on urine microscopy
- Hypertension (new or worsening)
- Oliguria (urine output <400 mL/24h or <0.5 mL/kg/h)
- Rising serum creatinine (acute or subacute)
- Proteinuria (typically 0.5-3.0 g/day, sub-nephrotic)
- Oedema (periorbital, peripheral)

> **Clinical Pearl:** Nephritic syndrome reflects active glomerular inflammation. The presence of RBC casts is pathognomonic of glomerulonephritis. Absence of RBC casts does not exclude GN but makes it less likely.

### Common Causes

| Disease | Typical Frequency | Key Clues |
|---|---|---|
| IgA Nephropathy (`iga`) | Very Common | Young adults, macroscopic haematuria (synpharyngitic), recurrent episodes, mesangial IgA |
| Post-Infectious GN (`irgn`) | Common | Children/young adults, 1-2 weeks after strep pharyngitis, low C3, "starry sky" IF |
| Lupus Nephritis (`lupus`) | Common | Young women, ANA/anti-dsDNA+, low C3/C4, full-house IF |
| ANCA-Associated Vasculitis (`anca`) | Common | Age >50, systemic features (sinusitis, lung nodules), pauci-immune, c-ANCA/PR3 or p-ANCA/MPO |
| Anti-GBM Disease (`antiGbm`) | Uncommon | Bimodal age (20-30, 50-70), pulmonary haemorrhage, linear IgG, anti-GBM antibody+ |
| C3 Glomerulopathy (`c3`) | Uncommon | Low C3, C3-dominant IF, alternative pathway activation |
| MPGN Pattern (`mpgn`) | Uncommon | Hepatitis C, cryoglobulinaemia, mixed immune complex deposits |
| Diabetic Nephropathy (`diabeticNephropathy`) | Uncommon (as nephritic) | DM, usually nephrotic; may have nephritic picture if superimposed infection/injury |

### Rare Causes

| Disease | Typical Frequency | Key Clues |
|---|---|---|
| Cryoglobulinaemic GN (`cryoglobulinemic`) | Rare | Hepatitis C, purpura, arthralgia, C4 very low, subendothelial deposits |
| Drug-Induced GN (`drugInducedGn`) | Rare | NSAIDs, checkpoint inhibitors, temporal association |
| Fibrillary Glomerulonephritis (`fibrillaryGlomerulonephritis`) | Rare | Non-amyloid fibrils, anti-DNAJB9, older adults |
| Dense Deposit Disease (`denseDepositDisease`) | Rare | C3 glomerulopathy, very low C3, ribbon-like GBM on EM |
| Thin Basement Membrane Nephropathy (`thinBasementMembrane`) | Rare (as nephritic) | Familial microscopic haematuria, thin GBM on EM, usually non-nephritic |

### Clinical Clues

- **Macroscopic haematuria after URI (synpharyngitic):** `iga` (within days)
- **Macroscopic haematuria 1-2 weeks post-pharyngitis:** `irgn` (post-streptococcal)
- **Young female, rash, joint pain, oral ulcers:** `lupus`
- **Older male, sinusitis, pulmonary nodules, neuropathy:** `anca`
- **Haemoptysis + haematuria (pulmonary-renal):** `antiGbm`, `anca` with lung, `lupus`
- **Palpable purpura + haematuria:** `cryoglobulinemic`, systemic vasculitis
- **Family history of haematuria:** `alport`, `thinBasementMembrane`
- **Recent infection (any):** `irgn`, post-streptococcal
- **History of transplant:** `antibodyMediatedRejection`, `tCellMediatedRejection`
- **Onset during pregnancy:** Pre-eclampsia, `lupus` nephritis, TMA
- **Low C3, normal C4:** `irgn`, C3 glomerulopathy, `mpgn`
- **Low C3, low C4:** `lupus`, `cryoglobulinemic`

### Laboratory Clues

- **Dysmorphic RBCs/RBC casts:** Glomerular source - confirms glomerular disease
- **Serum creatinine rising:** Active inflammation, assess severity and acuity
- **Proteinuria 0.5-3.0 g/day:** Sub-nephrotic - typical of nephritic pattern
- **Low C3, normal C4:** Post-infectious GN, C3 glomerulopathy, MPGN
- **Low C3, low C4:** Lupus, cryoglobulinaemia, shunt nephritis
- **ANA/anti-dsDNA positive:** Lupus
- **c-ANCA/PR3 positive:** Granulomatosis with polyangiitis
- **p-ANCA/MPO positive:** Microscopic polyangiitis, EGPA, drug-induced
- **Anti-GBM antibody positive:** Anti-GBM disease
- **ASO titre/anti-DNase B elevated:** Post-streptococcal GN
- **Hepatitis B and C:** Cryoglobulinaemia, MPGN
- **Serum cryoglobulins:** Cryoglobulinaemic GN
- **Urinary RBC casts:** Pathognomonic of glomerulonephritis - urgent assessment needed

### Biopsy Clues

- **Diffuse proliferative GN with subepithelial "humps" on EM:** `irgn`
- **Mesangial hypercellularity with IgA on IF:** `iga`
- **Full-house IF pattern:** `lupus`
- **Pauci-immune GN (minimal/no IF deposits, crescents):** `anca`, `antiGbm`
- **Linear IgG on IF:** `antiGbm`
- **C3-dominant IF with dense deposits on EM:** C3 glomerulopathy, `denseDepositDisease`
- **Subendothelial deposits with MPGN pattern:** `lupus`, `cryoglobulinemic`, `mpgn`
- **Crescents in >50% glomeruli:** RPGN - urgent treatment
- **Segmental necrosis and crescents:** ANCA vasculitis
- **Diffuse endocapillary proliferation:** `irgn`, early `lupus`

### Recommended Investigations

1. Urine microscopy - dysmorphic RBCs, RBC casts, WBC casts
2. uPCR - quantify proteinuria
3. Serum creatinine, eGFR, electrolytes
4. C3, C4, total haemolytic complement (CH50)
5. ANA, anti-dsDNA
6. c-ANCA/PR3, p-ANCA/MPO
7. Anti-GBM antibody
8. Hepatitis B surface antigen, Hepatitis C antibody
9. ASO titre, anti-DNase B (if post-infectious suspected)
10. Serum immunoglobulins
11. FBC, CRP, ESR
12. Renal ultrasound
13. **Second-line:** Serum cryoglobulins
14. **Second-line:** Anti-PLA2R (if `membranous` component)
15. **Second-line:** Complement factors H, I, C3 nephritic factor
16. **Second-line:** Renal biopsy with LM, IF, EM - essential for definitive diagnosis

### Differential Diagnosis

| Disease | Supporting Evidence | Evidence Against | Likelihood | Urgency |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| `iga` | Young adult, synpharyngitic haematuria, recurrent | Age >50, systemic features | High | Moderate - elective biopsy |
| `irgn` | Child, 1-2 wk post-infection, low C3, hypocomplementaemia | Adult, no antecedent infection | Moderate | Low - usually self-limiting |
| `lupus` | Young female, ANA+, multi-system | Male, no systemic features | Moderate | Moderate-High |
| `anca` | Age >50, sinusitis/lung, pauci-immune | Young, no systemic features | Moderate | High - urgent treatment |
| `antiGbm` | Haemoptysis + haematuria, linear IgG | No pulmonary symptoms | Low-Moderate | Critical - emergency |
| `c3` | Low C3, C3-dominant IF, no immune complexes | Normal C3 | Low-Moderate | Moderate |
| `mpgn` | Hepatitis C, mixed deposits | No viral infection | Low | Moderate |

### Immediate Management

- **Stabilise haemodynamics:** IV fluids if oliguric/prehypotensive; avoid fluid overload
- **Blood pressure control:** Target <140/90 mmHg (or <130/80 if proteinuric); ACEi/ARB if creatinine stable, otherwise CCB or labetalol
- **Urgent nephrology referral:** If creatinine rapidly rising, or anti-GBM/ANCA positive
- **Pulse methylprednisolone:** If RPGN suspected (crescents >50%) - 500-1000 mg IV x 3 days
- **Plasma exchange:** For anti-GBM disease (urgent), severe ANCA vasculitis with pulmonary haemorrhage
- **Cyclophosphamide or rituximab:** Initiate for ANCA vasculitis or severe `lupus` nephritis
- **Supportive care:** Electrolyte monitoring (hyperkalaemia risk), fluid balance
- **Avoid nephrotoxins:** Stop NSAIDs, ACEi (if AKI), aminoglycosides
- **Monitor:** Daily weights, strict I&O, electrolytes (K+, PO4), serum creatinine q24-48h

### Long-Term Evaluation

- **Renal biopsy:** Essential for all cases of nephritic syndrome unless clearly post-infectious with spontaneous resolution
- **Disease-specific monitoring:** See individual disease modules
- **Proteinuria target:** <0.5 g/day (or <0.3 g/day if hypertension present)
- **BP target:** <130/80 mmHg
- **eGFR monitoring:** Every 1-3 months during active disease
- **Relapse surveillance:** ANCA titres, anti-dsDNA, cryoglobulins as appropriate
- **Drug monitoring:** Immunosuppression protocols (FBC, LFTs, glucose)
- **Vaccination:** Pneumococcal, influenza - before/during immunosuppression
- **CKD surveillance:** If eGFR <60 - bone profile, Hb, urate, urinalysis

- `iga`
- `irgn`
- `lupus`
- `anca`
- `antiGbm`
- `c3`
- `mpgn`
- `cryoglobulinemic`
- `denseDepositDisease`
- `drugInducedGn`
- `alport`
- `fibrillaryGlomerulonephritis`
- `thinBasementMembrane`

### Expected Outcomes

- **`iga`:** Variable; 25% spontaneous remission, 20-30% progress to ESKD at 20 years. Immunosuppression (steroids, mycophenolate) may slow progression if severe histology.
- **`irgn`:** Excellent prognosis in children; >95% spontaneous resolution. Adults have higher risk of persistent disease (15-30%).
- **`lupus` (Class III/IV):** 70-90% respond to induction therapy (cyclophosphamide or mycophenolate + steroids). Relapse rate 30-50%. 10-20% progress to ESKD at 10 years.
- **`anca`:** 80-90% achieve remission with induction therapy. Relapse rate 30-50%. Renal survival >80% with early treatment. Poorer outcomes with pulmonary haemorrhage.
- **`antiGbm`:** Poor prognosis without treatment; with early plasma exchange + immunosuppression, 50% achieve renal survival. Worse if creatinine >600 umol/L at presentation or dialysis-dependent.
- **`c3`:** Variable; 50% progress to ESKD at 10 years. Limited treatment options. Complement inhibitors under investigation.
- **`mpgn`:** Depends on underlying cause. Hepatitis C-related MPGN improves with antiviral therapy. Idiopathic has variable prognosis.

## 3. Rapidly Progressive Glomerulonephritis (RPGN)

### Definition

Rapidly progressive glomerulonephritis (RPGN) is a clinical syndrome defined by rapid loss of renal function over days to weeks, accompanied by active urinary sediment (RBC casts, dysmorphic haematuria) and characteristic biopsy findings of crescentic glomerulonephritis. It is a nephrological emergency.

**Diagnostic Criteria:**
- Rapid decline in eGFR (doubling of creatinine or halving of eGFR within weeks to 3 months)
- Active urine sediment: RBC casts, dysmorphic RBCs, variable proteinuria
- Crescentic GN on renal biopsy (>20-50% of glomeruli with crescents, depending on classification)
- May be associated with systemic features depending on underlying aetiology

**Classification by Immunofluorescence Pattern:**
- **Type I (Anti-GBM):** Linear IgG deposits - `antiGbm`
- **Type II (Immune complex):** Granular deposits - `lupus`, `iga`, postInfectiousGN, `mpgn`
- **Type III (Pauci-immune):** Minimal/no deposits - `anca` (80%), idiopathic (20%)

> **Clinical Pearl:** RPGN is a medical emergency. Any patient with rising creatinine + active urine sediment should be considered for urgent biopsy and empiric treatment while awaiting results if clinical suspicion is high.

### Common Causes

| Disease | Typical Frequency | Key Clues |
|---|---|---|
| ANCA-Associated Vasculitis (`anca`) | Very Common (55-60%) | Age >50, systemic features, pauci-immune, p-ANCA or c-ANCA |
| Anti-GBM Disease (`antiGbm`) | Common (15-20%) | Bimodal age, pulmonary haemorrhage, linear IgG, anti-GBM Ab+ |
| Lupus Nephritis (`lupus`) | Common (10-15%) | Young female, ANA/anti-dsDNA+, full-house IF, low complements |
| IgA Nephropathy (`iga`) | Uncommon (5-10%) | Young adult, macroscopic haematuria, mesangial IgA, crescents |
| Post-Infectious GN (`irgn`) | Uncommon | Children, post-streptococcal, low C3, subepithelial humps |
| MPGN with crescents (`mpgn`) | Uncommon | Hepatitis C, cryoglobulinaemia, mixed deposits |

### Rare Causes

| Disease | Typical Frequency | Key Clues |
|---|---|---|
| Cryoglobulinaemic GN with crescents (`cryoglobulinemic`) | Rare | Hepatitis C, purpura, very low C4 |
| Drug-Induced GN (`drugInducedGn`) | Rare | Checkpoint inhibitors, temporal drug association |
| Fibrillary Glomerulonephritis with crescents (`fibrillaryGlomerulonephritis`) | Rare | Anti-DNAJB9, non-amyloid fibrils |
| C3 Glomerulopathy with crescents (`c3`) | Rare | Low C3, C3-dominant IF, alternative pathway |
| Polyarteritis Nodosa (renal) | Rare | Medium-vessel vasculitis, renal artery aneurysms, mesenteric ischaemia |

### Clinical Clues

- **Rapidly rising creatinine over days-weeks:** RPGN - urgent assessment
- **Haemoptysis:** `antiGbm`, `anca` with pulmonary involvement, severe `lupus`
- **Upper respiratory symptoms (sinusitis, otitis):** `anca` (granulomatosis with polyangiitis)
- **Skin vasculitis (palpable purpura):** `anca`, `cryoglobulinemic`, `lupus`
- **Joint pain/arthritis:** `lupus`, `anca`, cryoglobulinaemia
- **Age >50 with systemic features:** `anca` (most common cause of RPGN in this age group)
- **Young woman with rash, oral ulcers:** `lupus`
- **Age 20-30 or 50-70, pulmonary haemorrhage:** `antiGbm`
- **Oliguria/anuria:** Severe RPGN - may need dialysis
- **Fever, weight loss:** `anca`, infection-related GN, `lupus`
- **Mononeuritis multiplex:** `anca`, cryoglobulinaemia

### Laboratory Clues

- **Rising creatinine over days to weeks:** hallmark of RPGN
- **RBC casts on urine microscopy:** active glomerular disease
- **Proteinuria:** usually sub-nephrotic (0.5-3.0 g/day) but can be nephrotic
- **Anti-GBM antibody positive:** Type I RPGN - `antiGbm`
- **c-ANCA/PR3 positive:** GPA - `anca`
- **p-ANCA/MPO positive:** MPA or EGPA - `anca`
- **ANA/anti-dsDNA positive, low C3/C4:** `lupus`
- **Very low C4 with normal/low C3:** `cryoglobulinemic`
- **C3 low, C4 normal:** `irgn`, C3 glomerulopathy
- **Low Hb (normocytic anaemia of chronic disease or pulmonary haemorrhage):** `anca`, `antiGbm`, `lupus`
- **Eosinophilia:** EGPA (`anca`) - drug-induced
- **CRP/ESR elevated:** `anca`, `lupus`, infection-related
- **ABG:** Low pO2 if pulmonary haemorrhage (`antiGbm`, `anca`, `lupus`)

### Biopsy Clues

- **>50% crescents (cellular, fibrocellular, fibrous):** Severe RPGN
- **Linear IgG on IF:** `antiGbm` (Type I)
- **Full-house IF pattern:** `lupus` (Type II)
- **Pauci-immune (minimal/no IF deposits):** `anca` (Type III) - most common
- **Granular mesangial/capillary wall deposits:** Immune complex (Type II) - `iga`, `mpgn`, `irgn`
- **Necrotising lesions (segmental):** `anca`
- **Global sclerosis:** Chronic irreversible damage - poor prognosis
- **Interstitial fibrosis/tubular atrophy (IFTA):** Chronicity - limits recovery potential
- **"Wire-loop" lesions:** `lupus` (Class IV)

> **Clinical Pearl:** The degree of crescent formation and chronicity (IFTA, global sclerosis) determines prognosis. Cellular crescents are potentially reversible; fibrous crescents are not.

### Recommended Investigations

1. Serum creatinine, eGFR - STAT and serial q12-24h
2. Urine microscopy - RBC casts (critical)
3. uPCR
4. Anti-GBM antibody - STAT (do not delay)
5. c-ANCA/PR3, p-ANCA/MPO - STAT
6. ANA, anti-dsDNA
7. C3, C4
8. FBC, CRP, ESR
9. Hepatitis B and C serology
10. Serum immunoglobulins
11. Chest X-ray (pulmonary haemorrhage, nodules)
12. ABG if haemoptysis (CO diffusing capacity)
13. **Second-line:** Serum cryoglobulins
14. **Second-line:** Anti-DNAJB9 (if fibrillary suspected)
15. **Second-line:** Renal biopsy - URGENT within 24-48 hours if safe
16. **Second-line:** CT chest (if ANCA vasculitis - sinus, lung assessment)

### Differential Diagnosis

| Disease | Supporting Evidence | Evidence Against | Likelihood | Urgency |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| `anca` | Age >50, systemic features, pauci-immune | Young, isolated renal | High (>50%) | Critical - start treatment immediately |
| `antiGbm` | Haemoptysis, linear IgG, anti-GBM Ab+ | No pulmonary symptoms, young female | Moderate | Critical - plasma exchange needed |
| `lupus` | Young female, ANA+, multi-system | Male, no systemic features | Moderate | High - immunosuppression needed |
| `iga` | Young adult, synpharyngitic haematuria | Age >50, systemic features | Low-Moderate | High - assess severity |
| `irgn` | Child, post-infection, low C3 | Adult, no infection history | Low | Moderate - usually self-limiting |
| `cryoglobulinemic` | Hepatitis C, purpura, very low C4 | No hep C, no systemic features | Low | High - treat underlying cause |
| TTP/HUS (`thromboticMicroangiopathy) | MAHA, thrombocytopenia, schistocytes | Normal platelets, no MAHA | Low | Critical - plasma exchange |

### Immediate Management

- **Urgent nephrology admission:** All RPGN patients require inpatient management
- **Empiric immunosuppression:** If biopsy delayed and high clinical suspicion:
  - Pulse methylprednisolone 500-1000 mg IV daily x 3 days
  - Oral prednisolone 1 mg/kg/day (max 60 mg) after IV course
- **Plasma exchange (PLEX):** Indicated for:
  - Anti-GBM disease (mandatory - 50-60 mL/kg, alternate days x 7-14 sessions)
  - Severe ANCA vasculitis with pulmonary haemorrhage
  - RPGN with dialysis dependence (consider, though benefit debated)
- **Cyclophosphamide:** IV pulse (15 mg/kg q2-3 weeks x 3-6 pulses) or oral (2 mg/kg/day x 3-6 months) for ANCA vasculitis and severe `lupus`
  - **OR Rituximab:** 375 mg/m2 weekly x 4 or 1g x 2 (2 weeks apart) - non-inferior to cyclophosphamide for ANCA vasculitis
- **Infection prophylaxis:** Trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole (PJP), consider antivirals
- **Dialysis:** If hyperkalaemia, fluid overload, uraemic symptoms - may be temporary
- **Blood pressure control:** IV labetalol or nicardipine if hypertensive emergency
- **Monitor:** Daily creatinine, electrolytes, urine output, fluid balance

### Long-Term Evaluation

- **Maintenance immunosuppression:** Azathioprine (2 mg/kg/day) or rituximab (500 mg q6 months) for ANCA vasculitis - minimum 18-24 months
- **Steroid taper:** Slow reduction over 3-6 months guided by disease activity
- **Relapse monitoring:** ANCA titres (PR3 more relapsing than MPO), anti-dsDNA, urinalysis, creatinine
- **Renal recovery assessment:** Repeat creatinine at 3, 6, 12 months
- **CKD management:** If eGFR does not recover - standard CKD care
- **Bone health:** DEXA scan if prolonged steroids; bisphosphonate prophylaxis
- **Vaccination:** Pneumococcal, influenza, varicella (before rituximab)
- **Cancer screening:** Cyclophosphamide - bladder cancer risk (haematuria surveillance)
- **Transplant planning:** If ESKD - disease must be in remission >=12 months before transplant

### Linked GDES Diseases

- `anca`
- `antiGbm`
- `lupus`
- `iga`
- `irgn`
- `cryoglobulinemic`
- `mpgn`
- `drugInducedGn`
- `fibrillaryGlomerulonephritis`
- `c3`

### Expected Outcomes

- **`anca` (Type III):** 80-90% achieve remission with induction. 30-50% relapse. Renal survival 70-80% if treated early; worse if dialysis-dependent at presentation (>80% risk of permanent ESKD).
- **`antiGbm` (Type I):** Without treatment - universal ESKD/death. With PLEX + immunosuppression - 50% renal survival. Poor if creatinine >600 umol/L or dialysis-dependent at presentation. Relapse rare.
- **`lupus` (Class III/IV):** 70-90% respond to induction. Relapse rate 30-50%. 10-20% ESKD at 10 years with modern therapy.
- **`iga` with crescents:** 30-50% recover renal function with immunosuppression. Prognosis correlates with % crescents and chronicity.
- **`irgn`:** Excellent in children (>95% recovery). Adults: 60-80% recover; 20% may progress to CKD.
- **General rule:** Cellular crescents = potentially reversible. Fibrous crescents + IFTA = irreversible. Early treatment is the single most important determinant of outcome.

## 4. Asymptomatic Hematuria

### Definition

Asymptomatic haematuria is the incidental finding of blood in the urine (>=3 RBCs per high-power field on microscopy, or positive dipstick for blood on >=2 occasions) in the absence of symptoms such as dysuria, flank pain, renal colic, or visible discolouration. It may be isolated or accompanied by mild proteinuria or hypertension.

**Diagnostic Criteria:**
- Positive urine dipstick for blood on >=2 occasions (ideally separated by 2-4 weeks)
- Confirmed by urine microscopy: >=3 RBCs/HPF
- No urinary symptoms (no dysuria, frequency, pain, or visible haematuria)
- No recent vigorous exercise, trauma, menstruation, or catheterisation
- Negative urine culture (to exclude UTI as cause)

> **Clinical Pearl:** Asymptomatic haematuria is common (5-20% of general population on dipstick screening). The challenge is distinguishing benign causes (thin basement membrane, early IgA) from significant pathology. Persistent microscopic haematuria warrants nephrology referral.

### Common Causes

| Disease | Typical Frequency | Key Clues |
|---|---|---|
| IgA Nephropathy (`iga`) | Very Common (most common GN cause) | Young adults (20-40), may have intermittent macroscopic episodes, mesangial IgA |
| Thin Basement Membrane Nephropathy (`thinBasementMembrane`) | Very Common (familial microscopic haematuria) | Family history, benign course, thin GBM on EM, no proteinuria/HTN |
| Urological Causes (stones, tumour, infection) | Very Common | Age >40, painless visible haematuria, risk factors for urological malignancy |
| Alport Syndrome (`alport`) | Uncommon | Family history of ESKD/deafness, sensorineural hearing loss, abnormal GBM on EM |
| Lupus Nephritis (`lupus`) | Uncommon | Young female, ANA+, systemic features |
| Early Diabetic Nephropathy (`diabeticNephropathy`) | Uncommon | DM, microalbuminuria precedes haematuria |
| Focal Segmental Glomerulosclerosis (`fsgs`) | Uncommon | HTN, proteinuria may be present, Black/African ancestry |
| Membranous Nephropathy (`membranous`) | Uncommon | Usually nephrotic; microscopic haematuria may be early feature |

### Rare Causes

| Disease | Typical Frequency | Key Clues |
|---|---|---|
| C3 Glomerulopathy (`c3`) | Rare | Low C3, C3-dominant IF |
| Cryoglobulinaemic GN (`cryoglobulinemic`) | Rare | Hepatitis C, systemic features |
| Drug-Induced GN (`drugInducedGn`) | Rare | NSAIDs, temporal association |
| Fibrillary Glomerulonephritis (`fibrillaryGlomerulonephritis`) | Rare | Anti-DNAJB9, non-amyloid fibrils |
| Renal Cell Carcinoma | Rare (but important to exclude) | Age >50, risk factors, incidental finding on imaging |

### Clinical Clues

- **Young adult with persistent microscopic haematuria:** `iga` or `thinBasementMembrane`
- **Family history of haematuria/ESKD/deafness:** `alport` (X-linked most common), `thinBasementMembrane`
- **Intermittent macroscopic haematuria (synpharyngitic):** `iga`
- **Isolated microscopic haematuria, no proteinuria, no HTN:** `thinBasementMembrane` (most likely benign)
- **Age >40, painless visible haematuria:** Urological malignancy until proven otherwise - urgent CT urogram
- **Diabetes + haematuria:** `diabeticNephropathy` (haematuria unusual; consider alternative diagnosis)
- **Female, 20-40, haematuria + arthralgia:** `lupus`
- **Recurrent UTI with haematuria:** Exclude structural abnormality
- **Family history of CKD/deafness in males:** `alport` (X-linked)
- **Haematuria + hearing loss:** `alport`

### Laboratory Clues

- **Persistent microscopic haematuria (>=2 occasions):** Warrants investigation
- **No proteinuria:** Favourable - `thinBasementMembrane`, early `iga`
- **Microalbuminuria (ACR 3-30 mg/mmol):** Consider early `iga`, `diabeticNephropathy`, `fsgs`
- **Proteinuria >1 g/day:** More significant - consider `iga` (variant), `membranous`, `fsgs`
- **Normal renal function:** Favourable prognosis
- **Low C3:** `c3` glomerulopathy, post-infectious
- **ANA positive:** `lupus`
- **Family screening abnormal:** `alport` (carrier females may have microscopic haematuria)
- **Urine cytology:** If urological malignancy suspected
- **CT urogram:** If age >40 with risk factors for urological malignancy

### Biopsy Clues

- **Mesangial IgA deposits on IF:** `iga`
- **Thin GBM (<250 nm in adults) on EM:** `thinBasementMembrane`
- **Normal GBM, podocyte effacement only:** `mcd` (unlikely in this context)
- **Abnormal GBM (basket-weave pattern) on EM:** `alport`
- **C3-dominant IF:** C3 glomerulopathy
- **Normal light microscopy and IF:** Likely `thinBasementMembrane` or pre-clinical `iga`
- **Positive Congo red:** Amyloid (rare cause)

> **Clinical Pearl:** Biopsy is NOT routinely indicated for isolated microscopic haematuria. Indications include: persistent haematuria + proteinuria >0.5 g/day, hypertension, declining eGFR, or family history of genetic GN.

### Recommended Investigations

1. Repeat urine microscopy on >=2 occasions (2-4 weeks apart)
2. uPCR - quantify proteinuria
3. Blood pressure measurement
4. Serum creatinine, eGFR
5. Urine culture (exclude UTI)
6. **If age >40 or risk factors:** CT urogram (urological malignancy exclusion)
7. **If suspected renal cause:**
   - C3, C4
   - ANA, anti-dsDNA
   - IgA level
   - Hepatitis B and C
8. **If family history of haematuria/ESKD:**
   - Family screening (urine dipstick, BP, creatinine)
   - Consider genetic testing for `alport` (COL4A3/A4/A5 mutations)
9. **Second-line (if proteinuria or declining eGFR):** Renal biopsy
10. **Second-line:** Hearing assessment if `alport` suspected

### Differential Diagnosis

| Disease | Supporting Evidence | Evidence Against | Likelihood | Urgency |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| `iga` | Young adult, recurrent haematuria, may have macroscopic episodes | No haematuria history, older age | High | Low - elective workup |
| `thinBasementMembrane` | Family history, isolated microscopic haematuria, no proteinuria/HTN | No family history, proteinuria present | High | Low - benign |
| Urological cause | Age >40, risk factors, painless visible haematuria | Young, no risk factors | Moderate (age-dependent) | Moderate - CT urogram |
| `alport` | Family history of ESKD/deafness, male, hearing loss | No family history, normal hearing | Low-Moderate | Moderate |
| `lupus` | Young female, ANA+, systemic features | Male, no systemic features | Low | Moderate |
| Early `diabeticNephropathy` | DM, microalbuminuria | No DM | Low | Low |
| `fsgs` | Proteinuria, HTN, Black ancestry | No proteinuria, normal BP | Low | Low-Moderate |

### Immediate Management

- **No immediate treatment needed** for isolated microscopic haematuria without proteinuria/HTN
- **Investigate urological causes** if age >40 or risk factors - CT urogram
- **Treat UTI** if urine culture positive
- **ACEi/ARB:** If proteinuria >0.5 g/day or hypertension present
- **Nephrology referral:** If proteinuria >0.5 g/day, hypertension, declining eGFR, or family history of genetic GN
- **Observation:** If isolated microscopic haematuria with no other features - annual monitoring

### Long-Term Evaluation

- **Annual monitoring:** uPCR, creatinine, BP
- **If proteinuria develops:** Reassess and consider biopsy
- **If haematuria resolves:** Continue annual screening for 2 years
- **Family screening:** If genetic cause identified (Alport, thin BM)
- **Urological follow-up:** If urological cause excluded - reassure but continue surveillance
- **Avoid nephrotoxins:** NSAIDs, aminoglycosides
- **ACEi/ARB:** If proteinuria/HTN - renoprotective
- **Pregnancy counselling:** If Alport or thin BM - risk of transmission
- **Annual urine ACR:** Screen for microalbuminuria progression

### Linked GDES Diseases

- `iga`
- `thinBasementMembrane`
- `alport`
- `lupus`
- `diabeticNephropathy`
- `fsgs`
- `membranous`
- `c3`
- `drugInducedGn`

### Expected Outcomes

- **`iga`:** 25% spontaneous remission of microscopic haematuria. 20-30% develop proteinuria/HTN over 20 years. Overall good prognosis with modern management.
- **`thinBasementMembrane`:** Excellent prognosis. Persistent microscopic haematuria but virtually no progression to CKD. Reassurance is key.
- **`alport`:** Variable by genotype. X-linked: 90% of males progress to ESKD by age 40. Female carriers: 12% by age 60, variable expressivity. Deafness common in males by age 20.
- **Urological causes:** Excellent if malignancy detected early; poor if advanced.
- **`lupus`:** Excellent for microscopic haematuria alone (Class I/II). Close monitoring for progression.
- **Overall:** Isolated microscopic haematuria without proteinuria/HTN has an excellent prognosis. The key is excluding significant glomerular or urological pathology.

## 5. Isolated Proteinuria (Non-Nephrotic)

### Definition

Isolated proteinuria is the presence of abnormal amounts of protein in the urine (uPCR >30 mg/mmol or >300 mg/g) in the absence of significant haematuria, hypertension, or reduced eGFR, and below nephrotic range (<3.5 g/24h). It may be transient (orthostatic, fever, exercise) or persistent (indicating underlying glomerular or tubular disease).

**Diagnostic Criteria:**
- uPCR >30 mg/mmol (>300 mg/g) or urine ACR >30 mg/mmol (>300 mg/g)
- Absence of significant haematuria (<5 RBCs/HPF)
- Blood pressure <140/90 mmHg (or well-controlled on treatment)
- eGFR >60 mL/min/1.73m2
- Proteinuria <3.5 g/24h (non-nephrotic range)
- Confirmed on >=2 occasions (to exclude transient causes)

**Classification:**
- **Transient:** Orthostatic (postural), fever, exercise, emotional stress
- **Persistent:** Glomerular (early GN, FSGS, `membranous`) or tubular (interstitial nephritis, tubulointerstitial disease)
- **Overflow:** Monoclonal light chains, myoglobin, haemoglobin

> **Clinical Pearl:** Non-nephrotic proteinuria is often dismissed as "benign." However, persistent proteinuria >1 g/day is a powerful independent risk factor for CKD progression and cardiovascular disease and warrants investigation and treatment.

### Common Causes

| Disease | Typical Frequency | Key Clues |
|---|---|---|
| Early IgA Nephropathy (`iga`) | Very Common | Young adults, intermittent haematuria, mesangial IgA |
| Early Diabetic Nephropathy (`diabeticNephropathy`) | Very Common | DM, microalbuminuria progressing to overt proteinuria, retinopathy |
| Focal Segmental Glomerulosclerosis (`fsgs`) | Common | Obesity, Black ancestry, HTN, may be sub-nephrotic early |
| Early Membranous Nephropathy (`membranous`) | Common | Middle-aged, anti-PLA2R+, early stage may be sub-nephrotic |
| Orthostatic Proteinuria | Common | Young, lean, proteinuria only when upright, normal supine protein |
| Hypertensive Nephrosclerosis | Common | Long-standing HTN, LVH, retinopathy |
| Thin Basement Membrane Nephropathy (`thinBasementMembrane`) | Uncommon | Family haematuria, thin GBM, isolated microscopic haematuria +/- mild proteinuria |
| Lupus Nephritis (`lupus`) | Uncommon (Class I/II) | Young female, ANA+, may be isolated proteinuria |

### Rare Causes

| Disease | Typical Frequency | Key Clues |
|---|---|---|
| C3 Glomerulopathy (`c3`) | Rare | Low C3, C3-dominant IF |
| Fibrillary Glomerulonephritis (`fibrillaryGlomerulonephritis`) | Rare | Anti-DNAJB9, non-amyloid fibrils |
| Drug-Induced GN (`drugInducedGn`) | Rare | NSAIDs, lithium, temporal association |
| Amyloidosis | Rare | Age >60, organomegaly, paraprotein |
| Pre-eclampsia | Rare (non-pregnancy context) | Pregnancy, HTN, proteinuria >300 mg/day |

### Clinical Clues

- **Young, lean, no HTN:** Orthostatic proteinuria (likely benign)
- **Diabetes + proteinuria:** Early `diabeticNephropathy` - check HbA1c, retinopathy
- **Obesity + mild proteinuria:** `fsgs` (obesity-related or primary)
- **Family history of haematuria/CKD:** `alport`, `thinBasementMembrane`
- **Proteinuria only when upright:** Orthostatic (supine protein normal)
- **Long-standing HTN + LVH:** Hypertensive nephrosclerosis
- **Young female, arthralgia:** `lupus`
- **Proteinuria >1 g/day:** More significant - warrants investigation
- **Night-time urine protein > day-time:** Orthostatic proteinuria (normally day > night)
- **Recent drug change:** `drugInducedGn`

### Laboratory Clues

- **uPCR 30-300 mg/mmol:** Microalbuminuria range - early disease or benign
- **uPCR 300-3500 mg/mmol:** Non-nephrotic but significant - requires investigation
- **Proteinuria >1 g/day:** Independent cardiovascular and renal risk factor
- **Proteinuria >3 g/day:** Approaching nephrotic range - biopsy likely needed
- **Microalbuminuria (ACR 3-30):** Early `diabeticNephropathy`, hypertension, obesity
- **Orthostatic proteinuria:** ACR <30 supine, >30 when upright
- **Monoclonal band on SPE:** Overflow proteinuria - myeloma
- **Normal C3/C4:** Most causes of isolated proteinuria
- **Low C3:** `c3`, post-infectious
- **ANA positive:** `lupus`
- **HbA1c elevated:** `diabeticNephropathy`
- **Urine protein electrophoresis:** Monoclonal light chains

### Biopsy Clues

- **Mesangial IgA on IF:** `iga`
- **Segmental sclerosis on LM:** `fsgs`
- **Capillary wall thickening with subepithelial deposits:** Early `membranous`
- **Normal LM, minimal IF:** Orthostatic proteinuria (if biopsied)
- **Mesangial expansion, nodules:** `diabeticNephropathy`
- **"Full house" IF:** `lupus`
- **Thin GBM on EM:** `thinBasementMembrane`

### Recommended Investigations

1. Repeat uPCR on >=2 occasions (to confirm persistence and exclude transient causes)
2. Measure morning (supine) and ambulatory urine protein (to assess for orthostatic proteinuria)
3. Blood pressure - clinic and ambulatory BP monitoring
4. Serum creatinine, eGFR
5. HbA1c, fasting glucose (diabetes screening)
6. Urine microscopy - exclude haematuria
7. **If proteinuria >0.5 g/day:** ACEi/ARB initiation
8. **If persistent proteinuria >1 g/day:** Nephrology referral
9. **If proteinuria >3 g/day:** Renal biopsy (approaching nephrotic range)
10. **Second-line:** C3, C4, ANA, IgA
11. **Second-line:** SPE, urine protein electrophoresis (if overflow suspected)
12. **Second-line:** Renal biopsy (if proteinuria >1 g/day or uncertain cause)

### Differential Diagnosis

| Disease | Supporting Evidence | Evidence Against | Likelihood | Urgency |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Orthostatic proteinuria | Young, lean, proteinuria only upright, normal supine | Proteinuria persistent when supine | High (young adults) | Low - benign |
| Early `diabeticNephropathy` | DM, microalbuminuria, retinopathy | No DM | High (if DM present) | Low - planned management |
| Early `iga` | Young adult, intermittent haematuria, uPCR 0.3-1.0 g/day | No haematuria, older age | Moderate | Low-Moderate |
| `fsgs` | Obesity, Black ancestry, mild proteinuria, HTN | Lean, no HTN | Moderate | Moderate - assess histology |
| Early `membranous` | Middle-aged, anti-PLA2R+, sub-nephrotic proteinuria | Young, no PLA2R | Low-Moderate | Moderate |
| Hypertensive nephrosclerosis | Long-standing HTN, LVH, mild proteinuria | No HTN | Moderate (if HTN present) | Low - optimise BP |
| `lupus` (Class I/II) | Young female, ANA+ | Male, no systemic features | Low | Low-Moderate |

### Immediate Management

- **Confirm persistence:** Repeat uPCR in 2-4 weeks
- **Treat transient causes:** Rest, treat fever, avoid nephrotoxins
- **ACEi/ARB:** First-line for proteinuria >0.5 g/day (titrate to tolerance/BP)
- **BP control:** Target <130/80 mmHg if proteinuria present
- **Lifestyle:** Weight management, exercise, sodium restriction
- **Diabetes management:** Optimise HbA1c (<53 mmol/mol if tolerated)
- **Avoid nephrotoxins:** NSAIDs, aminoglycosides
- **Statin:** Consider for cardiovascular risk reduction
- **Monitor:** Repeat uPCR in 3-6 months to assess response

### Long-Term Evaluation

- **If proteinuria <0.5 g/day and stable:** Annual monitoring (uPCR, creatinine, BP)
- **If proteinuria 0.5-1.0 g/day:** ACEi/ARB, 3-monthly monitoring
- **If proteinuria >1 g/day:** Nephrology referral, consider biopsy, 1-3 monthly monitoring
- **If proteinuria increasing:** Escalate investigation (biopsy if indicated)
- **Cardiovascular risk:** Annual assessment (lipids, BP, HbA1c)
- **Diabetes screening:** Annual HbA1c in at-risk populations
- **Pregnancy:** Pre-pregnancy counselling if proteinuria >0.5 g/day
- **Bone health:** If on prolonged ACEi/ARB (rarely needed)

### Linked GDES Diseases

- `iga`
- `diabeticNephropathy`
- `fsgs`
- `membranous`
- `lupus`
- `thinBasementMembrane`
- `alport`
- `c3`
- `drugInducedGn`
- `fibrillaryGlomerulonephritis`

### Expected Outcomes

- **Orthostatic proteinuria:** Excellent prognosis. Benign condition, no progression to CKD. Reassurance only.
- **Early `diabeticNephropathy`:** Progressive if uncontrolled. SGLT2 inhibitors + ACEi/ARB slow progression by 30-40%. Microalbuminuria may regress with optimal management.
- **Early `iga`:** Variable. 25% spontaneous remission. ACEi/ARB + diet may control proteinuria. 20-30% progress to CKD at 20 years if severe.
- **Early `fsgs`:** 30-50% respond to immunosuppression. Genetic forms less responsive. ACEi/ARB renoprotective.
- **Hypertensive nephrosclerosis:** Controllable with BP optimisation. ACEi/ARB reduce proteinuria and slow progression.
- **Key message:** Non-nephrotic proteinuria >1 g/day is a significant cardiovascular and renal risk factor and should not be dismissed.

## 6. Acute Kidney Injury (AKI) — Nephrology Context

### Definition

Acute kidney injury (AKI) in the nephrology context refers to a rapid decline in renal function occurring over hours to days, resulting in accumulation of nitrogenous waste products and inability to maintain fluid, electrolyte, and acid-base homeostasis. Nephrologists are particularly interested in AKI due to intrinsic renal causes (glomerular, tubulointerstitial, vascular) as opposed to prerenal or obstructive causes managed primarily by other teams.

**Diagnostic Criteria (KDIGO 2012):**
- **Stage 1:** Serum creatinine increase >=26.5 umol/L within 48h OR increase >=1.5x baseline within 7 days OR urine volume <0.5 mL/kg/h for 6-12 hours
- **Stage 2:** Serum creatinine increase >=2.0x baseline OR urine volume <0.5 mL/kg/h for >=12 hours
- **Stage 3:** Serum creatinine increase >=3.0x baseline OR creatinine >=353.6 umol/L OR initiation of RRT OR urine volume <0.3 mL/kg/h for >=24h or anuria for >=12 hours

**Nephrology-Relevant Causes:**
- Acute glomerulonephritis (nephritic syndrome, RPGN)
- Acute interstitial nephritis (drug-induced)
- Thrombotic microangiopathy
- Atheroembolic renal disease
- Bilateral renal artery thrombosis/embolism
- Acute cortical necrosis
- Light chain cast nephropathy (myeloma kidney)
- Urate nephropathy (tumour lysis)

> **Clinical Pearl:** In the nephrology context, the key question is: "Is this AKI due to an intrinsic renal disease that requires specific treatment?" The answer changes management dramatically - immunosuppression for RPGN, plasma exchange for TMA, or discontinuation of offending drug for AIN.

### Common Causes

| Disease | Typical Frequency | Key Clues |
|---|---|---|
| Acute Interstitial Nephritis (`drugInducedGn`) | Very Common | Drug exposure (NSAIDs, antibiotics, PPIs), eosinophilia, WBC casts, rash/fever (classic triad uncommon) |
| Prerenal AKI (overlapping with intrinsic) | Very Common | Volume depletion, heart failure, sepsis, responds to fluid resuscitation |
| ANCA-Associated Vasculitis (`anca`) | Common | Age >50, systemic features, pauci-immune, crescents |
| Anti-GBM Disease (`antiGbm`) | Uncommon | Bimodal age, pulmonary haemorrhage, linear IgG |
| Lupus Nephritis (`lupus`) | Uncommon | Young female, ANA+, multi-system |
| Thrombotic Microangiopathy (`thromboticMicroangiopathy) | Uncommon | MAHA, thrombocytopenia, schistocytes |
| Post-Infectious GN (`irgn`) | Uncommon | Child, post-streptococcal, low C3 |
| Light Chain Cast Nephropathy | Uncommon | Myeloma, Bence-Jones proteinuria, cast nephropathy on biopsy |

### Rare Causes

| Disease | Typical Frequency | Key Clues |
|---|---|---|
| Atheroembolic Renal Disease | Rare | Cholesterol crystals, recent vascular procedure, livedo reticularis, eosinophilia |
| Acute Cortical Necrosis | Rare | Obstetric emergencies, sepsis, bilateral renal imaging changes |
| IgA Nephropathy with severe AKI (`iga`) | Rare | Young adult, macroscopic haematuria, crescents |
| Cryoglobulinaemic AKI (`cryoglobulinemic`) | Rare | Hepatitis C, purpura, very low C4 |
| Fibrillary Glomerulonephritis (`fibrillaryGlomerulonephritis`) | Rare | Anti-DNAJB9, non-amyloid fibrils |

### Clinical Clues

- **Recent drug change:** Acute interstitial nephritis (NSAIDs, antibiotics, PPIs)
- **Haemoptysis + AKI:** `antiGbm`, `anca` with lung, `lupus`
- **Purpura + AKI:** `cryoglobulinemic`, `thromboticMicroangiopathy, vasculitis
- **New rash + AKI:** Drug rash (AIN), `lupus`, vasculitis
- **Recent vascular procedure:** Atheroembolic disease
- **Malignancy + AKI:** Light chain cast nephropathy, tumour lysis, RCC
- **Palpable purpura + AKI:** Vasculitis, cryoglobulinaemia
- **Abdominal pain + AKI:** Atheroembolic, vasculitis, TMA
- **Fever + AKI:** Drug reaction, infection, vasculitis
- **Macroscopic haematuria + AKI:** `iga` (rare), `irgn`, crescentic GN
- **Sepsis + AKI:** Consider TMA, ATN, or prevenal component
- **Oliguria + dark urine:** Acute tubular necrosis or glomerular disease

### Laboratory Clues

- **Eosinophilia:** Drug-induced AIN, atheroembolic disease, EGPA
- **WBC casts:** AIN (highly suggestive), GN (with RBC casts)
- **RBC casts:** Glomerulonephritis
- **Schistocytes + thrombocytopenia + anaemia:** TMA
- **Very high LDH:** TMA, haemolysis
- **Low haptoglobin:** Haemolysis (TMA, autoimmune)
- **Anti-GBM Ab positive:** `antiGbm`
- **ANCA positive:** `anca`
- **ANA/anti-dsDNA positive:** `lupus`
- **Paraprotein on SPE:** Myeloma kidney
- **Bence-Jones proteinuria:** Light chain disease
- **Very high urate:** Tumour lysis syndrome
- **Low C3, normal C4:** Post-infectious, C3 glomerulopathy
- **Low C3, low C4:** `lupus`, `cryoglobulinemic`
- **Urine eosinophils:** AIN (poor sensitivity/specificity)
- **Fractional excretion of sodium (FENa):** <1% prerenal, >2% intrinsic (not reliable in all settings)

### Biopsy Clues

- **Interstitial oedema, lymphocyte/eosinophil infiltration, tubulitis:** AIN
- **Crescents (>20% glomeruli):** RPGN - `anca`, `antiGbm`, `lupus`
- **Linear IgG on IF:** `antiGbm`
- **Pauci-immune crescents:** `anca`
- **Full-house IF:** `lupus`
- **Thrombi in glomerular capillaries, fragmented RBCs:** TMA
- **Cast nephropathy (myeloma kidney):** Light chain casts, giant cell reaction
- **Cholesterol clefts in arterioles:** Atheroembolic disease
- **Diffuse cortical necrosis:** Bilateral, global cortical infarction

### Recommended Investigations

1. Serum creatinine - STAT and serial q12-24h
2. Urine microscopy - RBC casts, WBC casts, eosinophils, muddy brown casts
3. uPCR - quantify proteinuria
4. FBC (anaemia, thrombocytopenia, eosinophilia)
5. LDH, haptoglobin, reticulocyte count (haemolysis screen)
6. Blood film - schistocytes
7. C3, C4
8. ANA, anti-dsDNA
9. ANCA (c-ANCA/PR3, p-ANCA/MPO)
10. Anti-GBM antibody
11. SPE, immunoglobulins, serum free light chains
12. Hepatitis B and C
13. Blood cultures (if febrile)
14. Drug history review - comprehensive
15. Renal ultrasound (obstruction, kidney size)
16. **Second-line:** Renal biopsy (URGENT if RPGN suspected)
17. **Second-line:** Cryoglobulins, complement factors
18. **Second-line:** Renal Doppler (if renal artery thrombosis suspected)

### Differential Diagnosis

| Disease | Supporting Evidence | Evidence Against | Likelihood | Urgency |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Prerenal AKI | Volume depletion, respond to fluids, FENa <1% | Intrinsic features (casts, proteinuria) | High | Moderate - volume resuscitate |
| Drug-induced AIN | Recent drug change, eosinophilia, WBC casts | No drug change, no systemic features | Common | High - stop offending drug |
| `anca` | Age >50, systemic features, pauci-immune | Young, isolated renal | Moderate | Critical - start treatment |
| `antiGbm` | Haemoptysis, linear IgG, anti-GBM Ab+ | No pulmonary symptoms | Low-Moderate | Critical - plasma exchange |
| `lupus` | Young female, ANA+ | Male, no systemic features | Low-Moderate | High |
| TMA | MAHA, thrombocytopenia, schistocytes | Normal platelets | Low-Moderate | Critical - plasma exchange |
| Obstructive AKI | Anuria, dilated ureters on US, BPH history | Non-obstructive features | Moderate | High - decompress |

### Immediate Management

- **Stop nephrotoxins:** NSAIDs, ACEi (if creatinine rising), aminoglycosides, iodinated contrast
- **Drug-induced AIN:** Discontinue offending drug (most important intervention); consider steroids if no improvement in 7-10 days
- **Volume resuscitation:** If prerenal component - IV crystalloid (avoid hydroxyethyl starch)
- **Hyperkalaemia management:** Calcium gluconate (cardioprotection), insulin/dextrose, salbutamol, sodium bicarbonate, kayexalate
- **Metabolic acidosis:** Sodium bicarbonate (target pH >7.2)
- **Fluid overload:** Loop diuretics (furosemide IV); dialysis if refractory
- **Immunosuppression:** If RPGN/vasculitis suspected - pulse methylprednisolone 500-1000 mg IV
- **Plasma exchange:** For anti-GBM, severe ANCA with pulmonary haemorrhage, TMA
- **Renal replacement therapy:** If stage 3 AKI with refractory complications (hyperkalaemia, fluid overload, acidosis, uraemic symptoms)
- **Monitor:** Daily creatinine, electrolytes, fluid balance, urine output (catheterise if needed)

### Long-Term Evaluation

- **Renal recovery assessment:** Repeat creatinine at 3, 6, 12 months
- **CKD surveillance:** If incomplete recovery - standard CKD care
- **Avoid re-exposure:** Identify and document offending drug; inform patient and medical records
- **Drug avoidance list:** Maintain and update
- **ACEi/ARB:** Restart cautiously once creatinine stabilised (if proteinuric disease)
- **Vaccination:** If immunosuppressed - pre-vaccination
- **Relapse monitoring:** If vasculitis/AIN - periodic urinalysis, creatinine, disease-specific markers
- **Bone health:** If prolonged steroid use - DEXA, bisphosphonate prophylaxis

- **`antiGbm`:** 50% renal survival with PLEX + immunosuppression. Poor if dialysis-dependent at presentation.
- **`lupus`:** 70-90% respond to induction. 10-20% ESKD at 10 years.
- **TMA:** Depends on aetiology. aHUS: complement inhibition (eculizumab) excellent. TTP: PLEX + immunosuppression >90% survival. Shiga toxin HUS: supportive, usually self-limiting in children.

## 7. Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)

### Definition

Chronic kidney disease (CKD) is defined as abnormalities of kidney structure or function, present for >3 months, with implications for health. It includes all causes of progressive renal impairment, from early CKD (eGFR >60 with evidence of kidney damage) to end-stage kidney disease (ESKD) requiring renal replacement therapy.

**Diagnostic Criteria:**
- eGFR <60 mL/min/1.73m2 for >=3 months (GFR categories G3a-G5)
- OR markers of kidney damage (albuminuria ACR >=3 mg/mmol, haematuria, structural abnormality) for >=3 months
- eGFR categories: G1 (>90), G2 (60-89), G3a (45-59), G3b (30-44), G4 (15-29), G5 (<15)
- Albuminuria categories: A1 (<3 mg/mmol), A2 (3-30 mg/mmol), A3 (>30 mg/mmol)

**Classification:**
- **CKD Stage 1-2:** Preserved eGFR with kidney damage markers
- **CKD Stage 3a-3b:** Moderate CKD
- **CKD Stage 4:** Severe CKD
- **CKD Stage 5:** ESKD (eGFR <15 or on RRT)

> **Clinical Pearl:** CKD is a staging system, not a diagnosis. The cause of CKD determines treatment and prognosis. Always establish the aetiology - do not default to "hypertensive nephrosclerosis" without evidence.

### Common Causes

| Disease | Typical Frequency | Key Clues |
|---|---|---|
| Diabetic Nephropathy (`diabeticNephropathy`) | Very Common (40-50% of ESKD) | DM >10 years, retinopathy, gradual proteinuria increase, Kimmelstiel-Wilson nodules |
| Hypertensive Nephrosclerosis | Very Common (25-30% of ESKD) | Long-standing HTN, LVH, small kidneys, benign urinalysis |
| IgA Nephropathy (`iga`) | Common (10-15%) | Young adult onset, recurrent haematuria, mesangial IgA, progressive |
| Focal Segmental Glomerulosclerosis (`fsgs`) | Common (5-10%) | Obesity, Black ancestry, HTN, steroid resistance |
| Membranous Nephropathy (`membranous`) | Common | Middle-aged, anti-PLA2R+, nephrotic range, subepithelial deposits |
| ANCA-Associated Vasculitis (`anca`) | Common (5%) | Age >50, systemic features, pauci-immune, crescents |
| Lupus Nephritis (`lupus`) | Common | Young female, ANA+, systemic features, full-house IF |
| Polycystic Kidney Disease | Common (5%) | Bilateral enlarged cystic kidneys, family history, hypertension |

### Rare Causes

| Disease | Typical Frequency | Key Clues |
|---|---|---|
| Alport Syndrome (`alport`) | Rare | Family ESKD/deafness, sensorineural hearing loss, abnormal GBM on EM |
| C3 Glomerulopathy (`c3`) | Rare | Low C3, C3-dominant IF, alternative pathway |
| Amyloidosis | Rare | Age >60, organomegaly, paraprotein, Congo red positive |
| Fibrillary Glomerulonephritis (`fibrillaryGlomerulonephritis`) | Rare | Anti-DNAJB9, non-amyloid fibrils |
| Reflux Nephropathy | Rare | Recurrent UTIs in childhood, scarring on imaging, bilateral |

### Clinical Clues

- **Diabetes >10 years + retinopathy:** `diabeticNephropathy` (>90% have retinopathy)
- **Long-standing HTN + LVH + small kidneys:** Hypertensive nephrosclerosis
- **Young adult, recurrent haematuria, family history:** `iga`, `alport`
- **Bilateral enlarged cystic kidneys:** Polycystic kidney disease
- **Obesity + proteinuria:** `fsgs`
- **Young female, rash, joint pain:** `lupus`
- **Age >50, sinusitis, lung nodules:** `anca`
- **Family history of ESKD/deafness:** `alport`
- **Proteinuria >3.5 g/day in CKD:** Consider `membranous`, `fsgs`, `diabeticNephropathy`
- **Progressive anaemia + bone pain:** Amyloidosis, myeloma
- **Rapid decline in eGFR:** RPGN (superimposed on CKD), drug-induced, obstruction

### Laboratory Clues

- **eGFR <60 for >=3 months:** Confirms CKD staging
- **Microalbuminuria (ACR 3-30):** Early kidney damage - diabetes, hypertension
- **Overt proteinuria (ACR >30):** Significant kidney damage - requires cause investigation
- **HbA1c >6.5%:** `diabeticNephropathy`
- **ANA/anti-dsDNA positive:** `lupus`
- **Low C3/C4:** `lupus`, `cryoglobulinemic`, C3 glomerulopathy
- **Paraprotein on SPE:** Myeloma, amyloid
- **Low Hb (normocytic):** CKD anaemia (normochromic normocytic)
- **Hyperphosphataemia, raised PTH:** CKD-MBD (stages 4-5)
- **Metabolic acidosis:** CKD stage 4-5 (low bicarbonate)
- **Hyperkalaemia:** CKD stage 4-5, or RAAS inhibitor use
- **Uric acid elevated:** Gout, CKD
- **Small kidneys on US:** Hypertensive nephrosclerosis, chronic GN
- **Enlarged kidneys:** Diabetic nephropathy, amyloid, HIV, polycystic kidney disease

### Biopsy Clues

- **Diffuse nodular glomerulosclerosis (Kimmelstiel-Wilson):** `diabeticNephropathy`
- **Arteriolar hyalinosis:** Hypertensive nephrosclerosis, diabetes
- **Mesangial IgA deposits:** `iga`
- **Segmental sclerosis:** `fsgs`
- **Congo red positive, apple-green birefringence:** Amyloidosis
- **Global sclerosis:** End-stage CKD - chronic changes
- **Interstitial fibrosis and tubular atrophy (IFTA):** Chronicity marker - correlates with prognosis
- **"Wire-loop" lesions (chronic):** `lupus` (Class VI)
- **Normal or near-normal LM:** Early CKD, hypertensive nephrosclerosis (may not biopsy)

### Recommended Investigations

1. Serum creatinine, eGFR - confirm chronicity (repeat at 3 months)
2. uPCR or urine ACR - quantify albuminuria/proteinuria
3. Urine microscopy - haematuria, casts
4. Renal ultrasound - kidney size, echogenicity, obstruction, cysts
5. FBC (anaemia)
6. Bone profile (calcium, phosphate, PTH) - if eGFR <45
7. Bicarbonate (metabolic acidosis)
8. Urate
9. HbA1c, fasting glucose
10. Lipid profile
11. Urine ACR - annual if diabetic/hypertensive
12. **Second-line (if cause uncertain):** C3, C4, ANA, anti-dsDNA, IgA, SPE
13. **Second-line:** Renal biopsy (if non-diabetic, non-hypertensive, and cause uncertain)
14. **Second-line:** Genetic testing if family history (Alport, PKD)
15. **Second-line:** Hepatitis B and C

### Differential Diagnosis

| Disease | Supporting Evidence | Evidence Against | Likelihood | Urgency |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| `diabeticNephropathy` | DM >10 y, retinopathy, gradual onset | No DM | High (if DM present) | Low - planned management |
| Hypertensive nephrosclerosis | Long-standing HTN, LVH, small kidneys | No HTN, large kidneys | High (if HTN present) | Low |
| `iga` | Young adult, recurrent haematuria, progressive | Age >50, no haematuria | Moderate | Low-Moderate |
| `fsgs` | Obesity, Black ancestry, proteinuria | Lean, no proteinuria | Low-Moderate | Moderate |
| `lupus` | Young female, ANA+ | Male, no systemic features | Low-Moderate | Moderate |
| `anca` | Age >50, systemic features | Young, no systemic features | Low | High |
| Polycystic kidney disease | Bilateral cystic kidneys, family history | No cysts, no family history | Low-Moderate | Low |

### Immediate Management

- **Treat underlying cause:** Immunosuppression for GN, optimise diabetes, control BP
- **ACEi/ARB:** First-line for proteinuric CKD (uPCR >30 mg/mmol); titrate to tolerance
- **SGLT2 inhibitors:** Dapagliflozin or empagliflozin if eGFR >20 - reduce proteinuria and slow progression
- **BP target:** <130/80 mmHg (proteinuric); <140/90 mmHg (non-proteinuric)
- **HbA1c target:** <53 mmol/mol (individualised)
- **Anaemia management:** Erythropoiesis-stimulating agents (ESAs) if Hb <100 g/L (target Hb 100-115 g/L)
- **CKD-MBD:** Phosphate binders, vitamin D supplementation, cinacalcet if secondary hyperparathyroidism
- **Metabolic acidosis:** Sodium bicarbonate supplementation (target HCO3 >=22 mmol/L)
- **Hyperkalaemia management:** Dietary K+ restriction, sodium zirconium cyclosilicate, patiromer
- **Vaccination:** Hepatitis B (pre-dialysis), pneumococcal, influenza
- **Dietary counselling:** Sodium <2 g/day, protein 0.8 g/kg/day (pre-dialysis), phosphate restriction
- **Cardiovascular risk reduction:** Statin, BP control, glycaemic control

### Long-Term Evaluation

- **eGFR monitoring:** Every 1-3 months (stage 3b-5), every 3-6 months (stage 3a), every 6-12 months (stage 1-2)
- **Albuminuria monitoring:** Every 3-6 months if proteinuric
- **Bone profile:** Every 3-6 months (stage 4-5), annually (stage 3)
- **FBC:** Every 3-6 months (anaemia surveillance)
- **Bicarbonate:** Every 3-6 months (acidosis)
- **HbA1c:** Every 3-6 months (if diabetic)
- **Lipids:** Annually
- **Renal ultrasound:** Annual (if PKD or structural disease)
- **Pre-ESKD education:** Start at eGFR <20 - dialysis access planning, transplant workup
- **Dialysis access:** AV fistula creation at eGFR <20 (maturation takes 3-6 months)
- **Transplant workup:** Refer at eGFR <20 (pre-emptive transplant preferred)
- **Palliative care:** Discuss conservative management if not suitable for RRT

- **`diabeticNephropathy`:** Progressive; 5% eGFR decline/year with proteinuria. SGLT2i + ACEi/ARB slow progression by 30-40%. Median 10-20 years to ESKD from onset of proteinuria.
- **Hypertensive nephrosclerosis:** BP optimisation slows progression. ACEi/ARB renoprotective. Variable timeline to ESKD.

## 8. Hypertension with Urinary Abnormalities

### Definition

Hypertension with urinary abnormalities is the coexistence of sustained elevated blood pressure (>=140/90 mmHg on >=2 occasions) with one or more urinary abnormalities including proteinuria (uPCR >30 mg/mmol), haematuria (>=3 RBCs/HPF), or cellular casts. This combination raises suspicion for secondary causes of hypertension, particularly renal parenchymal disease, and may indicate a primary glomerular disease presenting with hypertension rather than vice versa.

**Diagnostic Criteria:**
- Systolic >=140 mmHg and/or diastolic >=90 mmHg
- PLUS at least one of:
  - Proteinuria: uPCR >30 mg/mmol (>300 mg/g)
  - Haematuria: >=3 RBCs/HPF
  - RBC casts
  - WBC casts
  - Renal impairment (eGFR <60) attributed to kidney disease

> **Clinical Pearl:** Hypertension is both a cause and consequence of kidney disease. When haematuria or significant proteinuria accompanies hypertension, always consider an underlying glomerular disease rather than attributing it solely to hypertensive nephrosclerosis - especially in younger patients or those without long-standing hypertension.

### Common Causes

| Disease | Typical Frequency | Key Clues |
|---|---|---|
| IgA Nephropathy (`iga`) | Very Common | Young adult, recurrent haematuria, HTN at diagnosis, mesangial IgA |
| Hypertensive Nephrosclerosis | Very Common | Long-standing HTN (>10 y), LVH, retinopathy, small kidneys |
| Diabetic Nephropathy (`diabeticNephropathy`) | Very Common | DM >10 years, retinopathy, microalbuminuria leading to overt proteinuria |
| Focal Segmental Glomerulosclerosis (`fsgs`) | Common | Obesity, Black ancestry, proteinuria, HTN at presentation |
| Chronic Lupus Nephritis (`lupus`) | Common | Young female, ANA+, chronic kidney damage, active urine sediment |
| ANCA-Associated Vasculitis (`anca`) | Uncommon | Age >50, systemic features, active sediment |
| Alport Syndrome (`alport`) | Uncommon | Family ESKD/deafness, young male, progressive |
| Polycystic Kidney Disease | Common | Bilateral enlarged cystic kidneys, family history, macroscopic haematuria |

### Rare Causes

| Disease | Typical Frequency | Key Clues |
|---|---|---|
| Renovascular Disease | Rare | Young female (fibromuscular dysplasia), atherosclerotic (elderly), renal bruit |
| Phaeochromocytoma | Rare | Episodic HTN, sweating, palpitations, raised metanephrines |
| Cushing Syndrome | Rare | Central obesity, striae, buffalo hump, hyperglycaemia |
| Hyperaldosteronism | Rare | Hypokalaemia, metabolic alkalosis, raised aldosterone/renin ratio |
| Coarctation of the Aorta | Rare | Upper >lower limb BP, radio-femoral delay, rib notching |

### Clinical Clues

- **Young adult (<40) with hypertension + haematuria:** `iga` (most common), `alport`, renal vascular
- **Family history of ESKD/deafness:** `alport`
- **Long-standing hypertension + LVH:** Hypertensive nephrosclerosis (but exclude primary GN)
- **Diabetes + hypertension + proteinuria:** `diabeticNephropathy`
- **Obesity + hypertension + proteinuria:** `fsgs`
- **Female + systemic features + hypertension:** `lupus`
- **Age >50 + systemic features + hypertension:** `anca`
- **Episodic hypertension + palpitations + sweating:** Phaeochromocytoma
- **Hypokalaemia + metabolic alkalosis:** Hyperaldosteronism
- **Upper >lower limb BP:** Coarctation of the aorta
- **Renal bruit:** Renovascular disease
- **Bilateral cystic kidneys:** Polycystic kidney disease
- **Resistant hypertension (>=4 agents):** Consider secondary cause

### Laboratory Clues

- **Persistent microscopic haematuria + HTN:** `iga` (most likely), `thinBasementMembrane`, `alport`
- **Proteinuria >1 g/day + HTN:** Primary GN rather than hypertensive nephrosclerosis
- **HbA1c >6.5% + HTN + proteinuria:** `diabeticNephropathy`
- **Low C3, normal C4:** Post-infectious GN, C3 glomerulopathy
- **ANA/anti-dsDNA positive:** `lupus`
- **Anti-PLA2R positive:** `membranous` (if proteinuria nephrotic-range)
- **Low C3, low C4:** `lupus`, `cryoglobulinemic`
- **Very high renin, low aldosterone:** Renovascular disease
- **Very high aldosterone, low renin:** Primary hyperaldosteronism
- **Elevated metanephrines:** Phaeochromocytoma
- **Elevated cortisol (dexamethasone suppression test abnormal):** Cushing syndrome
- **Genetic testing (COL4A3/A4/A5):** If `alport` suspected
- **Polycystin mutation testing:** If PKD suspected

### Biopsy Clues

- **Mesangial IgA on IF:** `iga`
- **Segmental sclerosis:** `fsgs`
- **Arteriolar hyalinosis, intimal fibrosis:** Hypertensive nephrosclerosis
- **Kimmelstiel-Wilson nodules:** `diabeticNephropathy`
- **Diffuse capillary wall thickening:** `membranous`
- **"Full house" IF:** `lupus`
- **Abnormal GBM (basket-weave) on EM:** `alport`

> **Clinical Pearl:** Hypertensive nephrosclerosis is a diagnosis of exclusion. Do not label a young patient with hypertension + haematuria/proteinuria as "hypertensive nephrosclerosis" without excluding primary glomerular disease - biopsy is often indicated.

### Recommended Investigations

1. Blood pressure - clinic and ambulatory BP monitoring (ABPM)
2. uPCR - quantify proteinuria
3. Urine microscopy - haematuria, casts
4. Serum creatinine, eGFR
5. ECG (LVH, strain pattern)
6. Echocardiogram (LVH, diastolic dysfunction)
7. HbA1c, fasting glucose
8. **If secondary cause suspected:**
   - Plasma aldosterone/renin ratio
   - 24-hour urine metanephrines (phaeochromocytoma)
   - Captopril renography or renal Doppler (renovascular)
   - CT/MRA renal arteries (renovascular)
9. **If glomerular disease suspected:**
   - C3, C4, ANA, anti-dsDNA, IgA
   - Hepatitis B and C
   - Anti-PLA2R
10. **Second-line:** Renal biopsy (if proteinuria >0.5 g/day, haematuria, or uncertain cause)
11. **Second-line:** Genetic testing (Alport, PKD) if family history
12. **Second-line:** Renal MRA or CT angiography (if renovascular suspected)

### Immediate Management

- **BP control:** ACEi/ARB first-line if proteinuria present (target <130/80)
- **Calcium channel blocker:** Add if BP not controlled on ACEi/ARB
- **Diuretic:** If fluid overload
- **Treat underlying cause:** Immunosuppression for GN, optimise diabetes

### Long-Term Evaluation

- **BP monitoring:** ABPM every 3-6 months
- **uPCR monitoring:** Every 3-6 months
- **eGFR monitoring:** Every 3-6 months (stage 3+), every 6-12 months (stage 1-2)
- **Echocardiogram:** Annually (LVH regression)

- **Secondary cause screening:** Periodic reassessment

- **Bone profile:** Every 6-12 months (stage 4-5)

## 9. Systemic Vasculitis (Renal Involvement)

### Definition

Systemic vasculitis with renal involvement refers to inflammation of blood vessels affecting the kidneys as part of a multisystem vasculitic process. The kidneys are involved in 30-70% of systemic vasculitides, most commonly as glomerulonephritis (pauci-immune, immune complex, or anti-GBM), but also as renal artery stenosis, renal infarction, or interstitial nephritis.

**Classification (Chapel Hill Consensus):**
- **ANCA-associated vasculitis (AAV):** Granulomatosis with polyangiitis (GPA), microscopic polyangiitis (MPA), EGPA - small vessel, pauci-immune
- **Immune complex vasculitis:** Lupus vasculitis, IgA vasculitis, cryoglobulinaemic vasculitis
- **Anti-GBM disease:** Anti-GBM vasculitis (overlaps with pulmonary)
- **Large vessel vasculitis:** Takayasu, giant cell arteritis (rarely renal)
- **Variable vessel vasculitis:** Behcet disease, Cogan syndrome

**Diagnostic Criteria (varies by type):**
- Systemic features (fever, weight loss, malaise)
- Renal involvement (haematuria, proteinuria, declining eGFR, crescents on biopsy)
- Other organ involvement (lungs, ENT, skin, nerves, joints)
- Serological markers (ANCA, ANA, complement, cryoglobulins)
- Histological confirmation (renal biopsy, tissue biopsy)

> **Clinical Pearl:** ANCA-associated vasculitis is the most common systemic vasculitis causing renal injury. GPA (c-ANCA/PR3) and MPA (p-ANCA/MPO) are the most frequent. All systemic vasculitis with renal involvement requires urgent nephrology and rheumatology/immunology input.

### Common Causes

| Disease | Typical Frequency | Key Clues |
|---|---|---|
| Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis (`anca`) | Very Common | Upper/lower respiratory tract + renal, c-ANCA/PR3 positive, sinonasal disease, lung nodules |
| Microscopic Polyangiitis (`anca`) | Very Common | Pulmonary-renal, p-ANCA/MPO positive, pauci-immune, no granulomas |
| Lupus Vasculitis (`lupus`) | Common | Young female, ANA/anti-dsDNA+, multi-system, low complements |
| IgA Vasculitis (Henoch-Schonlein) | Common | Children/young adults, palpable purpura, abdominal pain, arthritis, mesangial IgA |
| Cryoglobulinaemic Vasculitis (`cryoglobulinemic`) | Common | Hepatitis C, palpable purpura, arthralgia, C4 very low, mixed cryoglobulins |
| EGPA (Churg-Strauss) (`anca`) | Uncommon | Asthma, eosinophilia, p-ANCA/MPO positive, cardiac, neuropathy |
| Polyarteritis Nodosa | Uncommon | Medium-vessel, renal artery aneurysms, mesenteric ischaemia, mononeuritis multiplex |
| Anti-GBM Vasculitis (`antiGbm`) | Uncommon | Pulmonary haemorrhage + crescentic GN, linear IgG |

### Rare Causes

| Disease | Typical Frequency | Key Clues |
|---|---|---|
| Takayasu Arteritis | Rare | Young female, arm claudication, pulseless, aortic arch disease |
| Giant Cell Arteritis | Rare | Age >70, headache, jaw claudication, elevated ESR |
| Behcet Disease | Rare | Oral/genital ulcers, uveitis, pathergy, renal involvement uncommon |
| Cogan Syndrome | Rare | Interstitial keratitis, audiovestibular symptoms |
| IgG4-Related Disease | Rare | Multi-organ fibrosis, elevated IgG4, storiform fibrosis |

### Clinical Clues

- **Upper + lower respiratory + renal:** GPA (`anca`)
- **Pulmonary + renal (no upper respiratory):** MPA (`anca`), anti-GBM
- **Asthma + eosinophilia + renal:** EGPA (`anca`)
- **Palpable purpura + arthralgia + renal:** Cryoglobulinaemia, IgA vasculitis
- **Young female + multi-system:** Lupus
- **Hepatitis C + purpura + low C4:** Cryoglobulinaemia
- **Haemoptysis + haematuria:** Anti-GBM, GPA, MPA, severe `lupus`
- **Sinusitis + ear disease + haemoptysis:** GPA
- **Mononeuritis multiplex + renal:** Polyarteritis nodosa, cryoglobulinaemia, EGPA
- **Cardiac involvement + eosinophilia + renal:** EGPA
- **Renal bruit + HTN:** Renovascular involvement (PAN, Takayasu)
- **Abdominal pain + haematuria:** IgA vasculitis (children), PAN

### Laboratory Clues

- **c-ANCA/PR3 positive:** GPA (sensitivity ~90% for active disease)
- **p-ANCA/MPO positive:** MPA, EGPA, drug-induced
- **ANA/anti-dsDNA positive:** Lupus
- **Low C3, low C4:** Lupus, cryoglobulinaemia
- **Low C3, normal C4:** Post-infectious, C3 glomerulopathy

- **Anti-GBM positive:** Anti-GBM disease
- **Eosinophilia:** EGPA, drug-induced AIN
- **Elevated ESR, CRP:** Active vasculitis

- **Liver function abnormalities:** Cryoglobulinaemia (hepatitis C)
- **Hepatitis C positive:** Cryoglobulinaemia
- **Low complement (C3, C4):** Lupus, cryoglobulinaemia, post-infectious

### Biopsy Clues

- **Pauci-immune crescentic GN:** GPA, MPA (most common)
- **Necrotising granulomatous inflammation (ENT/lung):** GPA
- **Granulomatous inflammation without necrosis:** EGPA
- **Full-house IF:** Lupus
- **Mesangial IgA:** IgA vasculitis
- **Subendothelial deposits, cryoglobulins:** Cryoglobulinaemic vasculitis
- **"Wire-loop" lesions:** Lupus vasculitis
- **Segmental necrosis and crescents:** Pauci-immune vasculitis
- **Medium-vessel vasculitis:** PAN (rarely biopsied for renal)
- **Linear IgG on IF:** Anti-GBM

### Recommended Investigations

1. ANCA (c-ANCA/PR3, p-ANCA/MPO) - STAT
2. ANA, anti-dsDNA
3. Anti-GBM antibody
4. C3, C4
5. FBC, CRP, ESR
6. LDH, haptoglobin, reticulocyte count (haemolysis screen)
7. Blood film (schistocytes)
8. Serum cryoglobulins
9. Hepatitis B and C serology
10. Serum immunoglobulins, SPE
11. Urine microscopy (RBC casts, haematuria)
12. uPCR
13. Serum creatinine, eGFR - STAT and serial
14. Chest X-ray (nodules, infiltrates, haemorrhage)
15. **Second-line:** CT chest/sinuses (ENT involvement, lung nodules)
16. **Second-line:** Renal biopsy - URGENT
17. **Second-line:** Bronchoscopy with BAL (if pulmonary haemorrhage)
18. **Second-line:** Pulmonary function tests (diffusing capacity)
19. **Second-line:** Nerve conduction studies (mononeuritis multiplex)

### Differential Diagnosis

| Disease | Supporting Evidence | Evidence Against | Likelihood | Urgency |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| GPA (`anca`) | Upper + lower respiratory + renal, c-ANCA/PR3+ | No ENT, no lung, p-ANCA+ | High (if multi-system) | Critical - immunosuppress |
| MPA (`anca`) | Pulmonary + renal, p-ANCA/MPO+ | Upper respiratory, c-ANCA+ | High | Critical |
| Lupus | Young female, ANA+, multi-system, low C3/C4 | Male, no systemic features | Moderate | High |
| Cryoglobulinaemic | Hepatitis C, purpura, low C4, cryoglobulins+ | No hep C, no purpura | Moderate | High |

| Anti-GBM | Haemoptysis + haematuria, linear IgG | No pulmonary symptoms | Low | Critical |

### Immediate Management

- **Pulse methylprednisolone:** 500-1000 mg IV x 3 days (all severe vasculitis with renal involvement)
- **Oral prednisolone:** 1 mg/kg/day (max 60 mg) after IV pulse, taper over 3-6 months
- **Cyclophosphamide:** IV pulse (15 mg/kg q2-3 weeks x 3-6 pulses) or oral (2 mg/kg/day x 3-6 months)
  - **OR Rituximab:** 375 mg/m2 weekly x 4 or 1g x 2 (for AAV - non-inferior to cyclophosphamide)
- **Plasma exchange:** Indicated for:
  - Anti-GBM disease (mandatory)
  - Severe ANCA vasculitis with pulmonary haemorrhage
  - Dialysis-dependent RPGN (consider)
- **AVACOPAN (C5a receptor inhibitor):** May replace high-dose steroids in AAV
- **Hepatitis C-related cryoglobulinaemia:** Antiviral therapy (direct-acting antivirals) + rituximab
- **Dialysis:** If AKI severe - may be temporary
- **Infection prophylaxis:** TMP-SMX (PJP), antivirals (CMV if on rituximab)
- **Monitor:** Daily creatinine, electrolytes, CRP, urine output

### Long-Term Evaluation

- **Maintenance immunosuppression:** Azathioprine (2 mg/kg/day) or rituximab (500 mg q6 months) for AAV - minimum 18-24 months (indefinite for high-risk relapse)
- **Steroid taper:** Reduce to 5 mg/day by 3-6 months, consider discontinuation at 12-18 months
- **Relapse monitoring:** ANCA titres (PR3 more relapsing), CRP, urinalysis, creatinine - every 3 months
- **Disease-specific monitoring:**
  - GPA: Sinonasal imaging, ENT assessment
  - EGPA: Asthma control, eosinophil count
  - Cryoglobulinaemia: Hepatitis C viral load, complement levels
- **Bone health:** DEXA scan, bisphosphonate prophylaxis
- **Cardiovascular risk:** Annual assessment (statin, BP control)
- **Vaccination:** Pre-immunosuppression (pneumococcal, influenza, varicella)
- **Cancer surveillance:** Cyclophosphamide - bladder cancer risk; rituximab - lymphoma risk (low)

### Linked GDES Diseases

- `anca`
- `antiGbm`
- `lupus`
- `cryoglobulinemic`
- `iga`
- `drugInducedGn`

### Expected Outcomes

- **GPA/MPA:** 80-90% achieve remission with induction. 30-50% relapse (PR3 > MPO). Renal survival 70-80% with early treatment. Poorer if dialysis-dependent at presentation.
- **Lupus vasculitis:** 70-90% respond to immunosuppression. 10-20% ESKD at 10 years.
- **Cryoglobulinaemic:** Hepatitis C clearance (DAAs) achieves remission in >80%. Rituximab for refractory cases. Renal prognosis improved with antiviral therapy.
- **IgA vasculitis (children):** >95% renal recovery. Rare progression to CKD (<5%). Adults have worse prognosis.
- **EGPA:** 80-90% respond to immunosuppression. Cardiac involvement worsens prognosis.
- **Anti-GBM:** 50% renal survival with PLEX + immunosuppression. Relapse rare.
- **Overall:** Early diagnosis and treatment are critical. Delayed treatment (>2 weeks from dialysis initiation) significantly worsens renal outcomes.

## 10. Pulmonary-Renal Syndrome

### Definition

Pulmonary-renal syndrome is a clinical complex defined by the simultaneous occurrence of diffuse alveolar haemorrhage (DAH) and glomerulonephritis. It represents a nephrological emergency due to the dual threat of respiratory failure and acute renal injury.

**Diagnostic Criteria:**
- **Pulmonary component:** Haemoptysis (may be absent in 30-50%), diffuse bilateral alveolar infiltrates on chest X-ray/CT, falling haemoglobin, increasing CO diffusing capacity (DLCO), bloody BAL fluid
- **Renal component:** Active urine sediment (RBC casts, dysmorphic haematuria), rising creatinine, proteinuria
- **Histological confirmation:** Often requires lung and/or renal biopsy

**Key Aetiologies:**
- **Anti-GBM disease (Goodpasture):** Linear IgG, anti-GBM antibody
- **ANCA-associated vasculitis:** GPA, MPA - pauci-immune
- **Lupus:** Full-house IF, ANA+
- **Cryoglobulinaemic vasculitis:** Hepatitis C, low C4

> **Clinical Pearl:** Pulmonary-renal syndrome is a medical emergency. Mortality exceeds 50% without treatment. Any patient with haemoptysis + rising creatinine + RBC casts needs IMMEDIATE nephrology and respiratory input. Start empiric treatment while awaiting serology and biopsy results.

### Common Causes

| Disease | Typical Frequency | Key Clues |
|---|---|---|
| ANCA-Associated Vasculitis (`anca`) | Very Common (60-70%) | GPA (c-ANCA/PR3), MPA (p-ANCA/MPO), age >50, systemic features |
| Anti-GBM Disease (`antiGbm`) | Common (15-20%) | Bimodal age (20-30, 50-70), anti-GBM Ab+, linear IgG |
| Lupus Nephritis (`lupus`) | Common (10-15%) | Young female, ANA/anti-dsDNA+, low C3/C4, multi-system |
| Cryoglobulinaemic Vasculitis (`cryoglobulinemic`) | Uncommon | Hepatitis C, purpura, C4 very low, mixed cryoglobulins |
| Goodpasture Syndrome (anti-GBM) | Uncommon | Smoking + hydrocarbon exposure + anti-GBM |

### Rare Causes

| Disease | Typical Frequency | Key Clues |
|---|---|---|
| IgA Vasculitis with pulmonary involvement | Rare | Children, purpura, abdominal pain, mesangial IgA |
| Drug-Induced (methotrexate, penicillamine, cocaine) | Rare | Temporal drug association, eosinophilia |
| Granulomatosis with polyangiitis (limited) | Rare | ENT predominant, lung nodules, c-ANCA/PR3 |
| Behcet Disease | Rare | Oral/genital ulcers, uveitis, haemoptysis |
| Idiopathic pulmonary-renal vasculitis | Rare | Diagnosis of exclusion |

### Clinical Clues

- **Haemoptysis + rising creatinine + RBC casts:** Pulmonary-renal syndrome - URGENT
- **Bimodal age (20-30 or 50-70) + haemoptysis:** Anti-GBM
- **Upper + lower respiratory + renal:** GPA (sinusitis, otitis, lung nodules)
- **Young female + multi-system:** Lupus (rash, arthritis, oral ulcers)
- **Hepatitis C + purpura + haemoptysis:** Cryoglobulinaemia
- **Smoking + haemoptysis + haematuria:** Anti-GBM (smoking increases risk)
- **No haemoptysis (30-50%):** Does NOT exclude DAH - look for falling Hb, bilateral infiltrates, bloody BAL
- **Anaemia out of proportion to renal failure:** DAH - look for pulmonary source
- **Eosinophilia + asthma + haemoptysis:** EGPA
- **Fever + weight loss + haemoptysis:** GPA, infection, malignancy
- **Palpable purpura:** Cryoglobulinaemia, IgA vasculitis, `lupus`

### Laboratory Clues

- **Anti-GBM antibody positive:** Anti-GBM disease - IMMEDIATE treatment
- **c-ANCA/PR3 positive:** GPA
- **p-ANCA/MPO positive:** MPA, EGPA
- **ANA/anti-dsDNA positive:** Lupus
- **Low C3 and C4:** Lupus, cryoglobulinaemia
- **Very low C4:** Cryoglobulinaemia (more than `lupus`)
- **Cryoglobulins positive:** Cryoglobulinaemic vasculitis
- **Falling haemoglobin:** DAH
- **High DLCO:** DAH (alveolar haemoglobin increases DLCO)
- **Hypoxia (low PaO2):** DAH, pulmonary oedema
- **Metabolic acidosis:** AKI, lactic acidosis from hypoxia
- **Schistocytes:** TMA (rare cause)
- **Eosinophilia:** EGPA, drug-induced
- **Hepatitis C positive:** Cryoglobulinaemia
- **BAL bloody return:** Confirms DAH (but may be traumatic)

### Biopsy Clues

- **Linear IgG on renal IF:** Anti-GBM
- **Pauci-immune crescentic GN:** ANCA vasculitis
- **Full-house IF:** Lupus
- **Cryoglobulins in capillary lumina:** Cryoglobulinaemic vasculitis
- **Mesangial IgA:** IgA vasculitis
- **Pulmonary capillaritis:** ANCA vasculitis, anti-GBM
- **Alveolar haemorrhage (hemosiderin-laden macrophages):** DAH on BAL

### Recommended Investigations

1. Serum creatinine - STAT
2. Urine microscopy - RBC casts (STAT)
3. uPCR
4. Anti-GBM antibody - STAT
5. c-ANCA/PR3, p-ANCA/MPO - STAT
6. ANA, anti-dsDNA
7. C3, C4
8. Cryoglobulins
9. Hepatitis B and C
10. FBC (haemoglobin - look for fall)
11. CRP, ESR
12. ABG (PaO2, A-a gradient)
13. DLCO (elevated in DAH)
14. Chest X-ray - bilateral alveolar infiltrates
15. CT chest - ground-glass, consolidation
16. Bronchoscopy with BAL - bloody return, hemosiderin-laden macrophages
17. **Second-line:** Renal biopsy - URGENT
18. **Second-line:** Lung biopsy (if renal biopsy not diagnostic)
19. **Second-line:** Echocardiogram (cardiac cause of DAH)

### Differential Diagnosis

| Disease | Supporting Evidence | Evidence Against | Likelihood | Urgency |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| ANCA vasculitis (`anca`) | Age >50, systemic features, pauci-immune | Young, isolated renal | High (>50%) | Critical |
| Anti-GBM (`antiGbm`) | Anti-GBM Ab+, linear IgG, bimodal age | Negative serology, no linear IgG | Moderate | Critical - PLEX needed |
| Lupus (`lupus`) | Young female, ANA+, low complements | Male, no systemic features | Moderate | Critical |
| Cryoglobulinaemic (`cryoglobulinemic`) | Hepatitis C, purpura, very low C4 | No hep C, no purpura | Low-Moderate | High |
| Pulmonary oedema | Heart failure, raised JVP, bilateral crackles | Active urine sediment | Moderate | High - different treatment |
| Pulmonary embolism | Chest pain, hypoxia, DVT risk factors | Active urine sediment | Low | High - anticoagulate |
| Sepsis/ARDS | Fever, sepsis source, bilateral infiltrates | RBC casts, glomerular disease | Moderate | High - antibiotics |
| Infection (TB, fungal) | Fever, exposure, upper lobe disease | Active sediment, crescents | Low | High - antimicrobials |

### Immediate Management

- **Oxygen:** Target SpO2 >92%; high-flow if severe DAH
- **Mechanical ventilation:** If respiratory failure - consider early intubation
- **Empiric immunosuppression:** Do NOT wait for serology/biopsy if high clinical suspicion:
  - Pulse methylprednisolone 500-1000 mg IV x 3 days
- **Plasma exchange (PLEX):** Start IMMEDIATELY for:
  - Anti-GBM disease (mandatory - 50-60 mL/kg alternate days x 7-14)
  - Severe ANCA vasculitis with DAH (strongly consider)
  - Unknown aetiology (empiric)
- **Cyclophosphamide:** IV pulse (15 mg/kg q2-3 weeks) or oral (2 mg/kg/day) - start same day or next
  - **OR Rituximab:** 375 mg/m2 weekly x 4 - for ANCA vasculitis
- **Blood transfusion:** Target Hb >70 g/L (higher if haemodynamically unstable)
- **Dialysis:** If AKI stage 3 - may be temporary
- **Bronchoscopic BAL:** Confirm DAH, exclude infection
- **Infection prophylaxis:** TMP-SMX (PPEG), antivirals
- **Avoid fluid overload:** Careful fluid management - worsening DAH
- **Monitor:** ABG, Hb, creatinine, fluid balance, chest X-ray daily

### Long-Term Evaluation

- **Maintenance immunosuppression:** Azathioprine or rituximab for ANCA vasculitis - minimum 18-24 months
- **Relapse monitoring:** ANCA titres, urinalysis, creatinine, CRP - every 3 months
- **Pulmonary function:** DLCO monitoring (may remain impaired)
- **CT chest:** Serial imaging to assess resolution of DAH
- **ENT assessment:** If GPA - sinus and ear disease
- **Smoking cessation:** Critical - smoking increases anti-GBM relapse risk
- **Bone health:** DEXA scan, bisphosphonate prophylaxis
- **Vaccination:** Pre-immunosuppression
- **Transplant planning:** If ESKD - disease must be in remission >=12 months

### Linked GDES Diseases

- `anca`
- `antiGbm`
- `lupus`
- `cryoglobulinemic`
- `iga`

### Expected Outcomes

- **Anti-GBM:** Without treatment - fatal. With PLEX + immunosuppression - 50% renal survival, 80% survival. Poorer if creatinine >600 umol/L or dialysis-dependent. Relapse rare (<5%).
- **ANCA vasculitis with DAH:** 80-90% respond to induction. DAH mortality 10-20% even with treatment. Renal recovery 50-70% depending on severity. Relapse rate 30-50%.
- **Lupus with DAH:** 80-90% respond to immunosuppression. DAH usually resolves. Relapse possible.
- **Cryoglobulinaemic:** Antiviral therapy (DAAs) + rituximab >80% remission. DAH responds well to treatment.
- **General prognosis:** Pulmonary-renal syndrome has mortality of 20-40% even with modern treatment. Early recognition and treatment are the most important determinants of outcome.

## 11. Pregnancy-Associated Glomerular Disease

### Definition

Pregnancy-associated glomerular disease encompasses renal glomerular diseases that occur during pregnancy, worsen during pregnancy, or require specific management considerations due to pregnancy. It includes de novo glomerular disease presenting during pregnancy, pre-existing glomerular disease affected by pregnancy, and pregnancy-specific renal conditions (pre-eclampsia, HELLP syndrome, TMA).

**Key Considerations:**
- Pre-existing CKD (any cause) may worsen during pregnancy
- De novo glomerular disease may present during pregnancy (`lupus`, IgA, pre-eclampsia)
- Some diseases are unique to pregnancy (pre-eclampsia, HELLP)
- Drug safety limits treatment options
- Maternal and fetal outcomes are interdependent

**Diagnostic Approach:**
- Differentiate between pre-existing CKD, superimposed pre-eclampsia, and de novo disease
- Consider physiological changes of pregnancy (increased GFR, decreased creatinine, haemodilution)
- Normal creatinine in pregnancy: 35-70 umol/L (lower than non-pregnant)

> **Clinical Pearl:** Proteinuria increases in normal pregnancy (up to 300 mg/day) due to increased renal plasma flow. Nephrotic-range proteinuria during pregnancy is always pathological and requires investigation.

### Common Causes

| Disease | Typical Frequency | Key Clues |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-eclampsia/HELLP Syndrome | Very Common | HTN + proteinuria >300 mg/day after 20 weeks, HELLP (haemolysis, elevated LFTs, low platelets) |
| Lupus Nephritis (`lupus`) | Common | Young female, ANA+, anti-dsDNA+, low complements, may flare in pregnancy |
| IgA Nephropathy (`iga`) | Common | Recurrent haematuria, may worsen in pregnancy, mesangial IgA |
| Diabetic Nephropathy (`diabeticNephropathy`) | Common | DM, pre-existing proteinuria, may worsen |
| Focal Segmental Glomerulosclerosis (`fsgs`) | Uncommon | May relapse in pregnancy, proteinuria increase |
| Membranous Nephropathy (`membranous`) | Uncommon | Anti-PLA2R+, may flare in pregnancy |
| Thrombotic Microangiopathy (`thromboticMicroangiopathy) | Uncommon | MAHA, low platelets, may be pregnancy-triggered |
| Chronic Hypertension with superimposed pre-eclampsia | Common | Pre-existing HTN, worsening proteinuria, new features |

### Rare Causes

| Disease | Typical Frequency | Key Clues |
|---|---|---|
| Acute Fatty Liver of Pregnancy | Rare | Third trimester, coagulopathy, hypoglycaemia, elevated LFTs |
| IgA Vasculitis | Rare | Purpura, abdominal pain, haematuria |
| Post-partum TMA | Rare | Days-weeks post-partum, MAHA, AKI |
| ANCA Vasculitis (`anca`) | Rare | May flare in pregnancy, systemic features |
| Minimal Change Disease (`mcd`) | Rare | May relapse in pregnancy, nephrotic syndrome |

### Clinical Clues

- **New hypertension + proteinuria after 20 weeks:** Pre-eclampsia (most common cause)
- **MAHA + low platelets + elevated LFTs in pregnancy:** HELLP syndrome
- **Known `lupus` + pregnancy:** Risk of flare - monitor closely
- **Pre-existing CKD + worsening proteinuria/HTN in pregnancy:** Superimposed pre-eclampsia vs. disease flare
- **Macroscopic haematuria in pregnancy:** `iga` (synpharyngitic) - rare in pregnancy
- **Sudden onset nephrotic syndrome in pregnancy:** `membranous`, `mcd` (rare)
- **Thrombocytopenia + AKI in pregnancy:** TMA (aHUS, TTP), HELLP, pre-eclampsia
- **Third trimester + coagulopathy + hypoglycaemia:** Acute fatty liver of pregnancy
- **Recurrent pregnancy loss + HTN + proteinuria:** APS nephropathy, `lupus`
- **Family history of pre-eclampsia:** Genetic predisposition

### Laboratory Clues

- **Proteinuria >300 mg/day after 20 weeks:** Pathological - investigate
- **BP >140/90 after 20 weeks:** Pre-eclampsia until proven otherwise
- **Elevated LFTs (AST >70), low platelets (<100), haemolysis:** HELLP syndrome
- **Schistocytes + thrombocytopenia:** TMA (must differentiate from HELLP)
- **ANA/anti-dsDNA positive:** Lupus - monitor for flare
- **Low C3/C4:** Lupus flare
- **Elevated anti-PLA2R:** Membranous nephropathy
- **Serum creatinine in pregnancy:** <70 umol/L normal; >70 umol/L may indicate renal impairment (lower threshold than non-pregnant)
- **Uric acid >350 umol/L:** Associated with pre-eclampsia
- **LDH elevated:** Haemolysis (HELLP, TMA)
- **Haptoglobin low:** Haemolysis
- **Elevated liver enzymes:** HELLP, acute fatty liver
- **Normal C3/C4 in pre-eclampsia:** Differentiates from `lupus`

### Biopsy Clues (if performed)

- **Endotheliosis (glomerular):** Pre-eclampsia (classic finding)
- **Diffuse proliferative GN:** Lupus nephritis (Class III/IV)
- **Mesangial IgA:** IgA nephropathy
- **Thrombotic microangiopathy:** TMA, pre-eclampsia, HELLP
- **Membranous changes:** Membranous nephropathy
- **Segmental sclerosis:** FSGS

> **Clinical Pearl:** Renal biopsy in pregnancy is possible (usually second trimester) but carries fetal risk (1-2% fetal loss). Reserve for situations where diagnosis uncertainty significantly changes management (e.g., severe `lupus` nephritis requiring immunosuppression).

### Recommended Investigations

1. Blood pressure - regular monitoring (weekly in third trimester)
2. uPCR - monthly (more frequently if proteinuria increasing)
3. Serum creatinine, eGFR - monthly (lower normal range in pregnancy)
4. FBC (platelets, haemoglobin)
5. LFTs (AST, ALT, bilirubin)
6. LDH, haptoglobin (haemolysis screen)
7. Uric acid
8. Urine microscopy (haematuria, casts)
9. C3, C4
10. ANA, anti-dsDNA (if `lupus` suspected or known)
11. Anti-PLA2R (if `membranous` suspected)
12. Blood film (schistocytes)
13. Coagulation screen
14. Fetal monitoring (growth, Doppler, amniotic fluid)
15. **Second-line:** Renal biopsy (if diagnosis uncertainty changes management - usually second trimester)
16. **Second-line:** Complement levels (C3, C4, CH50) if TMA suspected

### Differential Diagnosis

| Disease | Supporting Evidence | Evidence Against | Likelihood | Urgency |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Pre-eclampsia | HTN + proteinuria after 20 weeks, HELLP features | Pre-existing renal disease, no HTN | High (most common) | High - delivery may be needed |
| Lupus flare (`lupus`) | ANA+, low complements, anti-dsDNA rise, multi-system | No `lupus` history, normal complements | Moderate | High - immunosuppression needed |
| HELLP | MAHA + low platelets + elevated LFTs, third trimester | No haemolysis, normal platelets | Moderate | Critical - delivery often needed |
| TMA | MAHA + low platelets + AKI, normal LFTs | Normal platelets, no haemolysis | Low-Moderate | Critical - plasma exchange |
| Superimposed pre-eclampsia on CKD | Pre-existing CKD + worsening HTN/proteinuria | No pre-existing CKD | Moderate | High |
| `iga` flare | Haematuria + mild proteinuria, known `iga` | No haematuria history | Low | Low-Moderate |
| `fsgs` relapse | Nephrotic syndrome + pregnancy, known `fsgs` | No nephrotic features | Low | Moderate |

### Immediate Management

- **Pre-eclampsia:** Antihypertensives (labetalol, nifedipine, methyldopa), magnesium sulphate for seizure prophylaxis, delivery at 37 weeks (or earlier if severe)
- **HELLP:** Delivery (often emergency), magnesium sulphate, blood products as needed
- **Lupus flare:** Hydroxychloroquine (safe), low-dose steroids; azathioprine if needed; avoid mycophenolate (teratogenic)
- **TMA (aHUS):** Plasma exchange + eculizumab (complement inhibitor)
- **TMA (TTP):** Plasma exchange + rituximab (avoid complement inhibitors unless aHUS confirmed)
- **Anticoagulation:** Low molecular weight heparin (LMWH) if thrombotic risk (`lupus` anticoagulant, severe proteinuria)
- **Fetal surveillance:** Regular growth scans, Doppler studies, NST
- **Pre-delivery optimisation:** Steroids for fetal lung maturity if <34 weeks
- **Monitor:** BP daily, uPCR weekly, creatinine monthly, fetal surveillance per obstetric protocol

### Long-Term Evaluation

- **Post-partum monitoring:** BP, proteinuria, creatinine at 6 weeks post-delivery
- **Disease reassessment:** Post-partum flare risk (`lupus`) - monitor for 6-12 months
- **Contraception:** Safe contraception (progesterone-only, IUD); avoid oestrogen-containing (thrombotic risk in `lupus`)
- **Future pregnancy counselling:** Discuss recurrence risk, optimal timing, pre-conception management
- **Pre-existing CKD:** Pre-conception optimisation (ACEi/ARB stop before conception, switch to safe antihypertensives)
- **Breastfeeding:** Compatible with most medications (hydroxychloroquine, low-dose steroids, azathioprine)
- **Long-term CKD surveillance:** If renal impairment persists post-partum
- **Bone health:** If prolonged steroid use during pregnancy

### Linked GDES Diseases

- `lupus`
- `iga`
- `fsgs`
- `membranous`
- `mcd`
- `diabeticNephropathy`
- `anca`

### Expected Outcomes

- **Pre-eclampsia:** Delivery is curative. 20-30% risk in subsequent pregnancies. Long-term cardiovascular risk increased.
- **HELLP:** Mortality <1% with modern care. Usually resolves after delivery. Recurrence in subsequent pregnancies possible.
- **Lupus nephritis in pregnancy:** 50-70% flares during pregnancy/post-partum. Modern immunosuppression improves outcomes. Fetal loss risk 10-20%.
- **`iga` in pregnancy:** Usually stable; may have mild worsening. Favourable maternal and fetal outcomes.
- **TMA in pregnancy:** aHUS: complement inhibition effective. TTP: PLEX + immunosuppression improves survival.
- **General:** Pregnancy in CKD (eGFR >45) usually feasible with monitoring. Poorer outcomes with eGFR <45, heavy proteinuria, uncontrolled HTN.

## 12. Post-Transplant Renal Dysfunction

### Definition

Post-transplant renal dysfunction refers to any decline in renal function following kidney transplantation, encompassing a broad differential including acute rejection (T-cell mediated or antibody-mediated), drug toxicity, recurrence of original disease, infection, and surgical complications. Early recognition and distinction between these causes is critical for graft salvage.

**Classification by Timing:**
- **Hyperacute (minutes-hours):** Pre-formed DSA, ABO incompatible - catastrophic, graft loss
- **Acute (days-months):** T-cell mediated rejection (TCMR), antibody-mediated rejection (ABMR), CNI toxicity, BK virus nephropathy, acute GN
- **Late (>6 months):** Chronic ABMR, transplant glomerulopathy, disease recurrence, CNI nephrotoxicity, new-onset diabetes (NODAT), BK virus nephropathy
- **Very late (>5 years):** Chronic ABMR, transplant glomerulopathy, disease recurrence, de novo GN

**Key Diagnostic Challenge:** Distinguishing rejection from infection (especially BK virus) from drug toxicity (CNI), as the treatments are diametrically opposed (increase immunosuppression for rejection vs. decrease for infection/toxicity).

> **Clinical Pearl:** The three most common causes of early graft dysfunction are: (1) TCMR, (2) CNI toxicity, and (3) BK virus nephropathy. A rising creatinine post-transplant should trigger a systematic approach: check drug levels, BK virus viral load, and perform biopsy if uncertainty persists.

### Common Causes

| Disease | Typical Frequency | Key Clues |
|---|---|---|
| T-Cell Mediated Rejection (`tCellMediatedRejection`) | Very Common | Fever, graft tenderness, rising creatinine, interstitial lymphocytic infiltrate, tubulitis |
| Antibody-Mediated Rejection (`antibodyMediatedRejection`) | Common | Rising creatinine, new DSA, C4d positivity, microvascular inflammation |
| CNI Toxicity (`cniToxicity`) | Very Common | High CNI trough levels, arteriolar hyalinosis, striped fibrosis, isometric vacuolisation |
| BK Virus Nephropathy (`bkVirusNephropathy`) | Common | BK viruria/viraemia, rising creatinine, viral cytopathic changes on biopsy |
| Transplant Glomerulopathy (`transplantGlomerulopathy`) | Common (late) | Late graft dysfunction, glomerular basement membrane double contours, DSA |
| Recurrent Disease (`iga`, `fsgs`, `membranous`) | Uncommon | Original disease markers, similar histology to native kidney biopsy |
| Acute Tubular Necrosis (early) | Common | Delayed graft function, ischaemia-reperfusion injury |
| Surgical Complications | Common (early) | Haematoma, ureteric leak, renal artery stenosis |

### Rare Causes

| Disease | Typical Frequency | Key Clues |
|---|---|---|
| De Novo Glomerulonephritis | Rare | New GN not present in native kidney, often `lupus` or IgA-like |
| Polyomavirus (other than BK) | Rare | JC virus, SV40, similar to BK |
| CMV Nephritis | Rare | CMV viraemia, interstitial nephritis |
| PTLD (Post-Transplant Lymphoproliferative Disorder) | Rare | EBV+, lymphoproliferative lesion, mass |
| Renal Artery Stenosis | Rare | Severe HTN, bruit over graft, FENa <1%, asymmetrical swelling |

### Clinical Clues

- **Rising creatinine 5-30 days post-transplant:** TCMR, CNI toxicity, BK virus - biopsy essential
- **Rising creatinine >3 months:** Chronic ABMR, transplant glomerulopathy, recurrent disease, CNI toxicity
- **Fever + graft tenderness + rising creatinine:** TCMR
- **High CNI trough + rising creatinine:** CNI toxicity (but overlap with rejection)
- **BK viruria/viraemia + rising creatinine:** BK virus nephropathy (biopsy to confirm)
- **Proteinuria >500 mg/day:** Transplant glomerulopathy, recurrent disease, chronic ABMR
- **New DSA + rising creatinine:** ABMR
- **Hypertension + graft bruit:** Renal artery stenosis
- **Macroscopic haematuria + proteinuria:** Recurrent glomerular disease (e.g., `iga`, `membranous`)
- **History of FSGS recurrence:** Primary FSGS recurs in ~30% post-transplant
- **De novo ANA/anti-dsDNA:** De novo `lupus` (very rare)

### Laboratory Clues

- **CNI trough levels:** High = toxicity; low = subtherapeutic (rejection risk)
- **BK virus PCR:** Blood viral load >10,000 copies/mL = significant viraemia; viruria alone less specific
- **Donor-specific antibodies (DSA):** New or rising MFI = ABMR
- **Complement C4d:** Positive on biopsy = ABMR (but not always present)
- **C3, C4:** Low in `lupus` recurrence, cryoglobulinaemia
- **Urinalysis:** Dysmorphic RBCs, RBC casts (glomerular disease), WBC casts (pyelonephritis/infection), viral inclusions
- **Urine PCR for BK virus:** Screening test (viruria may precede viraemia)
- **eGFR trend:** Acute vs. chronic decline
- **Proteinuria:** Quantify (uPCR) - glomerular vs. tubular pattern
- **FBC:** Lymphopenia (overimmunosuppression), anaemia (CKD)
- **Liver function:** If on tacrolimus (hepatotoxicity rare)

### Biopsy Clues

- **TCMR:** Interstitial lymphocytic infiltrate (>25% of parenchyma), tubulitis (>=4 lymphocytes per tubular cross-section), BANFF classification grading
- **ABMR:** Microvascular inflammation (glomerulitis + peritubular capillaritis), C4d deposition (linear peritubular capillaries), thrombotic microangiopathy, DSA positive
- **CNI toxicity:** Arteriolar hyalinosis (nodular, circumferential), isometric vacuolisation of tubular epithelium, striped tubulointerstitial fibrosis, arterial medial hyalinosis
- **BK virus nephropathy:** Viral cytopathic changes (nuclear inclusions, "ground glass" nuclei), interstitial lymphocytic infiltrate (mimics TCMR), SV40 immunostain positive
- **Transplant glomerulopathy:** Glomerular basement membrane double contours (silver stain), mesangial matrix expansion, endothelial swelling, C4d may be positive
- **Recurrent disease:** Similar to native kidney histology (e.g., mesangial IgA for `iga`, subepithelial deposits for `membranous`)
- **Pyelonephritis:** Neutrophilic infiltrate in tubules (bacterial infection)

> **Clinical Pearl:** BK virus nephropathy and TCMR can look histologically very similar on biopsy (both show interstitial infiltrates). SV40 immunostain is essential to distinguish them - treatment is opposite (reduce immunosuppression for BK, increase for TCMR).

### Recommended Investigations

1. Serum creatinine, eGFR - urgent if rising
2. CNI trough levels (tacrolimus, ciclosporin) - STAT
3. BK virus PCR (blood and urine)
4. Donor-specific antibodies (DSA) - if rejection suspected
5. Urine microscopy (casts, viral inclusions)
6. uPCR - quantify proteinuria
7. FBC, CRP
8. CMV PCR (if infection suspected)
9. EBV PCR (if PTLD suspected)
10. Renal transplant ultrasound (obstruction, vascular, perfusion)
11. **Second-line:** Renal transplant biopsy - ESSENTIAL if cause of dysfunction uncertain
12. **Second-line:** Immunostains (SV40 for BK, C4d for ABMR)
13. **Second-line:** Electron microscopy (transplant glomerulopathy, recurrent disease)
14. **Second-line:** HLA antibody screen, panel-reactive antibodies

### Differential Diagnosis

| Disease | Supporting Evidence | Evidence Against | Likelihood | Urgency |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| `tCellMediatedRejection` | Fever, graft tenderness, rising creatinine, tubulitis on biopsy | High CNI levels, BK+ | High (early) | Critical - increase immunosuppress |
| `antibodyMediatedRejection` | New DSA, C4d+, microvascular inflammation | No DSA, negative C4d | Moderate | Critical - PLEX + immunosuppress |
| `cniToxicity` | High CNI trough, arteriolar hyalinosis, vacuolisation | Normal/low levels, tubulitis | Common | High - reduce CNI dose |
| `bkVirusNephropathy` | BK viraemia >10,000, SV40+, viral inclusions | Negative BK PCR, no viraemia | Common | High - reduce immunosuppress |
| `transplantGlomerulopathy` | Late (>6 mo), proteinuria, double contours on biopsy, DSA | Early dysfunction, no proteinuria | Common (late) | Moderate |
| Recurrent disease | Original disease markers, similar histology | Different histology, no original markers | Low-Moderate | Moderate |
| Obstruction | Dilated collecting system on US, anuria | Normal US, non-obstructive | Low | High - decompress |

### Immediate Management

- **TCMR (biopsy-proven):**
  - Pulse methylprednisolone 500 mg IV x 3 days
  - If steroid-resistant: anti-thymocyte globulin (ATG) 1.5 mg/kg/day x 7-14 days
  - Adjust maintenance immunosuppression (increase tacrolimus target, add MMF if not on it)
- **ABMR (biopsy-proven):**
  - PLEX (5-7 sessions)
  - IVIG (2 g/kg in divided doses)
  - Rituximab (375 mg/m2) or bortezomib (proteasome inhibitor)
  - Eculizumab (complement inhibitor) for refractory cases
  - Increase immunosuppression
- **CNI toxicity:**
  - Reduce CNI dose or switch to alternative (e.g., everolimus)
  - Consider CNI-free regimen if recurrent toxicity
  - Monitor levels closely
- **BK virus nephropathy:**
  - REDUCE immunosuppression (first-line: reduce tacrolimus target, stop MMF/azathioprine)
  - Consider leflunomide (anti-BK activity, limited evidence)
  - IVIG (anti-viral properties)
  - No proven antiviral therapy (cidofovir toxic, limited efficacy)
  - Monitor BK viral load q2-4 weeks
- **Transplant glomerulopathy:**
  - Optimise immunosuppression (tacrolimus + MMF)
  - Treat any concurrent ABMR
  - ACEi/ARB for proteinuria
  - No proven specific therapy
- **Obstruction:** Urgent decompression (ureteric stent, nephrostomy)

### Long-Term Evaluation

- **CNI levels:** Monitor q1-3 months (tacrolimus target 4-8 ng/mL stable; higher in early post-transplant)
- **BK viral load:** Screen q3 months for first 2 years; treat if >10,000 copies/mL
- **DSA monitoring:** Every 3-6 months for first year, then annually
- **eGFR trend:** Monthly for first year, then q3 months
- **uPCR:** Monthly for first year, then q3 months (proteinuria >500 mg/day = biopsy)
- **Protocol biopsies:** Some centres perform at 3, 12 months (controversial)
- **CMV monitoring:** PCR q2 weeks for first 3-6 months
- **NODAT screening:** HbA1c q3 months for first year
- **Bone health:** DEXA if prolonged steroids
- **Cardiovascular risk:** Annual assessment
- **Cancer screening:** Skin (squamous cell carcinoma), PTLD (EBV PCR), cervical (increased risk)
- **Vaccination:** Live vaccines contraindicated if immunosuppressed; inactivated vaccines safe
- **Transplant education:** Medication adherence, infection signs, rejection signs, skin protection

### Linked GDES Diseases

- `tCellMediatedRejection`
- `antibodyMediatedRejection`
- `cniToxicity`
- `bkVirusNephropathy`
- `transplantGlomerulopathy`
- `iga` (recurrent)
- `fsgs` (recurrent)
- `membranous` (recurrent)
- `lupus` (recurrent, rare)
- `anca` (recurrent, rare)
- `c3` (recurrent)

### Expected Outcomes

- **TCMR:** >90% respond to pulse steroids. ATG effective for steroid-resistant. 1-year graft survival >95% with treatment.
- **ABMR:** Variable; 60-80% graft salvage with PLEX + immunosuppression. Chronic ABMR has poorer prognosis (5-year graft loss 30-50%).
- **CNI toxicity:** Usually reversible with dose adjustment. Chronic CNI nephrotoxicity contributes to late graft loss (interstitial fibrosis/tubular atrophy).
- **BK virus nephropathy:** 30-50% graft loss if untreated. With reduction of immunosuppression, 50-70% graft survival. Early detection (preemptive reduction based on viral load) improves outcomes.
- **Transplant glomerulopathy:** Progressive; 5-year graft loss 30-50%. No proven specific therapy. Often associated with chronic ABMR.
- **Recurrence of primary disease:**
  - `fsgs`: ~30% recurrence post-transplant; plasmapheresis may be effective
  - `iga`: ~30% recurrence on protocol biopsy; usually benign; <5% graft loss at 10 years
  - `membranous`: ~5-10% recurrence; usually responsive to immunosuppression
  - `lupus`: Rare recurrence (<1%); excellent graft survival
  - `anca`: 5-10% recurrence; usually mild; responds to treatment
  - `c3` glomerulopathy: ~50% recurrence; poor prognosis

## 13. Macroscopic (Visible) Hematuria

### Definition

Macroscopic (visible) haematuria is the presence of blood in the urine visible to the naked eye, ranging from pink/discoloured to frankly bloody urine. It may be painless (raise concern for malignancy, glomerular disease) or painful (suggesting stones, infection, or obstruction). In the nephrology context, painless macroscopic haematuria with glomerular features is of particular concern.

**Diagnostic Criteria:**
- Visible discolouration of urine by blood (pink, red, brown, cola-coloured)
- Confirmed by urine dipstick (strongly positive for blood) and microscopy (RBCs)
- Classification by timing:
  - **Initial haematuria:** Urethral/bladder neck source
  - **Terminal haematuria:** Bladder/ureteric source
  - **Total haematuria:** Kidney/ureteric source (glomerular or urological)
- Glomerular vs. urological origin assessment:
  - Glomerular: Dysmorphic RBCs, RBC casts, proteinuria, cola/tea-coloured urine
  - Urological: Isomorphic RBCs, no casts, clots, bright red blood

> **Clinical Pearl:** All painless visible haematuria requires investigation for urological malignancy (CT urogram, cystoscopy) regardless of suspected glomerular cause. Glomerular disease and urological pathology can coexist, especially in older patients.

### Common Causes

| Disease | Typical Frequency | Key Clues |
|---|---|---|
| IgA Nephropathy (`iga`) | Very Common (most common glomerular cause of macroscopic haematuria) | Young adults (20-40), synpharyngitic (haematuria within days of URI), recurrent episodes, cola-coloured urine, mesangial IgA |
| Urological Malignancy (bladder, renal, prostate) | Very Common (age >40) | Painless visible haematuria, age >40, risk factors (smoking, chemical exposure), isomorphic RBCs |
| Urolithiasis (renal/ureteric colic) | Very Common | Severe loin pain, nausea/vomiting, radiopaque stones on CT, isomorphic RBCs |
| Urinary Tract Infection | Very Common | Dysuria, frequency, urgency, positive urine culture, WBCs, fever |
| Prostatic Disease (BPH, prostatitis) | Common (men >50) | Nocturia, hesitancy, dribbling, elevated PSA, isomorphic RBCs |
| Glomerulonephritis (various) | Common | Dysmorphic RBCs, RBC casts, proteinuria, rising creatinine |
| Bladder Stones/Foreign Body | Uncommon | Recurrent haematuria, history of instrumentation, bladder discomfort |
| Exercise-Induced Haematuria | Uncommon | Strenuous exercise, resolves within 48 hours, no proteinuria |

### Rare Causes

| Disease | Typical Frequency | Key Clues |
|---|---|---|
| Renal Cell Carcinoma | Rare (but important) | Age >50, incidental finding on imaging, polycythaemia, loin pain |
| Hereditary Nephritis (Alport) (`alport`) | Rare | Family history of ESKD/deafness, young males, progressive |
| Thin Basement Membrane Nephropathy (`thinBasementMembrane`) | Uncommon | Family microscopic haematuria, usually not macroscopic |
| Lupus Nephritis (`lupus`) | Rare (as macroscopic haematuria) | Young female, ANA+, multi-system |
| Vasculitis (`anca`) | Rare | Systemic features, crescents, age >50 |
| Papillary Necrosis | Rare | Diabetes, analgesic abuse, sickle cell disease, renal colic |
| Arteriovenous Malformation | Rare | Large volume painless haematuria, angiography shows AVM |

### Clinical Clues

- **Young adult with synpharyngitic macroscopic haematuria:** `iga` (most likely)
- **Recurrent macroscopic haematuria at same time each year:** `iga` (seasonal URI trigger)
- **Painless visible haematuria + age >40:** Urological malignancy - urgent investigation
- **Loin pain + haematuria:** Renal/ureteric colic (stones), papillary necrosis, renal infarction
- **Dysuria + frequency + haematuria:** UTI
- **Haematuria + proteinuria + rising creatinine:** Glomerulonephritis - nephrology referral
- **Family history of ESKD/deafness:** `alport` (X-linked males)
- **Haematuria + hearing loss:** `alport`
- **Haematuria + arthritis/arthralgia:** `lupus`, IgA vasculitis
- **Recent strenuous exercise:** Exercise-induced haematuria (usually resolves)
- **Bright red blood + clots:** Urological source (urethral/bladder)
- **Cola/tea-coloured urine + no clots:** Glomerular source
- **Haematuria during menstruation:** Consider endometriosis of urinary tract

### Laboratory Clues

- **Dysmorphic RBCs + RBC casts:** Glomerular source - investigate for GN
- **Isomorphic RBCs, no casts:** Urological source - investigate for stones/malignancy
- **Proteinuria >0.5 g/day with haematuria:** Significant glomerular disease - biopsy
- **Low C3:** Post-infectious GN, C3 glomerulopathy
- **ANA/anti-dsDNA positive:** `lupus`
- **Anti-PLA2R positive:** `membranous` (if proteinuria)
- **Normal C3/C4:** `iga`, `thinBasementMembrane`, urological
- **Positive urine culture:** UTI
- **Elevated PSA:** Prostatic disease/malignancy
- **Haemoglobin stable:** Chronic/recurrent glomerular source
- **Falling haemoglobin:** Significant bleeding (urological source more common)
- **Urine cytology:** Abnormal cells - urological malignancy
- **CT urogram:** Gold standard for urological malignancy investigation

### Biopsy Clues

- **Mesangial IgA on IF:** `iga`
- **Thin GBM on EM:** `thinBasementMembrane`
- **Abnormal GBM (basket-weave) on EM:** `alport`
- **Crescents on LM:** RPGN (rare cause of macroscopic haematuria)
- **Full-house IF:** `lupus`
- **Normal histology:** May be normal glomeruli with IgA (biopsy often not needed for isolated macroscopic haematuria without proteinuria)

> **Clinical Pearl:** Macroscopic haematuria in `iga` often occurs within 24-72 hours of upper respiratory infection ("synpharyngitic") - distinguishing it from post-infectious GN (which occurs 1-2 weeks later). The urine is often described as "cola-coloured" or "tea-coloured."

### Recommended Investigations

1. Urine dipstick and microscopy - confirm haematuria, assess for dysmorphic RBCs, casts
2. Urine culture - exclude UTI
3. Serum creatinine, eGFR
4. uPCR - quantify proteinuria
5. **If age >40 or risk factors for malignancy:**
   - CT urogram (triple phase) - URGENT
   - Flexible cystoscopy (exclude bladder malignancy)
6. **If glomerular source suspected:**
   - C3, C4
   - ANA, anti-dsDNA
   - IgA level
   - Anti-GBM antibody (if AKI present)
   - ANCA (if systemic features)
7. **If family history:**
   - Family screening
   - Genetic testing (Alport - COL4A3/A4/A5)
8. **Second-line:** Renal biopsy (if proteinuria >0.5 g/day, declining eGFR, or uncertain cause)
9. **Second-line:** Renal Doppler ultrasound (if AVM suspected)
10. **Second-line:** Angiography (if AVM suspected on imaging)

### Differential Diagnosis

| Disease | Supporting Evidence | Evidence Against | Likelihood | Urgency |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| `iga` | Young adult, synpharyngitic, recurrent, cola-coloured | Age >40, no URI association | High (if young) | Moderate |
| Urological malignancy | Age >40, painless, risk factors | Young, glomerular features | High (age >40) | High - urgent CT urogram |
| Renal/ureteric stone | Loin pain, vomiting, radiopaque on CT | No pain, normal CT | High (if pain present) | High - analgesia, urology |
| UTI | Dysuria, frequency, positive culture | No dysuria, negative culture | High (if symptoms present) | Low - antibiotics |
| Prostatic disease | Men >50, nocturia, elevated PSA | Women, no LUTS | Moderate (men >50) | Low-Moderate |
| `lupus` | Young female, ANA+, systemic features | Male, no systemic features | Low | Moderate |
| `thinBasementMembrane` | Family history, usually microscopic | No family history, usually not macroscopic | Low | Low - benign |

### Immediate Management

- **Exclude urological malignancy:** CT urogram + cystoscopy if age >40 or risk factors
- **Treat UTI:** If positive urine culture
- **Analgesia:** For renal/ureteric colic (NSAIDs, paracetamol, opioids)
- **Hydration:** Encourage fluid intake
- **Avoid nephrotoxins:** NSAIDs if renal function impaired
- **Urology referral:** If urological malignancy suspected
- **Nephrology referral:** If glomerular cause suspected (proteinuria, RBC casts, declining eGFR)
- **Monitoring:** Repeat urine dipstick in 2-4 weeks to confirm resolution
- **Reassurance:** If exercise-induced and resolves within 48 hours

### Long-Term Evaluation

- **Follow-up urine testing:** Confirm resolution of haematuria
- **If `iga` confirmed:** Annual monitoring (uPCR, creatinine, BP)
- **If urological malignancy excluded:** Reassure but maintain surveillance
- **Family screening:** If genetic cause identified
- **ACEi/ARB:** If proteinuria develops - renoprotective
- **Avoid nephrotoxins:** NSAIDs, aminoglycosides
- **Pregnancy counselling:** If genetic GN identified
- **Urological follow-up:** If stones - metabolic workup, dietary modification
- **Annual screening:** For patients with risk factors for urological malignancy

### Linked GDES Diseases

- `iga`
- `alport`
- `thinBasementMembrane`
- `lupus`
- `anca`
- `c3`
- `membranous`
- `fsgs`

### Expected Outcomes

- **`iga`:** Macroscopic haematuria episodes usually self-limit within days. 50-70% have recurrent episodes over 10-20 years. 20-30% develop proteinuria/HTN. ACEi/ARB renoprotective. Immunosuppression for severe histology.
- **`alport`:** X-linked males: progressive, 90% ESKD by age 40 without treatment. ACEi/ARB delay progression. Transplant usually curative but risk of anti-GBM disease (5%).
- **`thinBasementMembrane`:** Excellent prognosis. Persistent microscopic haematuria, no progression. Macroscopic episodes unusual.
- **Urological malignancy:** Prognosis depends on stage at diagnosis. Early detection excellent; advanced poor.
- **UTI:** Complete resolution with appropriate antibiotics. Recurrent UTI warrants investigation.
- **Stones:** Good prognosis with treatment. Metabolic workup to prevent recurrence.
- **Overall:** Macroscopic haematuria is a symptom that requires systematic investigation. The most important distinction is glomerular vs. urological source.

## 14. Complement-Mediated Glomerular Disease

### Definition

Complement-mediated glomerular disease encompasses conditions in which dysregulation of the complement system (classical, lectin, or alternative pathway) plays a central pathogenic role in glomerular injury. It includes diseases with complement consumption (low C3/C4), C3 glomerulopathy, and conditions with complement-mediated injury without hypocomplementaemia.

**Classification:**
- **Alternative pathway dysregulation:** C3 glomerulopathy, dense deposit disease, CFHR mutations, C3 nephritic factor
- **Classical pathway activation:** Lupus nephritis, cryoglobulinaemic GN
- **Lectin pathway activation:** Post-infectious GN, IgA nephropathy
- **Complement-mediated TMA:** aHUS (Factor H, Factor I, MCP mutations, anti-Factor H antibodies)
- **Combined pathway:** MPGN (immune complex-mediated)

**Diagnostic Criteria:**
- Low C3 with or without low C4
- C3-dominant immunofluorescence on renal biopsy
- Dense deposits on electron microscopy (dense deposit disease)
- Alternative pathway complement abnormalities (Factor H, Factor I, C3 nephritic factor, autoantibodies)
- May present with nephrotic syndrome, nephritic syndrome, or mixed pattern

> **Clinical Pearl:** Isolated low C3 with normal C4 narrows the differential to: post-infectious GN, C3 glomerulopathy, MPGN (non-cryoglobulinaemic), and `lupus` (rarely). The combination of low C3 + dense deposits on EM defines dense deposit disease, while C3-dominant IF without dense deposits defines C3 glomerulopathy (GN type).

### Common Causes

| Disease | Typical Frequency | Key Clues |
|---|---|---|
| Post-Infectious GN (`irgn`) | Very Common | Children/young adults, 1-2 wk post-infection, low C3, hypocomplementaemia, subepithelial humps |
| C3 Glomerulopathy (`c3`) | Common | Low C3, C3-dominant IF, no/subendothelial deposits, alternative pathway mutations |
| Dense Deposit Disease (`denseDepositDisease`) | Common (C3 glomerulopathy subtype) | Very low C3, ribbon-like GBM on EM, "tram-track" appearance, drusen (ocular) |
| Lupus Nephritis (`lupus`) | Common | Young female, ANA+, anti-dsDNA+, full-house IF, low C3 and C4 |
| Membranoproliferative Glomerulonephritis (`mpgn`) | Common | MPGN pattern, low C3, hepatitis C, immune complex-mediated |
| Post-Streptococcal GN | Common | Children, strep pharyngitis/skin infection, low C3, self-limiting |
| Cryoglobulinaemic GN (`cryoglobulinemic`) | Uncommon | Hepatitis C, very low C4, mixed cryoglobulins, purpura |

### Rare Causes

| Disease | Typical Frequency | Key Clues |
|---|---|---|
| CFHR Mutations | Rare | C3 glomerulopathy, familial, genetic |
| C3 Nephritic Factor (C3NeF) | Rare | Persistent low C3, stabilises C3 convertase, MPGN |
| Anti-Factor H Antibody | Rare | aHUS + C3 glomerulopathy, low C3, autoimmune |
| Factor I Deficiency | Rare | Very low C3, recurrent infections, aHUS, C3 glomerulopathy |
| Monoclonal Ig-Associated C3 Glomerulopathy | Rare | Paraprotein, older adults, C3-dominant IF |

### Clinical Clues

- **Low C3, normal C4, young adult:** Post-infectious GN, C3 glomerulopathy
- **Low C3, low C4:** Lupus, cryoglobulinaemia (classical pathway)
- **Very low C3 with C3 glomerulopathy features:** Dense deposit disease, C3NeF
- **Family history of renal disease:** CFHR mutations, Factor H deficiency
- **Recurrent infections + low C3:** Factor I deficiency, complement pathway defects
- **Hepatitis C + low C4 + purpura:** Cryoglobulinaemia
- **Drusen on retinal examination:** Dense deposit disease (ocular involvement)
- **MPGN pattern on biopsy + low C3:** C3 glomerulopathy vs. MPGN
- **Young adult with nephrotic/nephritic syndrome + low C3:** C3 glomerulopathy
- **Paraprotein + low C3:** Monoclonal Ig-associated C3 glomerulopathy
- **aHUS + low C3:** Alternative pathway mutation (Factor H, I, MCP)

### Laboratory Clues

- **Very low C3 (<0.5 g/L) with normal C4:** C3 glomerulopathy, dense deposit disease
- **Moderately low C3 (0.5-0.8 g/L) with normal C4:** Post-infectious GN (usually transient)
- **Low C3, low C4:** Lupus, cryoglobulinaemia (classical/lectin pathway)
- **C3 nephritic factor (C3NeF):** Stabilises C3 convertase - causes persistent low C3
- **Anti-Factor H antibody:** Autoimmune aHUS + C3 glomerulopathy
- **Factor H, I, MCP levels:** May be normal (mutations may not affect serum levels)
- **Genetic testing:** CFHR genes, complement pathway genes
- **Hepatitis C:** Cryoglobulinaemia
- **ANA/anti-dsDNA:** Lupus
- **Serum complement profile:** C3, C4, CH50, AP50 (alternative pathway haemolytic assay)
- **Urine sediment:** Dysmorphic RBCs, RBC casts (nephritic), oval fat bodies (nephrotic)
- **Serum creatinine, eGFR:** Variable depending on disease

### Biopsy Clues

- **C3-dominant IF (>=2+ C3, minimal/no IgG):** C3 glomerulopathy
- **Dense, ribbon-like deposits on EM (GBM):** Dense deposit disease
- **Subepithelial humps on EM:** Post-infectious GN
- **MPGN pattern (mesangial proliferation + capillary wall thickening):** C3 glomerulopathy or MPGN
- **Full-house IF:** Lupus
- **Subendothelial deposits:** MPGN (immune complex), `lupus`
- **Mesangial deposits:** C3 glomerulopathy (GN type)
- **"Starry sky" IF:** Post-infectious GN (C3 + IgG, but less dominant)
- **Double contours (tram-track) on silver stain:** MPGN pattern
- **Glomerulonephritis with dense deposits on EM:** Dense deposit disease

> **Clinical Pearl:** The distinction between C3 glomerulopathy and post-infectious GN is crucial. C3 glomerulopathy is chronic/relapsing and often progressive; post-infectious GN is usually self-limiting. C3-dominant IF with dense deposits on EM in a patient without recent infection strongly suggests C3 glomerulopathy.

### Recommended Investigations

1. C3, C4 - quantification
2. CH50 (total haemolytic complement) - assess classical pathway
3. AP50 (alternative pathway haemolytic assay) - assess alternative pathway
4. C3 nephritic factor (C3NeF) - stabilises C3 convertase
5. Factor H, Factor I, MCP (CD46) levels and genetic testing
6. Anti-Factor H antibody
7. ANA, anti-dsDNA (`lupus` exclusion)
8. Hepatitis B and C serology
9. Serum immunoglobulins, SPE
10. Serum cryoglobulins
11. Renal biopsy - ESSENTIAL for diagnosis (LM, IF, EM)
12. Ophthalmological examination (drusen - dense deposit disease)
13. **Second-line:** Genetic testing (CFHR genes, complement pathway genes)
14. **Second-line:** Complement functional assays (CH50, AP50)
15. **Second-line:** Serum free light chains (monoclonal Ig-associated)

### Differential Diagnosis

| Disease | Supporting Evidence | Evidence Against | Likelihood | Urgency |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Post-infectious GN (`irgn`) | Child, 1-2 wk post-infection, low C3, self-limiting | Adult, no infection, persistent | High (children) | Low - usually self-limiting |
| C3 glomerulopathy (`c3`) | Low C3, C3-dominant IF, chronic/relapsing | Normal C3, full-house IF | Moderate | Moderate - progressive |
| Dense deposit disease (`denseDepositDisease`) | Very low C3, ribbon-like GBM, drusen | No drusen, normal C3 | Low-Moderate | Moderate - progressive |
| `lupus` | Young female, ANA+, low C3/C4, full-house IF | Male, no systemic features | Moderate | High |
| `mpgn` | MPGN pattern, hepatitis C, low C3 | No hep C, normal C3 | Moderate | Moderate |
| `cryoglobulinemic` | Hepatitis C, very low C4, purpura | No hep C, normal C4 | Low-Moderate | High |
| Monoclonal Ig-associated | Older adult, paraprotein, C3-dominant IF | Young, no paraprotein | Low | Moderate |

### Immediate Management

- **Post-infectious GN:** Supportive care (BP control, fluid management). Usually self-limiting. Monitor creatinine, C3 (should normalise in 6-8 weeks).
- **C3 glomerulopathy:** ACEi/ARB for proteinuria/BP. Consider mycophenolate or rituximab (limited evidence). Eculizumab (complement inhibitor) under investigation.
- **Dense deposit disease:** ACEi/ARB. Consider eculizumab (limited efficacy). Monitor for drusen progression. Prognosis guarded.
- **Lupus:** Immunosuppression (mycophenolate/cyclophosphamide + steroids)
- **MPGN:** Treat underlying cause (hepatitis C antivirals). Immunosuppression if idiopathic.
- **Cryoglobulinaemia:** Antivirals (DAAs for hep C) + rituximab
- **BP control:** Target <130/80 mmHg if proteinuric
- **Dialysis:** If AKI severe - may be temporary

### Long-Term Evaluation

- **Complement levels:** Monitor C3, C4 every 3-6 months
- **Proteinuria monitoring:** uPCR every 3-6 months
- **eGFR monitoring:** Every 1-3 months during active disease
- **Ophthalmological screening:** Annual fundoscopy for dense deposit disease (drusen)
- **Genetic counselling:** If complement gene mutations identified
- **Complement inhibitor therapy:** If available and indicated (aHUS, refractory C3 glomerulopathy)
- **Renal biopsy:** Repeat if clinical course changes (relapse, deterioration)
- **Transplant planning:** C3 glomerulopathy may recur post-transplant (50%); complement inhibitors may be needed
- **Bone health:** If prolonged immunosuppression
- **Infection prophylaxis:** Meningococcal vaccination if on complement inhibitors

### Linked GDES Diseases

- `c3`
- `denseDepositDisease`
- `irgn`
- `lupus`
- `mpgn`
- `cryoglobulinemic`
- `iga`

### Expected Outcomes

- **Post-infectious GN:** Excellent prognosis. >95% spontaneous resolution. C3 normalises in 6-8 weeks. Rarely progresses to CKD (<5%).
- **C3 glomerulopathy:** Guarded. 50% progress to ESKD at 10 years. May recur post-transplant. Complement inhibitors under investigation.
- **Dense deposit disease:** Poor prognosis. 50% ESKD at 10 years. Ocular involvement (drusen) may progress. May recur post-transplant.
- **Lupus nephritis:** 70-90% respond to immunosuppression. 10-20% ESKD at 10 years.
- **MPGN (hepatitis C-related):** Antiviral therapy (DAAs) achieves virological cure and improves renal outcomes.
- **MPGN (idiopathic):** Variable; 30-50% progress to ESKD at 10 years.
- **Cryoglobulinaemic GN:** Hepatitis C clearance (DAAs) + rituximab >80% remission.
- **Overall:** Complement-mediated diseases are often chronic and progressive. Early identification of complement pathway abnormalities may enable targeted therapy (complement inhibitors).

## 15. Thrombotic Microangiopathy (TMA)

### Definition

Thrombotic microangiopathy (TMA) is a pathological and clinical syndrome characterised by microangiopathic haemolytic anaemia (MAHA), thrombocytopenia, and organ damage due to thrombotic occlusion of small vessels by platelet-rich thrombi. Renal involvement is common and may range from subclinical to end-stage kidney disease.

**Diagnostic Criteria:**
- **MAHA:** Schistocytes on blood film, elevated LDH, low/absent haptoglobin, elevated reticulocyte count, negative direct antiglobulin test (Coombs-negative)
- **Thrombocytopenia:** Platelet count <150 x 10^9/L (may be mild in aHUS)
- **Organ damage:** Renal (AKI, proteinuria, hypertension), neurological (seizures, confusion, stroke), cardiac, gastrointestinal
- **Renal biopsy:** Thrombotic microangiopathy - fibrin thrombi in glomerular capillaries and arterioles, endothelial swelling, fragmented RBCs

**Classification:**
- **Shiga toxin-associated HUS (STEC-HUS):** Diarrhoea-positive (D+HUS), usually children, E. coli O157:H7
- **Atypical HUS (aHUS):** Complement pathway mutations (Factor H, Factor I, MCP, CFB, C3), anti-Factor H antibodies
- **Thrombotic Thrombocytopenic Purpura (TTP):** ADAMTS13 deficiency (<10% activity), autoantibody or congenital
- **Secondary TMA:** Pregnancy (pre-eclampsia/HELLP), drugs (calcineurin inhibitors, OKT3, clopidogrel, quinine, cocaine), autoimmune (`lupus`, antiphospholipid syndrome), malignant hypertension, cobalamin deficiency

> **Clinical Pearl:** TTP is a medical emergency with >90% mortality if untreated. The classic pentad (MAHA, thrombocytopenia, fever, neurological symptoms, renal failure) is present in <10% of cases. Any patient with unexplained thrombocytopenia + MAHA should have ADAMTS13 activity checked IMMEDIATELY.

### Common Causes

| Disease | Typical Frequency | Key Clues |
|---|---|---|
| Shiga Toxin-Associated HUS (STEC-HUS) | Very Common (children) | Bloody diarrhoea, E. coli O157:H7, children <5, usually self-limiting |
| Atypical HUS (aHUS) | Common | Complement mutations, no diarrhoea, relapsing, renal-predominant |
| Thrombotic Thrombocytopenic Purpura (TTP) | Common | ADAMTS13 <10%, neurological symptoms, fever, less renal-predominant |
| Drug-Induced TMA | Common | Temporal drug association (CNI, OKT3, clopidogrel, quinine), improves with drug withdrawal |
| Pregnancy-Related TMA | Common | Pre-eclampsia/HELLP, third trimester, MAHA + low platelets + elevated LFTs |
| Malignant Hypertension-Associated TMA | Uncommon | Severely elevated BP (>180/120), papilloedema, retinopathy, AKI |
| Lupus-Associated TMA | Uncommon | Young female, ANA+, low complements, multi-system |
| Antiphospholipid Syndrome-Associated TMA | Uncommon | Recurrent thrombosis, aPL antibodies, livedo reticularis |

### Rare Causes

| Disease | Typical Frequency | Key Clues |
|---|---|---|
| Cobalamin C Deficiency | Rare | Homocystinuria, methylmalonic aciduria, infantile onset |
| Disseminated Malignancy | Rare | Known malignancy, DIC-like picture |
| Heparin-Induced Thrombocytopenia (HIT) | Rare | Recent heparin exposure, thrombosis, 4T score |
| Calciphylaxis | Rare | CKD, calcium-phosphate imbalance, painful skin necrosis |
| Catastrophic Antiphospholipid Syndrome | Rare | Multi-organ thrombosis, aPL positive, rapid deterioration |

### Clinical Clues

- **Bloody diarrhoea + MAHA + AKI (children):** STEC-HUS
- **No diarrhoea + MAHA + AKI + neurological features:** TTP or aHUS
- **MAHA + thrombocytopenia + fever + confusion:** TTP - check ADAMTS13 IMMEDIATELY
- **MAHA + thrombocytopenia + AKI + hypertension:** aHUS or malignant HTN
- **Recent drug change + MAHA:** Drug-induced TMA
- **Third trimester + MAHA + low platelets + elevated LFTs:** HELLP (not pure TMA)
- **Known `lupus` + MAHA + low platelets:** Lupus-associated TMA
- **Recurrent thrombosis + MAHA:** Antiphospholipid syndrome
- **Severely elevated BP + papilloedema + MAHA:** Malignant hypertension
- **Neurological symptoms (seizures, stroke, confusion) + MAHA:** TTP (neurological predominant)
- **Cardiac involvement + MAHA:** TTP or aHUS (cardiac thrombi)
- **Young patient with unexplained AKI + MAHA:** aHUS - check complement genetics

### Laboratory Clues

- **Schistocytes on blood film:** MAHA (>=1% or >5/high-power field)
- **Elevated LDH:** Haemolysis + tissue ischaemia (often >1000 IU/L in TTP)
- **Low/absent haptoglobin:** Haemolysis (<0.1 g/L)
- **Elevated reticulocyte count:** Haemolysis
- **Negative direct antiglobulin test (Coombs-negative):** Microangiopathic (not autoimmune haemolytic anaemia)
- **Low platelets:** Thrombocytopenia (<150 x 10^9/L)
- **ADAMTS13 activity <10%:** TTP (autoantibody or congenital)
- **ADAMTS13 activity >10%:** aHUS, STEC-HUS, secondary TMA
- **Stool culture for E. coli O157:H7:** STEC-HUS (Shiga toxin-positive)
- **Complement levels (C3, C4):** Low in aHUS (some mutations)
- **Complement genetics (Factor H, Factor I, MCP, CFB, C3):** aHUS
- **Anti-Factor H antibody:** Autoimmune aHUS
- **Serum creatinine, eGFR:** AKI (variable severity)
- **Urine sediment:** Haematuria, proteinuria, RBC casts
- **Coagulation screen:** Usually normal in TMA (unlike DIC)
- **Blood cultures:** Exclude sepsis (may mimic TMA)

### Biopsy Clues (Renal)

- **Fibrin thrombi in glomerular capillaries and arterioles:** TMA (pathognomonic)
- **Endothelial swelling and detachment:** TMA
- **Fragmented RBCs in capillary lumina:** TMA
- **Glomerular ischaemic changes (wrinkled GBM, solidified tufts):** Chronic TMA
- **Arterial/arteriolar intimal thickening and onion-skinning:** Malignant hypertension
- **Corticomedullary necrosis:** Severe TMA
- **Mesangiolysis:** TMA
- **C4d positivity:** May be present in aHUS (complement activation)

> **Clinical Pearl:** Renal biopsy in suspected TMA is important to confirm diagnosis but should not delay treatment of TTP (PLASM exchange is time-critical). In TTP, the kidney is often less severely affected than the brain. In aHUS, the kidney is predominantly affected.

### Recommended Investigations

1. Blood film - schistocytes (STAT)
2. LDH, haptoglobin, reticulocyte count (STAT)
3. Direct antiglobulin test (Coombs) - should be negative
4. Platelet count
5. ADAMTS13 activity - STAT (do not wait for confirmatory test)
6. ADAMTS13 inhibitor/autoantibody
7. Serum creatinine, eGFR, electrolytes
8. Urine microscopy, uPCR
9. Stool culture + Shiga toxin assay (if STEC-HUS suspected)
10. Complement levels (C3, C4)
11. Anti-Factor H antibody
12. Complement genetics (if aHUS suspected)
13. Coagulation screen (PT, APTT, fibrinogen, D-dimer) - to exclude DIC
14. ANA, anti-dsDNA (`lupus` exclusion)
15. Antiphospholipid antibodies (`lupus` anticoagulant, anti-cardiolipin, anti-B2GPI)
16. Blood cultures (if febrile)
17. **Second-line:** Renal biopsy (if diagnosis uncertain and patient stable)
18. **Second-line:** Echocardiogram (cardiac thrombi, endocarditis)
19. **Second-line:** CT brain (if neurological symptoms)

### Differential Diagnosis

| Disease | Supporting Evidence | Evidence Against | Likelihood | Urgency |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| TTP | ADAMTS13 <10%, neurological symptoms, fever, less renal-predominant | ADAMTS13 >10%, no neurological | Moderate | Critical - PLEX immediately |
| aHUS | Complement mutation, AKI predominant, no diarrhoea | Normal complement, STEC+ | Moderate | Critical - complement inhibitor |
| STEC-HUS | Bloody diarrhoea, children, E. coli O157:H7 | No diarrhoea, adult | High (children) | High - supportive care |
| Drug-induced TMA | Recent drug withdrawal, improves | No drug exposure | Moderate | High - stop drug |
| HELLP | Third trimester, elevated LFTs, pregnancy | Not pregnant | Moderate (pregnancy) | Critical - delivery |
| Malignant HTN | BP >180/120, papilloedema, retinopathy | Normal BP | Low-Moderate | Critical - BP control |
| DIC | Elevated D-dimer, low fibrinogen, prolonged PT/PTT | Normal coagulation | Low | High - treat cause |
| Lupus TMA | ANA+, low complements, young female | No `lupus` features | Low | High - immunosuppress |

### Immediate Management

- **TTP (ADAMTS13 <10%):**
  - PLEX IMMEDIATELY (daily until ADAMTS13 >50% and platelet >150)
  - Corticosteroids (prednisolone 1 mg/kg/day)
  - Rituximab (375 mg/m2 weekly x 4) - second-line or relapsing
  - Caplacizumab (anti-vWF nanobody) - reduces thrombosis
  - Avoid platelet transfusion (may worsen thrombosis)
  - Monitor: Platelets q12h, LDH, ADAMTS13 qweek
- **aHUS (complement-mediated):**
  - Eculizumab (complement C5 inhibitor) - FIRST-LINE (loading 900 mg weekly x 4, then 1200 mg q2 weeks)
  - PLEX may be used as bridge but NOT effective long-term
  - Meningococcal vaccination BEFORE eculizumab (lifelong prophylaxis)
  - Monitor: Platelets, creatinine, LDH, complement levels
- **STEC-HUS:**
  - Supportive care (fluids, electrolytes, dialysis if needed)
  - Antibiotics CONTROVERSIAL (may worsen Shiga toxin release)
  - Anti-motility agents CONTRAINDICATED (may worsen toxin absorption)
  - Eculizumab if severe/progressive (limited evidence)
  - Usually self-limiting in children (>90% recover)
- **Drug-induced TMA:**
  - STOP offending drug (most important intervention)
  - Supportive care (dialysis if needed)
  - PLEX if severe (limited evidence)
  - Complement inhibitors if refractory
- **Malignant hypertension:**
  - Gradual BP reduction (IV labetalol, nicardipine)
  - Target 25% reduction in first 24 hours
  - Avoid rapid BP reduction (may worsen renal ischaemia)
- **Lupus TMA:**
  - Immunosuppression (mycophenolate/cyclophosphamide + steroids)
  - Consider complement inhibitors if refractory
- **Supportive care (all TMA):**
  - Dialysis if AKI severe
  - Blood transfusion if Hb <70 g/L (avoid in TTP if possible)
  - Seizure prophylaxis/treatment if neurological involvement
  - Monitor: Platelets q6-12h, LDH q12-24h, creatinine daily

### Long-Term Evaluation

- **TTP:**
  - Monitor ADAMTS13 activity q3-6 months (risk of relapse if <10%)
  - Rituximab for relapsing TTP (ADAMTS13 <10%)
  - Lifelong surveillance (relapse rate 20-30%)
  - Avoid oestrogen-containing contraceptives (thrombotic risk)
  - Pregnancy counselling (relapse risk)
- **aHUS:**
  - Lifelong eculizumab (may trial withdrawal after 6-12 months remission in some patients)
  - Genetic counselling (complement mutations - autosomal dominant)
  - Renal transplant (aHUS may recur post-transplant - prophylactic eculizumab)
  - Monitor: Platelets, creatinine, complement levels q3 months
- **STEC-HUS:**
  - Usually resolves; long-term sequelae (CKD, hypertension) in 10-20%
  - Annual monitoring (uPCR, creatinine, BP)
  - Renal ultrasound if CKD develops
- **Drug-induced TMA:**
  - Avoid re-exposure to offending drug
  - Document drug allergy
  - Monitor renal recovery at 3, 6, 12 months
- **General:**
  - Cardiovascular risk assessment (increased risk in all TMA)
  - CKD management if eGFR does not recover
  - Bone health if prolonged immunosuppression
  - Vaccination (pneumococcal, influenza, meningococcal if complement inhibitors)

### Linked GDES Diseases

- `c3`
- `denseDepositDisease`
- `lupus`
- `iga`
- `anca`
- `membranous`
- `fsgs`

### Expected Outcomes

- **TTP:** >90% survival with PLEX + immunosuppression. Relapse rate 20-30%. Neurological sequelae in 10-20%. Renal recovery usually complete if treated early.
- **aHUS:** Eculizumab >90% renal survival (vs. <10% without treatment). May trial withdrawal after sustained remission. Genetic forms: variable prognosis depending on mutation.
- **STEC-HUS:** >90% recovery in children. 10-20% develop CKD/hypertension long-term. Rare mortality (<5%).
- **Drug-induced TMA:** >90% recover if drug stopped early. Persistent CKD in 10-30%.
- **Lupus TMA:** 70-90% respond to immunosuppression. Depends on severity.
- **Malignant HTN-associated TMA:** Renal prognosis guarded. 50% recover some function with BP control.
- **Overall:** TTP and aHUS are medical emergencies requiring immediate specific treatment. Delayed treatment significantly worsens outcomes.

## 16. Nephrotic-Range Proteinuria with Rapid Decline

### Definition

Nephrotic-range proteinuria with rapid decline is a clinical presentation combining heavy proteinuria (>=3.5 g/24h or uPCR >=300 mg/mmol) with a rapid decline in renal function (eGFR falling >5 mL/min/1.73m2 per year or doubling of creatinine within months). This combination suggests an aggressive glomerular disease requiring urgent investigation and treatment.

**Diagnostic Criteria:**
- Nephrotic-range proteinuria: uPCR >=300 mg/mmol (>=3.5 g/g) OR 24-hour urine protein >=3.5 g
- Rapid eGFR decline: >5 mL/min/1.73m2 per year OR doubling of serum creatinine within 3-6 months
- May be new presentation or superimposed on pre-existing kidney disease
- Often accompanied by worsening hypoalbuminaemia, oedema, and hyperlipidaemia

**Key Pathogenic Mechanisms:**
- Active glomerular inflammation (crescents, endocapillary proliferation)
- Podocyte injury (collapsing FSGS, MCD with AKI)
- Thrombotic microangiopathy (glomerular capillary thrombosis)
- Progressive scarring (IFTA, global sclerosis) - less likely to be "rapid" but may accelerate

> **Clinical Pearl:** Nephrotic-range proteinuria with rapid eGFR decline is an emergency. The combination suggests either (1) aggressive GN (crescents superimposed on nephrotic disease), (2) collapsing FSGS (HIV-associated or idiopathic), (3) thrombotic microangiopathy, or (4) superimposed AKI on chronic nephrotic disease. Urgent biopsy is usually required.

### Common Causes

| Disease | Typical Frequency | Key Clues |
|---|---|---|
| Collapsing FSGS (`fsgs`) | Common | HIV+, Black/African ancestry, nephrotic-range + rapidly falling eGFR, collapsing variant on biopsy, viral infections |
| Membranous Nephropathy with AKI (`membranous`) | Common | Anti-PLA2R+, superimposed AKI (interstitial nephritis, renal vein thrombosis, drug-induced), nephrotic + rapid decline |
| Lupus Nephritis (Class III/IV/V) (`lupus`) | Common | Young female, ANA+, anti-dsDNA+, low complements, nephrotic + nephritic features, crescents |
| Focal Segmental Glomerulosclerosis (`fsgs`) | Common | Obesity, Black ancestry, steroid-resistant, nephrotic + falling eGFR, NOS or collapsing variant |
| ANCA Vasculitis with Nephrotic Features (`anca`) | Uncommon | Age >50, systemic features, crescents on biopsy, nephrotic-range proteinuria (unusual but possible) |
| Membranoproliferative Glomerulonephritis (`mpgn`) | Uncommon | Hepatitis C, cryoglobulinaemia, mixed deposits, nephrotic + nephritic |
| C3 Glomerulopathy (`c3`) | Uncommon | Low C3, C3-dominant IF, progressive, nephrotic + falling eGFR |
| Amyloidosis | Uncommon | Age >60, organomegaly, paraprotein, Congo red positive, nephrotic + progressive |

### Rare Causes

| Disease | Typical Frequency | Key Clues |
|---|---|---|
| Fibrillary Glomerulonephritis (`fibrillaryGlomerulonephritis`) | Rare | Anti-DNAJB9, non-amyloid fibrils, progressive, nephrotic-range |
| HIV-Associated Nephropathy (HIVAN) | Rare (in HAART era) | HIV+, collapsing FSGS, young Black males, nephrotic-range + rapid decline |
| Dengue-Associated Glomerulonephritis | Rare | Travel history, dengue serology, nephrotic + AKI |
| Light Chain Deposition Disease | Rare | Myeloma, monoclonal light chains, nodular glomerulosclerosis, progressive |
| IgA Nephropathy with Nephrotic Syndrome | Rare | Young adult, macroscopic haematuria, nephrotic-range proteinuria, crescents |

### Clinical Clues

- **HIV + nephrotic + rapid decline:** Collapsing FSGS (HIVAN) - very aggressive
- **Black/African ancestry + nephrotic + rapid decline:** Collapsing FSGS (idiopathic or HIV)
- **Nephrotic + rising creatinine over weeks-months:** Aggressive GN, collapsing FSGS, TMA
- **Nephrotic + haematuria + rising creatinine:** Lupus (Class III/IV/V), crescentic GN superimposed on nephrotic
- **Anti-PLA2R + nephrotic + AKI:** Membranous with superimposed AKI (drug, interstitial nephritis, renal vein thrombosis)
- **Nephrotic + purpura + low C4:** Cryoglobulinaemic GN
- **Nephrotic + age >60 + organomegaly:** Amyloidosis, light chain deposition disease
- **Nephrotic + very low C3 + dense deposits:** Dense deposit disease, C3 glomerulopathy
- **Sudden worsening of previously stable nephrotic syndrome:** Renal vein thrombosis (especially in `membranous`), drug-induced AKI, infection
- **Nephrotic + fever + weight loss:** Systemic disease (`lupus`, vasculitis, amyloid)
- **Nephrotic + new hypertension:** Aggressive GN, collapsing FSGS
- **Rapid oedema accumulation:** Nephrotic + AKI (fluid retention worsened by oliguria)

### Laboratory Clues

- **Nephrotic-range proteinuria (uPCR >300 mg/mmol):** Confirms nephrotic component
- **Rapidly falling eGFR:** >5 mL/min/1.73m2 per year or doubling creatinine in 3-6 months
- **Hypoalbuminaemia (<25 g/L):** Severe nephrotic syndrome
- **Anti-PLA2R positive:** `membranous` (if `membranous` suspected)
- **ANA/anti-dsDNA positive, low C3/C4:** `lupus`
- **HIV positive:** Collapsing FSGS (HIVAN) - high suspicion
- **Low C3 with normal C4:** C3 glomerulopathy, post-infectious
- **Very low C3:** Dense deposit disease, C3 glomerulopathy
- **Hepatitis C positive:** `cryoglobulinemic`, `mpgn`
- **Serum cryoglobulins:** Cryoglobulinaemia
- **SPE/immunoglobulins/free light chains:** Myeloma, amyloid, light chain deposition disease
- **Serum amyloid A (SAA):** Amyloidosis (AA type)
- **LDH, haptoglobin:** If TMA suspected
- **Blood film:** Schistocytes (TMA)
- **ADAMTS13:** If TTP suspected
- **Urinalysis:** Dysmorphic RBCs, RBC casts (nephritic component), oval fat bodies (nephrotic)
- **Renal ultrasound:** Kidney size (may be enlarged in HIVAN, amyloid; small in chronic disease)

### Biopsy Clues

- **Collapsing FSGS:** Global or segmental collapse of glomerular tuft with podocyte hyperplasia and hypertrophy, viral inclusions (HIV), SV40 positive if polyomavirus
- **Membranous with AKI:** Subepithelial deposits with spikes, superimposed interstitial nephritis or TMA
- **Lupus nephritis (Class III/IV/V):** Diffuse proliferative GN + full-house IF + crescents + subendothelial deposits
- **Segmental sclerosis (NOS):** FSGS
- **C3-dominant IF with dense deposits on EM:** C3 glomerulopathy, dense deposit disease
- **Congo red positive, apple-green birefringence:** Amyloidosis
- **Non-amyloid fibrils (12-20 nm) on EM:** Fibrillary glomerulonephritis
- **Thrombotic microangiopathy:** Fibrin thrombi, fragmented RBCs, endothelial swelling
- **MPGN pattern:** Mesangial proliferation + capillary wall thickening
- **"Wire-loop" lesions:** Lupus nephritis
- **Nodular glomerulosclerosis (non-Kimmelstiel-Wilson):** Light chain deposition disease, amyloid

> **Clinical Pearl:** Collapsing FSGS is the most aggressive variant of FSGS and carries the worst prognosis. It is strongly associated with HIV and other viral infections. Early biopsy and treatment (if HIV-related: optimise ART; if idiopathic: immunosuppression) is critical. Response to treatment is poor compared to other forms of FSGS.

### Recommended Investigations

1. Serum creatinine, eGFR - STAT and serial q1-2 weeks
2. uPCR - quantify nephrotic-range proteinuria
3. Serum albumin
4. FBC, CRP
5. Renal function trend - compare with previous values
6. Anti-PLA2R antibody (if `membranous` suspected)
7. ANA, anti-dsDNA, C3, C4 (if `lupus` suspected)
8. HIV serology (if collapsing FSGS suspected)
9. Hepatitis B and C
10. SPE, immunoglobulins, serum free light chains
11. LDH, haptoglobin, reticulocyte count (TMA screen)
12. Blood film (schistocytes)
13. ADAMTS13 activity (if TTP suspected)
14. Cryoglobulins (if vasculitic features)
15. Serum amyloid A (if amyloid suspected)
16. Renal ultrasound (kidney size, echogenicity)
17. **Second-line:** Renal biopsy - URGENT (essential for diagnosis and treatment)
18. **Second-line:** Genetic testing (if familial FSGS or complement mutations)
19. **Second-line:** Bone marrow biopsy (if myeloma/amyloid suspected)

### Differential Diagnosis

| Disease | Supporting Evidence | Evidence Against | Likelihood | Urgency |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Collapsing FSGS (`fsgs`) | HIV+, Black ancestry, nephrotic + rapid decline, collapsing on biopsy | HIV-, no rapid decline | Moderate (if HIV+) | Critical - biopsy + ART |
| Lupus nephritis (Class IV/V) (`lupus`) | Young female, ANA+, low complements, crescents, nephrotic + nephritic | Male, no systemic features | Moderate | Critical - immunosuppress |
| `membranous` with AKI | Anti-PLA2R+, nephrotic, superimposed AKI | No AKI, negative PLA2R | Moderate | High - investigate AKI cause |
| `fsgs` (NOS/cellular) | Obesity, Black ancestry, steroid-resistant, nephrotic + falling eGFR | Lean, steroid-responsive | Moderate | High |
| `anca` with nephrotic features | Age >50, systemic features, crescents, nephrotic | Young, no systemic features | Low-Moderate | Critical |
| Amyloidosis | Age >60, organomegaly, paraprotein, Congo red+ | Young, no systemic features | Low-Moderate | High |
| `cryoglobulinemic` | Hepatitis C, purpura, low C4, nephrotic + nephritic | No hep C, no purpura | Low | High |

### Immediate Management

- **Urgent nephrology admission:** All patients with nephrotic-range proteinuria + rapid eGFR decline
- **Renal biopsy:** URGENT (within 24-48 hours if safe) - essential for diagnosis
- **Empiric immunosuppression:** If biopsy delayed and high clinical suspicion:
  - Pulse methylprednisolone 500-1000 mg IV x 3 days (if crescents or aggressive GN suspected)
- **HIV-associated collapsing FSGS:** Optimise antiretroviral therapy (ART) - most important intervention. Consider corticosteroids if no response to ART alone.
- **Lupus nephritis (Class IV/V):** Induction immunosuppression (mycophenolate or cyclophosphamide + steroids)
- **Membranous with AKI:** Identify and treat AKI cause (stop drug, treat interstitial nephritis, anticoagulate if renal vein thrombosis)
- **Crescentic GN:** Standard RPGN treatment (steroids, cyclophosphamide/rituximab, PLEX if anti-GBM or severe ANCA)
- **TMA:** Standard TMA management (see Syndrome 15)
- **Supportive care:**
  - Fluid and sodium restriction
  - Loop diuretics for oedema (furosemide 40-80 mg, titrate)
  - ACEi/ARB if BP allows (may need to hold if AKI severe)
  - Anticoagulation if renal vein thrombosis (heparin, then warfarin/DOAC)
  - Albumin infusion for severe symptomatic oedema with diuretic resistance
- **Dialysis:** If AKI severe (hyperkalaemia, fluid overload, acidosis, uraemic symptoms)
- **Monitor:** Daily creatinine, electrolytes, fluid balance, daily weights, uPCR q1-2 weeks

### Long-Term Evaluation

- **Disease-specific monitoring:** See individual disease modules and above syndromes
- **Renal recovery assessment:** Repeat creatinine at 3, 6, 12 months
- **Proteinuria target:** Aim for >=50% reduction in uPCR from baseline
- **eGFR monitoring:** Every 1-3 months during active disease, every 3-6 months in remission
- **Serum albumin:** Monthly until >30 g/L
- **VTE prophylaxis:** If anticoagulated (especially `membranous`)
- **Steroid-sparing agent monitoring:** FBC, LFTs, glucose as per drug protocol
- **Bone health:** DEXA scan if prolonged corticosteroid use
- **Relapse monitoring:** Monthly uPCR for first year, then quarterly
- **CKD management:** If eGFR does not recover - standard CKD care
- **Pre-ESKD education:** If eGFR declining towards 20 - dialysis access planning, transplant workup
- **Transplant planning:** If ESKD - disease must be in remission >=12 months. Collapsing FSGS has high recurrence risk post-transplant.
- **Vaccination:** Pneumococcal, influenza, hepatitis B (pre-dialysis)
- **Long-term complication screening:** Infection, thrombosis, cardiovascular disease, CKD progression

- `fsgs`
- `membranous`
- `lupus`
- `anca`
- `c3`
- `denseDepositDisease`
- `mpgn`
- `cryoglobulinemic`
- `iga`
- `fibrillaryGlomerulonephritis`
- `alport`

- `drugInducedGn`

### Expected Outcomes

- **Collapsing FSGS:** Poor prognosis. <20% respond to immunosuppression. HIV-related: ART improves outcomes but renal prognosis remains guarded. 50-80% progress to ESKD within 5 years. High recurrence post-transplant.
- **Lupus nephritis (Class III/IV/V):** 70-90% respond to induction. Relapse rate 30-50%. 10-20% ESKD at 10 years with modern therapy.
- **Membranous with AKI:** Depends on cause of AKI. If drug-induced: >90% recover. If renal vein thrombosis: variable recovery. Primary `membranous`: 30-40% spontaneous remission.
- **FSGS (NOS):** 30-50% respond to immunosuppression. 30-40% ESKD at 10 years. Genetic forms less responsive.
- **ANCA vasculitis with nephrotic features:** 80-90% achieve remission. Relapse rate 30-50%. Renal outcome depends on severity and chronicity.
- **Amyloidosis:** Poor prognosis without treatment of underlying cause. AA amyloid: treat underlying inflammatory disease. AL amyloid: chemotherapy (bortezomib-based). Median survival 2-5 years.
- **C3 glomerulopathy:** 50% ESKD at 10 years. May recur post-transplant. Complement inhibitors under investigation.
- **Dense deposit disease:** 50% ESKD at 10 years. Ocular involvement (drusen). May recur post-transplant.
- **Cryoglobulinaemic GN:** Hepatitis C clearance (DAAs) + rituximab >80% remission.
- **Fibrillary glomerulonephritis:** Progressive; 50% ESKD at 5-10 years. Limited treatment options. Rituximab may slow progression.
- **Overall:** Nephrotic-range proteinuria with rapid eGFR decline is a medical emergency. The key to improving outcomes is EARLY BIOPSY and DISEASE-SPECIFIC TREATMENT. Delayed treatment (especially in crescentic GN and collapsing FSGS) significantly worsens prognosis.
