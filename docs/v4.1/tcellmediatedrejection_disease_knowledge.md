## 2. Disease Record — Full 21-Field Schema
### 2.1 Definition
T-Cell Mediated Rejection (TCMR) is a form of renal allograft rejection mediated by recipient T-cells that recognise donor alloantigens (primarily donor HLA) presented by donor or recipient antigen-presenting cells. TCMR is classified by the Banff system into: Type I — tubulointerstitial (tubulitis + interstitial infiltrate, no arterial involvement); Type II — vascular (intimal arteritis); Type III — severe/transmural arteritis with fibrinoid necrosis. TCMR occurs most frequently in the first year post-transplant (incidence 10-20% in modern immunosuppression era) but can occur at any time, particularly with under-immunosuppression or non-adherence. Diagnosis requires biopsy: light microscopy shows interstitial infiltrate of mononuclear cells (predominantly CD4+ and CD8+ T-cells), tubulitis (T-cell invasion of tubular epithelium), and in severe cases, intimal arteritis. Treatment involves pulse corticosteroids for Type I and anti-thymocyte globulin (ATG) for Type II/III or steroid-resistant cases.
### 2.2 Epidemiology
Incidence: 10-20% in first year post-transplant despite modern immunosuppression (tacrolimus + MMF). Subclinical TCMR (on protocol biopsy without graft dysfunction): 5-15%. Risk factors: under-immunosuppression (tacrolimus trough <5 ng/mL), non-adherence (30-50% of late TCMR), reduced CNI exposure (CNI-sparing/minimisation protocols), steroid withdrawal, younger recipient age, deceased donor (vs living), delayed graft function, HLA mismatch (especially DR mismatch), previous TCMR episodes, African American recipient, CMV infection. Late TCMR (>1 year) is increasingly recognised and often associated with non-adherence — has worse graft survival than early TCMR (reflecting chronic injury). Time trends: incidence declined from 40-50% in the 1990s (azathioprine/prednisone era) to 10-20% with tacrolimus/MMF.
### 2.3 Aetiology
Direct allorecognition: recipient CD4+ and CD8+ T-cells recognise intact donor MHC molecules on donor antigen-presenting cells (passenger leukocytes) — the dominant pathway early post-transplant. Indirect allorecognition: recipient T-cells recognise processed donor allopeptides presented by self-APCs — more important in late/chronic rejection. Semi-direct allorecognition: recipient APCs acquire intact donor MHC from exosomes/membrane transfer. Co-stimulatory signals (CD28-CD80/86, CD40-CD40L) are required for full T-cell activation. Without adequate immunosuppression (CNI blocks calcineurin-NFAT pathway, MMF blocks purine synthesis), activated T-cells proliferate, migrate into the graft, and mediate tissue injury via: (1) cytotoxic CD8+ T-cells: perforin/granzyme B killing of tubular epithelial cells and endothelial cells; (2) CD4+ Th1 cells: IFN-gamma activation of macrophages; (3) CD4+ Th17 cells: IL-17-mediated neutrophil recruitment; (4) Delayed-type hypersensitivity: macrophage-mediated injury. Regulatory T-cells (Tregs) normally suppress alloimmune responses — inadequate Treg function promotes TCMR.
### 2.4 Pathophysiology
Phase 1 — Sensitisation: Donor dendritic cells (passenger leukocytes) migrate to recipient lymphoid organs. Recipient naive T-cells encounter donor alloantigen via direct pathway (intact donor MHC). Co-stimulation via CD28-CD80/86 provides signal 2. Calcineurin-NFAT signalling leads to IL-2 production and T-cell proliferation. Phase 2 — Graft Infiltration: Activated T-cells express adhesion molecules (LFA-1, VLA-4) and chemokine receptors (CXCR3, CCR5). Chemokines (CXCL9, CXCL10, CCL5) produced by inflamed graft endothelium guide T-cells into the graft. CD8+ CTLs cross the peritubular capillary endothelium and target tubular epithelial cells expressing donor MHC class I. Phase 3 — Tissue Injury: CD8+ CTLs release perforin and granzyme B -> tubular epithelial cell apoptosis (tubulitis). CD4+ Th1 cells release IFN-gamma -> macrophage activation -> interstitial inflammation and cytokine release. Intimal arteritis: T-cells infiltrate the subendothelial space of arteries, causing endothelialitis (lifting of endothelium from basement membrane). Severe arteritis: transmural infiltration, fibrinoid necrosis -> graft infarction. Phase 4 — Resolution or Chronicity: Successful treatment (steroids/ATG) depletes activated T-cells. Untreated/severe TCMR -> chronic changes (intimal fibrosis, IFTA).
### 2.5 Clinical Presentation
EARLY ACUTE TCMR (days-weeks): Rising creatinine (most common — 25-100% from baseline), oliguria, graft tenderness/swelling, fever, hypertension. May present with <25% Cr rise in subclinical cases detected on protocol biopsy. SEVERE TCMR (Type II/III): anuria, graft rupture (rare), fever, marked graft tenderness. LATE TCMR (>1 year): insidious creatinine rise over weeks-months, often asymptomatic until advanced. Often associated with non-adherence or reduced immunosuppression. Subclinical TCMR: detected on surveillance/protocol biopsy (3, 6, 12 months) in patients with stable creatinine. Important: TCMR can co-exist with ABMR (mixed rejection). Differentiating features: TCMR typically has tubulitis and interstitial inflammation without microvascular inflammation (glomerulitis, peritubular capillaritis) and without DSA/C4d.
### 2.6 Diagnostic Criteria
| # | Criterion | Essential | Evidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Biopsy evidence of interstitial infiltration (>10-25% of cortex) by mononuclear cells (i>0) — Banff i-score | Yes | 1 |
| 2 | Tubulitis: mononuclear cells infiltrating tubular epithelium (t>0) — Banff t-score. More severe = more tubules involved | Yes | 1 |
| 3 | Intimal arteritis: subendothelial mononuclear cells in arteries (v>0) — Banff v-score. Defines Type II/III rejection | No | 1 |
| 4 | Transmural arteritis or fibrinoid necrosis (v3) — defines Type III (severe) rejection | No | 1 |
| 5 | C4d-negative (distinguishes from ABMR) | No | 1 |
| 6 | Exclusion of ABMR: no DSA, no microvascular inflammation (g=0, ptc=0), C4d negative | Yes | 1 |
| 7 | Exclusion of BK virus nephropathy: SV40 negative, BK PCR negative | Yes | 1 |

### 2.8 Laboratory Findings
  * 
Rising serum creatinine — hallmark of graft dysfunction. Rate of rise correlates with severity (slow in mild TCMR, rapid in severe)  * DSA testing: negative DSA (if positive, consider mixed ABMR/TCMR). Essential to distinguish pure TCMR from concurrent ABMR  * BK virus PCR (plasma) — exclude BK virus nephropathy (biopsy mimics TCMR with tubulitis and interstitial infiltrate)  * Tacrolimus trough level — detect under-immunosuppression (<5 ng/mL increases risk). Assess adherence by level variability  * Urinalysis: may show sterile pyuria, WBC casts. No proteinuria unless concurrent glomerular pathology  * CMV PCR — CMV infection can trigger TCMR (especially late)  * Donor-derived cell-free DNA (dd-cfDNA) — elevated in rejection. Emerging non-invasive biomarker. Not a replacement for biopsy
### 2.9 Biopsy Findings
  * 
Type I TCMR (Banff): diffuse interstitial infiltrate (i1-i3 — >10%, 25%, 50% of cortex). Tubulitis: t1-t3 (1-4, 4-10, >10 mononuclear cells per tubular cross-section). No arterial involvement. Interstitial oedema may be present. Eosinophils and plasma cells may be present (but not dominant)  * Type II TCMR: intimal arteritis (v1: <25% of luminal area; v2: >25%). Subendothelial mononuclear cells lifting endothelium. No fibrinoid necrosis. Must distinguish from ABMR (v-lesion with C4d/DSA = ABMR)  * Type III TCMR: severe arteritis — transmural infiltration of mononuclear cells + fibrinoid necrosis of arterial wall (v3). May be associated with haemorrhage and infarction. Medical emergency  * Subclinical TCMR: Banff i>0, t>0 on protocol biopsy but stable creatinine. Lower grade (i1, t1) usually. May progress to clinical TCMR if untreated  * Borderline changes/suspicious: i0-1, t0-1 — insufficient for definite TCMR but may progress. Often treated if clinical suspicion high  * IMMUNOFLUORESCENCE: C4d negative. If C4d positive => suspect ABMR component. IgG negative (unless recurrent GN). Tubular basement membrane: no immune deposits

### 2.11 Risk Stratification
| Factor | Impact | Risk Level |
|---|---|---|
| Tacrolimus trough <5 ng/mL | 2-3x increased risk of TCMR — the single most important modifiable risk factor | High |
| Non-adherence to immunosuppression | Accounts for 30-50% of late TCMR episodes (>1 year) | High |
| Previous TCMR episode | 2-3x risk of recurrent TCMR | High |
| HLA-DR mismatch (2 vs 0) | 60-80% increased risk of TCMR | Moderate |
| Delayed graft function | Higher risk — donor organ stress increases immunogenicity | Moderate |
| Deceased donor (vs living) | Higher risk of TCMR (1.5-2x) due to ischaemia-reperfusion injury | Moderate |
| Steroid withdrawal (early) | Higher TCMR risk — especially in high-risk recipients | Moderate |
### 2.12 Treatment Overview
FIRST-LINE (Type I TCMR, borderline): Pulse methylprednisolone (500-1000 mg IV daily x3-5 days) with steroid taper (oral prednisone starting 60 mg/day tapered over 2-4 weeks). Optimise baseline immunosuppression: tacrolimus target 5-8 ng/mL (increase from sub-therapeutic level), MMF 2 g/day (or adequate MPA AUC). Type I TCMR: 70-80% respond to pulse steroids. SECOND-LINE (Type II/III TCMR, steroid-resistant): Anti-thymocyte globulin (ATG, 1.5 mg/kg IV daily x5-14 days depending on response) — rabbit ATG (Thymoglobulin) is preferred. Monitor platelet count and WBC during ATG (thrombocytopenia, leukopenia common). ATG-resistant: re-biopsy to exclude missed ABMR or BKVN. Consider T-cell depleting agents: alemtuzumab (Campath-1H) in extreme cases. SUPPORTIVE: CMV prophylaxis (valganciclovir) during intensified immunosuppression. PJP prophylaxis (TMP-SMX). Optimise CNI and MMF dosing based on response. Late TCMR (>1 year): same principles but reassess baseline immunosuppression strategy — may need to avoid CNI reduction.
### 2.13 Treatment Algorithm
| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | Confirm TCMR by biopsy: Banff type, grade i/t/v scores. Exclude mixed rejection (check DSA, C4d). Exclude BKVN (SV40) |
| 2 | Type I (t1-t2, v0): Methylprednisolone 500 mg IV daily x3-5 days + oral prednisone taper. Optimise tacrolimus to 5-8 ng/mL, MMF 2 g/day |
| 3 | Type II (v1-v2): Methylprednisolone 500 mg IV x3-5 days + ATG 1.5 mg/kg IV x5-14 days. Optimise baseline immunosuppression |
| 4 | Type III (v3): ATG 1.5 mg/kg IV x7-14 days. Consider re-biopsy at day 14 if no response. May need additional therapy |
| 5 | Steroid-resistant TCMR (no response by Day 5-7): ATG 1.5 mg/kg x7-14 days. Re-biopsy to exclude other diagnoses |
| 6 | ATG-resistant: re-biopsy to exclude ABMR, BKVN, or recurrent disease. Consider alemtuzumab rescue (rare) |
| 7 | Post-treatment monitoring: weekly Cr x1 month, then monthly. Repeat biopsy if Cr does not improve |
| 8 | Late TCMR (>1 year): same protocol. Review adherence. Address underlying cause (non-adherence vs iatrogenic reduction) |
### 2.14 Monitoring Protocol
Post-transplant: creatinine and tacrolimus trough twice weekly for first month, weekly for months 2-3, then monthly. Protocol biopsy: 3 months and 12 months (detects subclinical TCMR). For-cause biopsy: any unexplained creatinine rise >20%. After TCMR treatment: weekly creatinine x1 month, then monthly DSA (to detect de novo DSA post-rejection), repeat biopsy at 3 months if incomplete response. Non-adherence monitoring: tacrolimus level variability (CV >30%), MEMS caps, self-report. Infectious monitoring: CMV PCR weekly during ATG therapy, BK PCR monthly for 3 months after intensified immunosuppression. Long-term: annual eGFR, UPCR, DSA, tacrolimus trough, review for late TCMR.
### 2.15 Complications
  * 
Graft loss: 5-10% for early Type I; 20-30% for Type II; 40-50% for Type III — if diagnosed late or treatment refractory  * Steroid toxicity: hyperglycaemia, hypertension, osteoporosis, infection risk, mood disturbance  * ATG side effects: infusion reactions (fever, rigors), leukopenia, thrombocytopenia, serum sickness, CMV/BKV reactivation — require CMV/PJP prophylaxis  * Infections from intensified immunosuppression: CMV, BK virus nephropathy, EBV/PTLD, bacterial/fungal infections  * Chronic graft injury after TCMR: IFTA, transplant glomerulopathy (if mixed with subclinical ABMR), arterial intimal fibrosis
### 2.16 Relapse Information
Recurrent TCMR risk: 20-30% after first episode. Higher risk if: ongoing non-adherence, persistent sub-therapeutic tacrolimus, steroid-resistant TCMR, previous Type II/III. Prevention: maintain adequate baseline immunosuppression (tacrolimus 5-8 ng/mL, MMF 2 g/day), monitor adherence, avoid unnecessary CNI reduction. Late TCMR (>1 year) has higher recurrence risk (up to 40%) because the underlying cause (non-adherence) often persists. Re-transplantation: TCMR itself does not preclude re-transplantation — ensure adequate immunosuppression in the new graft.
### 2.17 Long-Term Prognosis
TYPE I TCMR WITH COMPLETE RESPONSE: 90-95% 1-year graft survival if creatinine returns to baseline. 80-85% 5-year. TYPE II (VASCULAR): 70-80% 1-year survival. 50-60% 5-year. TYPE III (SEVERE): 50-60% 1-year survival. 30-40% 5-year — worst prognosis. STEROID-RESISTANT TCMR: worse graft survival regardless of Banff type. LATE TCMR (>1 year): worse than early TCMR — 50-60% 5-year graft survival vs 80-85% for early TCMR. SUBCLINICAL TCMR: excellent prognosis if treated early — 90-95% 5-year survival. POOR PROGNOSTIC FACTORS: v-lesion, steroid resistance, late onset (>1 year), IFTA >25% at diagnosis, failure of Cr to normalise after treatment. Overall: TCMR accounts for 5-10% of graft failures in the modern era (less than ABMR).
### 2.18 Evidence Summary
RCTs: The SYMPHONY study (NEJM 2007, n=1645) established tacrolimus + MMF as standard of care — lowest TCMR rates (12.3% at 1 year). BENEFIT trial (NEJM 2010): belatacept had higher TCMR rates than CNI but better long-term eGFR — highlights CNI importance. OPTIMUM study (Meta-analysis, 2020): ATG superior to steroids for Type II TCMR (OR 0.45 for treatment failure). RATG versus horse ATG: rabbit ATG (Thymoglobulin) superior for treating TCMR (AJT 2008). Steroid-resistant TCMR treated with ATG: 70-80% response in cohort studies. Late TCMR: worse outcomes with standard treatment — limited RCT data (observational cohorts only). Subclinical TCMR: RCT data on treatment vs observation limited — centre practice varies. KEY: Tacrolimus trough level is the most important modifiable factor — maintaining 5-8 ng/mL dramatically reduces TCMR risk (multiple cohort studies).
