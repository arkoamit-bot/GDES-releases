## 2. CASE-FSGS-TYP-001: Primary FSGS NOS in Young Adult

**Classification**: Typical Presentation  
**Variant**: NOS (Not Otherwise Specified)  
**Source**: Based on FSGS CT Consortium study populations  

### Presentation

A 28-year-old African American male presents with 4 weeks of progressive lower extremity oedema. He reports gaining 8 kg over the past month, with puffiness around his eyes upon waking. He denies haematuria, dysuria, or recent illness. No family history of kidney disease.

### Investigations

| Test | Result | Reference Range |
|------|--------|-----------------|
| UPCR | 6.2 g/g | <0.3 g/g |
| Serum albumin | 2.1 g/dL | 3.5–5.0 g/dL |
| Serum creatinine | 1.1 mg/dL | 0.7–1.3 mg/dL |
| eGFR | 88 mL/min/1.73m² | >90 mL/min/1.73m² |
| Total cholesterol | 320 mg/dL | <200 mg/dL |
| LDL | 210 mg/dL | <100 mg/dL |
| Urine sediment | 5–10 RBCs/hpf | <3 RBCs/hpf |
| ANA | Negative | Negative |
| C3/C4 | Normal | Normal |
| HIV | Negative | Negative |
| APOL1 genotype | G1/G0 | — |

### Renal Biopsy

- **LM**: 18 glomeruli; 3 show segmental sclerosis with matrix expansion; 1 adhesion; perihilar hyalinosis; no crescents; mild tubular atrophy (10%); no arteriolar hyalinosis
- **IF**: IgM and C3 trapping in sclerotic segments; no full house pattern
- **EM**: 85% foot process effacement; no electron-dense deposits; microvillous transformation
- **Columbia Classification**: NOS variant

### Diagnosis

Primary (idiopathic) FSGS, NOS variant, in a young African American male. APOL1 G1 heterozygous.

### Management

1. **Induction**: Prednisone 1 mg/kg/day (80 mg/day)
2. **Monitoring**: Weekly blood pressure, fasting glucose; monthly eGFR, proteinuria
3. **Response at 16 weeks**: Complete remission (UPCR 0.2 g/g, albumin 4.0 g/dL)
4. **Taper**: Prednisone tapered over 20 weeks to discontinuation
5. **Supportive**: Ramipril 5 mg/day, atorvastatin 20 mg/day
6. **Follow-up**: Stable remission at 24 months; eGFR 85 mL/min/1.73m²

### Teaching Points

| Point | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Classic presentation | Young adult, nephrotic syndrome, no secondary causes |
| APOL1 significance | G1 heterozygous: intermediate risk; monitoring for progression |
| Steroid response | Typical SR-FSGS; responds well to standard steroid course |
| Long-term outlook | Excellent if steroid-sensitive; monitor for relapse |

## 3. CASE-FSGS-COL-001: Collapsing FSGS in APOL1-High-Risk

**Classification**: Rapid Progression  
**Variant**: Collapsing  
**Source**: Based on Kopp et al. (APOL1 and kidney disease) and published case series  

### Presentation

A 32-year-old African American female with known HIV (CD4 280 cells/uL, on antiretroviral therapy) presents with rapid onset of generalised oedema over 2 weeks, foamy urine, and 10 kg weight gain. She reports oliguria for 1 week. Serum creatinine has risen from 0.9 to 3.4 mg/dL over 10 days.

### Investigations

| Test | Result | Reference Range |
|------|--------|-----------------|
| UPCR | 12.8 g/g | <0.3 g/g |
| Serum albumin | 1.5 g/dL | 3.5–5.0 g/dL |
| Serum creatinine | 3.4 mg/dL | 0.7–1.3 mg/dL |
| eGFR | 20 mL/min/1.73m² | >90 mL/min/1.73m² |
| Total cholesterol | 380 mg/dL | <200 mg/dL |
| HIV viral load | 450 copies/mL | Undetectable |
| CD4 count | 280 cells/uL | >500 cells/uL |
| APOL1 genotype | G1/G2 | — |
| ANA/C3/C4 | Negative/Normal/Normal | — |

### Renal Biopsy

- **LM**: 12 glomeruli; 9 show global or segmental collapse with pronounced podocyte hypertrophy and hyperplasia; tubular microcysts; severe interstitial inflammation; arteriolar hyalinosis
- **IF**: Non-specific IgM/C3 trapping
- **EM**: Near-total foot process effacement; podocyte dedifferentiation; no immune deposits
- **Columbia Classification**: Collapsing variant

### Diagnosis

Collapsing FSGS (HIV-associated nephropathy component) in an APOL1 G1/G2 high-risk patient.

### Management

1. **Optimise antiretroviral therapy**: Ensure viral suppression (switch if needed)
2. **Immunosuppression**: Tacrolimus 0.05 mg/kg/day (target trough 5–8 ng/mL) + low-dose prednisone 0.5 mg/kg/day
3. **Supportive care**: Ramipril 2.5 mg/day (cautious with eGFR 20), empagliflozin 10 mg/day
4. **Monitoring**: Weekly creatinine, trough levels; bi-weekly proteinuria
5. **Outcome**: Partial response at 3 months (UPCR 5.2 g/g, eGFR 25); continued decline despite therapy
6. **Plan**: Evaluation for renal transplantation; ongoing dialysis preparation

### Teaching Points

| Point | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Collapsing variant | Most aggressive; tuft collapse with podocyte hyperplasia |
| APOL1 association | G1/G2 high-risk genotype; 10-fold increased risk |
| HIVAN component | HIV-driven; viral suppression essential but often insufficient |
| Poor prognosis | 10-year renal survival ~30%; often progresses to ESKD |
| CNI role | May provide partial benefit; combination therapy considered |
| Transplant planning | High recurrence risk post-transplant (50%); APOL1 risk assessment important |

## 4. CASE-FSGS-RES-001: Steroid-Resistant FSGS

**Classification**: Resistant Disease  
**Variant**: NOS  
**Source**: Based on FSGS CT Consortium resistant populations  

### Presentation

A 45-year-old Caucasian male presents with nephrotic syndrome (UPCR 8.4 g/g, albumin 1.8 g/dL). He was started on prednisone 1 mg/kg/day by his primary nephrologist 14 weeks ago. Despite 14 weeks of therapy, his proteinuria has not improved (UPCR 7.9 g/g at week 12) and he has developed significant steroid side effects: moon facies, new-onset diabetes (fasting glucose 180 mg/dL), and insomnia.

### Investigations

| Test | Result | Reference Range |
|------|--------|-----------------|
| UPCR | 7.9 g/g | <0.3 g/g |
| Serum albumin | 1.9 g/dL | 3.5–5.0 g/dL |
| Serum creatinine | 1.6 mg/dL | 0.7–1.3 mg/dL |
| eGFR | 52 mL/min/1.73m² | >90 mL/min/1.73m² |
| Fasting glucose | 180 mg/dL | <100 mg/dL |
| HbA1c | 8.2% | <5.7% |
| APOL1 genotype | G0/G0 | — |
| Prior biopsy | NOS variant (14 glomeruli) | — |

### Diagnosis

Steroid-resistant primary FSGS, NOS variant, with steroid-induced diabetes.

### Management

1. **Discontinue prednisone** (taper over 2 weeks due to steroid-induced diabetes)
2. **Initiate cyclosporine** 3 mg/kg/day in 2 divided doses (target trough 150 ng/mL)
3. **Add low-dose prednisone** 0.15 mg/kg/day (20 mg/day) with CNI
4. **Diabetes management**: Insulin therapy; endocrinology referral
5. **Monitor**: Weekly cyclosporine trough; weekly creatinine; fortnightly proteinuria
6. **Response at 6 months**: Partial remission (UPCR 2.8 g/g, eGFR 58)
7. **Continue**: Cyclosporine for 18 months; slow taper

### Teaching Points

| Point | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Steroid resistance definition | No CR/PR after 16 weeks of adequate steroid therapy |
| Steroid toxicity | Significant morbidity; early recognition and discontinuation needed |
| CNI as second-line | 60–70% response rate in steroid-resistant FSGS |
| Monitoring importance | CNI trough and creatinine monitoring essential |
| Partial remission | Acceptable outcome; associated with improved long-term renal survival |
| Diabetes management | Steroid-induced diabetes may persist; requires ongoing treatment |

## 5. CASE-FSGS-REL-001: Relapsing FSGS After Steroid Withdrawal

**Classification**: Relapse  
**Variant**: NOS  
**Source**: Based on relapse series from FSGS CT Consortium  

### Presentation

A 35-year-old Hispanic male with known FSGS (diagnosed 3 years ago) presents with recurrence of lower extremity oedema, weight gain of 5 kg over 2 weeks, and foamy urine. He initially responded to prednisone (CR at 16 weeks) and completed a 24-week taper off steroids 2 months ago. This is his second relapse; the first occurred during the initial taper and responded to dose increase.

### Investigations

| Test | Result | Reference Range |
|------|--------|-----------------|
| UPCR | 5.8 g/g | <0.3 g/g |
| Serum albumin | 2.4 g/dL | 3.5–5.0 g/dL |
| Serum creatinine | 1.0 mg/dL | 0.7–1.3 mg/dL |
| eGFR | 92 mL/min/1.73m² | >90 mL/min/1.73m² |
| Total cholesterol | 290 mg/dL | <200 mg/dL |

### Diagnosis

Relapsing primary FSGS (steroid-dependent phenotype — 2 relapses within 6 months of steroid withdrawal).

### Management

1. **Restart prednisone** at 1 mg/kg/day (80 mg/day) — second response expected
2. **Add mycophenolate mofetil** 1 g twice daily as steroid-sparing agent
3. **Expect response**: CR at 8 weeks on combination therapy
4. **Steroid taper**: Slower taper over 36 weeks with MMF maintenance
5. **MMF continuation**: 24 months minimum; taper if sustained remission
6. **Relapse prevention**: Close monitoring q1 month; early intervention if sub-nephrotic proteinuria returns

### Teaching Points

| Point | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Relapse definition | ≥50% increase in proteinuria from remission baseline; return to nephrotic range |
| Steroid-dependent phenotype | ≥2 relapses during taper or within 6 months of discontinuation |
| Steroid-sparing agents | MMF or CNI needed to prevent further relapses |
| Taper strategy | Slower taper with steroid-sparing agent reduces relapse risk |
| Long-term outlook | Relapsing FSGS has intermediate prognosis; steroid-sensitivity preserved |

## 6. CASE-FSGS-GEN-001: Genetic FSGS NPHS2 Mutation

**Classification**: Special Case  
**Variant**: NOS histologically  
**Source**: Based on published NPHS2 familial FSGS series  

### Presentation

A 14-year-old Caucasian male of consanguineous parents presents with nephrotic syndrome (UPCR 10.2 g/g, albumin 1.6 g/dL, oedema). Two siblings have ESKD from biopsy-proven FSGS. He was treated with prednisone 1 mg/kg/day for 16 weeks with no response (steroid-resistant from outset). Renal biopsy shows NOS variant.

### Investigations

| Test | Result | Reference Range |
|------|--------|-----------------|
| UPCR | 10.2 g/g | <0.3 g/g |
| Serum albumin | 1.6 g/dL | 3.5–5.0 g/dL |
| Serum creatinine | 0.9 mg/dL | 0.4–0.8 mg/dL (age-adjusted) |
| eGFR | 105 mL/min/1.73m² | >90 mL/min/1.73m² |
| Genetic testing | NPHS2 homozygous (R257X) | — |
| Family history | 2 siblings with ESKD from FSGS | — |

### Diagnosis

Genetic FSGS due to NPHS2 homozygous mutation (R257X), autosomal recessive inheritance.

### Management

1. **Discontinue all immunosuppression** (ineffective in genetic FSGS)
2. **Initiate ramipril** 2.5 mg/day (titrate to 10 mg/day) — renoprotective
3. **Empagliflozin** 10 mg/day — renoprotective, antiproteinuric
4. **Blood pressure control** — target <130/80 mmHg
5. **Proteinuria monitoring** — q3 months
6. **Genetic counselling** — family planning implications
7. **Transplant preparation** — early evaluation given family history of ESKD
8. **Avoid nephrotoxins** — no NSAIDs, no contrast unless essential

### Teaching Points

| Point | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Genetic FSGS | Immunosuppression is INEFFECTIVE; do not expose to steroid toxicity |
| NPHS2 mutations | Most common genetic cause of FSGS; autosomal recessive |
| Consanguinity | Increases risk of homozygous mutations |
| Family history | Siblings affected; autosomal recessive inheritance |
| Supportive care | RAAS blockade, SGLT2 inhibitors, BP control — mainstay of management |
| Early transplant | Genetic FSGS does not recur post-transplant; excellent graft survival |

## 7. CASE-FSGS-OBS-001: Obesity-Related FSGS

**Classification**: Advanced / Secondary  
**Variant**: Perihilar  
**Source**: Based on secondary FSGS literature  

### Presentation

A 58-year-old African American female with BMI 42 kg/m², type 2 diabetes (HbA1c 8.1%), hypertension (160/95 mmHg on 3 antihypertensives), and CKD stage 3a (eGFR 48) presents for nephrology evaluation. Proteinuria was discovered incidentally on routine diabetes monitoring. She denies oedema or haematuria. No family history of kidney disease.

### Investigations

| Test | Result | Reference Range |
|------|--------|-----------------|
| UPCR | 2.8 g/g | <0.3 g/g |
| Serum albumin | 3.2 g/dL | 3.5–5.0 g/dL |
| Serum creatinine | 1.4 mg/dL | 0.7–1.3 mg/dL |
| eGFR | 48 mL/min/1.73m² | >90 mL/min/1.73m² |
| HbA1c | 8.1% | <5.7% |
| Total cholesterol | 260 mg/dL | <200 mg/dL |
| LDL | 160 mg/dL | <100 mg/dL |
| APOL1 genotype | G0/G0 | — |
| HIV/HBV/HCV | Negative | Negative |
| ANA/C3/C4 | Negative/Normal/Normal | — |

### Renal Biopsy

- **LM**: 22 glomeruli; 4 show perihilar sclerosis with hyalinosis at the vascular pole; 2 globally sclerosed; moderate arteriolar hyalinosis; moderate tubular atrophy (30%); interstitial fibrosis (25%)
- **IF**: Non-specific IgM/C3 trapping
- **EM**: 70% foot process effacement; no immune deposits; GBM thickening consistent with diabetic changes
- **Columbia Classification**: Perihilar variant

### Diagnosis

Secondary (adaptive) FSGS, perihilar variant, in the context of obesity, type 2 diabetes, and hypertension. Glomerulomegaly from hyperfiltration is the primary driver.

### Management

1. **NO immunosuppression** (secondary/adaptive FSGS does not respond to immunosuppression)
2. **Weight management**: Refer to obesity medicine; consider GLP-1 receptor agonist (semaglutide) for weight loss and renal protection
3. **Optimise glycaemic control**: Target HbA1c <7%; consider SGLT2 inhibitor (empagliflozin) — dual benefit for diabetes and CKD
4. **Aggressive blood pressure control**: Target <130/80 mmHg; maximise ACEi/ARB; add MRA if needed
5. **RAAS blockade**: Ramipril 10 mg/day (titrate to maximum tolerated dose)
6. **Lipid management**: Atorvastatin 40 mg/day
7. **Nephroprotective strategy**: Combined ACEi + SGLT2i + BP control
8. **Lifestyle**: Dietary sodium restriction (<2 g/day), regular exercise, weight loss goal 10% body weight
9. **Monitoring**: q3 months (UPCR, eGFR, electrolytes)
10. **Surgical consideration**: Bariatric surgery evaluation if BMI remains >40 despite medical management

### Teaching Points

| Point | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Perihilar variant | Classic for adaptive/secondary FSGS; glomerulomegaly-driven |
| Obesity-related | Podocyte stress from hyperfiltration; podocyte-to-GBM mismatch |
| No immunosuppression | Immunosuppression is not indicated for secondary FSGS |
| Treat underlying cause | Weight loss is the most important intervention |
| Multifactorial approach | BP, glucose, lipids, weight — all must be optimised |
| SGLT2 inhibitors | Dual benefit: glycaemic control + renoprotection + weight loss |
| Prognosis | Depends on success of risk factor modification; may stabilize or improve with weight loss |

## 8. CASE-FSGS-TIP-001: Tip-Variant FSGS with Steroid Response

**Classification**: Typical Presentation  
**Variant**: Tip  
**Source**: Based on tip variant literature (Kodner, D'Agati)  

### Presentation

A 24-year-old Caucasian female presents with nephrotic syndrome (UPCR 5.6 g/g, albumin 2.0 g/dL, generalised oedema) of 3 weeks duration. No significant past medical history. No family history of kidney disease. She reports foamy urine and periorbital puffiness.

### Investigations

| Test | Result | Reference Range |
|------|--------|-----------------|
| UPCR | 5.6 g/g | <0.3 g/g |
| Serum albumin | 2.0 g/dL | 3.5–5.0 g/dL |
| Serum creatinine | 0.8 mg/dL | 0.6–1.1 mg/dL |
| eGFR | 108 mL/min/1.73m² | >90 mL/min/1.73m² |
| Total cholesterol | 300 mg/dL | <200 mg/dL |
| ANA/C3/C4 | Negative/Normal/Normal | — |
| HIV | Negative | Negative |

### Renal Biopsy

- **LM**: 20 glomeruli; 2 show segmental sclerosis at the tubular pole (tip lesion); no hyalinosis; no crescents; minimal tubular atrophy (5%)
- **IF**: Faint IgM/C3 in sclerotic segments
- **EM**: 80% foot process effacement; no electron-dense deposits; preserved GBM structure
- **Columbia Classification**: Tip variant

### Diagnosis

Primary FSGS, tip variant, in a young female. Favourable histological variant.

### Management

1. **Prednisone** 1 mg/kg/day (60 mg/day)
2. **Expected response**: CR at 12 weeks (tip variant has highest steroid response rate)
3. **Faster taper**: Begin taper at week 12 if CR; taper over 16 weeks
4. **Supportive care**: Ramipril 5 mg/day, atorvastatin 20 mg/day
5. **Response at 12 weeks**: CR (UPCR 0.15 g/g, albumin 4.2 g/dL)
6. **Steroid taper completed**: Off steroids by week 28
7. **Follow-up**: Stable CR at 18 months; eGFR 105 mL/min/1.73m²

### Teaching Points

| Point | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Tip variant | Best prognosis among Columbia variants; ~90% 10-year renal survival |
| Steroid sensitivity | Highest response rate; often CR within 12 weeks |
| Shorter steroid course | May tolerate faster taper due to favourable variant |
| Young patient | Good baseline renal function; excellent long-term outlook |
| Histological detail | Tubular pole localisation is pathognomonic |
| Monitoring | Even with favourable variant, long-term monitoring for relapse is essential |

## 9. CASE-FSGS-TXR-001: Recurrent FSGS Post-Transplant

**Classification**: Complications  
**Variant**: Collapsing (recurrent)  
**Source**: Based on post-transplant recurrence literature  

### Presentation

A 40-year-old African American male, 6 months post-renal transplantation (living donor, preemptive), presents with progressive proteinuria (UPCR 3.2 g/g) and rising serum creatinine (1.4 from baseline 1.0 mg/dL) over 4 weeks. His native kidney disease was collapsing FSGS with APOL1 G1/G2 genotype. He received standard transplant induction (basiliximab) and maintenance immunosuppression (tacrolimus, mycophenolate, prednisone).

### Investigations

| Test | Result | Reference Range |
|------|--------|-----------------|
| UPCR | 3.2 g/g | <0.3 g/g |
| Serum creatinine | 1.4 mg/dL | 0.8–1.2 mg/dL |
| eGFR | 62 mL/min/1.73m² | >60 mL/min/1.73m² |
| Tacrolimus trough | 8 ng/mL | 5–10 ng/mL |
| BK virus PCR | Negative | Negative |
| Donor-specific antibodies | Negative | Negative |
| APOL1 genotype (recipient) | G1/G2 | — |

### Transplant Biopsy

- **LM**: 15 glomeruli; 6 show segmental collapse with podocyte hyperplasia; tubular microcysts; mild interstitial inflammation; no rejection features
- **IF**: Non-specific IgM/C3; no rejection pattern
- **EM**: Near-total foot process effacement; podocyte dedifferentiation; no immune deposits
- **Diagnosis**: Recurrent collapsing FSGS in allograft

### Management

1. **Plasmapheresis**: 2–3 sessions per week for 4 weeks (target remove circulating permeability factors)
2. **Optimise immunosuppression**: Increase tacrolimus target trough to 8–12 ng/mL
3. **Add rituximab** 375 mg/m² x 4 weekly doses (investigational for recurrence)
4. **Monitor**: Weekly proteinuria, creatinine; q2-week eGFR
5. **Outcome**: Partial response at 3 months (UPCR 1.8 g/g, eGFR 55); incomplete recovery
6. **Long-term**: Continue CNI maintenance; monitor for graft loss; discuss re-transplantation planning

### Teaching Points

| Point | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Post-transplant recurrence | 20–40% overall; up to 50% in collapsing variant |
| Early presentation | Often within first 6 months post-transplant |
| Collapsing recurrence | Highest risk of graft loss (~50% of recurrent cases) |
| APOL1 role | High-risk genotype may contribute to recurrence |
| Plasmapheresis | First-line for recurrence; removes circulating factors |
| Rituximab | Investigational; may provide additional benefit |
| Graft loss risk | High; re-transplantation planning essential |
| Prognosis | Guarded; many patients return to dialysis |
