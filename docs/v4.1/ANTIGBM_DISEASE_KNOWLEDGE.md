# Anti-GBM Disease (Goodpasture Syndrome) Disease Knowledge Specification

**Document ID**: ANTIGBM_DK  
**Version**: 1.0  
**Date**: 2026-07-10  
**Domain**: Anti-Glomerular Basement Membrane Antibody Disease  
**Classification**: Type II Hypersensitivity Autoimmune Glomerulonephritis

---

## 1. Overview

Anti-glomerular basement membrane (anti-GBM) disease, eponymously known as Goodpasture syndrome when presenting with pulmonary-renal involvement, is a rare but life-threatening autoimmune disorder characterised by the production of autoantibodies directed against the non-collagenous-1 domain of the alpha-3 chain of type IV collagen (alpha-3(IV)NC1). These pathogenic antibodies bind to the glomerular and alveolar basement membranes, triggering a rapidly progressive crescentic glomerulonephritis (RPGN) with or without diffuse alveolar haemorrhage (DAH). Untreated, the disease follows a fulminant course leading to irreversible kidney injury and death from respiratory or renal failure within weeks to months. The discovery of anti-GBM antibodies and the pathognomonic linear IgG staining on immunofluorescence transformed the understanding of this disease and established it as the prototypical antibody-mediated autoimmune kidney disease.

## 2. 21-Field Disease Knowledge Schema

### 2.1 Definition

Anti-GBM disease is a type II hypersensitivity autoimmune disorder defined by the presence of circulating autoantibodies targeting the alpha-3(IV)NC1 domain of type IV collagen, a major constituent of the glomerular and alveolar basement membranes. The disease manifests as rapidly progressive crescentic glomerulonephritis (RPGN) in virtually all cases, with concomitant pulmonary haemorrhage occurring in approximately 50-60% of patients, constituting the classic Goodpasture syndrome. Renal-limited disease (anti-GBM GN without pulmonary involvement) occurs predominantly in older adults, particularly females. The histopathological hallmark is a crescentic necrotising glomerulonephritis with linear deposition of IgG (and often C3) along the glomerular basement membrane on immunofluorescence microscopy. This linear staining pattern is pathognomonic and distinguishes anti-GBM disease from immune-complex mediated glomerulonephritides and pauci-immune ANCA-associated vasculitis. The disease is a medical emergency requiring immediate initiation of immunosuppressive therapy and plasma exchange to remove circulating pathogenic antibodies.

The bimodal age distribution is a defining epidemiological feature. The younger peak (20-30 years) predominantly affects males and is strongly associated with pulmonary haemorrhage and smoking history. The older peak (60-70 years) predominantly affects females with renal-limited disease. This bimodality reflects age-dependent differences in alveolar basement membrane susceptibility to antibody-mediated injury. HLA-DRB1*1501 is the strongest genetic risk factor identified, with an odds ratio greater than 8, though the population-attributable risk is low given the rarity of the disease.

### 2.3 Aetiology

The aetiology of anti-GBM disease involves a complex interaction between genetic predisposition, environmental triggers, and loss of immune tolerance to the alpha-3(IV)NC1 autoantigen. The strongest genetic association is with HLA-DRB1*1501 and HLA-DRB1*1502 alleles within the major histocompatibility complex class II region. These alleles present alpha-3(IV)NC1 peptides to T helper cells, initiating the autoimmune cascade. Genome-wide association studies have also identified non-MHC susceptibility loci including FCGR2B (encoding Fc-gamma receptor IIB, an inhibitory receptor) and CTLA4, consistent with impaired regulatory control of autoantibody production.

Environmental triggers are critical for disease initiation in genetically susceptible individuals. Cigarette smoking is the most strongly established trigger, with an odds ratio of 3-4 for pulmonary haemorrhage, likely through alveolar basement membrane damage exposing cryptic epitopes. Hydrocarbon solvent exposure (paint thinners, petroleum distillates, dry cleaning chemicals) has been associated with disease onset through inhalational alveolar injury. Other reported triggers include upper respiratory tract infections (influenza, parvovirus B19), cocaine inhalation, and lithotripsy-induced renal trauma. Case reports link the disease to interferon-alpha and alemtuzumab therapy. The latency from trigger exposure to clinical onset is typically weeks to months.

Molecular mimicry has been proposed but not definitively proven. The disease may arise when environmental damage to the basement membrane exposes the normally sequestered alpha-3(IV)NC1 epitope, allowing HLA-DRB1*1501 to present the cryptic antigen to autoreactive T cells in individuals with impaired regulatory immune mechanisms.

### 2.4 Pathophysiology

The pathophysiology of anti-GBM disease follows a linear sequence from initial autoantibody generation to antibody-mediated tissue destruction and crescent formation. Seven key steps define the pathogenetic cascade.

Step 1 — Loss of Immune Tolerance: In genetically susceptible individuals (HLA-DRB1*1501), exposure of the normally sequestered alpha-3(IV)NC1 epitope following environmental trigger (smoking, hydrocarbon, infection) leads to presentation by antigen-presenting cells to CD4+ T helper cells. Autoreactive T cells escape thymic deletion and peripheral regulatory control.

Step 2 — T-Cell Help and B-Cell Activation: Activated CD4+ T cells provide help to B cells recognising the same alpha-3(IV)NC1 antigen. The HLA class II restriction explains the strong genetic association. T-cell involvement is inferred from the IgG subclass distribution (predominantly IgG1 and IgG4) indicating T-dependent B-cell responses and class switching.

Step 3 — Autoantibody Production: B cells differentiate into plasma cells producing high-affinity IgG antibodies targeting the alpha-3(IV)NC1 domain. The dominant epitope is localised to the EA and EB regions of the alpha-3(IV)NC1 monomer. Antibody production is self-sustaining through ongoing antigen exposure and B-cell memory.

Step 4 — Antibody Binding to Basement Membrane: Circulating anti-GBM antibodies bind to their target antigen exposed on the glomerular basement membrane (and alveolar basement membrane in patients with pulmonary involvement). This binding produces the pathognomonic linear IgG deposition along the GBM on immunofluorescence. The alpha-3(IV)NC1 epitope is normally cryptic within the triple-helical collagen IV network but becomes exposed through conformational changes or proteolytic damage.

Step 5 — Complement Activation: Antibody binding activates the classical complement pathway. C1q binding is followed by C3 deposition, generating C3a and C5a anaphylatoxins and the membrane attack complex C5b-9. Linear C3 staining accompanying IgG is common. Complement activation amplifies the inflammatory response through neutrophil and macrophage recruitment.

Step 6 — Neutrophil and Macrophage Recruitment: Fc-gamma receptor-mediated engagement of effector cells, particularly neutrophils and macrophages, leads to their recruitment into the glomerulus. These cells release reactive oxygen species, proteolytic enzymes (including matrix metalloproteinases), and pro-inflammatory cytokines, causing direct glomerular basement membrane damage and endothelial injury.

Step 7 — Crescent Formation: The resulting severe necrotising injury to the glomerular capillary wall causes disruption of the GBM, allowing fibrin and plasma proteins to leak into Bowman space. Fibrin acts as a scaffold for extracapillary cellular proliferation. Parietal epithelial cells proliferate and macrophages infiltrate, forming crescents. Unlike ANCA vasculitis where crescents are pauci-immune, anti-GBM crescents are associated with prominent linear IgG staining. The proportion of cellular versus fibrous crescents at diagnosis determines renal recovery potential. Without treatment, virtually all glomeruli become involved within days to weeks.

### 2.5 Clinical Presentation

| Presentation | Frequency | Description |
|-------------|-----------|-------------|
| Rapidly progressive GN (RPGN) | >95% | Oliguric or anuric AKI over days-weeks; active urinary sediment (dysmorphic RBCs, RBC casts); hypertension; oedema |
| Pulmonary haemorrhage (Goodpasture) | 50-60% | Haemoptysis (may be absent despite significant alveolar bleeding); dyspnoea; hypoxaemia; bilateral alveolar infiltrates on imaging |
| Renal-limited disease | 40-50% | RPGN without pulmonary involvement; more common in elderly females |
| Constitutional symptoms | 70-80% | Fatigue, malaise, anorexia, weight loss, fever |
| Oliguria or anuria | 40-60% | Severe renal impairment at presentation |
| Gross haematuria | 20-30% | Visible blood in urine |
| Arthralgias | 10-20% | Non-specific joint pains, no frank arthritis |
| Pulmonary-renal syndrome | 50-60% | Concurrent DAH and RPGN; medical emergency |
| Normocytic anaemia | 80-90% | Anaemia of chronic disease + iron deficiency from pulmonary haemorrhage |

RPGN presents with oliguric or anuric acute kidney injury, hypertension, oedema, and active urinary sediment. Serum creatinine rises rapidly over days to weeks. Pulmonary haemorrhage may present with frank haemoptysis or be clinically silent, detected only by a rising DLCO (diffusing capacity for carbon monoxide) on pulmonary function testing or by bronchoalveolar lavage showing progressively bloodier aliquots. The combination of RPGN and DAH (Goodpasture syndrome) constitutes a medical emergency requiring simultaneous respiratory support, haemodynamic stabilisation, and immediate initiation of immunosuppression with plasma exchange.

### 2.12 Treatment Overview

Anti-GBM disease is a medical emergency requiring immediate initiation of triple therapy: plasma exchange (PLEX) to remove circulating pathogenic antibodies, cyclophosphamide (CyP) and corticosteroids to suppress ongoing antibody production, and high-dose glucocorticoids for rapid anti-inflammatory effect. Treatment should be initiated on the same day of diagnosis without awaiting complete biopsy results if serology is strongly positive.

PLEX is the cornerstone of acute management and should be initiated as soon as possible, typically with 60 mL/kg exchanges using 5% albumin replacement (with fresh frozen plasma cover if post-procedure bleeding). A typical course involves daily PLEX for 14 days or until anti-GBM antibodies become undetectable. PLEX removes both IgG antibodies and inflammatory mediators; its prompt initiation is the single most important determinant of renal recovery.

Cyclophosphamide 2-3 mg/kg/day orally (or IV pulse 15 mg/kg q3-4 weeks) is started concurrently with PLEX. The duration of CyP therapy is typically 2-3 months, after which it is switched to azathioprine for maintenance. In patients who cannot tolerate CyP (infection risk, cytopenia, older age), rituximab has been used as an alternative, though the evidence base is derived from case series rather than randomised trials.

Corticosteroids are given as methylprednisolone 500-1000 mg IV daily for 3 consecutive days, followed by oral prednisone 1 mg/kg/day (max 60-80 mg) with gradual taper over 6-12 months. Steroids dampen the inflammatory response and reduce neutrophil recruitment to sites of antibody binding.

Maintenance immunosuppression with azathioprine 1-2 mg/kg/day and low-dose prednisone is continued for 6-12 months following CyP discontinuation. Unlike ANCA vasculitis, anti-GBM disease has a very low relapse rate (<3%), allowing for earlier withdrawal of immunosuppression. Patients with double-positive disease (anti-GBM + ANCA) require longer and more intensive maintenance therapy due to the ANCA relapse risk.

### 2.13 Treatment Algorithms

| Step | Regimen | Indication |
|------|---------|------------|
| 1 | Methylprednisolone 500-1000 mg IV x 3 days | All patients at diagnosis |
| 2 | PLEX 60 mL/kg daily x 14 days (or until anti-GBM negative) | All patients with RPGN, DAH, or dialysis-dependence |
| 3 | Cyclophosphamide 2-3 mg/kg/day oral (or IV 15 mg/kg q3-4wk) x 2-3 months | All patients, unless contraindicicated |
| 3a | Rituximab 375 mg/m2 IV weekly x 4 (alternative to CyP) | CyP intolerant, infection risk, elderly |
| 4 | Prednisone 1 mg/kg/day (max 80 mg) with taper over 6-12 months | All patients |
| 5 | Azathioprine 1-2 mg/kg/day + low-dose prednisone | Maintenance after CyP completion |
| 6 | TMP-SMX 800/160 mg daily or 3x/week | PJP prophylaxis during induction |
| 7 | Consider stopping immunosuppression at 12-18 months | Anti-GBM negative, sustained remission (<3% relapse) |

### 2.14 Monitoring Protocol

Monitoring during the acute phase is intensive. During PLEX (first 2 weeks), daily clinical assessment for haemoptysis, oxygenation, urine output, and fluid balance is required. Anti-GBM antibody titres, serum creatinine, and haemoglobin should be measured every 1-2 days. After PLEX completion, weekly monitoring continues for the first 2 months, then monthly through month 6, then every 2-3 months through month 12, and every 6 months thereafter. At each visit, clinical assessment, serum creatinine/eGFR, urinalysis with RBC cast quantification, UPCR, and anti-GBM antibody titres are performed. Drug-specific monitoring includes weekly CBC for cyclophosphamide (target WBC 3000-5000/uL), LFTs, and urine cytology (haemorrhagic cystitis surveillance). Long-term monitoring includes blood pressure, fasting glucose, bone density, and ophthalmologic exam for steroid toxicity. After immunosuppression withdrawal, anti-GBM antibodies should be checked every 6-12 months for early detection of relapse (though relapse is rare).

### 2.16 Relapse Information

Anti-GBM disease is exceptional among autoimmune glomerulonephritides for its very low relapse rate, estimated at less than 3% across all published cohorts. This contrasts sharply with ANCA-associated vasculitis (30-50% relapse at 5 years) and lupus nephritis. The low relapse rate reflects the self-limited nature of the autoantibody response in most patients; once the trigger is removed and the immune response is suppressed, the autoreactive B-cell clone appears to be eliminated or durably silenced.

Relapse typically occurs within the first 2 years, often in the context of persistent smoking, ongoing hydrocarbon exposure, or intercurrent infection. Relapse after 5 years is exceptional. When relapse does occur, it is managed by reinstituting the same triple therapy (PLEX + CyP + steroids). Anti-GBM antibody titres should be monitored during and after initial therapy; persistent or rising titres after immunosuppression withdrawal warrant consideration of continued maintenance therapy, particularly in double-positive patients.

The risk factors for relapse are poorly defined given the rarity of the event. Double-positive patients (anti-GBM + ANCA) have a higher relapse rate driven by the ANCA component and require ongoing immunosuppression akin to ANCA vasculitis. HLA-DRB1*1501 homozygosity and high initial antibody titres have been suggested as potential risk factors.

### 2.17 Long-Term Prognosis

The prognosis of anti-GBM disease has been transformed by the introduction of PLEX and immunosuppression. Before the modern treatment era (pre-1975), mortality exceeded 90% at 1 year. With current triple therapy (PLEX + CyP + steroids), overall patient survival exceeds 80% at 5 years.

Renal prognosis depends critically on renal function at presentation. Patients presenting with serum creatinine <5.7 mg/dL have a >80% chance of renal recovery. Those presenting with creatinine >5.7 mg/dL have approximately 50% renal recovery. Patients requiring dialysis at presentation have less than a 10-20% chance of recovering independent renal function. Biopsy findings provide independent prognostic information: patients with <10% normal glomeruli or >50% fibrous crescents have negligible renal recovery potential.

Pulmonary haemorrhage carries significant mortality risk (10-15%) in the acute phase from respiratory failure, but survivors typically have complete pulmonary recovery without long-term respiratory impairment.

ESKD develops in 30-50% of surviving patients. Patients who reach ESKD are candidates for renal transplantation, ideally after a period of serological remission (anti-GBM negative for 6-12 months). Post-transplant recurrence of anti-GBM disease is rare (<5%) when the antibody is undetectable at the time of transplantation. Protocol biopsies may detect subclinical linear IgG deposition without active disease.

### 2.18 Evidence Summary

The evidence base for anti-GBM disease management derives from cohort studies and case series rather than randomised trials due to disease rarity. The seminal report by Lockwood et al. (1975, Lancet) first described the combination of PLEX and immunosuppression. Johnson et al. (1985, Medicine) established the prognostic value of presenting renal function. Levy et al. (2001, JASN) reported the largest single-centre cohort (71 patients) demonstrating that dialysis dependence at presentation predicted <10% renal recovery. Cui et al. (2011, KI) provided comprehensive Chinese cohort data confirming the prognostic value of biopsy chronicity. Heitz et al. (2021, NDT) validated the association between the proportion of normal glomeruli and renal outcomes. McAdoo et al. (2017, KI) characterised the outcomes of double-positive disease, establishing its distinct clinical course. Hellmark et al. (2023, NDT) provided contemporary outcome data from the European cohort confirming <3% relapse. The KDIGO 2021 and 2025 guidelines synthesise this evidence into treatment recommendations.

### 2.21 Notes

This knowledge specification is intended for clinical decision support in anti-GBM disease. Key clinical pearls include: (1) anti-GBM disease is a medical emergency requiring same-day initiation of PLEX; waiting for full biopsy results is not acceptable if serology is positive; (2) linear IgG on immunofluorescence is pathognomonic and distinguishes anti-GBM from all other glomerulonephritides; (3) the bimodal age distribution requires high clinical suspicion in both young males with haemoptysis and elderly females with RPGN; (4) renal function at presentation is the dominant prognostic factor and determines counselling about expected outcomes; (5) <3% relapse means immunosuppression can be withdrawn at 12-18 months in most patients, unlike ANCA vasculitis; (6) double-positive patients (5%) have worse renal outcomes and require ANCA-style maintenance therapy; (7) smoking cessation is critical to reduce the risk of pulmonary haemorrhage and relapse; (8) renal transplantation after serological remission carries <5% recurrence risk.
