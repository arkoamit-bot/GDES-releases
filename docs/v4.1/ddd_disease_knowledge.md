## 2. Disease Record — Full 21-Field Schema

### 2.1 Definition

Dense Deposit Disease (DDD), formerly classified as MPGN type II, is a rare C3 glomerulopathy defined by the presence of highly electron-dense ribbon-like deposits within the glomerular basement membrane (GBM) on electron microscopy. It is caused by uncontrolled activation of the alternative complement pathway, most commonly due to C3 nephritic factor (C3NeF), resulting in continuous C3 cleavage and dense GBM transformation. DDD primarily affects children and young adults, presenting with nephrotic syndrome, hematuria, hypertension, and progressive CKD, with 50% reaching ESKD within 10 years. It is distinct from C3 glomerulonephritis (C3GN) by the characteristic EM appearance and more aggressive clinical course.

### 2.2 Epidemiology

Prevalence: extremely rare — estimated 2-3 per million population. Accounts for <1% of all GN biopsies. Peak incidence: children aged 5-15 years (60% of cases). Female predominance (2:1). Geographic variation: higher prevalence in Europe than Asia/Africa. No known racial predilection. Familial cases reported rarely (complement gene mutations). Associated with partial lipodystrophy (acquired — 10-20% of DDD patients have C3NeF and lipodystrophy). DRASH syndrome (rare: DDD, renal agenesis, skin abnormalities).

### 2.3 Aetiology

C3 NEPHRITIC FACTOR (C3NeF): Autoantibody that stabilizes C3 convertase (C3bBb) — most common cause (40-60% of DDD). COMPLEMENT GENE MUTATIONS: CFH (factor H) mutations/deficiencies, CFI (factor I) mutations, CFHR5 mutations/gene rearrangements, MCP (CD46) mutations. COMPLEMENT AUTOANTIBODIES: Anti-CFH autoantibodies (10-15%), anti-CFB autoantibodies, anti-factor I antibodies. INFECTION TRIGGERS: preceding streptococcal infection (some cases — molecular mimicry hypothesis), meningococcal infection. ASSOCIATED CONDITIONS: Partial lipodystrophy (10-20% — C3NeF causes complement-mediated adipocyte lysis), monoclonal gammopathy (rare — MGUS-related DDD in older adults).

### 2.4 Pathophysiology

Alternative complement pathway dysregulation is central. Normally, C3 is activated at low levels and tightly regulated by CFH, CFI, and MCP. In DDD: C3NeF binds to C3bBb (C3 convertase) and protects it from CFH-mediated decay, increasing its half-life 10-fold. This leads to uncontrolled C3 cleavage and massive C3b deposition along the GBM. The dense transformation of the GBM results from continuous C3b deposition with cross-linking and modification of the collagen IV matrix over time. The GBM becomes thickened, electron-dense, and ribbon-like due to accumulated complement proteins (C3, C5b-9, complement regulators) and altered basement membrane components. C5b-9 (MAC) formation drives podocyte injury, endothelial damage, and progressive glomerulosclerosis. Mesangial proliferation and matrix expansion occur. The process is distinct from C3GN: in DDD, deposits are primarily within the GBM proper (intramembranous), while in C3GN they are subendothelial and mesangial.

### 2.5 Clinical Presentation

CHILDREN/ADOLESCENTS: Nephrotic syndrome (50-60%) — generalized edema, foamy urine, hypoalbuminemia, hyperlipidemia. Hematuria: microscopic (90%), macroscopic (10-20%). Hypertension (30-40%). Non-nephrotic proteinuria with hematuria (20-30%). Acute nephritic syndrome (5-10%): oliguria, hypertension, hematuria, AKI. Partial lipodystrophy: loss of facial and upper body subcutaneous fat (10-20%). ADULTS: Less nephrotic — proteinuria with progressive CKD (40%). Hematuria common. Hypertension (50%). ESKD at presentation (10%). OCULAR: Drusen-like deposits in Bruch membrane (similar to age-related macular degeneration — can cause visual impairment). Acquired partial lipodystrophy (facial fat loss). Monoclonal gammopathy-associated DDD (older adults).

### 2.6 Diagnostic Criteria

| # | Criterion | Essential | Evidence Level |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Dense ribbon-like intramembranous deposits within GBM on EM (pathognomonic) | Yes | 1 |
| 2 | C3-dominant immunofluorescence (± low IgG, IgM, IgA) | Yes | 1 |
| 3 | Low serum C3 (due to alternative pathway consumption) with normal C4 | Yes | 2 |
| 4 | C3 nephritic factor (C3NeF) positive | No | 2 |
| 5 | Complement gene mutation or autoantibody (CFH, CFI, CFHR) | No | 2 |
| 6 | Mesangial hypercellularity ± endocapillary proliferation on LM | No | 2 |
| 7 | Exclusion of lupus nephritis (full-house IF), cryoglobulinemic GN, post-infectious GN | Yes | 1 |

### 2.7 Differential Diagnosis

c3, mpgn, lupus, iga, irgn, cryoglobulinemic

### 2.8 Laboratory Findings

- Low serum C3 (alternative pathway consumption) — hallmark, seen in 50-70%
- Normal serum C4 — distinguishes DDD from IC-MPGN and lupus (which also consume C4)
- C3 nephritic factor (C3NeF) positive in 40-60%
- Elevated sC5b-9 (terminal complement complex) — reflects ongoing complement activation
- Low CFH, CFI or CH50 (if complement deficiency)
- Anti-CFH autoantibodies (10-15%)
- Genetic testing: CFH, CFI, MCP, CFHR5 mutations (20-30%)
- Acanthosis nigricans / partial lipodystrophy (clinical exam)
- Ophthalmology: drusen-like deposits on fundoscopy
- Nephrotic-range proteinuria (UPCR >3.5), hypoalbuminemia
- eGFR: variable — normal early, progressive decline
- Serum creatinine: rising as CKD advances

### 2.9 Biopsy Findings

- LM: Mesangial hypercellularity (diffuse or focal) — 80%
- LM: Endocapillary proliferation (50%)
- LM: GBM thickening with ribbon-like appearance on silver stain (advanced)
- LM: Nodular mesangial sclerosis (resembling DKD) in advanced cases
- LM: Crescent formation (10-20%) — poor prognosis
- LM: Tubular atrophy and interstitial fibrosis (proportional to CKD stage)
- IF: C3-dominant (3+) staining along GBM and mesangium — granular or linear
- IF: C5b-9 (MAC) staining along GBM
- IF: Immunoglobulins (IgG, IgM, IgA): negative or trace — key distinction from IC-MPGN
- IF: Properdin and CFH staining may be positive
- EM: PATHOGNOMONIC: highly electron-dense, continuous ribbon-like transformation of GBM (intramembranous)
- EM: Dense deposits in mesangial matrix and Bowman capsule (also dense)
- EM: No subepithelial or subendothelial immune complex deposits
- EM: Podocyte foot process effacement (progressive)

| eGFR <30 at diagnosis | Very high risk of ESKD within 1-2 years | High |
| Male gender | Faster progression to ESKD | Moderate |
| Childhood onset | Better treatment response than adult onset | Low |
| C3NeF persistence | Ongoing complement activation correlates with progression | Moderate |
| Post-transplant recurrence | Up to 50% recurrence — graft loss risk | High |

### 2.12 Treatment Overview

No proven disease-modifying therapy exists for DDD. Treatment is largely supportive and extrapolated from C3GN experience. RAASi (ACEi/ARB) for proteinuria reduction and renoprotection (KDIGO 1B). Immunosuppression: corticosteroids (prednisone 1 mg/kg) are commonly used (KDIGO 2C) but evidence is weak — may benefit children more than adults. MMF: steroid-sparing agent — some benefit in case series (KDIGO 2D). Complement-directed therapy: eculizumab (C5 inhibitor) — case reports show benefit in rapidly progressive DDD, but not consistently effective (KDIGO 2D). Emerging: iptacopan (factor B inhibitor), avacopan (C5aR inhibitor), and other complement inhibitors in clinical trials. Plasma exchange: for anti-CFH antibody-mediated DDD or RPGN (KDIGO 2D). Mycophenolate mofetil: used as steroid-sparing agent (limited data). Transplant: definitive for ESKD but recurrence risk 40-50% in allograft. Routine ophthalmology: monitor for drusen and macular degeneration. Avoid live vaccines (complement inhibition). Meningococcal vaccination before eculizumab.

### 2.13 Treatment Algorithm

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | Confirm diagnosis: renal biopsy (EM for dense deposits), complement studies (C3, C4, C3NeF, CH50, CFH, CFI), genetic testing |
| 2 | Supportive therapy: RAASi (ACEi/ARB) for proteinuria. BP target <130/80 |
| 3 | Consider immunosuppression: prednisone 1 mg/kg/day x8 wks then taper (especially in children). Add MMF if steroid-dependent |
| 4 | Complement blockade: eculizumab 900 mg IV weekly x4 then 1200 mg q2w if rapidly progressive or crescentic DDD (meningococcal vaccination required) |
| 5 | PLEX + immunosuppression for Anti-CFH antibody-mediated DDD or severe RPGN |
| 6 | ESKD preparation: RRT when eGFR <20. Transplant evaluation — counsel on 40-50% recurrence risk |
| 7 | Post-transplant monitoring: complement studies, urine protein, graft biopsy for recurrence surveillance |
| 8 | Ophthalmology: annual fundoscopy for drusen/macular degeneration |

### 2.14 Monitoring Protocol

Active disease: Q1-3 months: eGFR, UPCR, C3, C3NeF, BP. On eculizumab: CH50 (target <10%), meningococcal antibody titers annually, monitor for Neisseria infection. During immunosuppression: monthly CBC, LFTs, glucose. Stable: Q3-6 months: eGFR, UPCR, BP. Post-transplant: Q1 month x6 then Q3 months — UPCR, eGFR, complement. Biopsy if proteinuria recurs. Ophthalmology: annual fundoscopy for drusen/macular degeneration (start at diagnosis). Ongoing complement monitoring even post-transplant.

### 2.15 Complications

- ESKD — 50% within 10 years; 90% by 20 years; most common cause of death
- Nephrotic syndrome — infections (peritonitis, cellulitis), thromboembolism (DVT, PE, renal vein thrombosis), hyperlipidemia
- Partial lipodystrophy — progressive loss of facial fat (psychological impact), may precede DDD by years
- Ocular drusen/macular degeneration — complement deposits in Bruch membrane; can cause vision loss
- Post-transplant recurrence — 40-50% in allograft; graft loss 30% within 5 years post-recurrence
- Infection risk — complement deficiency (low C3) + immunosuppression + eculizumab (Neisseria)
- Hypertension — contributes to progressive CKD
- Cardiovascular disease — accelerated by CKD, nephrotic hyperlipidemia

### 2.16 Relapse Information

DDD is a chronic progressive disease with no true relapsing-remitting pattern. Proteinuria may fluctuate with treatment but disease progression is relentless in most cases. Treatment response is defined as proteinuria reduction and stabilization of eGFR, not complete remission. Post-transplant recurrence: up to 40-50% — occurs at a median of 6-12 months post-transplant. Recurrence risk factors: C3NeF positivity at transplant, rapid original disease course, live-related donor (some series). Recurrent DDD can cause graft loss in 30% within 5 years. C3GN recurrence in transplant is lower (20-30%). Management of recurrence: eculizumab may stabilize or improve graft function in some cases.

### 2.17 Long-Term Prognosis

Children: 50% ESKD within 10 years of diagnosis. 90% ESKD by 20 years. Better renal survival than adults. Adult-onset: more aggressive — 50% ESKD within 5 years. Worse prognosis with: crescents >20%, eGFR <30 at diagnosis, nephrotic-range proteinuria, hypertension. Treatment effects: limited evidence — immunosuppression may slow progression in some children. Eculizumab: selected cases show stabilization but no prospective trial. Transplant: patient survival excellent but recurrence risk high. Mortality: primarily due to ESKD and cardiovascular disease. Partial lipodystrophy: cosmetic only — does not affect survival. Ocular drusen: can cause visual impairment in long-term survivors.

### 2.18 Evidence Summary

No RCTs specific to DDD — all evidence from case series, registry studies, and expert opinion. Largest series: n=80 DDD patients from the Rare GN Registry (C3G Registry). RAASi: observational evidence for renoprotection (KDIGO 1B). Corticosteroids: limited benefit in children; no proven benefit in adults (case series). Eculizumab: ~30 reported DDD cases; 50% showed stabilization or improvement; predictors of response unclear. C3NeF assay: standardized ELISA available; correlates with disease activity. Complement genetics: next-generation sequencing panel available for CFH, CFI, MCP, CFHR genes. Post-transplant recurrence: C3G Registry data shows 50% recurrence for DDD vs 20% for C3GN. KDIGO 2025 C3G Update: expanded section on complement inhibitor therapy.
