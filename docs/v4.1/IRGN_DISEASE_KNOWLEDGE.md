## 2. Disease Record — Full 21-Field Schema

### 2.1 Definition

Infection-Related Glomerulonephritis (IRGN) is an immune-complex-mediated glomerular disease triggered by an infection at a non-renal site, most commonly group A Streptococcus (GAS). It is characterised by acute nephritic syndrome developing 1-4 weeks after infection, transient low serum C3 with normal C4, and glomerular deposition of IgG and C3 producing a granular "lumpy-bumpy" pattern on immunofluorescence. Classic post-streptococcal GN (PSGN) is the prototypical form, but numerous other bacterial, viral, fungal, and parasitic infections can trigger an identical renal histology.

### 2.2 Epidemiology

Incidence has declined dramatically in developed countries over the past 50 years. Current incidence: 0.3-2.0/100,000/year in developed nations; 9-28/100,000/year in developing regions. Peak age 2-12 years (classic childhood PSGN). Second peak in adults >60 years (often with comorbidities). Male:female 2:1. GAS pharyngitis strains: M types 1, 3, 5, 6, 12, 18, 49, 55, 57, 59, 60, 63. GAS impetigo strains: M types 2, 42, 49, 55, 56, 57, 58, 60, 61. Overall PSGN attack rate: 5-10% after pharyngitis, 15-25% after impetigo. In developing countries: leading cause of paediatric acute nephritis. Non-streptococcal IRGN increasing in adults (Staph aureus, MRSA, Gram-negative).

### 2.3 Aetiology

BACTERIAL (most common): Group A Streptococcus (M types 1, 12, 49), Group C and G Streptococcus, Staphylococcus aureus (including MRSA), Streptococcus pneumoniae, Haemophilus influenzae, Mycoplasma pneumoniae, Brucella, Leptospira, Salmonella typhi, Treponema pallidum, Coxiella burnetii, Mycobacterium leprae, Neisseria meningitidis. VIRAL: Hepatitis B and C, HIV, EBV, CMV, Varicella zoster, Influenza, Parvovirus B19, Coxsackie, Adenovirus, Measles. FUNGAL: Candida, Coccidioides, Histoplasma. PARASITIC: Plasmodium malariae (quartan malaria), P. falciparum, Schistosoma mansoni, Toxoplasma gondii, Wuchereria bancrofti. The common mechanism: infection triggers immune response → circulating immune complexes deposit in glomeruli → in situ immune complex formation with planted antigens → complement activation.

### 2.4 Pathophysiology

Group A Streptococcus produces nephritogenic antigens (NAPlr, SpeB, zymogen) that are structurally homologous to glomerular proteins. These antigens are deposited in the glomerular basement membrane, where they bind circulating antibodies forming in situ immune complexes. The classical pathway of complement is activated (IgG-containing complexes), leading to C3 and C5b-9 deposition. Subepithelial electron-dense deposits (humps) are pathognomonic on EM. Cellular infiltration (neutrophils, macrophages, T-cells) contributes to endocapillary proliferation. In severe cases: crescent formation (cellular/fibrocellular) driven by fibrin and macrophage activation. The alternative complement pathway is also activated through C3b deposition on GBM, explaining low C3 with normal C4.

### 2.5 Clinical Presentation

Latent period: 1-3 weeks (mean 10 days) after GAS pharyngitis; 3-6 weeks after GAS impetigo. Acute nephritic syndrome (classic tetrad): (1) Haematuria (cola-coloured urine in 30-50%, microscopic in nearly all), (2) Hypertension (60-80%), (3) Periorbital oedema (85%), (4) Oliguria (50%). Proteinuria: mild to moderate (subnephrotic), nephrotic range in 5-10%. Systemic symptoms: fever, headache, malaise, nausea, anorexia. Severe presentations: acute kidney injury (20-40%), hypertensive encephalopathy (5-10%), pulmonary oedema from fluid overload, rapidly progressive GN (1-5%). In adults: often less classic presentation; hypertension and AKI more prominent. Elderly: atypical presentation with confusion, volume overload, multiple comorbidities.

### 2.8 Laboratory Findings

- Urinalysis: dysmorphic RBCs, RBC casts, proteinuria (variable, usually <3 g/day)
- Low C3 (transient, normalises in 4-8 weeks) — KEY DIAGNOSTIC FEATURE
- Normal C4 (alternative pathway activation)
- Low CH50
- Elevated ASO titre (strep pharyngitis) or anti-DNAse B (impetigo)
- Anti-NAPlr antibodies (specific for PSGN)
- Elevated ESR, CRP
- Serum creatinine: elevated in 20-40%, may require dialysis temporarily
- Serum albumin: normal or mildly low (nephrotic range in 5-10%)
- Blood cultures and throat/ skin cultures for active infection
- Cryoglobulins (may be positive)
- ANA, ANCA, anti-GBM: negative (important to exclude other causes)

### 2.9 Biopsy Findings

- LM: Diffuse endocapillary proliferative GN (hypercellularity from endothelial and mesangial cell proliferation, neutrophil and monocyte infiltration)
- LM: Capillary lumen narrowing or occlusion by proliferating cells and infiltrating leucocytes
- LM: Mesangial hypercellularity and matrix expansion
- LM: Crescents in severe cases (cellular > fibrocellular)
- IF: Granular IgG and C3 deposits along capillary walls and in mesangium ("lumpy-bumpy" pattern) — PATHOGNOMONIC
- IF: C3 typically brightest; properdin, C5b-9 also present
- IF: IgA and IgM may be present in lesser intensity
- EM: Subepithelial electron-dense "humps" — PATHOGNOMONIC
- EM: Mesangial and intramembranous deposits also common
- EM: GBM normal thickness and contour (no spikes or splitting)

### 2.11 Risk Stratification

| Factor | Impact | Risk Level |
|---|---|---|
| Age | Children (<12): excellent prognosis. Adults >40: worse outcomes. Elderly >65: highest ESKD risk | High |
| Crescents on biopsy | >50% crescents associated with progressive CKD and ESKD | High |
| Nephrotic-range proteinuria | Associated with worse long-term outcomes | Moderate |
| AKI at presentation (Cr >3.0) | Higher risk of incomplete renal recovery | High |
| Oliguria >1 week | Predicts prolonged recovery and possible progression | Moderate |
| Hypertension severity | Persistent HTN >6 months associated with worse outcomes | Moderate |
| Comorbidities (diabetes, HIV, cirrhosis) | Worse prognosis; slower resolution | High |
| Persistent low C3 >8 weeks | Suggests underlying complement abnormality or alternate diagnosis | High |

### 2.12 Treatment Overview

SUPPORTIVE CARE (mainstay): BP control (amlodipine, nifedipine, labetalol — ACEi/ARB if persistent proteinuria), diuretics for oedema/volume overload, sodium and fluid restriction. TREAT INFECTION: appropriate antibiotics for active infection (penicillin for GAS). STEROIDS: NOT recommended for typical childhood PSGN. Consider methylprednisolone pulse (10-30 mg/kg/day x 3) followed by oral taper for crescentic >50% or RPGN. CYCLOPHOSPHAMIDE: reserved for severe crescentic disease with rapid progression. MMF: limited evidence; considered for steroid-dependent proteinuria. PLASMA EXCHANGE: for RPGN with dialysis dependency or pulmonary haemorrhage. DIALYSIS: temporary for AKI with fluid overload, hyperkalaemia, uraemia. RENAL TRANSPLANT: ESKD in <1% children; defer until 12 months after recovery (recurrence exceptional).

### 2.13 Treatment Algorithms

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | Confirm diagnosis: nephritic syndrome + latent period + low C3 + evidence of infection |
| 2 | Hospitalise if hypertensive, oliguric, Cr elevated, or complications |
| 3 | All patients: supportive care — BP control, diuretics, fluid/sodium restriction |
| 4 | Treat active infection: antibiotics as indicated (penicillin for GAS 10 days) |
| 5 | Monitor: daily weight, BP, urine output, Cr, C3 weekly |
| 6 | If >50% crescents or RPGN: pulse methylprednisolone then oral taper; consider cyclophosphamide |
| 7 | If dialysis-dependent with crescents: consider plasma exchange |
| 8 | Long-term follow-up: BP, Cr, urinalysis at 1, 3, 6, 12 months then annually |

### 2.14 Monitoring Protocol

ACUTE PHASE (daily): BP, urine output, weight, dipstick protein/haematuria. Weekly: Cr, electrolytes, C3, UPCR. CONVALESCENT (monthly): Cr, UPCR, C3 until normalised (usually 4-8 weeks). LONG-TERM: Cr, BP, urinalysis at 3, 6, 12 months post-recovery. If normal at 12 months: discharge. If proteinuria or HTN persists: annual monitoring indefinitely.

### 2.15 Complications

1. Acute kidney injury requiring dialysis (20-40% of adults, 5-10% of children)
2. Hypertensive encephalopathy (5-10% of children)
3. Pulmonary oedema from fluid overload
4. Rapidly progressive GN / crescentic GN (1-5%)
5. Progressive CKD / ESKD (<1% children, 10-20% adults)
6. Persistent proteinuria (>1 year: 10-20%)
7. Persistent hypertension (>1 year: 10-15%)
8. Nephrotic syndrome (5-10%)
9. Infection complications (sepsis, endocarditis from untreated infection)

### 2.16 Relapse Information

True relapse (recurrence after complete recovery) is RARE in classic PSGN (<1%). Recurrent PSGN can occur with repeated GAS infections or in complement-deficient patients. Persistent or progressive disease after 6-12 months suggests alternative diagnosis or underlying renal disease. Post-transplant recurrence: EXCEPTIONAL (<1%) — typically defer transplant until 12 months after complete recovery.

### 2.17 Long-Term Prognosis

CHILDREN: excellent. Full recovery in 90-95% at 6-12 months. Microscopic haematuria may persist 1-2 years. ESKD <1%. ADULTS: worse prognosis. Full recovery in 50-70%. Persistent proteinuria in 15-25%. HTN in 10-20%. ESKD in 10-20% (higher if >40 years). ELDERLY: poorest. Full recovery in 40-60%. Persistent disease in 30-40%. ESKD in 20-30%. Predictors of poor outcome: crescents >50%, nephrotic-range proteinuria, advanced age, diabetes, persistent low C3 >8 weeks.

### 2.18 Evidence Summary

No large RCTs for IRGN therapy (self-limited disease in most). Supportive care based on observational studies and consensus. Steroids: no RCT evidence for routine use; reserved for crescentic disease (case series, GRADE 2D). Cyclophosphamide: case series in RPGN/crescentic (GRADE 2D). Antibiotics: proven benefit for treating active infection but does not alter renal outcome of established PSGN (RCT data). Key cohort studies: NIH PSGN natural history study (Baldwin 1974), Niaudet 1999 (paediatric outcomes), Nasr 2013 (adult and elderly IRGN). No FDA-approved therapy specific to IRGN.

## 4. Evidence Base

### 4.1 Key Clinical Studies

- **Natural history of PSGN (Baldwin 1974):** Landmark cohort defining renal outcomes
- **Paediatric PSGN outcomes (Niaudet 1999):** 95% recovery in children
- **Adult and elderly IRGN (Nasr 2013):** Worse outcomes in older populations
- **Staphylococcal IRGN (Worawichawong 2020):** Increasing incidence; distinction from classic PSGN
- **NAPlr antigen in pathogenesis (Mosquera 2019):** Key nephritogenic antigen

### 4.2 Ongoing Knowledge Gaps

- No completed RCTs for any IRGN-specific therapy
- Optimal immunosuppression regimen for crescentic disease
- Biomarkers to predict progression in adults
- Standardised treatment algorithms for non-strep IRGN
- Long-term cardiovascular outcomes in adult IRGN survivors
