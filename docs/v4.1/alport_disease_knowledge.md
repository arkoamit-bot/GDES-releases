## 2. Disease Record — Full 21-Field Schema

### 2.1 Definition

Alport syndrome is a hereditary disorder of type IV collagen caused by mutations in COL4A5 (X-linked), COL4A3, or COL4A4 (autosomal) genes. It is characterised by progressive nephropathy with haematuria and proteinuria leading to ESKD, sensorineural hearing loss, and ocular abnormalities (anterior lenticonus, dot-and-fleck retinopathy). The classic form is X-linked (85%), with males severely affected and females having variable penetrance. Autosomal recessive (14%) and dominant (1%) forms also exist.

### 2.2 Epidemiology

Prevalence ~1/50,000 live births. X-linked Alport (XLAS): 85% — COL4A5 mutations. Males: ESKD by age 20-40. Females: ESKD by 50-70 (incomplete penetrance). Autosomal recessive (ARAS): 14% — COL4A3/COL4A4 biallelic; ESKD by 20-30. Autosomal dominant (ADAS): 1% — milder, slower progression. Estimated 60,000-100,000 affected individuals in the US and Europe. Accounts for 0.5-1% of ESKD in young adults. Underdiagnosed due to variable phenotypes and overlap with TBMD.

### 2.3 Aetiology

XLAS: COL4A5 (Xq22.3) mutations — missense (30%), nonsense (15%), splice-site (20%), deletion/rearrangement (35%). Glycine substitutions in the collagenous domain are most common. ARAS: biallelic COL4A3 or COL4A4 mutations. ADAS: heterozygous COL4A3/COL4A4 with dominant-negative effect. De novo mutations: 10-15% of XLAS. Genetic heterogeneity: digenic inheritance (two genes) described in rare cases. The defective collagen IV alpha-3/4/5 network compromises GBM, cochlea, eye, and skin basement membrane integrity.

### 2.4 Pathophysiology

Type IV collagen [alpha-3,4,5] heterotrimer is the major structural component of mature GBM. Mutations disrupt heterotrimer assembly and secretion. XLAS: absent alpha-5 chain → reduced GBM alpha-3/4/5 network. The GBM is initially thin (childhood), then undergoes progressive thickening, splitting, and lamellation (basket-weave appearance) with alternating dense and lucent layers. The abnormal GBM is susceptible to mechanical stress and progressive scarring. Cochlea: abnormal capillary basement membranes in stria vascularis → high-frequency hearing loss (age 10-20 in males). Eye: abnormal anterior lens capsule basement membrane → anterior lenticonus (pathognomonic); abnormal retinal basement membrane → dot-and-fleck retinopathy. Podocyte injury and loss drive proteinuria progression.

### 2.5 Clinical Presentation

CHILDHOOD: Persistent microscopic haematuria (present from infancy in affected males). Episodic macroscopic haematuria (common). ADOLESCENCE: Progressive proteinuria (initially microalbuminuria, then nephrotic-range). Hypertension develops. Hearing loss begins in late childhood (high-frequency, bilateral, progressive). ADULTHOOD: ESKD develops in males by 20-40 (XLAS). Females: 15-30% develop ESKD by 60. Ocular: anterior lenticonus (protrusion of lens — pathognomonic, 20-30%), dot-and-fleck retinopathy (60-70%), recurrent corneal erosions. EXTRA-RENAL: sensorineural hearing loss (90% of XLAS males by age 40), anterior lenticonus, retinopathy. Leiomyomatosis (rare: contiguous COL4A5 + COL4A6 deletion). Aortic root dilation (increased risk, screen with echo).

### 2.7 Differential Diagnosis

thinBasementMembrane, iga, membranous, fsgs

### 2.8 Laboratory Findings

- Hematuria: persistent microscopic from infancy (dysmorphic RBCs, RBC casts)
- Proteinuria: progressive (microalbuminuria → macroalbuminuria → nephrotic-range)
- eGFR: normal early, progressive decline starting adolescence
- Serum creatinine: rising as CKD advances
- Genetic testing: COL4A5 (X-linked), COL4A3/COL4A4 (autosomal) mutations
- Skin biopsy: absent alpha-5(IV) in epidermal basement membrane (XLAS males)
- Renal biopsy IF: absent/ reduced alpha-5(IV) in GBM
- Audiometry: bilateral high-frequency sensorineural hearing loss
- Ophthalmology: anterior lenticonus, perimacular dot-and-fleck retinopathy
- Urine protein: increasing over time

### 2.9 Biopsy Findings

- LM: Early — normal or mild mesangial proliferation. Progressive — mesangial expansion, segmental/global glomerulosclerosis, tubular atrophy, interstitial fibrosis
- LM: Foam cells in interstitium (characteristic but not specific)
- IF: Negative for immunoglobulins and complement (no immune deposits)
- IF: Absent or reduced collagen alpha-5(IV) chain staining in GBM (XLAS males)
- IF: Normal alpha-5(IV) in Bowman capsule (positive internal control)
- IF: Skin biopsy: absent alpha-5(IV) in epidermal basement membrane (XLAS males)
- EM: EARLY: GBM thinning (childhood) — may resemble TBMD
- EM: PROGRESSIVE: GBM thickening, splitting, lamellation (basket-weave) — PATHOGNOMONIC
- EM: Alternating lucent and dense layers in thickened GBM
- EM: Podocyte foot process effacement (progressive)
- EM: No electron-dense immune deposits

### 2.11 Risk Stratification

| Male gender (XLAS) | ESKD by 20-40 vs females 50-70 — most significant risk factor | High |
| Truncating COL4A5 mutation | ESKD by age 20 (vs 40 for missense) | High |
| Proteinuria >1 g/day | Strong predictor of progression and ESKD | High |
| Large deletion (COL4A5 + COL4A6) | Leiomyomatosis risk; aortic dilation | High |
| Hearing loss onset before age 10 | Predicts severe renal phenotype | Moderate |
| Biallelic COL4A3/COL4A4 (ARAS) | ESKD by 20-30, similar severity to XLAS males | High |
| Obesity and hypertension | Accelerate CKD progression | Moderate |

### 2.12 Treatment Overview

RAASi (ACEi/ARB) from time of diagnosis — reduces proteinuria by 40-60% and slows ESKD progression by 50% (observational studies). Start as soon as proteinuria develops, even in childhood. SGLT2i: emerging evidence for additional renoprotection (dapagliflozin/empagliflozin — extrapolated from DKD trials). BP target <130/80. Avoid nephrotoxins (NSAIDs, aminoglycosides). Hearing aids for hearing loss. Ophthalmology: regular monitoring for lenticonus; surgical management if needed. Renal transplantation: definitive treatment — NO recurrence in allograft (recipient lacks COL4A5, donor kidney expresses normal collagen IV). COL4A5-negative females may be living kidney donors (low risk). Family planning: genetic counseling for inheritance risk. Aortic root screening by echocardiogram (rare associated dilation). Gene therapy and targeted therapies in development.

### 2.13 Treatment Algorithm

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | Confirm diagnosis: genetic testing (COL4A5/4A3/4A4), renal biopsy, audiometry, ophthalmology |
| 2 | Initiate RAASi: ACEi/ARB as soon as proteinuria develops (even microalbuminuria). Titrate to max tolerated dose |
| 3 | Add SGLT2i: dapagliflozin 10 mg for additional renoprotection (eGFR >=20) |
| 4 | BP management: target <130/80; add CCB/thiazide if needed |
| 5 | Monitor progression: Q3 months: eGFR, UPCR, BP. Annual: audiometry, ophthalmology |
| 6 | Genetic counseling: inheritance risk, family planning, cascade testing of at-risk relatives |
| 7 | ESKD preparation: RRT planning when eGFR <20. Living donor preferred. NO recurrence post-transplant |
| 8 | Multidisciplinary: nephrology + audiology + ophthalmology + genetics + cardiology (aortic screening) |

### 2.14 Monitoring Protocol

CHILDHOOD/ADOLESCENCE: Annual: BP, eGFR, UPCR, audiometry. Ophthalmology every 2 years. ADULTHOOD: Q3 months: eGFR, UPCR, BP if proteinuria present. Annual: audiometry, ophthalmology, echocardiogram (aortic root). During RAASi/SGLT2i initiation: monthly Cr, K+ for 3 months. Pre-transplant: cardiac workup, genetic counseling. Post-transplant: standard monitoring (no Alport recurrence). Aortic root screening: echocardiogram at diagnosis and every 3-5 years.

### 2.15 Complications

- ESKD requiring RRT — universal in XLAS males, 15-30% in XLAS females
- Sensorineural hearing loss — 90% of XLAS males by age 40; hearing aids needed
- Anterior lenticonus — 20-30%; may require cataract surgery
- Dot-and-fleck retinopathy — 60-70%; usually asymptomatic
- Aortic root dilation — increased risk; screen with echocardiogram
- Leiomyomatosis (rare) — COL4A5 + COL4A6 contiguous deletion; esophageal/genital smooth muscle tumors
- Depression and anxiety — chronic disease, hearing loss, genetic implications for family

### 2.16 Relapse Information

Alport syndrome is a progressive, not relapsing-remitting disease. Proteinuria increases gradually over years. ESKD progression is relentless without treatment. RAASi slows but does not halt progression. NO recurrence after kidney transplant (donor kidney expresses normal collagen IV alpha-3/4/5). This is unique among genetic kidney diseases. Post-transplant anti-GBM disease (rare complication — 2-5% of XLAS males receiving transplant, due to immune response to normal collagen IV alpha-5 chain in donor kidney; presents as crescentic GN).

### 2.17 Long-Term Prognosis

XLAS males: ESKD by 20-40 (truncating mutations: ~20; missense: ~30-40). Without RAASi: faster progression. Hearing loss by 10-20. XLAS females: ESKD by 50-60 (15-30% penetrance). ARAS: ESKD by 20-30, both sexes equally. ADAS: slower — ESKD in 50-60s. Treatment slows ESKD onset by 5-10 years. Transplant outcomes: excellent — no recurrence. 5-year graft survival 85-90%. Anti-GBM disease post-transplant: 2-5% risk (higher in XLAS males with large deletions).

### 2.18 Evidence Summary

No RCTs specific to Alport syndrome — treatment is based on observational cohort studies and extrapolation from IgA nephropathy and DKD trials. RAASi: largest observational study (n=300+ Alport patients) showed 50% reduction in ESKD risk; proteinuria reduction 40-60%. SGLT2i: EMPA-KIDNEY and DAPA-CKD included Alport patients in subgroup analyses; benefit expected but not proven separately in Alport. Gene therapy: antisense oligonucleotides in clinical trials for specific COL4A5 mutations. Animal models (COL4A5 knockout mice) show benefit of RAASi and SGLT2i. No FDA-approved therapy specifically for Alport syndrome.
